<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Forecourt">Forecourt</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Megalithic_architectural_elements&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Forecourt">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Archaeology" title="Archaeology">archaeology</a>, a forecourt is the name given to the area in front of certain types of <a href="/wiki/Chamber_tomb" title="Chamber tomb">chamber tomb</a>. Forecourts were probably the venue for <a href="/wiki/Ritualism" title="Ritualism" class="mw-redirect">ritual</a> practices connected with the burial and commemoration of the dead in the past societies that built these types of <a href="/wiki/Tomb" title="Tomb">tombs</a>.</p>
<p>In European <a href="/wiki/Megalithic" title="Megalithic" class="mw-redirect">megalithic</a> architecture, forecourts are curved in plan with the entrance to the tomb at the apex of the open semicircle enclosure that the forecourt creates. The sides were built up by either large upright stones or walls of smaller stones laid atop one another.</p>
<p>Some also had paved floors and some had blocking stones erected in front of them to seal the tomb such as at <a href="/wiki/West_Kennet_Long_Barrow" title="West Kennet Long Barrow">West Kennet Long Barrow</a>. Their shape, which suggests an attempt to focus attention on the tomb itself may mean that they were used ceremonially as a kind of open air auditorium during ceremonies. Excavation within some forecourts has recovered animal <a href="/wiki/Bone" title="Bone">bone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pottery" title="Pottery">pottery</a> and evidence of burning suggesting that they served as locations for <a href="/wiki/Votive" title="Votive" class="mw-redirect">votive</a> offerings or feasting dedicated to the <a href="/wiki/Dead" title="Dead" class="mw-redirect">dead</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Kerb_or_peristalith">Kerb or peristalith</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Megalithic_architectural_elements&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Kerb or peristalith">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<dl>
<dd><i>See <a href="/wiki/Curb_(road)" title="Curb (road)" class="mw-redirect">curb (road)</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Road" title="Road">roadside</a> edge.</i></dd>
</dl>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Coldrumeast.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Coldrumeast.jpg/400px-Coldrumeast.jpg" width="400" height="231" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Coldrumeast.jpg/600px-Coldrumeast.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Coldrumeast.jpg/800px-Coldrumeast.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1248" data-file-height="720" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Coldrumeast.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Although the barrow mound is now almost gone, the surrounding peristalith at <a href="/wiki/Coldrum_Stones" title="Coldrum Stones" class="mw-redirect">Coldrum Stones</a> in <a href="/wiki/Kent" title="Kent">Kent</a> still survives (foreground). The stone burial chamber can be seen in the background.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Archaeology" title="Archaeology">archaeology</a>, kerb or peristalith is the name for a stone ring built to enclose and sometimes <a href="/wiki/Revetting" title="Revetting" class="mw-redirect">revet</a> the <a href="/wiki/Cairn" title="Cairn">cairn</a> or <a href="/wiki/Tumulus" title="Tumulus">barrow</a> built over a <a href="/wiki/Chamber_tomb" title="Chamber tomb">chamber tomb</a>.</p>
<p>European <a href="/wiki/Dolmen" title="Dolmen">dolmens</a> especially <a href="/wiki/Hunebed" title="Hunebed" class="mw-redirect">hunebed</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dyss" title="Dyss" class="mw-redirect">dyss</a> burials often provide examples of the use of kerbs in <a href="/wiki/Megalith" title="Megalith">megalithic</a> architecture but they were also added to other kinds of <a href="/wiki/Chamber_tomb" title="Chamber tomb">chamber tomb</a>. Kerbs may be built in a <a href="/wiki/Dry_stone_wall" title="Dry stone wall" class="mw-redirect">dry stone wall</a> method employing small blocks or more commonly using larger stones set in the ground. When larger stones are employed, peristalith is the term more properly used. Often, when the earth barrow has been weathered away, the surviving kerb can give the impression of being a <a href="/wiki/Stone_circle" title="Stone circle">stone circle</a> although these monuments date from considerably later. Excavation of barrows without stone rings such as Fussell's Lodge in <a href="/wiki/Wiltshire" title="Wiltshire">Wiltshire</a> suggests that, in these examples, timber or turf was used to define a kerb instead.</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a>, the enclosing nature of kerbs has been suggested to be analogous to later <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a> stone and <a href="/wiki/Timber_circle" title="Timber circle">timber circles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Henge" title="Henge">henges</a> which also demonstrate an attempt to demarcate a distinct, <a href="/wiki/Circle" title="Circle">round</a> area for <a href="/wiki/Ritual" title="Ritual">ritual</a> or <a href="/wiki/Funeral" title="Funeral">funerary</a> purposes. Famous sites with kerbs include <a href="/wiki/Newgrange" title="Newgrange">Newgrange</a> where many of the stones are etched with <a href="/wiki/Megalithic_art" title="Megalithic art">megalithic art</a>. An example of the dry stone wall type of kerb can be seen at <a href="/wiki/Parc_le_Breos" title="Parc le Breos">Parc le Breos</a> in <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Orthostat">Orthostat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Megalithic_architectural_elements&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Orthostat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>An <b>orthostat</b> is a large stone with a more or less slab-like shape that has been artificially set upright (so a cube-shaped block is not an orthostat). <a href="/wiki/Menhir" title="Menhir">Menhirs</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Standing_stone" title="Standing stone" class="mw-redirect">standing stones</a> are technically orthostats although the term is only used by <a href="/wiki/Archaeologist" title="Archaeologist" class="mw-redirect">archaeologists</a> to describe individual prehistoric stones that constitute part of larger structures. Common examples include the walls of <a href="/wiki/Chamber_tomb" title="Chamber tomb">chamber tombs</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Megalith" title="Megalith">megalithic</a> monuments and the vertical elements of the trilithons at <a href="/wiki/Stonehenge" title="Stonehenge">Stonehenge</a>. Especially later, othostats may carved with decoration in <a href="/wiki/Relief" title="Relief">relief</a>, a common feature of <a href="/wiki/Hittite_architecture" title="Hittite architecture" class="mw-redirect">Hittite architecture</a> among other styles.</p>
<p>Many orthostats were a focus for <a href="/wiki/Megalithic_art" title="Megalithic art">megalithic art</a>, as at <a href="/wiki/Knowth" title="Knowth">Knowth</a> in Ireland.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Port-hole_slab">Port-hole slab</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Megalithic_architectural_elements&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Port-hole slab">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Megalith" title="Megalith">megalithic</a> <a href="/wiki/Archaeology" title="Archaeology">archaeology</a> a port-hole slab is the name of an orthostat with a hole in it sometimes found forming the entrance to a <a href="/wiki/Chamber_tomb" title="Chamber tomb">chamber tomb</a>. The hole is usually circular but square examples or those made from two adjoining slabs each with a notch cut in it are known. They are common in the <a href="/wiki/Gallery_grave" title="Gallery grave">gallery graves</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Seine-Oise-Marne_culture" title="Seine-Oise-Marne culture">Seine-Oise-Marne culture</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Portal_stones">Portal stones</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Megalithic_architectural_elements&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Portal stones">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Portal stones are a pair of Megalithic orthostats, usually flanking the entrance to a <a href="/wiki/Chamber_tomb" title="Chamber tomb">chamber tomb</a>. They are commonly found in <a href="/wiki/Dolmens" title="Dolmens" class="mw-redirect">dolmens</a>. Examples may be seen at <a href="/wiki/Bohonagh" title="Bohonagh">Bohonagh</a> and <a href="/wiki/Knocknakilla" title="Knocknakilla">Knocknakilla</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Trilithon">Trilithon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Megalithic_architectural_elements&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Trilithon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Trilithon" title="Trilithon">Trilithon</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Trilithon_at_Stonehenge.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Trilithon_at_Stonehenge.jpg/220px-Trilithon_at_Stonehenge.jpg" width="220" height="331" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Trilithon_at_Stonehenge.jpg/330px-Trilithon_at_Stonehenge.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Trilithon_at_Stonehenge.jpg/440px-Trilithon_at_Stonehenge.jpg 2x" data-file-width="680" data-file-height="1024" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Trilithon_at_Stonehenge.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A trilithon at Stonehenge</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A trilithon (or trilith) is a structure consisting of two large vertical stones supporting a third stone set horizontally across the top. Commonly used in the context of <a href="/wiki/Megalithic" title="Megalithic" class="mw-redirect">megalithic</a> monuments, the most famous trilithons are those at <a href="/wiki/Stonehenge" title="Stonehenge">Stonehenge</a> and those found in the <a href="/wiki/Megalithic_Temples_of_Malta" title="Megalithic Temples of Malta">Megalithic Temples of Malta</a>.</p>
<p>The word <i>trilithon</i> is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> 'having three stones' (<b>Tri</b> - <i>three</i>, <b>lithos</b> - <i>stone</i>) and was first used by <a href="/wiki/William_Stukeley" title="William Stukeley">William Stukeley</a>. The term also describes the groups of three stones in the Hunebed tombs of the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> and the three massive stones forming part of the wall of the Roman Temple of <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a> at <a href="/wiki/Baalbek" title="Baalbek">Baalbek</a>, Lebanon.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
