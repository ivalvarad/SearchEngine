<p><b>Pan-Africanism</b> is an ideology and movement that encourages the solidarity of Africans worldwide.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> It is based on the belief that unity is vital to economic, social, and political progress and aims to "unify and uplift" people of African descent.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> The ideology asserts that the fate of all African peoples and countries are intertwined. At its core Pan-Africanism is "a belief that <a href="/wiki/Ethnic_groups_in_Africa" title="Ethnic groups in Africa" class="mw-redirect">African peoples</a>, both on the continent and in the <a href="/wiki/African_Diaspora" title="African Diaspora" class="mw-redirect">diaspora</a>, share not merely a common history, but a common destiny".<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Organization_of_African_Unity" title="Organization of African Unity" class="mw-redirect">Organization of African Unity</a> (now the <a href="/wiki/African_Union" title="African Union">African Union</a>) was established in 1963 to safeguard the sovereignty and territorial integrity of its Member States and to promote global relations within the framework of the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/African_Union_Commission" title="African Union Commission">African Union Commission</a> has its seat in <a href="/wiki/Addis_Ababa" title="Addis Ababa">Addis Ababa</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Pan-African_Parliament" title="Pan-African Parliament">Pan-African Parliament</a> has its seat in <a href="/wiki/Johannesburg" title="Johannesburg">Johannesburg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Midrand" title="Midrand">Midrand</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pan-Africanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_National_Archives_UK_-_CO_1069-50-1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/The_National_Archives_UK_-_CO_1069-50-1.jpg/170px-The_National_Archives_UK_-_CO_1069-50-1.jpg" width="170" height="233" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/The_National_Archives_UK_-_CO_1069-50-1.jpg/255px-The_National_Archives_UK_-_CO_1069-50-1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/The_National_Archives_UK_-_CO_1069-50-1.jpg/340px-The_National_Archives_UK_-_CO_1069-50-1.jpg 2x" data-file-width="680" data-file-height="931" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:The_National_Archives_UK_-_CO_1069-50-1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Kwame_Nkrumah" title="Kwame Nkrumah">Kwame Nkrumah</a>, an icon of Pan-Africanism</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Pan-Africanism stresses the need for "collective self-reliance".<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research#Primary.2C_secondary_and_tertiary_sources" title="Wikipedia:No original research"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable secondary sources. (October 2015)">non-primary source needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Pan-Africanism exists as a governmental and <a href="/wiki/Grassroots" title="Grassroots">grassroots</a> objective. Pan-African advocates include leaders such as <a href="/wiki/Haile_Selassie" title="Haile Selassie">Haile Selassie</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ahmed_Sekou_Toure" title="Ahmed Sekou Toure" class="mw-redirect">Ahmed Sekou Toure</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kwame_Nkrumah" title="Kwame Nkrumah">Kwame Nkrumah</a> and <a href="/wiki/Muammar_Gaddafi" title="Muammar Gaddafi">Muammar Gaddafi</a>, grassroots organizers such as <a href="/wiki/Marcus_Garvey" title="Marcus Garvey">Marcus Garvey</a> and <a href="/wiki/Malcolm_X" title="Malcolm X">Malcolm X</a>, academics such as <a href="/wiki/W._E._B._Du_Bois" title="W. E. B. Du Bois">W. E. B. Du Bois</a>, and others in the <a href="/wiki/African_Diaspora" title="African Diaspora" class="mw-redirect">diaspora</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Pan-Africanism_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pan-Africanism-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> Solidarity will enable self-reliance, allowing the continent's potential to independently provide for its people to be fulfilled. Crucially, an all-African alliance would empower African people globally.</p>
<p>The realization of the Pan-African objective would lead to "power consolidation in Africa", which "would compel a reallocation of global resources, as well as unleashing a fiercer psychological energy and political assertion...that would unsettle social and political (power) structures...in the Americas".<sup id="cite_ref-Agyeman.2C_O._1998_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Agyeman.2C_O._1998-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (October 2015)">page&#160;needed</span></a></i>]</sup> United, African nations will have the economic, political and social clout to act and compete on the world stage as do other large entities, such as the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> and the United States.</p>
<p>Advocates of Pan-Africanism – i.e. "Pan-Africans" or "Pan-Africanists" - often champion socialist principles and tend to be opposed to external political and economic involvement on the continent. Critics accuse the ideology of homogenizing the experience of people of African descent. They also point to the difficulties of reconciling current divisions within countries on the continent and within communities in the diaspora.<sup id="cite_ref-Agyeman.2C_O._1998_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Agyeman.2C_O._1998-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (October 2015)">page&#160;needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pan-Africanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As a philosophy, Pan-Africanism represents the aggregation of the historical, cultural, spiritual, artistic, scientific, and philosophical legacies of Africans from past times to the present. Pan-Africanism as an ethical system traces its origins from ancient times, and promotes values that are the product of the African civilization and the struggles against <a href="/wiki/Slavery" title="Slavery">slavery</a>, <a href="/wiki/Racism" title="Racism">racism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Colonialism" title="Colonialism">colonialism</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Neo-colonialism" title="Neo-colonialism" class="mw-redirect">neo-colonialism</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Pan-Africanism_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pan-Africanism-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Alongside a large number of slave insurrections, by the end of the 18th century a political movement developed across the <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> that sought to weld these disparate movements into a network of solidarity putting an end to this oppression. In <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>, the <i>Sons of Africa</i> was a political group addressed by <a href="/wiki/Quobna_Ottobah_Cugoano" title="Quobna Ottobah Cugoano" class="mw-redirect">Quobna Ottobah Cugoano</a> in the 1791 edition of his book <i>Thoughts and Sentiments on the Evil of Slavery</i>. The group addressed meetings and organised letter-writing campaigns, published campaigning material and visited <a href="/wiki/Parliament" title="Parliament">parliament</a>. They wrote to figures such as <a href="/wiki/Granville_Sharp" title="Granville Sharp">Granville Sharp</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_Pitt_the_Younger" title="William Pitt the Younger">William Pitt</a> and other members of the <a href="/wiki/White_people" title="White people">white</a> <a href="/wiki/Abolitionism_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Abolitionism in the United Kingdom">abolition</a> movement, as well as King <a href="/wiki/George_III" title="George III" class="mw-redirect">George III</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Prince_of_Wales" title="Prince of Wales">Prince of Wales</a>, the future <a href="/wiki/George_IV_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="George IV of the United Kingdom">George IV</a>.</p>
<p>Modern Pan-Africanism began around the start of the twentieth century. The African Association, later renamed the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pan-African_Association&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Pan-African Association (page does not exist)">Pan-African Association</a>, was established around 1897 by <a href="/wiki/Henry_Sylvester-Williams" title="Henry Sylvester-Williams">Henry Sylvester-Williams</a>, who organized the <a href="/wiki/First_Pan-African_Conference" title="First Pan-African Conference">First Pan-African Conference</a> in London in 1900.<sup id="cite_ref-The_History_of_Pan-Africanism_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_History_of_Pan-Africanism-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research#Primary.2C_secondary_and_tertiary_sources" title="Wikipedia:No original research"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable secondary sources. (October 2015)">non-primary source needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability#Self-published_sources" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag may rely on a self-published source. (October 2015)">self-published source?</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, the term is closely associated with <a href="/wiki/Afrocentrism" title="Afrocentrism">Afrocentrism</a>, an ideology of <a href="/wiki/African-American" title="African-American" class="mw-redirect">African-American</a> <a href="/wiki/Identity_politics" title="Identity politics">identity politics</a> that emerged during the <a href="/wiki/Civil_rights_movement" title="Civil rights movement" class="mw-redirect">civil rights movement</a> of the 1960s to 1970s.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (October 2015)">page&#160;needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Concept">Concept</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pan-Africanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Concept">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Pan_Africanism_mural_in_Tanzania.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Pan_Africanism_mural_in_Tanzania.jpg/220px-Pan_Africanism_mural_in_Tanzania.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Pan_Africanism_mural_in_Tanzania.jpg/330px-Pan_Africanism_mural_in_Tanzania.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Pan_Africanism_mural_in_Tanzania.jpg/440px-Pan_Africanism_mural_in_Tanzania.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4608" data-file-height="3456" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Pan_Africanism_mural_in_Tanzania.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A mural in western <a href="/wiki/Tanzania" title="Tanzania">Tanzania</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>As originally conceived by <a href="/wiki/Henry_Sylvester-Williams" title="Henry Sylvester-Williams">Henry Sylvester-Williams</a> (note: some history books credit this idea to <a href="/wiki/Edward_Wilmot_Blyden" title="Edward Wilmot Blyden">Edward Wilmot Blyden</a>), Pan-Africanism referred to the unity of all continental Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-Sculpting_a_Pan-African_Culture_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sculpting_a_Pan-African_Culture-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>During <a href="/wiki/Apartheid" title="Apartheid">apartheid</a> South Africa there was a <a href="/wiki/Pan_Africanist_Congress" title="Pan Africanist Congress" class="mw-redirect">Pan Africanist Congress</a> that dealt with the oppression of Africans in South Africa under Apartheid rule. Other pan-Africanist organizations include <a href="/wiki/Marcus_Garvey" title="Marcus Garvey">Garvey</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Universal_Negro_Improvement_Association" title="Universal Negro Improvement Association" class="mw-redirect">Universal Negro Improvement Association-African Communities League</a>, TransAfrica and the <a href="/wiki/Uhuru_Movement" title="Uhuru Movement">International People's Democratic Uhuru Movement</a>.</p>
<p>Additionally, Pan-Africanism is seen as an endeavour to return to what are deemed by its proponents singular, traditional African concepts about culture, society, and values. Examples of this include <a href="/wiki/L%C3%A9opold_S%C3%A9dar_Senghor" title="Léopold Sédar Senghor">Léopold Sédar Senghor's</a> <a href="/wiki/N%C3%A9gritude" title="Négritude">Négritude</a> movement, and <a href="/wiki/Mobutu_Sese_Seko" title="Mobutu Sese Seko">Mobutu Sese Seko's</a> view of <a href="/wiki/Authenticit%C3%A9" title="Authenticité">Authenticité</a>.</p>
<p>An important theme running through much pan-Africanist literature concerns the historical links between different countries on the continent, and the benefits of cooperation as a way of resisting imperialism and colonialism.</p>
<p>In the 21st century, some Pan-Africans aim to address <a href="/wiki/Globalization" title="Globalization">globalisation</a> and the problems of <a href="/wiki/Environmental_injustice" title="Environmental injustice" class="mw-redirect">environmental justice</a>. For instance, at the conference "Pan-Africanism for a New Generation"<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> held at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Oxford" title="University of Oxford">University of Oxford</a>, June 2011, Ledum Mittee, the current president of the <a href="/wiki/Movement_for_the_Survival_of_the_Ogoni_People" title="Movement for the Survival of the Ogoni People">Movement for the Survival of the Ogoni People (MOSOP)</a>, argues that environmental justice movements across the African continent should create horizontal linkages in order to better protect the interests of threatened peoples and the ecological systems in which they are embedded, and upon which their survival depends.</p>
<p>Some universities have gone as far as creating "Departments of Pan-African Studies" in the late 1960s. This includes the <a href="/wiki/California_State_University" title="California State University">California State University</a>, where that department was founded in 1969 as a direct reaction to the <a href="/wiki/Civil_rights_movement" title="Civil rights movement" class="mw-redirect">civil rights movement</a>, and is today dedicated to "teaching students about the African World Experience", to "demonstrate to the campus and the community the richness, vibrance, diversity, and vitality of African, African American, and Caribbean cultures" and to "presenting students and the community with an Afrocentric analysis" of anti-black <a href="/wiki/Racism" title="Racism">racism</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Syracuse_University" title="Syracuse University">Syracuse University</a> also offers a <a href="/wiki/Department_of_African_American_Studies_-_Syracuse_University#Pan_African_Studies" title="Department of African American Studies - Syracuse University" class="mw-redirect">master's degree in "Pan African Studies</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pan-African_banner">Pan-African banner</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pan-Africanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Pan-African banner">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Pan-African_flag" title="Pan-African flag">Pan-African flag</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pan-African_colours" title="Pan-African colours">Pan-African colours</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Flag_of_the_UNIA.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Flag_of_the_UNIA.svg/170px-Flag_of_the_UNIA.svg.png" width="170" height="113" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Flag_of_the_UNIA.svg/255px-Flag_of_the_UNIA.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Flag_of_the_UNIA.svg/340px-Flag_of_the_UNIA.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="900" data-file-height="600" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Flag_of_the_UNIA.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The red, black, and green <a href="/wiki/Pan-African_flag" title="Pan-African flag">Pan-African flag</a> designed by the <a href="/wiki/UNIA" title="UNIA" class="mw-redirect">UNIA</a> in 1920.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Pan-African_flag" title="Pan-African flag">Pan-African flag</a>, also known as the <a href="/wiki/Universal_Negro_Improvement_Association_and_African_Communities_League" title="Universal Negro Improvement Association and African Communities League">UNIA</a> (Universal Negro Improvement Association) flag, is a tri-color <a href="/wiki/Flag" title="Flag">flag</a> consisting of three equal horizontal bands of (from top down) <a href="/wiki/Red" title="Red">red</a>, <a href="/wiki/Black" title="Black">black</a> and <a href="/wiki/Green" title="Green">green</a>. The UNIA formally adopted it on August 13, 1920,<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> during its month-long convention at <a href="/wiki/Madison_Square_Garden_(1890)" title="Madison Square Garden (1890)">Madison Square Garden</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Variations of the flag have been used in various countries and territories in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a> to represent Pan-Africanist ideologies.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Among these are the flags of <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_Malawi" title="Flag of Malawi">Malawi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_Kenya" title="Flag of Kenya">Kenya</a> and <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_Saint_Kitts_and_Nevis" title="Flag of Saint Kitts and Nevis">Saint Kitts and Nevis</a>. Several Pan-African organizations and movements have also often employed the emblematic red, black and green tri-color scheme in variety of contexts.</p>
<p>Additionally, the flags of a number of nations in Africa and of Pan-African groups use green, yellow and red. This color combination was originally adopted from the 1897 <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_Ethiopia" title="Flag of Ethiopia">flag of Ethiopia</a>, and was inspired by the fact that <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a> is the continent's oldest independent nation.<sup id="cite_ref-Smith_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Smith-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Maafa_studies">Maafa studies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pan-Africanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Maafa studies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Maafa" title="Maafa">Maafa</a> is an aspect of Pan-African studies. The term collectively refers to 500 years of suffering (including the present) of people of African heritage through slavery, imperialism, colonialism, and other forms of oppression.<sup id="cite_ref-Temple_News_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Temple_News-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Swagga_Definition_of_Maafa_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Swagga_Definition_of_Maafa-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> In this area of study, both the actual history and the legacy of that history are studied as a single discourse. The emphasis in the historical narrative is on African agents, as opposed to non-African agents.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<li>The <a href="/wiki/Council_on_African_Affairs" title="Council on African Affairs">Council on African Affairs</a> (CAA): founded in 1937 by <a href="/wiki/Max_Yergan" title="Max Yergan">Max Yergan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paul_Robeson" title="Paul Robeson">Paul Robeson</a>, the CAA was the first major U.S. organization whose focus was on providing pertinent and up-to-date information about Pan-Africanism across the United States, particularly to African Americans. Probably the most successful campaign of the Council was for South African famine relief in 1946. The CAA was hopeful that, following <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, there would be a move towards <a href="/wiki/Third_World" title="Third World">Third World</a> independence under the trusteeship of the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Duberman_296-297_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Duberman_296-297-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> To the CAA's dismay, the proposals introduced by the U.S. government to the conference in April/May 1945 set no clear limits on the duration of colonialism and no motions towards allowing territorial possessions to move towards self-government.<sup id="cite_ref-Duberman_296-297_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Duberman_296-297-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> Liberal supporters abandoned the CAA, and the <a href="/wiki/Federal_government_of_the_United_States" title="Federal government of the United States">federal government</a> cracked down on its operations. In 1953 the CAA was charged with subversion under the <a href="/wiki/McCarran_Act" title="McCarran Act" class="mw-redirect">McCarran Act</a>. Its principal leaders, including Robeson, <a href="/wiki/W._E._B._Du_Bois" title="W. E. B. Du Bois">W. E. B. Du Bois</a>, and Alphaeus Hunton (1903–70), were subjected to harassment, indictments, and in the case of Hunton, imprisonment. Under the weight of internal disputes, government repression, and financial hardships, the Council on African Affairs disbanded in 1955.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/US_Organization" title="US Organization">US Organization</a> was founded in 1965 by <a href="/wiki/Maulana_Karenga" title="Maulana Karenga">Maulana Karenga</a>, following the <a href="/wiki/Watts_riots" title="Watts riots">Watts riots</a>. It is based on the synthetic <a href="/wiki/African_philosophy" title="African philosophy">African philosophy</a> of <a href="/wiki/Kawaida" title="Kawaida" class="mw-redirect">kawaida</a>, and is perhaps best known for creating <a href="/wiki/Kwaanza" title="Kwaanza" class="mw-redirect">Kwaanza</a> and the <i>Nguzo Saba</i> ("seven principles"). In the words of its founder and chair, Karenga, "the essential task of our organization Us has been and remains to provide a philosophy, a set of principles and a program which inspires a personal and social practice that not only satisfies human need but transforms people in the process, making them self-conscious agents of their own life and liberation".<sup id="cite_ref-The_US_Organization_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_US_Organization-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pan-African_concepts_and_philosophies">Pan-African concepts and philosophies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pan-Africanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Pan-African concepts and philosophies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Afrocentric_Pan-Africanism">Afrocentric Pan-Africanism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pan-Africanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Afrocentric Pan-Africanism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Afrocentric" title="Afrocentric" class="mw-redirect">Afrocentric</a> Pan-Africanism is espoused by Kwabena Faheem Ashanti in his book <i>The Psychotechnology of Brainwashing: Crucifying Willie Lynch</i>. Another newer movement that has evolved from the early Afrocentric school is the Afrisecal movement or Afrisecaism of Francis Ohanyido, a Nigerian philosopher-poet.<sup id="cite_ref-Bio_Ohanyido_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bio_Ohanyido-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Black_Nationalism" title="Black Nationalism" class="mw-redirect">Black Nationalism</a> is sometimes associated with this form of pan-Africanism; a representative of Afrocentric Pan-Africanism in the Spanish-speaking world is <a href="/wiki/Antumi_Toasij%C3%A9" title="Antumi Toasijé">Antumi Toasijé</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hip_Hop">Hip Hop</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pan-Africanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Hip Hop">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the past three decades <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop" title="Hip hop">hip hop</a> has emerged as a powerful force shaping black and African identities worldwide. In his article "Hip-hop Turns 30: Whatcha Celebratin' For?", <a href="/wiki/Greg_Tate" title="Greg Tate">Greg Tate</a> describes hip-hop culture as the product of a Pan-African state of mind.<sup id="cite_ref-Tate.2C_Greg_2005_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tate.2C_Greg_2005-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> It is an "ethnic enclave/empowerment zone that has served as a foothold for the poorest among us to get a grip on the land of the prosperous".<sup id="cite_ref-Tate.2C_Greg_2005_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tate.2C_Greg_2005-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> Hip-hop unifies those of African descent globally in its movement towards greater economic, social and political power. Andreana Clay in her article "Keepin' it Real: Black Youth, Hip-Hop Culture, and Black Identity" states that hip-hop provides the world with "vivid illustrations of Black lived experience", creating bonds of black identity across the globe.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> Hip hop authenticates a black identity, and in doing so, creates a unifying uplifting force among Africans as Pan-Africanism sets out to achieve.</p>
