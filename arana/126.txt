<p><b>Paul Klee</b> (<small>German:</small> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_German" title="Help:IPA for German">[paʊ̯l ˈkleː]</a></span>; 18 December 1879 – 29 June 1940) was a Swiss-German painter. His highly individual style was influenced by movements in art that included <a href="/wiki/Expressionism" title="Expressionism">Expressionism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cubism" title="Cubism">Cubism</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Surrealism" title="Surrealism">Surrealism</a>. Klee was a natural draftsman who experimented with and eventually deeply explored <a href="/wiki/Color_theory" title="Color theory">color theory</a>, writing about it extensively; his lectures <i>Writings on Form and Design Theory</i> (<i>Schriften zur Form und Gestaltungslehre</i>), published in English as the <i><a href="/wiki/Paul_Klee_Notebooks" title="Paul Klee Notebooks">Paul Klee Notebooks</a></i>, are held to be as important for modern art as <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_da_Vinci" title="Leonardo da Vinci">Leonardo da Vinci</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/A_Treatise_on_Painting" title="A Treatise on Painting" class="mw-redirect">A Treatise on Painting</a></i> for the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance" class="mw-redirect">Renaissance</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-EdinburghShow_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EdinburghShow-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> He and his colleague, Russian painter <a href="/wiki/Wassily_Kandinsky" title="Wassily Kandinsky">Wassily Kandinsky</a>, both taught at the <a href="/wiki/Bauhaus" title="Bauhaus">Bauhaus</a> school of art, design and architecture. His works reflect his dry humor and his sometimes childlike perspective, his personal moods and beliefs, and his musicality.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_training">Early life and training</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paul_Klee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and training">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>First of all, the art of living; then as my ideal profession, poetry and philosophy, and as my real profession, <a href="/wiki/Plastic_arts" title="Plastic arts">plastic arts</a>; in the last resort, for lack of income, illustrations.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— Paul Klee.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>Paul Klee was born in <a href="/wiki/M%C3%BCnchenbuchsee" title="Münchenbuchsee">Münchenbuchsee</a>, <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a>, as the second child of German music teacher Hans Wilhelm Klee (1849–1940) and Swiss singer Ida Marie Klee, née Frick (1855–1921).<sup class="reference" id="ref_fn_1a"><a href="#endnote_fn_1a">[a]</a></sup> His sister Mathilde (died 6 December 1953) was born on 28 January 1876 in <a href="/wiki/Walzenhausen" title="Walzenhausen">Walzenhausen</a>. Their father came from <a href="/wiki/Tann,_Hesse" title="Tann, Hesse">Tann</a> and studied at the <a href="/wiki/State_University_of_Music_and_Performing_Arts_Stuttgart" title="State University of Music and Performing Arts Stuttgart">Stuttgart Conservatory</a> singing, piano, organ and violin, meeting there his future wife Ida Frick. Hans Wilhelm Klee was active as a music teacher at the Bern State Seminary in <a href="/wiki/Hofwil" title="Hofwil">Hofwil</a> near Bern until 1931. Klee was able to develop his music skills as his parents encouraged and inspired him until his death.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> In 1880, his family moved to Bern, where they moved 17 years later after numerous changes of residence into a house at the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kirchenfeld&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Kirchenfeld (page does not exist)">Kirchenfeld</a> district.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> From 1886 to 1890, Klee visited primary school and received, at the age of 7, violin classes at the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Musikschule_Konservatorium_Bern&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Musikschule Konservatorium Bern (page does not exist)">Municipal Music School</a>. He was so talented on violin that, aged 11, he received an invitation to play as an extraordinary member of the Bern Music Association.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>In his early years, following his parents’ wishes, Klee focused on becoming a musician; but he decided on the <a href="/wiki/Visual_arts" title="Visual arts">visual arts</a> during his teen years, partly out of rebellion and partly because of a belief that modern music lacked meaning for him. He stated, "I didn't find the idea of going in for music creatively particularly attractive in view of the decline in the history of musical achievement."<sup id="cite_ref-Partsch.2C_p._9_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Partsch.2C_p._9-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> As a musician, he played and felt emotionally bound to traditional works of the <a href="/wiki/Classical_period_(music)" title="Classical period (music)">eighteenth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Romantic_music" title="Romantic music">nineteenth</a> century, but as an artist he craved the freedom to explore radical ideas and styles.<sup id="cite_ref-Partsch.2C_p._9_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Partsch.2C_p._9-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> At sixteen, Klee’s landscape drawings already show considerable skill.<sup id="cite_ref-Kagan.2C_p._54_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kagan.2C_p._54-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Around 1897, Klee started his diary, which he kept until 1918, and which has provided scholars with valuable insight into his life and thinking.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> During his school years, he avidly drew in his school books, in particular drawing <a href="/wiki/Caricatures" title="Caricatures" class="mw-redirect">caricatures</a>, and already demonstrating skill with line and volume.<sup id="cite_ref-Partsch.2C_p._10_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Partsch.2C_p._10-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> He barely passed his final exams at the "Gymnasium" of Bern, where he qualified in the <a href="/wiki/Humanities" title="Humanities">Humanities</a>. With his characteristic dry wit, he wrote, "After all, it’s rather difficult to achieve the <i>exact</i> minimum, and it involves risks."<sup id="cite_ref-Kagan.2C_p._22_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kagan.2C_p._22-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> On his own time, in addition to his deep interests in music and art, Klee was a great reader of literature, and later a writer on <a href="/wiki/Art_theory" title="Art theory" class="mw-redirect">art theory</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aesthetics" title="Aesthetics">aesthetics</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Jardi.2C_p._8_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jardi.2C_p._8-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>With his parents' reluctant permission, in 1898 Klee began studying art at the <a href="/wiki/Academy_of_Fine_Arts,_Munich" title="Academy of Fine Arts, Munich">Academy of Fine Arts</a> in <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a> with <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Knirr" title="Heinrich Knirr">Heinrich Knirr</a> and <a href="/wiki/Franz_von_Stuck" title="Franz von Stuck" class="mw-redirect">Franz von Stuck</a>. He excelled at drawing but seemed to lack any natural color sense. He later recalled, "During the third winter I even realized that I probably would never learn to paint."<sup id="cite_ref-Kagan.2C_p._22_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kagan.2C_p._22-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> During these times of youthful adventure, Klee spent much time in pubs and had affairs with lower class women and artists' models. He had an illegitimate son in 1900 who died several weeks after birth.<sup id="cite_ref-Partsch.2C_p._11_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Partsch.2C_p._11-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>After receiving his Fine Arts degree, Klee went to Italy from October 1901 to May 1902<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> with friend <a href="/wiki/Hermann_Haller_(sculptor)" title="Hermann Haller (sculptor)">Hermann Haller</a>. They stayed in Rome, Florence, and Naples, and studied the master painters of past centuries.<sup id="cite_ref-Partsch.2C_p._11_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Partsch.2C_p._11-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> He exclaimed, "The <a href="/wiki/Roman_Forum" title="Roman Forum">Forum</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Holy_See" title="Holy See">Vatican</a> have spoken to me. <a href="/wiki/Humanism" title="Humanism">Humanism</a> wants to suffocate me."<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> He responded to the colors of Italy, but sadly noted, "that a long struggle lies in store for me in this field of color."<sup id="cite_ref-Kagan.2C_p._23_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kagan.2C_p._23-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> For Klee, color represented the optimism and nobility in art, and a hope for relief from the pessimistic nature he expressed in his black-and-white grotesques and satires.<sup id="cite_ref-Kagan.2C_p._23_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kagan.2C_p._23-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> Returning to Bern, he lived with his parents for several years, and took occasional art classes. By 1905, he was developing some experimental techniques, including drawing with a needle on a blackened pane of glass, resulting in fifty-seven works including his <i>Portrait of My Father</i> (1906).<sup id="cite_ref-Partsch.2C_p._10_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Partsch.2C_p._10-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> In the years 1903-5 he also completed a cycle of eleven zinc-plate <a href="/wiki/Etching" title="Etching">etchings</a> called <i>Inventions</i>, his first exhibited works, in which he illustrated several grotesque characters.<sup id="cite_ref-Partsch.2C_p._11_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Partsch.2C_p._11-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> He commented, "though I'm fairly satisfied with my etchings I can't go on like this. I’m not a specialist."<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> Klee was still dividing his time with music, playing the violin in an orchestra and writing concert and theater reviews.<sup id="cite_ref-Partsch.2C_p._12_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Partsch.2C_p._12-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Marriage_and_early_years">Marriage and early years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paul_Klee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Marriage and early years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Marriage">Marriage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paul_Klee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Marriage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Paul_Klee_Flower_Myth_1918.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Paul_Klee_Flower_Myth_1918.jpg/170px-Paul_Klee_Flower_Myth_1918.jpg" width="170" height="311" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c5/Paul_Klee_Flower_Myth_1918.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c5/Paul_Klee_Flower_Myth_1918.jpg 2x" data-file-width="195" data-file-height="357" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Paul_Klee_Flower_Myth_1918.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>Flower Myth</i> (<i>Blumenmythos</i>) 1918, watercolor on pastel foundation on fabric and newsprint mounted on board, <a href="/wiki/Sprengel_Museum" title="Sprengel Museum">Sprengel Museum</a>, Hannover, Germany</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Klee married Bavarian pianist Lily Stumpf in 1906 and they had one son named Felix Paul in the following year. They lived in a suburb of Munich, and while she gave piano lessons and occasional performances, he kept house and tended to his art work. His attempt to be a magazine illustrator failed.<sup id="cite_ref-Partsch.2C_p._12_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Partsch.2C_p._12-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> Klee's art work progressed slowly for the next five years, partly from having to divide his time with domestic matters, and partly as he tried to find a new approach to his art. In 1910, he had his first solo exhibition in Bern, which then traveled to three Swiss cities.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Affiliation_to_the_.22Blaue_Reiter.22.2C_1911">Affiliation to the "Blaue Reiter", 1911</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paul_Klee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Affiliation to the &quot;Blaue Reiter&quot;, 1911">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In January 1911 <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Kubin" title="Alfred Kubin">Alfred Kubin</a> met Klee in Munich and encouraged him to illustrate <a href="/wiki/Voltaire" title="Voltaire">Voltaire</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Candide" title="Candide">Candide</a></i>. Around this time, Klee's graphic work increased. His early inclination towards the absurd and the sarcastic was well received by Kubin, who befriended Klee and became one of his first significant collectors.<sup id="cite_ref-Baumgartner207_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baumgartner207-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> Klee met, through Kubin, the art critic Wilhelm Hausenstein in 1911. Klee was a foundation member and manager of the Munich artists' union <i>Sema</i> that summer.<sup id="cite_ref-Verspohl90_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Verspohl90-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> In autumn he made an acquaintance with <a href="/wiki/August_Macke" title="August Macke">August Macke</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wassily_Kandinsky" title="Wassily Kandinsky">Wassily Kandinsky</a>, and in winter he joined the editorial team of the almanac <i><a href="/wiki/Der_Blaue_Reiter" title="Der Blaue Reiter">Der Blaue Reiter</a></i>, founded by <a href="/wiki/Franz_Marc" title="Franz Marc">Franz Marc</a> and Kandinsky. On meeting Kandinsky, Klee recorded, "I came to feel a deep trust in him. He is somebody, and has an exceptionally beautiful and lucid mind."<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> Other members included Macke, <a href="/wiki/Gabriele_M%C3%BCnter" title="Gabriele Münter">Gabriele Münter</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marianne_von_Werefkin" title="Marianne von Werefkin">Marianne von Werefkin</a>. Klee became in a few months one of the most important and independent members of the <i>Blaue Reiter</i>, but he was not yet fully integrated.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The release of the almanac was delayed for the benefit of an exhibition. The first <i>Blaue Reiter</i> exhibition took place from 18 December 1911 to 1 January 1912 in the Moderne Galerie Heinrich Thannhauser in Munich. Klee did not attend it, but in the second exhibition, which occurred from 12 February to 18 March 1912 in the <a href="/wiki/Hans_Goltz" title="Hans Goltz">Galerie Goltz</a>, 17 of his graphic works were shown. The name of this art exhibition was <i>Schwarz-Weiß</i>, as it only regarded graphic painting.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> Initially planned to be released in 1911, the release date of the <i>Der Blau Reiter</i> almanac by Kandinsky and Marc was delayed in May 1912, including the reproduced ink drawing <i>Steinhauer</i> by Klee. At the same time, Kandinsky published his art history writing <i>Über das Geistige in der Kunst</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Participation_on_art_exhibitions.2C_1912.2F1913">Participation on art exhibitions, 1912/1913</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paul_Klee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Participation on art exhibitions, 1912/1913">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The association opened Klee's mind to modern theories of color. His travels to Paris in 1912 also exposed him to the ferment of <a href="/wiki/Cubism" title="Cubism">Cubism</a> and the pioneering examples of "pure painting", an early term for <a href="/wiki/Abstract_art" title="Abstract art">abstract art</a>. The use of bold color by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Delaunay" title="Robert Delaunay">Robert Delaunay</a> and <a href="/wiki/Maurice_de_Vlaminck" title="Maurice de Vlaminck">Maurice de Vlaminck</a> also inspired him.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> Rather than copy these artists, Klee began working out his own color experiments in pale watercolors and did some primitive landscapes, including <i>In the Quarry</i> (1913) and <i>Houses near the Gravel Pit</i> (1913), using blocks of color with limited overlap.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> Klee acknowledged that "a long struggle lies in store for me in this field of color" in order to reach his "distant noble aim." Soon, he discovered "the style which connects drawing and the realm of color."<sup id="cite_ref-Kagan.2C_p._23_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kagan.2C_p._23-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Trip_to_Tunis.2C_1914">Trip to Tunis, 1914</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paul_Klee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Trip to Tunis, 1914">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Klee's artistic breakthrough came in 1914 when he briefly visited Tunisia with <a href="/wiki/August_Macke" title="August Macke">August Macke</a> and <a href="/wiki/Louis_Moilliet" title="Louis Moilliet">Louis Moilliet</a> and was impressed by the quality of the light there. He wrote, "Color has taken possession of me; no longer do I have to chase after it, I know that it has hold of me forever... Color and I are one. I am a painter."<sup id="cite_ref-Partsch.2C_p._20_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Partsch.2C_p._20-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> With that realization, faithfulness to nature faded in importance. Instead, Klee began to delve into the "cool romanticism of abstraction".<sup id="cite_ref-Partsch.2C_p._20_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Partsch.2C_p._20-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> In gaining a second artistic vocabulary, Klee added color to his abilities in draftsmanship, and in many works combined them successfully, as he did in one series he called "operatic paintings".<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> One of the most literal examples of this new synthesis is <i>The Bavarian Don Giovanni</i> (1919).<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>After returning home, Klee painted his first pure abstract, <i>In the Style of Kairouan</i> (1914), composed of colored rectangles and a few circles.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup> The colored rectangle became his basic building block, what some scholars associate with a musical note, which Klee combined with other colored blocks to create a color harmony analogous to a musical composition. His selection of a particular color palette emulates a musical key. Sometimes he uses complementary pairs of colors, and other times "dissonant" colors, again reflecting his connection with musicality.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Military_career">Military career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paul_Klee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Military career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Paul_Klee,_1916.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Paul_Klee%2C_1916.jpg/170px-Paul_Klee%2C_1916.jpg" width="170" height="238" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Paul_Klee%2C_1916.jpg/255px-Paul_Klee%2C_1916.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Paul_Klee%2C_1916.jpg/340px-Paul_Klee%2C_1916.jpg 2x" data-file-width="765" data-file-height="1070" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Paul_Klee,_1916.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Paul Klee as a soldier, 1916</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A few weeks later, <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> began. At first, Klee was somewhat detached from it, as he wrote ironically, "I have long had this war in me. That is why, inwardly, it is none of my concern." <sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup> Klee was <a href="/wiki/Conscription" title="Conscription">conscripted</a> as a <i>Landsturmsoldat</i> (soldier of the reserve forces in <a href="/wiki/Prussia" title="Prussia">Prussia</a> or Imperial Germany) on 5 March 1916. The deaths of his friends <a href="/wiki/August_Macke" title="August Macke">August Macke</a> and <a href="/wiki/Franz_Marc" title="Franz Marc">Franz Marc</a> in battle began to affect him. Venting his distress, he created several pen and ink <a href="/wiki/Lithographs" title="Lithographs" class="mw-redirect">lithographs</a> on war themes including <i>Death for the Idea</i> (1915).<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup> After finishing the military training course, which began on 11 March 1916, he was committed as a soldier behind the front. Klee moved on 20 August to the aircraft maintenance company<sup class="reference" id="ref_fn_2b"><a href="#endnote_fn_2b">[b]</a></sup> in <a href="/wiki/Oberschleissheim" title="Oberschleissheim" class="mw-redirect">Oberschleissheim</a>, executing skilled manual work, such as restoring aircraft <a href="/wiki/Camouflage" title="Camouflage">camouflage</a>, and accompanying aircraft transports. On 17 January 1917, he was transferred to the Royal Bavarian flying school in <a href="/wiki/Gersthofen" title="Gersthofen">Gersthofen</a> (which 54 years later became the <a href="/wiki/USASA_Field_Station_Augsburg" title="USASA Field Station Augsburg">USASA Field Station Augsburg</a>) to work as a clerk for the treasurer until the end of the war. This allowed him to stay in a small room outside of the barrack block and continue painting.<sup id="cite_ref-Baumgartner214f_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baumgartner214f-37"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>He continued to paint during the entire war and managed to exhibit in several shows. By 1917, Klee's work was selling well and art critics acclaimed him as the best of the new German artists.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup> His <i><a href="/wiki/Ab_ovo_(painting)" title="Ab ovo (painting)">Ab ovo</a></i> (1917) is particularly noteworthy for its sophisticated technique. It employs watercolor on gauze and paper with a chalk ground, which produces a rich texture of triangular, circular, and crescent patterns.<sup id="cite_ref-Partsch.2C_p._20_29-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Partsch.2C_p._20-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> Demonstrating his range of exploration, mixing color and line, his <i>Warning of the Ships</i> (1918) is a colored drawing filled with symbolic images on a field of suppressed color.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mature_career">Mature career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paul_Klee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Mature career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>In 1919, Klee applied for a teaching post at the Academy of Art in Stuttgart.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup> This attempt failed but he had a major success in securing a three-year contract (with a minimum annual income) with dealer <a href="/wiki/Hans_Goltz" title="Hans Goltz">Hans Goltz</a>, whose influential gallery gave Klee major exposure, and some commercial success. A retrospective of over 300 works in 1920 was also notable.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Klee taught at the <a href="/wiki/Bauhaus" title="Bauhaus">Bauhaus</a> from January 1921 to April 1931.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup> He was a "Form" master in the bookbinding, stained glass, and mural painting workshops and was provided with two studios.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup> In 1922, Kandinsky joined the staff and resumed his friendship with Klee. Later that year the first Bauhaus exhibition and festival was held, for which Klee created several of the advertising materials.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup> Klee welcomed that there were many conflicting theories and opinions within the Bauhaus: "I also approve of these forces competing one with the other if the result is achievement."<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:GUGG_Tropical_Gardening.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/GUGG_Tropical_Gardening.jpg/220px-GUGG_Tropical_Gardening.jpg" width="220" height="97" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/GUGG_Tropical_Gardening.jpg/330px-GUGG_Tropical_Gardening.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/GUGG_Tropical_Gardening.jpg/440px-GUGG_Tropical_Gardening.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3000" data-file-height="1324" /></a>

<p>Klee was also a member of <a href="/wiki/Die_Blaue_Vier" title="Die Blaue Vier" class="mw-redirect">Die Blaue Vier</a> (The Blue Four), with Kandinsky, <a href="/wiki/Lyonel_Feininger" title="Lyonel Feininger">Lyonel Feininger</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Alexej_von_Jawlensky" title="Alexej von Jawlensky">Alexej von Jawlensky</a>; formed in 1923, they lectured and exhibited together in the USA in 1925. That same year, Klee had his first exhibits in Paris, and he became a hit with the French Surrealists.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup> Klee visited Egypt in 1928, which impressed him less than Tunisia. In 1929, the first major <a href="/wiki/Monograph" title="Monograph">monograph</a> on Klee's work was published, written by Will Grohmann.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Nocturnal_Festivity.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Nocturnal_Festivity.JPG/220px-Nocturnal_Festivity.JPG" width="220" height="183" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Nocturnal_Festivity.JPG/330px-Nocturnal_Festivity.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Nocturnal_Festivity.JPG/440px-Nocturnal_Festivity.JPG 2x" data-file-width="709" data-file-height="589" /></a>

</div>
</div>
<p>Klee also taught at the Düsseldorf Academy from 1931 to 1933, and was singled out by a Nazi newspaper, "Then that great fellow Klee comes onto the scene, already famed as a Bauhaus teacher in Dessau. He tells everyone he's a thoroughbred Arab, but he's a typical Galician Jew."<sup id="cite_ref-Partsch.2C_p._73_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Partsch.2C_p._73-49"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup> His home was searched by the <a href="/wiki/Gestapo" title="Gestapo">Gestapo</a> and he was fired from his job.<sup id="cite_ref-EdinburghShow_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EdinburghShow-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup> His self-portrait <i>Struck from the List</i> (1933) commemorates the sad occasion.<sup id="cite_ref-Partsch.2C_p._73_49-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Partsch.2C_p._73-49"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup> In 1933-4, Klee had shows in London and Paris, and finally met <a href="/wiki/Pablo_Picasso" title="Pablo Picasso">Pablo Picasso</a>, whom he greatly admired.<sup id="cite_ref-Jardi.2C_p._23_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jardi.2C_p._23-51"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup> The Klee family emigrated to Switzerland in late 1933.<sup id="cite_ref-Jardi.2C_p._23_51-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jardi.2C_p._23-51"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Klee was at the peak of his creative output. His <i>Ad Parnassum</i> (1932) is considered his masterpiece and the best example of his <a href="/wiki/Pointillism" title="Pointillism">pointillist style</a>; it is also one of his largest, most finely worked paintings.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52"><span>[</span>52<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53"><span>[</span>53<span>]</span></a></sup> He produced nearly 500 works in 1933 during his last year in Germany.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54"><span>[</span>54<span>]</span></a></sup> However, in 1933, Klee began experiencing the symptoms of what was diagnosed as <a href="/wiki/Scleroderma" title="Scleroderma">scleroderma</a> after his death. The progression of his fatal disease, which made swallowing very difficult, can be followed through the art he created in his last years. His output in 1936 was only 25 pictures. In the later 1930s, his health recovered somewhat and he was encouraged by a visit from Kandinsky and Picasso.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55"><span>[</span>55<span>]</span></a></sup> Klee's simpler and larger designs enabled him to keep up his output in his final years, and in 1939 he created over 1,200 works, a career high for one year.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56"><span>[</span>56<span>]</span></a></sup> He used heavier lines and mainly geometric forms with fewer but larger blocks of color. His varied color palettes, some with bright colors and others sober, perhaps reflected his alternating moods of optimism and pessimism.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57"><span>[</span>57<span>]</span></a></sup> Back in Germany in 1937, seventeen of Klee's pictures were included in an exhibition of "<a href="/wiki/Degenerate_art" title="Degenerate art">Degenerate art</a>" and 102 of his works in public collections were seized by the Nazis.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58"><span>[</span>58<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paul_Klee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Klee suffered from a wasting disease, <a href="/wiki/Scleroderma" title="Scleroderma">scleroderma</a>, toward the end of his life, enduring pain that seems to be reflected in his last works of art. One of his last paintings, <i><a href="/wiki/Death_and_Fire" title="Death and Fire">Death and Fire</a></i>, features a skull in the center with the German word for death, "Tod", appearing in the face. He died in <a href="/wiki/Muralto" title="Muralto">Muralto</a>, Locarno, Switzerland, on 29 June 1940 without having obtained Swiss citizenship, despite his birth in that country. His art work was considered too revolutionary, even degenerate, by the Swiss authorities, but eventually they accepted his request six days after his death.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59"><span>[</span>59<span>]</span></a></sup> His legacy comprises about 9,000 works of art.<sup id="cite_ref-Kagan.2C_p._23_17-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kagan.2C_p._23-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> The words on his tombstone, Klee's credo, placed there by his son Felix, say, "I cannot be grasped in the here and now, For my dwelling place is as much among the dead, As the yet unborn, Slightly closer to the heart of creation than usual, But still not close enough."<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60"><span>[</span>60<span>]</span></a></sup> He was buried at <a href="/wiki/Schosshaldenfriedhof" title="Schosshaldenfriedhof" class="mw-redirect">Schosshaldenfriedhof</a>, Bern, Switzerland.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Style_and_methods">Style and methods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paul_Klee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Style and methods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Klee has been variously associated with <a href="/wiki/Expressionism" title="Expressionism">Expressionism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cubism" title="Cubism">Cubism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Futurism" title="Futurism">Futurism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Surrealism" title="Surrealism">Surrealism</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Abstract_art" title="Abstract art">Abstraction</a>, but his pictures are difficult to classify. He generally worked in isolation from his peers, and interpreted new art trends in his own way. He was inventive in his methods and technique. Klee worked in many different media—<a href="/wiki/Oil_paint" title="Oil paint">oil paint</a>, <a href="/wiki/Watercolor" title="Watercolor" class="mw-redirect">watercolor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ink" title="Ink">ink</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pastel" title="Pastel">pastel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Etching" title="Etching">etching</a>, and others. He often combined them into one work. He used canvas, burlap, muslin, linen, gauze, cardboard, metal foils, fabric, wallpaper, and newsprint.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61"><span>[</span>61<span>]</span></a></sup> Klee employed spray paint, knife application, stamping, glazing, and impasto, and <a href="/wiki/Mixed_media" title="Mixed media">mixed media</a> such as oil with watercolor, watercolor with pen and India ink, and oil with tempera.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62"><span>[</span>62<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>He was a natural draftsman, and through long experimentation developed a mastery of color and tonality. Many of his works combine these skills. He uses a great variety of color palettes from nearly <a href="/wiki/Monochrome" title="Monochrome">monochromatic</a> to highly <a href="/wiki/Polychrome" title="Polychrome">polychromatic</a>. His works often have a fragile childlike quality to them and are usually on a small scale. He often used geometric forms as well as letters, numbers, and arrows, and combined them with figures of animals and people. Some works were completely abstract. Many of his works and their titles reflect his dry humor and varying moods; some express political convictions. They frequently allude to poetry, music and dreams and sometimes include words or <a href="/wiki/Musical_notation" title="Musical notation">musical notation</a>. The later works are distinguished by spidery <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_hieroglyphs" title="Egyptian hieroglyphs">hieroglyph</a>-like symbols. <a href="/wiki/Rainer_Maria_Rilke" title="Rainer Maria Rilke">Rainer Maria Rilke</a> wrote about Klee in 1921, "Even if you hadn’t told me he plays the violin, I would have guessed that on many occasions his drawings were transcriptions of music."<sup id="cite_ref-Jardi.2C_p._8_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jardi.2C_p._8-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Pamela Kort observed: "Klee's 1933 drawings present their beholder with an unparalleled opportunity to glimpse a central aspect of his <a href="/wiki/Aesthetics" title="Aesthetics">aesthetics</a> that has remained largely unappreciated: his lifelong concern with the possibilities of <a href="/wiki/Parody" title="Parody">parody</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wit" title="Wit">wit</a>. Herein lies their real significance, particularly for an audience unaware that Klee's art has <a href="/wiki/Political" title="Political" class="mw-redirect">political</a> dimensions."<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63"><span>[</span>63<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Among the few plastic works are <a href="/wiki/Hand_puppet" title="Hand puppet">hand puppets</a> made between 1916 and 1925, for his son Felix. The artist neither counts them as a component of his oeuvre, nor does he list them in his <a href="/wiki/Catalogue_raisonn%C3%A9" title="Catalogue raisonné">catalogue raisonné</a>. Thirty of the preserved puppets are stored at the <a href="/wiki/Zentrum_Paul_Klee" title="Zentrum Paul Klee">Zentrum Paul Klee</a>, Bern.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64"><span>[</span>64<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Works">Works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paul_Klee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_works">Early works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paul_Klee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Early works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some of Klee's early preserved children's drawings, to which his grandmother encouraged, were listed on his <a href="/wiki/Catalogue_raisonn%C3%A9" title="Catalogue raisonné">catalogue raisonné</a>. A total of 19 etchings were produced during the Bern years; ten of these were made between 1903 and 1905 in the cycle "Inventionen" (Inventions),<sup id="cite_ref-R.C3.BCmelin12ff_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-R.C3.BCmelin12ff-65"><span>[</span>65<span>]</span></a></sup> which were presented in June 1906 at the "Internationale Kunstausstellung des Vereins bildender Künstler Münchens '<a href="/w/index.php?title=M%C3%BCnchner_Secession&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Münchner Secession (page does not exist)">Secession</a>'" (International Art Exhibition of the Association for Graphic Arts, Munich, Secession), his first appearance as a painter in the public.<sup id="cite_ref-Baumgartner203_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baumgartner203-66"><span>[</span>66<span>]</span></a></sup> Klee had removed the third Invention, <i>Pessimistische Allegorie des Gebirges</i> (Pessimistic Allegory of the Mountain), in February 1906 from his cycle.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67"><span>[</span>67<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Satire" title="Satire">satirical</a> etchings, for example <i>Jungfrau im Baum/Jungfrau (träumend)</i> (Virgin on the tree/Virgin (dreaming)) from 1903 and <i>Greiser Phoenix</i> (Aged Phoenix) from 1905, were classified by Klee as "surrealistic outposts". <i>Jungfrau im Baum</i> ties on the motive <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Le_cattive_madri&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Le cattive madri (page does not exist)">Le cattive madri</a></i> (1894) by <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Segantini" title="Giovanni Segantini">Giovanni Segantini</a>. The picture was influenced by grotesk lyric poetries of <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Jarry" title="Alfred Jarry">Alfred Jarry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Max_Jacob" title="Max Jacob">Max Jacob</a> and <a href="/wiki/Christian_Morgenstern" title="Christian Morgenstern">Christian Morgenstern</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68"><span>[</span>68<span>]</span></a></sup> It features an cultural pessimism, which can be found at the turn of the 20th century in works by <a href="/wiki/Symbolism_(arts)" title="Symbolism (arts)">Symbolists</a>. The Invention Nr. 6, the 1903 etching <i>Zwei Männer, einander in höherer Stellung vermutend</i> (Two Men, Supposing to be in Major Position), depicts two naked men, presumably emperor <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_II,_German_Emperor" title="Wilhelm II, German Emperor">Wilhelm II</a> and <a href="/wiki/Franz_Joseph_I_of_Austria" title="Franz Joseph I of Austria">Franz Joseph I of Austria</a>, recognizable by their hairstyle and beards. As their clothes and <a href="/wiki/Insignia" title="Insignia">insignia</a> were bereft, "both of them have no clue if their conventional salute […] is in order or not. As they assume that their counterpart could have been higher rated", they <a href="/wiki/Bow_and_scrape" title="Bow and scrape" class="mw-redirect">bow and scrape</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-R.C3.BCmelin15_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-R.C3.BCmelin15-69"><span>[</span>69<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>Klee began to introduce a new technique in 1905: scratching on a blackened glass panel with a needle. In that manner he created about 57 Verre églomisé pictures, among those the 1905 <i>Gartenszene</i> (Scene on a Garden) and the 1906 <i>Porträt des Vaters</i> (Portrait of a Father), with which he tried to combine painting and scratching.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70"><span>[</span>70<span>]</span></a></sup> Klee's solitary early work ended in 1911, the year he met and was inspired by the graphic artist <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Kubin" title="Alfred Kubin">Alfred Kubin</a>, and became associated with the artists of the <i>Blaue Reiter</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71"><span>[</span>71<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mystical-abstract_period.2C_1914.E2.80.931919">Mystical-abstract period, 1914–1919</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paul_Klee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Mystical-abstract period, 1914–1919">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During his twelve-day educational trip to <a href="/wiki/Tunis" title="Tunis">Tunis</a> in April 1914 Klee produced with Macke and Moilliet <a href="/wiki/Watercolor" title="Watercolor" class="mw-redirect">watercolor</a> paintings, which implement the strong light and color stimulus of the North African countryside in the fashion of <a href="/wiki/Paul_C%C3%A9zanne" title="Paul Cézanne">Paul Cézanne</a> and Robert Delaunays' <a href="/wiki/Orphism_(art)" title="Orphism (art)">cubistic</a> form concepts. The aim was not to imitate nature, but to create compositions analogous to nature's formative principle, as in the works <i>In den Häusern von Saint-Germain</i> (In the Houses of Saint-Germain) and <i>Straßencafé</i> (Streetcafé). Klee conveyed the scenery in a grid, so that it dissolves into colored harmony. He also created abstract works in that period such as <i>Abstract</i> and <i>Farbige Kreise durch Farbbänder verbunden</i> (Colored Circles Tied Through Inked Ribbons).<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72"><span>[</span>72<span>]</span></a></sup> He never abandoned the object; a permanent segregation never took place. It took over ten years that Klee worked on experiments and analysis of the color, resulting to an independent artificial work, whereby his design ideas were based on the colorful oriental world.</p>

<p><i>Föhn im Marc'schen Garten</i> (Foehn at Marc's Garden) was made after the Turin trip. It indicates the relations between color and the stimulus of Macke and Delaunay. Although elements of the garden are clearly visible, a further steering towards abstraction is noticeable. In his diary Klee wrote the following note at that time:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>In the large molding pit are lying ruins, on which one partially hangs. They provide the material for the abstraction. […] The terrible the world, the abstract the art, while a happy world produces secularistic art.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73"><span>[</span>73<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Under the impression of his military service he created the painting <i>Trauerblumen</i> (Velvetbells) in 1917, which, with its graphical signs, vegetal and phantastic shapes, is a forerunner of his future works, harmonically combining graphic, color and object. For the first time birds appear in the pictures, such as in <i>Blumenmythos</i> (Flower Myth) from 1918, mirroring the flying and falling planes he saw in Gersthofen, and the photographed plane crashes.</p>
<p>In the 1918 watercolor painting <i>Einst dem Grau der Nacht enttaucht</i>, a compositional implemented poem, possible written by Klee, he incorporated letters in small, in terms of color separated squares, cutting off the first verse from the second one with silver paper. At the top of the cardboard, which carries the picture, the verses are inscribed in manuscript form. Here, Klee did not lean on Delaunay's colors, but on Marc's, although the picture content of both painters does not correspond with each other. <a href="/wiki/Herwarth_Walden" title="Herwarth Walden">Herwarth Walden</a>, Klee's art dealer, saw in them a "Wachablösung" (changing of the guard) of his art.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74"><span>[</span>74<span>]</span></a></sup> Since 1919 he often used oil colors, with which he combined watercolors and colored pencil. The <i>Villa R</i> (Kunstmuseum Basel) from 1919 unites visible realities such as sun, moon, mountains, trees and architectures, as well as surreal pledges and sentiment readings.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75"><span>[</span>75<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Works_in_the_Bauhaus_period_and_in_D.C3.BCsseldorf">Works in the Bauhaus period and in Düsseldorf</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paul_Klee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Works in the Bauhaus period and in Düsseldorf">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>His works during this time include abstract graphical elements such as <i>betroffener Ort</i> (Affected Place) (1922). From that period he created <i><a href="/wiki/Twittering_Machine" title="Twittering Machine">Die Zwitscher-Maschine</a></i> (The Twittering Machine), which was later removed from the <a href="/wiki/National_Gallery_(Berlin)" title="National Gallery (Berlin)">National Gallery</a>. After being named defamatory in the Munich exhibition "<a href="/wiki/Entartete_Kunst" title="Entartete Kunst" class="mw-redirect">Entartete Kunst</a>", the painting was later bought by the Buchholz Gallery, New York, and then transferred in 1939 to the <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_Modern_Art" title="Museum of Modern Art">Museum of Modern Art</a>. The "twittering" in the title refers to the open-beaked birds, while the "machine" is illustrated by the crank.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76"><span>[</span>76<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:GUGG_In_Angel%27s_Care.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/GUGG_In_Angel%27s_Care.jpg/220px-GUGG_In_Angel%27s_Care.jpg" width="220" height="171" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/GUGG_In_Angel%27s_Care.jpg/330px-GUGG_In_Angel%27s_Care.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/GUGG_In_Angel%27s_Care.jpg/440px-GUGG_In_Angel%27s_Care.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3000" data-file-height="2335" /></a>

<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:GUGG_In_Angel%27s_Care.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>In Engelshut,</i> 1931, watercolor and colored inks on paper, mounted on paper, <a href="/wiki/Solomon_R._Guggenheim_Museum" title="Solomon R. Guggenheim Museum">Guggenheim Museum</a></div>
</div>

<p>The watercolor painting appears at a first glance childish, but it allows more interpretations. The picture can be interpreted as a critic by Klee, who shows through denaturation of the birds, that the world technization heist the creatures' self-determination.<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77"><span>[</span>77<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Other examples from that period are <i>der Goldfisch</i> (The Goldfish) from 1925, <i>Katze und Vogel</i> (Cat and Bird), from 1928, and <i>Hauptweg und Nebenwege</i> (Mainway and Sideways) from 1929. Through variations of the canvas ground and his combinated painting techniques Klee created new color effects and picture impressions.</p>
<p>In 1931, Klee transferred to Düsseldorf to teach in the Akademie; the Nazis shut down the Bauhaus soon after.<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78"><span>[</span>78<span>]</span></a></sup> During this time, Klee illustrated a series of guardian angels. Among these figurations is "In Engelshut" (In the Angel's Care). Its overlaying technique evinces the polyphonic character of his drawing method between 1920 and 1932 .<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79"><span>[</span>79<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The 1932 painting <i>Ad Parnassum</i> was also created in the Düsseldorfer period. With 100&#160;cm ×&#160;126&#160;cm (39&#160;in ×&#160;50&#160;in) it is one of his largest paintings, as he usually worked with small formats. In this mosaic-like work in the style of <a href="/wiki/Pointillism" title="Pointillism">pointillism</a> he combined different techniques and compositional principles. Influenced by his trip to Egypt from 1928 to 1929, Klee built a color field from individually stamped dots, surrounded by likewise stamped lines, which results in a pyramid. Above the roof of the "<a href="/wiki/Parnassus" title="Parnassus" class="mw-redirect">Parnassus</a>" there is a sun. The title identifies the picture as the home of <a href="/wiki/Apollo" title="Apollo">Apollo</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Muse" title="Muse">Muses</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80"><span>[</span>80<span>]</span></a></sup> In 1933, the last year in Germany, he created a range of paintings and drawings; the catalogue raisonné comprised 482 works. The self-portrait in the same year – with the programmatic title <i>von der Liste gestrichen</i> (removed from the list) – provides information about his feeling after losing professorship. The abstract portrait was painted in dark colors and shows closed eyes and compressed lips, while on the back part of his head there is a large "X", symbolizing that his art was no longer valued in Germany.<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81"><span>[</span>81<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Last_works_in_Switzerland">Last works in Switzerland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paul_Klee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Last works in Switzerland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In this period Klee mainly worked on large-sized pictures. After the onset of illness, there were about 25 works in the 1936 catalogue, but his productivity increased in 1937 to 264 pictures, 1938 to 489, and 1939 – his most productive year – to 1254. They dealt with <a href="/wiki/Ambivalence" title="Ambivalence">ambivalent</a> themes, expressing his personal fate, the political situation and his joke. Examples are the watercolor painting <i>Musiker</i> (musician), a stickman face with partially serious, partially smiling mouth; and the <i>Revolution des Viadukts</i> (Revolution of the Viadukt), an <a href="/wiki/Anti-fascism" title="Anti-fascism">anti-fascist</a> art. In <i>Viadukt</i> (1937) the bridge arches split from the bank as they refuse to be linked to a chain and are therefore rioting.<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82"><span>[</span>82<span>]</span></a></sup> Since 1938, Klee worked more intensively with hieroglyphic-like elements. The painting <i>Insula dulcamara</i> from the same year, which is one of his largest (88&#160;cm ×&#160;176&#160;cm (35&#160;in ×&#160;69&#160;in)), shows a white face in the middle of the elements, symbolizing death with its black-circled eye sockets. Bitterness and sorrow are not rare in much of his works during this time.</p>

<p>Klee created in 1940 a picture which strongly differs from the previous works, leaving it unsigned on the scaffold. The comparatively realistic <a href="/wiki/Still_life" title="Still life">still life</a>, <i>Ohne Titel</i>, later named as <i>Der Todesengel</i> (Angel of Death), depicts flowers, a green pot, sculpture and an angel. The moon on black ground is separated from these groups. During his 60th birthday Klee was photographed in front of this picture.<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83"><span>[</span>83<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">”</td>

<p>"Klee's act is very prestigious. In a minimum of one line he can reveal his wisdom. He is everything; profound, gentle and many more of the good things, and this because: he is innovative", wrote <a href="/wiki/Oskar_Schlemmer" title="Oskar Schlemmer">Oskar Schlemmer</a>, Klee's future artist colleague at the Bauhaus, in his September 1916 diary.<sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84"><span>[</span>84<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Novelist and Klee's friend <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Hausenstein" title="Wilhelm Hausenstein">Wilhelm Hausenstein</a> wrote in his work <i>Über Expressionismus in der Malerei</i> (On Expressionism in Painting), "Maybe Klee's attitude is in general understandable for musical people – how Klee is one of the most delightsome violinist playing Bach and Händel, who ever walked on earth. […] For Klee, the German classic painter of the Cubism, the world music became his companion, possibly even a part of his art; the composition, written in notes, seems to be not dissimilar."<sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85"><span>[</span>85<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>When Klee visited the Paris surrealism exhibition in 1925, <a href="/wiki/Max_Ernst" title="Max Ernst">Max Ernst</a> was impressed by his work. His partially morbid motifs appealed to the surrealists. <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Breton" title="André Breton">André Breton</a> helped to develop the surrealism and renamed Klee's 1912 painting <i>Zimmerperspektive mit Einwohnern</i> (Room Perspective with People) to <i>chambre spirit</i> in a catalogue. Critic <a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Crevel" title="René Crevel">René Crevel</a> called the artist a "dreamer" who "releases a swarm of small lyrical louses from mysterious abysses." Paul Klee's confidante <a href="/wiki/Will_Grohmann" title="Will Grohmann">Will Grohmann</a> argued in the <i>Cahiers d'art</i> that he "stands definitely well solid on his feet. He is by no means a dreamer; he is a modern person, who teaches as a professor at the Bauhaus." Whereupon Breton, as <a href="/wiki/Joan_Mir%C3%B3" title="Joan Miró">Joan Miró</a> remembers, was critical of Klee: "Masson and I have both discovered Paul Klee. <a href="/wiki/Paul_%C3%89luard" title="Paul Éluard">Paul Éluard</a> and Crevel are also interested in Klee, and they have even visited him. But Breton despises him."<sup id="cite_ref-86" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-86"><span>[</span>86<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The art of mentally ill people inspired Klee as well as Kandinsky and Max Ernst, after <a href="/wiki/Hans_Prinzhorn" title="Hans Prinzhorn">Hans Prinzhorns</a> book <i>Bildnerei der Geisteskranken</i> (Artistry of the Mentally Ill) was published in 1922. In 1937, some papers from Prinzhorn's anthology were presented at the National Socialist <a href="/wiki/Propaganda" title="Propaganda">propaganda</a> exhibition "<a href="/wiki/Entartete_Kunst" title="Entartete Kunst" class="mw-redirect">Entartete Kunst</a>" in Munich, with the purpose of defaming the works of <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Ludwig_Kirchner" title="Ernst Ludwig Kirchner">Kirchner</a>, Klee, <a href="/wiki/Emil_Nolde" title="Emil Nolde">Nolde</a> and other artists by likening them to the works of the insane.<sup id="cite_ref-87" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-87"><span>[</span>87<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In 1949 <a href="/wiki/Marcel_Duchamp" title="Marcel Duchamp">Marcel Duchamp</a> commented on Paul Klee: "The first reaction in front of a Klee painting is the very pleasant discovery, what everyone of us could or could have done, to try drawing like in our childhood. Most of his compositions show at the first glance a plain, naive expression, found in children's drawings. […] At a second analyse one can discover a technique, which takes as a basis a large maturity in thinking. A deep understanding of dealing with watercolors to paint a personal method in oil, structured in decorative shapes, let Klee stand out in the contemporary art and make him incomparable. On the other side, his experiment was adopted in the last 30 years by many other artists as a basis for newer creations in the most different areas in painting. His extreme productivity never shows evidence of repetition, as is usually the case. He had so much to say, that a Klee never became an other Klee."<sup id="cite_ref-Herbert376_88-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Herbert376-88"><span>[</span>88<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>One of Klee's paintings, <i><a href="/wiki/Angelus_Novus" title="Angelus Novus">Angelus Novus</a></i>, was the object of an interpretative text by <a href="/wiki/German_people" title="German people" class="mw-redirect">German</a> <a href="/wiki/Philosopher" title="Philosopher">philosopher</a> and <a href="/wiki/Literary_critic" title="Literary critic" class="mw-redirect">literary critic</a> <a href="/wiki/Walter_Benjamin" title="Walter Benjamin">Walter Benjamin</a>, who purchased the painting in 1921. In his "Theses on the Philosophy of History" Benjamin suggests that the angel depicted in the painting might be seen as representing the angel of history.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Musical_interpretations">Musical interpretations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paul_Klee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Musical interpretations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Paul_Klee_Zentrum.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Zentrum_Paul_Klee" title="Zentrum Paul Klee">Zentrum Paul Klee</a> in Bern, Switzerland, designed by <a href="/wiki/Renzo_Piano" title="Renzo Piano">Renzo Piano</a></div>

<p>Unlike his taste for adventurous modern experiment in painting, Klee, though musically talented, was attracted to older traditions of music; he neither appreciated composers of the late 19th century, such as <a href="/wiki/Richard_Wagner" title="Richard Wagner">Wagner</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anton_Bruckner" title="Anton Bruckner">Bruckner</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gustav_Mahler" title="Gustav Mahler">Mahler</a>, nor contemporary music. <a href="/wiki/Johann_Sebastian_Bach" title="Johann Sebastian Bach">Bach</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wolfgang_Amadeus_Mozart" title="Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart">Mozart</a> were for him the greatest composers; he most enjoyed playing the works by the latter.<sup id="cite_ref-Baumgartner208_89-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baumgartner208-89"><span>[</span>89<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Klee's work has influenced composers including Argentinian <a href="/wiki/Roberto_Garc%C3%ADa_Morillo" title="Roberto García Morillo">Roberto García Morillo</a> in 1943, with <i>Tres pinturas de Paul Klee</i>, and the American artist <a href="/wiki/David_Diamond_(composer)" title="David Diamond (composer)">David Diamond</a> in 1958, with the four-part <a href="/wiki/Opus_number" title="Opus number">Opus</a> <i>Welt von Paul Klee</i> (World of Paul Klee) <a href="/wiki/Gunther_Schuller" title="Gunther Schuller">Gunther Schuller</a> with <i>Sieben Studien über Klee'sche Bilder</i> (Seven Studies about Klee Pictures) in the years 1959/60, and the Spanish composer <a href="/wiki/Benet_Casablancas" title="Benet Casablancas">Benet Casablancas</a> with <i>Alter Klang</i>, Impromptu for Orchestra after Klee (2006);<sup id="cite_ref-90" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-90"><span>[</span>90<span>]</span></a></sup> Casablancas is author also of the <i>Retablo on texts by Paul Klee</i>, Cantata da Camera for Soprano, Mezzo and Piano (2007).<sup id="cite_ref-91" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-91"><span>[</span>91<span>]</span></a></sup> Other works are <i>Abstraktes Terzett</i> <i>(Abstract Trio)</i>, <i>Little Blue Devil</i>, <i>Zwitscher-Maschine</i> <i>(Twittering Machine)</i>, <i>Arab Village</i>, <i>Ein unheimlicher Moment</i> <i>(An Eerie Moment)</i> and <i>Pastorale</i>. In 1950, <a href="/wiki/Giselher_Klebe" title="Giselher Klebe">Giselher Klebe</a> performed his orchestral work <i>Die Zwitschermaschine</i> with the subtitle <i>Metamorphosen über das Bild von Paul Klee</i> at the <a href="/wiki/Donaueschinger_Musiktage" title="Donaueschinger Musiktage" class="mw-redirect">Donaueschinger Musiktage</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-92" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-92"><span>[</span>92<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>8 Pieces on Paul Klee</i> is the title of the debut album by the <a href="/wiki/Ensemble_Sortisatio" title="Ensemble Sortisatio">Ensemble Sortisatio</a>, recorded February and March 2002 in <a href="/wiki/Leipzig" title="Leipzig">Leipzig</a> and August 2002 in <a href="/wiki/Luzern" title="Luzern" class="mw-redirect">Luzern</a>, Switzerland. The composition "Wie der Klee vierblättrig wurde" (How the clover became four-leaved) was inspired by the watercolor painting <i>Hat Kopf, Hand, Fuss und Herz</i> (1930), <i>Angelus Novus</i> and <i>Hauptweg und Nebenwege</i>.</p>
<p>In 1968, a jazz group called The National Gallery featuring composer <a href="/wiki/Chuck_Mangione" title="Chuck Mangione">Chuck Mangione</a> released the album <i>Performing Musical Interpretations of the Paintings of Paul Klee</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-93" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-93"><span>[</span>93<span>]</span></a></sup> In 1995 the Greek experimental filmmaker, Kostas Sfikas, created a film based entirely on Paul Klee's paintings. The film is entitled "<i>Paul Klee's Prophetic Bird of Sorrows</i>", and draws its title from Klee's Landscape with Yellow Birds. It was made using portions and cutouts from Paul Klee's paintings.<sup id="cite_ref-94" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-94"><span>[</span>94<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Architectural_honors">Architectural honors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paul_Klee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Architectural honors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Since 1995, the "Paul Klee-Archiv" (Paul Klee archive) of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Jena" title="University of Jena">University of Jena</a> houses an extensive collection of works by Klee. It is located within the art history department, established by Franz-Joachim Verspohl. It encompasses the private library of book collector Rolf Sauerwein which contains nearly 700 works from 30 years composed of monographs about Klee, exhibition catalogues, extensive secondary literature as well as originally illustrated issues, a postcard and a signed photography portrait of Klee.<sup id="cite_ref-97" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-97"><span>[</span>97<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-98" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-98"><span>[</span>98<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Architect <a href="/wiki/Renzo_Piano" title="Renzo Piano">Renzo Piano</a> constructed the <a href="/wiki/Zentrum_Paul_Klee" title="Zentrum Paul Klee">Zentrum Paul Klee</a> in June 2005. Located in Bern, the museum exhibits about 150 (of 4000 Klee works overall) in a six-month rotation, as it is impossible to show all of his works at once. Furthermore, his pictures require rest periods; they contain relatively photosensitive colors, inks and papers, which may bleach, change, turn brown and become brittle if exposed to light for too long.<sup id="cite_ref-99" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-99"><span>[</span>99<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Museum_of_Modern_Art" title="San Francisco Museum of Modern Art">San Francisco Museum of Modern Art</a> has a comprehensive Klee collection, donated by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Djerassi" title="Carl Djerassi">Carl Djerassi</a>. Other exhibitions include the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sammlung_Rosengart&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Sammlung Rosengart (page does not exist)">Sammlung Rosengart</a> in Luzern, the <a href="/wiki/Albertina" title="Albertina">Albertina</a> in Wien and the <a href="/wiki/Berggruen_Museum" title="Berggruen Museum">Berggruen Museum</a> in Berlin. Schools in Gersthofen, <a href="/wiki/L%C3%BCbeck" title="Lübeck">Lübeck</a>; <a href="/wiki/Klein-Winternheim" title="Klein-Winternheim">Klein-Winternheim</a>, <a href="/wiki/Overath" title="Overath">Overath</a>; his place of birth <a href="/wiki/M%C3%BCnchenbuchsee" title="Münchenbuchsee">Münchenbuchsee</a> and <a href="/wiki/D%C3%BCsseldorf" title="Düsseldorf">Düsseldorf</a> bear his name.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Notes">Notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paul_Klee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Notes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li><span class="citation wikicite" id="endnote_fn_1a"><a href="#ref_fn_1a"><b><sup>a</sup></b></a></span> Paul Klee's father was a German citizen; his mother was Swiss. Swiss law determined citizenship along paternal lines, and thus Paul inherited his father's German citizenship. He served in the German army during World War I. Klee grew up in Berne, Switzerland, and returned there often, even before his final emigration from Germany in 1933. He died before his application for Swiss citizenship was processed.<sup id="cite_ref-fayal_100-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fayal-100"><span>[</span>100<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-zpk_naturalisation_101-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-zpk_naturalisation-101"><span>[</span>101<span>]</span></a></sup></li>

</ul>
