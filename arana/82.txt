			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Pragmatic Sanction of 1713</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
				</div>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><p>The <b>Pragmatic Sanction</b> (<a href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language" class="mw-redirect">Latin</a>: <span lang="la" xml:lang="la"><i>Sanctio Pragmatica</i></span>) was an edict issued by <a href="/wiki/Charles_VI,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles VI, Holy Roman Emperor">Charles VI</a> on 19 April 1713, to ensure that the <a href="/wiki/Habsburg_Monarchy" title="Habsburg Monarchy">Habsburg hereditary possessions</a> could be inherited by a daughter. The Head of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Habsburg" title="House of Habsburg">House of Habsburg</a> ruled the <a href="/wiki/Archduchy_of_Austria" title="Archduchy of Austria">Archduchy of Austria</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Hungary_(1526%E2%80%931867)" title="Kingdom of Hungary (1526–1867)">Kingdom of Hungary</a>, the Kingdom of Croatia,the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Bohemia" title="Kingdom of Bohemia">Kingdom of Bohemia</a>, the Italian territories awarded to Austria by the Treaty of Utrecht (<a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Milan" title="Duchy of Milan">Duchy of Milan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Naples" title="Kingdom of Naples">Kingdom of Naples</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Sicily" title="Kingdom of Sicily">Kingdom of Sicily</a>), and the <a href="/wiki/Austrian_Netherlands" title="Austrian Netherlands">Austrian Netherlands</a>. The Pragmatic Sanction did not affect the office of <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Holy Roman Emperor">Holy Roman Emperor</a> because the Imperial crown was elective, not hereditary; even though successive elected Habsburg rulers headed the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a> since 1438.</p>
<p>Since their marriage in 1708, Charles and his wife <a href="/wiki/Elisabeth_Christine_of_Brunswick-Wolfenb%C3%BCttel" title="Elisabeth Christine of Brunswick-Wolfenbüttel">Elizabeth Christine</a> had not had children, and since 1711 Charles had been the sole surviving male member of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Habsburg" title="House of Habsburg">House of Habsburg</a>. Charles's elder brother <a href="/wiki/Joseph_I,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Joseph I, Holy Roman Emperor">Joseph I</a> had died without male issue, making accession of a female a very plausible contingency. Because <a href="/wiki/Salic_law" title="Salic law">Salic law</a> precluded female inheritance, Charles VI needed to take extraordinary measures to avoid a succession dispute.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> Charles VI was, indeed, ultimately succeeded by his elder daughter <a href="/wiki/Maria_Theresa" title="Maria Theresa">Maria Theresa</a> (born 1717). Despite the promulgation of the Pragmatic Sanction, however, her accession in 1740 resulted in the outbreak of the <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Austrian_Succession" title="War of the Austrian Succession">War of the Austrian Succession</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Events_leading_to_the_Pragmatic_Sanction">Events leading to the Pragmatic Sanction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pragmatic_Sanction_of_1713&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Events leading to the Pragmatic Sanction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mutual_Pact_of_Succession" title="Mutual Pact of Succession">Mutual Pact of Succession</a></div>
<p>In 1700, the senior (oldest, first-in-line) line of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Habsburg" title="House of Habsburg">House of Habsburg</a> went extinct with the death of <a href="/wiki/Charles_II_of_Spain" title="Charles II of Spain">Charles II of Spain</a>. The <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Spanish_Succession" title="War of the Spanish Succession">War of the Spanish Succession</a> ensued, with <a href="/wiki/Louis_XIV_of_France" title="Louis XIV of France">Louis XIV of France</a> claiming the crowns of Spain for his grandson <a href="/wiki/Philip_V_of_Spain" title="Philip V of Spain">Philip</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leopold_I,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Leopold I, Holy Roman Emperor">Leopold I</a> claiming them for his son <a href="/wiki/Charles_VI,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles VI, Holy Roman Emperor">Charles</a>. In 1703, Charles and <a href="/wiki/Joseph_I,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Joseph I, Holy Roman Emperor">Joseph</a>, the sons of Leopold, signed the <a href="/wiki/Mutual_Pact_of_Succession" title="Mutual Pact of Succession">Mutual Pact of Succession</a>, granting succession rights to the daughters of Joseph and Charles in case of complete extinction of the male line, but favouring Joseph's daughters over Charles's, because Joseph was older.</p>
<p>In 1705, Leopold I died and was succeeded by his elder son, Joseph I. Six years later, Joseph I died leaving behind two daughters, Archduchesses <a href="/wiki/Maria_Josepha_of_Austria" title="Maria Josepha of Austria">Maria Josepha</a> and <a href="/wiki/Maria_Amalia_of_Austria" title="Maria Amalia of Austria">Maria Amalia</a>. Charles succeeded Joseph, according to the Pact, and Maria Josepha became his <a href="/wiki/List_of_heirs_to_the_Austrian_throne" title="List of heirs to the Austrian throne">heir presumptive</a>. However, Charles soon expressed a wish to amend the Pact in order to give his own future daughters precedence over his nieces. On 19 April 1713, the Emperor announced the changes in a secret session of the council.<sup id="cite_ref-Holborn.2C_128_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Holborn.2C_128-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Securing the right to succeed for his own daughters, who were not even born yet, became Charles's obsession. The previous succession laws had also forbidden the partition of the Habsburg dominions and provided for succession by females but they had been mostly hypothetical. The Pragmatic Sanction was the first such document to be publicly announced and as such required formal acceptance by the estates of the realms it concerned.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Foreign_recognition">Foreign recognition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pragmatic_Sanction_of_1713&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Foreign recognition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>For 10 years, Charles VI labored, with the support of his closest advisor <a href="/w/index.php?title=Johann_Christoph_von_Bartenstein&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Johann Christoph von Bartenstein (page does not exist)">Johann Christoph von Bartenstein</a>, to have his sanction accepted by the courts of Europe. Only the Electorate of Saxony and the Electorate of Bavaria did not accept, because it was detrimental to their inheritance rights. (<a href="/wiki/Augustus_III_of_Poland" title="Augustus III of Poland">Frederick Augustus II</a>, <a href="/wiki/Elector_of_Saxony" title="Elector of Saxony" class="mw-redirect">Elector of Saxony</a> was married to <a href="/wiki/Maria_Josepha_of_Austria" title="Maria Josepha of Austria">Maria Josepha of Austria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Charles_VII,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles VII, Holy Roman Emperor">Charles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Elector_of_Bavaria" title="Elector of Bavaria" class="mw-redirect">Elector of Bavaria</a> to <a href="/wiki/Maria_Amalia_of_Austria" title="Maria Amalia of Austria">Maria Amalia of Austria</a>, both daughters of Charles's deceased elder brother <a href="/wiki/Joseph_I,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Joseph I, Holy Roman Emperor">Joseph I</a>)</p>
<ul>
<li>France accepted in exchange for the duchy of Lorraine, under the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Vienna_(1738)" title="Treaty of Vienna (1738)">Treaty of Vienna (1738)</a>.</li>
<li>Spain's acceptance was also gained under the Treaty of Vienna (1738). In 1731, the 15-year-old Spanish prince Charles became the Duke of Parma and Piacenza, as Charles I, on the death of his childless granduncle Antonio Farnese. He went on to conquer Naples and Sicily, after which he returned Parma to the Emperor by the Treaty of Vienna (1738). In 1759 he became King of Spain as <a href="/wiki/Charles_III_of_Spain" title="Charles III of Spain">Charles III</a>.</li>
<li>Great Britain accepted in exchange for the cessation of operations of the <a href="/wiki/Ostend_Company" title="Ostend Company">Ostend Company</a>.</li>
<li>King <a href="/wiki/Frederick_I_of_Prussia" title="Frederick I of Prussia">Frederick I of Prussia</a> approved for his loyalty to the Emperor.</li>
</ul>
<p>Charles VI made commitments with Russia and Augustus of Saxony, King of Poland from which came two wars: the <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Polish_Succession" title="War of the Polish Succession">War of the Polish Succession</a> against France and Spain, which cost him Naples and Sicily, and the <a href="/wiki/Austro-Russian%E2%80%93Turkish_War_(1735%E2%80%9339)" title="Austro-Russian–Turkish War (1735–39)">Austro-Russian–Turkish War</a>, which cost him Little Wallachia and northern Serbia, including the <a href="/wiki/Fortress_of_Belgrade" title="Fortress of Belgrade" class="mw-redirect">Fortress of Belgrade</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Internal_recognition">Internal recognition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pragmatic_Sanction_of_1713&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Internal recognition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a>, which had an elective kingship, had accepted the house of Habsburg as hereditary kings in the male line without election in 1687 but not <a href="/wiki/Semi-Salic" title="Semi-Salic" class="mw-redirect">semi-Salic</a> inheritance. The Emperor-King agreed that if the Habsburg male line became extinct, Hungary would once again have an elective monarchy. This was the rule in the Kingdom of Bohemia too. Maria Theresa, however, still gained the throne of Hungary; the Hungarian Parliament voted its own Pragmatic Sanction in 1723 in which the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Hungary" title="Kingdom of Hungary">Kingdom of Hungary</a> accepted female <a href="/wiki/Inheritance" title="Inheritance">inheritance</a> supporting her to become queen of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Hungary" title="Kingdom of Hungary">Hungary</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Croatia was one of the crown lands that supported Emperor Charles's Pragmatic Sanction of 1713<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> and supported Empress Maria Theresa in the War of the Austrian Succession of 1741–48 and the Croatian Parliament in 1712 signed their own <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pragmatic_Santion_of_1712&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Pragmatic Santion of 1712 (page does not exist)">Pragmatic Santion of 1712</a>. Subsequently, the empress made significant contributions to Croatian matters, by making several changes in the administrative control of the Military Frontier, the feudal and tax system. She also gave the independent port of Rijeka to Croatia in 1776.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sanction.27s_failure">Sanction's failure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pragmatic_Sanction_of_1713&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Sanction's failure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Charles VI spent the time of his reign preparing Europe for a female ruler, but he did not prepare his daughter, Maria Theresa. He would not read documents to her, take her to meetings, or allow her to be introduced to ministers or have any preparation for the power she would receive in 1740. It is possible that it was because such instruction would imply an acceptance of his inability to produce a male heir.</p>
<p>Charles VI managed to get the great European powers to agree to the Pragmatic Sanction (for the time being) and died in 1740 with no male heirs. However, France, <a href="/wiki/Prussia" title="Prussia">Prussia</a>, Bavaria and Saxony broke their promises and contested the claims of his daughter Maria Theresa on his Austrian lands, and initiated the <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Austrian_Succession" title="War of the Austrian Succession">War of the Austrian Succession</a>, in which Austria lost <a href="/wiki/Silesia" title="Silesia">Silesia</a> to Prussia. The elective office of <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Holy Roman Emperor">Holy Roman Emperor</a> was filled by Joseph I's son-in-law <a href="/wiki/Charles_VII,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles VII, Holy Roman Emperor">Charles Albert</a> of Bavaria, marking the first time in several hundred years that the position was not held by a Habsburg.</p>
<p>As Emperor Charles VII, he lost his own country, Bavaria, to the Austrian army of his wife's cousin Maria Theresa and then died. His son, <a href="/wiki/Maximilian_III_Joseph,_Elector_of_Bavaria" title="Maximilian III Joseph, Elector of Bavaria">Maximilian III Joseph, Elector of Bavaria</a>, renounced claims on Austria in exchange for the return of his paternal duchy of Bavaria. Maria Theresa's husband was elected Holy Roman Emperor as <a href="/wiki/Francis_I,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Francis I, Holy Roman Emperor">Francis I</a> in 1745. The <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Aix-la-Chapelle_(1748)" title="Treaty of Aix-la-Chapelle (1748)">Treaty of Aix-la-Chapelle</a>, in 1748, finally recognized Maria Theresa's rule.</p>
