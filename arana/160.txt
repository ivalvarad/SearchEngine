<center><a href="/wiki/Human_geography" title="Human geography">Human geography</a></center>
<p><a href="/wiki/File:Snow-cholera-map-1.jpg" class="image" title="Original map by John Snow showing the clusters of cholera cases in the London epidemic of 1854"><img alt="Original map by John Snow showing the clusters of cholera cases in the London epidemic of 1854" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Snow-cholera-map-1.jpg/200px-Snow-cholera-map-1.jpg" width="200" height="187" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Snow-cholera-map-1.jpg/300px-Snow-cholera-map-1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Snow-cholera-map-1.jpg/400px-Snow-cholera-map-1.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3045" data-file-height="2840" /></a><br /></p>

<p><b><a href="/wiki/Geography" title="Geography">Geography</a></b> is the <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">science</a> that studies the lands, the features, the inhabitants, and the phenomena of the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>. A literal translation would be "to describe or write about the Earth". The first person to use the word "geography" was <a href="/wiki/Eratosthenes" title="Eratosthenes">Eratosthenes</a> (276–194 BC). Four historical traditions in geographical research are the <a href="/wiki/Spatial_analysis" title="Spatial analysis">spatial analysis</a> of the natural and the <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">human</a> phenomena (geography as the study of distribution), the <a href="/wiki/Area_studies" title="Area studies">area studies</a> (places and regions), the study of the human-land relationship, and research in the <a href="/wiki/Earth_science" title="Earth science">Earth sciences</a>. Modern geography is an all-encompassing discipline that foremost seeks to understand the Earth and all of its human and natural complexities—not merely where objects are, but how they have changed and come to be. Geography has been called "the world discipline" and "the bridge between the human and the <a href="/wiki/Physical_science" title="Physical science" class="mw-redirect">physical science</a>". Geography is divided into two main branches: <a href="/wiki/Human_geography" title="Human geography">human geography</a> and <a href="/wiki/Physical_geography" title="Physical geography">physical geography</a>.</p>

<div style="float:left;margin-right:1em"><a href="/wiki/File:Pulteney_Bridge,_daytime,_from_weir.jpg" class="image" title="Bath, Somerset"><img alt="Bath, Somerset" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Pulteney_Bridge%2C_daytime%2C_from_weir.jpg/120px-Pulteney_Bridge%2C_daytime%2C_from_weir.jpg" width="120" height="88" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Pulteney_Bridge%2C_daytime%2C_from_weir.jpg/180px-Pulteney_Bridge%2C_daytime%2C_from_weir.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Pulteney_Bridge%2C_daytime%2C_from_weir.jpg/240px-Pulteney_Bridge%2C_daytime%2C_from_weir.jpg 2x" data-file-width="815" data-file-height="598" /></a></div>
<b><a href="/wiki/Bath,_Somerset" title="Bath, Somerset">Bath</a></b> is a <a href="/wiki/City" title="City">city</a> in south-west <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, most famous for its baths fed by three <a href="/wiki/Hot_spring" title="Hot spring">hot springs</a>. The city was first recorded as a <a href="/wiki/Roman_Britain" title="Roman Britain">Roman</a> <a href="/wiki/Spa" title="Spa">spa</a>, though tradition suggests an earlier foundation. The waters from its spring were considered to be a cure for many afflictions. From <a href="/wiki/Elizabethan" title="Elizabethan" class="mw-redirect">Elizabethan</a> to <a href="/wiki/Georgian_era" title="Georgian era">Georgian</a> times it was a resort city for the wealthy. As a result of its popularity during the latter period, the city contains many fine examples of <a href="/wiki/Georgian_architecture" title="Georgian architecture">Georgian architecture</a>, particularly <a href="/wiki/The_Royal_Crescent" title="The Royal Crescent" class="mw-redirect">The Royal Crescent</a>. The city has a population of over 90,000 and is a <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a>.

<h2 style="font-family:sans-serif; font-size:100%; font-weight:bold; border: none; margin: 0; padding:0; padding-bottom:.1em; color:green;"><span class="mw-headline" id="Featured_biography">Featured biography</span></h2>
</div>
<div style="border: 1px solid #FABD23; border-top-width: 1px; vertical-align: top;background: white;opacity: 1; color: black; text-align: left; margin: 0 0 10px; padding: 1em;padding-top: .3em;">
<div style="float:right;margin-left:0.2em"><a href="/wiki/File:Mary_Anning_painting.jpg" class="image" title="Mary Anning"><img alt="Mary Anning" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Mary_Anning_painting.jpg/120px-Mary_Anning_painting.jpg" width="120" height="165" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Mary_Anning_painting.jpg/180px-Mary_Anning_painting.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Mary_Anning_painting.jpg/240px-Mary_Anning_painting.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1163" data-file-height="1600" /></a></div>
<b><a href="/wiki/Mary_Anning" title="Mary Anning">Mary Anning</a></b> was a British <a href="/wiki/Fossil_collector" title="Fossil collector" class="mw-redirect">fossil collector</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fossil_trade" title="Fossil trade" class="mw-redirect">dealer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Palaeontology" title="Palaeontology" class="mw-redirect">palaeontologist</a> who became known around the world for important finds she made in the <a href="/wiki/Jurassic" title="Jurassic">Jurassic</a> marine fossil beds at <a href="/wiki/Lyme_Regis" title="Lyme Regis">Lyme Regis</a> where she lived. Her work contributed to fundamental changes in scientific thinking about prehistoric life and the history of the earth. Her discoveries included the first <a href="/wiki/Ichthyosaur" title="Ichthyosaur">ichthyosaur</a> skeleton to be correctly identified, found when she was just twelve years old; the first two <a href="/wiki/Plesiosaur" title="Plesiosaur" class="mw-redirect">plesiosaur</a> skeletons ever found; the first <a href="/wiki/Pterosaur" title="Pterosaur">pterosaur</a> skeleton located outside Germany; and important fish fossils. Her observations were critical to the discovery that <a href="/wiki/Coprolite" title="Coprolite">coprolites</a> were fossilised faeces. Geologist <a href="/wiki/Henry_De_la_Beche" title="Henry De la Beche">Henry De la Beche</a>'d <i><a href="/wiki/Duria_Antiquior" title="Duria Antiquior">Duria Antiquior</a></i>, the first widely circulated pictorial representation of a scene from prehistoric life derived from fossil reconstructions, was based largely on fossils Anning had found. Her gender and social class prevented her from fully participating in the scientific community of 19th-century Britain, prevented her from joining the <a href="/wiki/Geological_Society_of_London" title="Geological Society of London">Geological Society of London</a>, and prevented her from getting full credit for her work during her lifetime. After her death her unusual life story attracted increasing interest. In 2010 the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society" title="Royal Society">Royal Society</a> included Anning in a list of the ten British women who have most influenced the history of science.
