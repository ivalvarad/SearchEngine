<p><b>Arab socialism</b> (<a href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language" class="mw-redirect">Arabic</a>: <span lang="ar" dir="rtl" xml:lang="ar">الاشتراكية العربية</span>‎, <i>Al-Ishtirākīya Al-‘Arabīya</i>) is a political ideology based on an amalgamation of <a href="/wiki/Pan-Arabism" title="Pan-Arabism">Pan-Arabism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Socialism" title="Socialism">socialism</a>. Arab socialism is distinct from the much broader tradition of socialist thought in the <a href="/wiki/Arab_world" title="Arab world">Arab world</a>, which predates Arab socialism by as much as fifty years. The term "Arab socialism" was coined by <a href="/wiki/Michel_Aflaq" title="Michel Aflaq">Michel Aflaq</a>, the principal founder of <a href="/wiki/Ba%27athism" title="Ba'athism">Ba'athism</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Ba%27ath_Party" title="Ba'ath Party">Arab Socialist Ba'ath Party</a>, in order to distinguish his version of socialist ideology from the international socialist movement.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Original_meaning">Original meaning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arab_socialism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Original meaning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Michel_Aflaq" title="Michel Aflaq">Michel Aflaq</a>, the co-founder of the Ba'ath Party and Ba'athist thought, coined the term Arab socialism.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDevlin197532_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDevlin197532-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> Socialism was a major tenet in Ba'athist thought, and it featured in the party's trinity slogan "Unity, <a href="/wiki/Liberty" title="Liberty">liberty</a>, socialism".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDevlin197532_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDevlin197532-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> However, by using the term Arab socialism Aflaq did not mean socialism as used normally; his version equated socialism with <a href="/wiki/Arab_nationalism" title="Arab nationalism">Arab nationalism</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDevlin197532_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDevlin197532-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> In a written statement from 1946, Aflaq wrote "The Arab nationalists are socialists", hence "there is neither incompatibility nor contradiction nor war between nationalists and socialists."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDevlin197532_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDevlin197532-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> Socialism in his mind was subservient to the <a href="/wiki/Arab_Union" title="Arab Union">Arab unity project</a> and liberty, however, he did believe that fighting for Arab liberation and unity was the same as fighting for socialism, believing they were the same side of the same coin,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDevlin197532_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDevlin197532-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> The goal of the Arab liberation struggle was to fight <a href="/wiki/Imperialism" title="Imperialism">imperialism</a>, opposing the <a href="/wiki/Ruling_class" title="Ruling class">ruling classes</a> and to fight for <a href="/wiki/Social_justice" title="Social justice">social justice</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDevlin197532_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDevlin197532-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> He further noted that "the social economic question is the issue of prime importance in our life, but it is directly related to the wider issue of nationalism" and "we want socialism to serve our nationalism."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDevlin197533_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDevlin197533-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The socialism envisaged in the party's constitution of 1947 and in later writings up to the establishment of the <a href="/wiki/United_Arab_Republic" title="United Arab Republic">United Arab Republic</a>, is moderate and shows little, if any signs of <a href="/wiki/Marxism" title="Marxism">Marxism</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDevlin197533_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDevlin197533-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> In the party's 1947 constitution it reads "socialism is a necessity which emanates from the depths of Arab nationalism .... Socialism constitutes the ideal social order [for] the <a href="/wiki/Arabs" title="Arabs">Arab people</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDevlin197533_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDevlin197533-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> It should also be noted that the Ba'ath Party was founded in 1947 as the Arab Ba'ath Party, it became the Arab Socialist Ba'ath Party in 1952 when it merged with the <a href="/wiki/Arab_Socialist_Movement" title="Arab Socialist Movement">Arab Socialist Party</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDevlin197533_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDevlin197533-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Later, in 1950, Aflaq defined socialism as "not an aim in itself, but rather a necessary means to guarantee society the highest standard of production with the farthest limit of cooperation and solidarity among the citizens ... Arab society ... needs a social order with deeper foundations, wider horizons, and more forceful realization that moderate British socialism."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDevlin197534_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDevlin197534-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> A Soviet analyst on the subject of the Ba'athist movement noted "The concept of socialist structure [as it] appeared in the articles and speeches ... [in] the period of the birth of the new movement [the Ba'ath] ... was just a hazy outline on a barely developed ideological negative."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDevlin197534_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDevlin197534-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The party <a href="/wiki/Constitution" title="Constitution">constitution</a> of 1947 called for a "just redistribution of wealth", <a href="/wiki/State_ownership" title="State ownership">state ownership</a> of <a href="/wiki/Public_utility" title="Public utility">public utilities</a>, <a href="/wiki/Natural_resource" title="Natural resource">natural resources</a>, large <a href="/wiki/Industry" title="Industry">industry</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Transport" title="Transport">transport</a>, state control over <a href="/wiki/International_trade" title="International trade">foreign</a> and <a href="/wiki/Domestic_trade" title="Domestic trade">domestic trade</a>, limiting the agricultural holdings of owners to the amount the owner could cultivate, an <a href="/wiki/Economy" title="Economy">economy</a> under some sort of state supervision, <a href="/wiki/Co-determination" title="Co-determination">workers' participation</a> in <a href="/wiki/Management" title="Management">management</a> and <a href="/wiki/Profit_sharing" title="Profit sharing">profit sharing</a>, respected inheritance and the rights of <a href="/wiki/Private_property" title="Private property">private property</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDevlin197534_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDevlin197534-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Prominent in Ba'athist writings from the 1940s and the 1950s, was the concern of <a href="/wiki/Exploitation_of_labour" title="Exploitation of labour">exploitation</a> of one group of citizens by another.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDevlin197534_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDevlin197534-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> The party forbade exploitation in its constitution.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDevlin197534_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDevlin197534-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> It further called for the abolition of class and class differences in the future envisaged society.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDevlin197534_3-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDevlin197534-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> In 1955 Aflaq defined socialism as "the sharing of the resources of the country by its citizens."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDevlin197534_3-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDevlin197534-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>While Aflaq always found it easier to define socialism as what it was not, one thing he and <a href="/wiki/Salah_al-Din_al-Bitar" title="Salah al-Din al-Bitar">Salah al-Din al-Bitar</a> were certain of, was that Arab socialism was not <a href="/wiki/Communism" title="Communism">communist</a> or related in any way to communism.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDevlin197534_3-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDevlin197534-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Part of the reason for this was the <a href="/wiki/French_Communist_Party" title="French Communist Party">French Communist Party</a>'s support for lengthening the <a href="/wiki/French_Mandate_of_Syria" title="French Mandate of Syria" class="mw-redirect">French Mandate of Syria</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDevlin197534_3-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDevlin197534-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> In a published shortly after that decision was made, Aflaq wrote "If I am asked to define socialism, I shall not look for it in the works of <a href="/wiki/Karl_Marx" title="Karl Marx">[Karl] Marx</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Lenin" title="Vladimir Lenin">[Vladimir] Lenin</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDevlin197535_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDevlin197535-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> The main cardinal difference between Arab socialism and communism was, according to Aflaq and Ba'athist in general, was the cardinal role given to <a href="/wiki/Nationalism" title="Nationalism">nationalism</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDevlin197535_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDevlin197535-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Since everything in Ba'athist thought was somehow linked to Arab nationalism, Aflaq could not bridge the gap between nationalism and <a href="/wiki/Proletarian_internationalism" title="Proletarian internationalism">communist internationalism</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDevlin197535_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDevlin197535-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> However, he did note <a href="/wiki/Josip_Broz_Tito" title="Josip Broz Tito">Josip Broz Tito</a>'s policy of <a href="/wiki/Workers%27_self_management" title="Workers' self management" class="mw-redirect">self-determination</a> while governing <a href="/wiki/Socialist_Federal_Republic_of_Yugoslavia" title="Socialist Federal Republic of Yugoslavia">Yugoslavia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDevlin197534_3-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDevlin197534-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Another difference was that Aflaq did not support the communist idea that <a href="/wiki/Class_struggle" title="Class struggle" class="mw-redirect">class struggle</a> was the central piece throughout human history, given that role instead to nationalism.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDevlin197535_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDevlin197535-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_term.27s_radicalization">The term's radicalization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arab_socialism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The term's radicalization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>However, in the 1950s, changing attitudes of socialism within the Ba'ath Party began to be apparent.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDevlin197536_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDevlin197536-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Jamal_al-Atassi" title="Jamal al-Atassi">Jamal al-Atassi</a>, in a writing dating to 1956, wrote that while Arab socialism was not communism, the party could learn from the experience of the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Bloc" title="Eastern Bloc">socialist countries</a> of how to construct a socialist society.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDevlin197536_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDevlin197536-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> It was around this time communist-inspired terms such as "masses of the people" and "people's organization" began to heavily used in Ba'athist literature, while at the same time emphasizing class conflict more than before.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDevlin197536_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDevlin197536-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> He wrote that "Socialism cannot realize its goals unless it starts from the [fact of] division, difference, and conflict among society's structures and classes."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDevlin197536_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDevlin197536-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Atassi ended the article by calling for the "oppressed classes" of the workers, peasants and "other strugglers" to join in the effort to overthrow the oppressors to establish a united Arab society.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDevlin197536_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDevlin197536-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> In short, he called for revolutionary struggle.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDevlin197536_5-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDevlin197536-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> While Aflaq did believe class conflict existed, he believed it to be subordinate to nationalism.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDevlin197536_5-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDevlin197536-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Munif_al-Razzaz" title="Munif al-Razzaz">Munif al-Razzaz</a>, a Jordanese Ba'athist, wrote the Ba'athist classic "Why Socialism Now?" in 1957.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDevlin197536_5-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDevlin197536-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> In it he takes a "very different" approach on interpreting the meaning of socialism from Aflaq.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDevlin197536_5-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDevlin197536-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> As he wrote, "Socialism is a way of life, not just an economic order. It extends to all aspects of life—<a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Politics" title="Politics">politics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Training" title="Training">training</a>, <a href="/wiki/Education" title="Education">education</a>, social life, <a href="/wiki/Health" title="Health">health</a>, <a href="/wiki/Morality" title="Morality">morals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Literature" title="Literature">literature</a>, <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">science</a>, <a href="/wiki/History" title="History">history</a>, and others, both great and small."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDevlin197536_5-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDevlin197536-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> In contrast to official party writings which stressed Arab unity above else, Razzaz tried to demonstrate the interdependent nature of unity, liberty and socialism.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDevlin197536_5-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDevlin197536-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> He criticized the view that socialism had to come after Arab unity, stating "Socialism, freedom and unity are not different names for different things, ... but different facets of one basic law from which they spring."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDevlin197537_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDevlin197537-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> He further noted that "If I believe in man and in man's worth, then I should believe in unity, nationalism, <a href="/wiki/Freedom" title="Freedom" class="mw-redirect">freedom</a>, and socialism because each of them represents a facet of man's fundamental value."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDevlin197537_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDevlin197537-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Razzaz ended the article, stating that socialism had been achieved to the same degree as "freedom and unity have been achieved."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDevlin197537_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDevlin197537-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="External_reception">External reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arab_socialism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: External reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The website of the Arab Socialist Ba'ath Party has an entry dated 1 January 2011, stating: "In 28/10/2003, by the attendance of comrade Al-Ahmar and Mr. Ching, the Arab Socialist Ba'ath Party and the <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_China" title="Communist Party of China">Communist Party of China</a> agreed to sign an agreement at the end of the discussions for three years, 2004/2005/2006. The two parties wish to promote and enhance the relations of friendship and cooperation between their two parties and co-ordinate their efforts for embodying their common objectives for the wellbeing of their two friendly peoples" [minor proofreading edits].<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>On the other hand, the Arabist Bernard Lewis has stated: "Nobody seems to have a good word to say for Arab socialism. Commercial, professional, and middle class elements bring against it the usual complaints which are brought against socialism in <a href="/wiki/Western_world" title="Western world">Western countries</a>. <a href="/wiki/Left-wing_politics" title="Left-wing politics">Left-wingers</a> dismiss Arab socialism with contempt as a half-hearted and inefficient compromise which has the merits neither of socialism nor of capitalism."<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
