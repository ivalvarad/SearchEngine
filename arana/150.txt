<div class="hatnote">This is a <a href="/wiki/Chinese_name" title="Chinese name">Chinese name</a>; the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_surname" title="Chinese surname">family name</a> is <a href="/wiki/Luo_(surname)" title="Luo (surname)">Luo</a>.</div>
<p><b>Luo Yixiu</b><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>a<span>]</span></a></sup> (<a href="/wiki/Simplified_Chinese_characters" title="Simplified Chinese characters">simplified Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh-Hans" xml:lang="zh-Hans">罗一秀</span>; <a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_characters" title="Traditional Chinese characters">traditional Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh-Hant" xml:lang="zh-Hant">羅一秀</span>; October 20, 1889 – February 11, 1910), a <a href="/wiki/Han_Chinese" title="Han Chinese">Han Chinese</a> woman, was the first wife of the future Chinese <a href="/wiki/Communism" title="Communism">communist</a> revolutionary and political leader <a href="/wiki/Mao_Zedong" title="Mao Zedong">Mao Zedong</a>, to whom she was married from 1908 until her death. Coming from the area around <a href="/wiki/Shaoshan" title="Shaoshan">Shaoshan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hunan" title="Hunan">Hunan</a>, in south central China – the same region as Mao – her family were impoverished local landowners.</p>
<p>Most of what is known about their marriage comes from an account Mao gave to American reporter <a href="/wiki/Edgar_Snow" title="Edgar Snow">Edgar Snow</a> in 1936, which Snow included in his book <i><a href="/wiki/Red_Star_Over_China" title="Red Star Over China">Red Star Over China</a></i>. According to Mao, he and Luo Yixiu were the subject of an <a href="/wiki/Arranged_marriage" title="Arranged marriage">arranged marriage</a> organised by their respective fathers, <a href="/wiki/Mao_Yichang" title="Mao Yichang">Mao Yichang</a> and Luo Helou. Luo was eighteen and Mao just fourteen years old at the time of their betrothal. Although Mao took part in the wedding ceremony, he later stated that he was unhappy with the marriage, never consummating it and refusing to live with his wife. Socially disgraced, she lived with Mao's parents for two years until she died of <a href="/wiki/Dysentery" title="Dysentery">dysentery</a>, while he moved out of the village to continue his studies elsewhere, eventually becoming a founding member of the <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_China" title="Communist Party of China">Communist Party of China</a>. Various biographers have suggested that Mao's experience of this marriage affected his later views, leading him to become a critic of arranged marriage and a vocal <a href="/wiki/Feminism" title="Feminism">feminist</a>. He would marry three more times throughout his life, to <a href="/wiki/Yang_Kaihui" title="Yang Kaihui">Yang Kaihui</a>, <a href="/wiki/He_Zizhen" title="He Zizhen">He Zizhen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jiang_Qing" title="Jiang Qing">Jiang Qing</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luo_Yixiu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Born on October 20, 1889, Luo Yixiu was the eldest daughter of Luo Helou (<span lang="hans" xml:lang="hans">罗合楼</span>; 1871–1943), a <i>shenshi</i> (<span lang="hans" xml:lang="hans">绅士</span>)&#160;–  or rural intellectual who earned his living as a farmer&#160;–  and his wife (1869–1912), whose surname was Mao and who was a distant great-aunt of Mao Zedong.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPantsovLevine201225HuLiu1996111_.28dates_of_Luo_Helou.3B_Luo_Yixiu_as_his_eldest_daughter.3B_relation_to_Mao.27s_family.29_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPantsovLevine201225HuLiu1996111_.28dates_of_Luo_Helou.3B_Luo_Yixiu_as_his_eldest_daughter.3B_relation_to_Mao.27s_family.29-10"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>b<span>]</span></a></sup> Although historian <a href="/wiki/Lee_Feigon" title="Lee Feigon">Lee Feigon</a> stated that the Luo family was locally important,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFeigon200223_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFeigon200223-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> Mao biographers Alexander V. Pantsov and Steven I. Levine claimed that they had fallen into poverty.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPantsovLevine201225_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPantsovLevine201225-13"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> Luo Helou and his wife had five sons and five daughters, but seven of these children died, leaving them only three daughters. The couple's lack of adult sons diminished their social status, for in Chinese society at the time, only sons could continue the family lineage.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPantsovLevine201226_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPantsovLevine201226-14"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_marriage">The marriage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luo_Yixiu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The marriage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparation">Preparation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luo_Yixiu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Preparation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tmulti tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:278px;max-width:278px">
<div class="tsingle" style="float:left;margin:1px;width:125px;max-width:125px">
<div class="thumbimage"><a href="/wiki/File:Mao_Yichang.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Mao_Yichang.jpg/123px-Mao_Yichang.jpg" width="123" height="192" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Mao_Yichang.jpg/185px-Mao_Yichang.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Mao_Yichang.jpg/246px-Mao_Yichang.jpg 2x" data-file-width="260" data-file-height="405" /></a></div>
</div>
<div class="tsingle" style="float:left;margin:1px;width:149px;max-width:149px">
<div class="thumbimage"><a href="/wiki/File:Mao_Zedong_1913.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/a/a8/Mao_Zedong_1913.jpg/147px-Mao_Zedong_1913.jpg" width="147" height="194" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/a/a8/Mao_Zedong_1913.jpg/221px-Mao_Zedong_1913.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/a/a8/Mao_Zedong_1913.jpg/294px-Mao_Zedong_1913.jpg 2x" data-file-width="395" data-file-height="520" /></a></div>

<div style="clear:left"></div>
<div class="thumbcaption" style="clear:left;text-align:left;background-color:transparent">Mao Yichang (left) organised the marriage for his eldest son, Mao Zedong (right).</div>

<p>Mao Zedong had been born and raised at his father's farm in Shaoshanchong, a small rural village named for the nearby Shaoshan mountain.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPantsovLevine201211_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPantsovLevine201211-15"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> His disciplinarian father, <a href="/wiki/Mao_Yichang" title="Mao Yichang">Mao Yichang</a>, had decided to deal with Zedong's rebellious attitude in a manner typical of the time, by forcing him into an arranged marriage that would compel him to take family matters seriously.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETerrill198012Feigon200223HuLiu1996111.E2.80.93112_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETerrill198012Feigon200223HuLiu1996111.E2.80.93112-16"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> Yichang also desired a helper for his own wife, <a href="/wiki/Wen_Qimei" title="Wen Qimei">Wen Qimei</a>, whose health had deteriorated through years of heavy agricultural labour.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPantsovLevine201226_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPantsovLevine201226-14"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> He selected Luo Yixiu in either late 1907 or 1908.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPantsovLevine201225_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPantsovLevine201225-13"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> Her <a href="/wiki/Kinship" title="Kinship">kinship</a> to the Maos may have helped in this selection, as Luo Yixiu's mother's four brothers, surnamed Mao, lived only two <i><a href="/wiki/Li_(unit)" title="Li (unit)">li</a></i> from Mao Yichang's home in Shaoshanchong.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHuLiu1996111_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHuLiu1996111-11"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> Following traditional procedures, a matchmaker would have been sent to the Luo family house, and the Luo family would have been socially expected to accept the marriage proposal immediately.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPantsovLevine201226_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPantsovLevine201226-14"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> Luo Helou was happy to see his eldest daughter married.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPantsovLevine201226_14-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPantsovLevine201226-14"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> The two families exchanged gifts and signed the marriage contract, after which the marriage was considered inviolable.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPantsovLevine201226_14-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPantsovLevine201226-14"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Zedong first met Yixiu on the day that the contract was signed.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPantsovLevine201226_14-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPantsovLevine201226-14"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> Years later, his granddaughter Kong Dongmei stated that Mao was unhappy with his father's choice, and that he instead was in love with his cousin, Wang Shigu. However, marriage to Wang had been ruled out by a local <a href="/wiki/Divination" title="Divination">diviner</a> because their <a href="/wiki/Chinese_astrology" title="Chinese astrology">horoscopes</a> were incompatible.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPantsovLevine201226_14-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPantsovLevine201226-14"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> Although displeased by the arrangement, Mao agreed to marry Luo.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPantsovLevine201226_14-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPantsovLevine201226-14"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> At the time he was fourteen, and later erroneously informed <a href="/wiki/Edgar_Snow" title="Edgar Snow">Edgar Snow</a> that Luo was aged twenty,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESnow1961145_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESnow1961145-17"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> a claim independently accepted by Mao biographers <a href="/wiki/Ross_Terrill" title="Ross Terrill">Ross Terrill</a> and <a href="/wiki/Philip_Short" title="Philip Short">Philip Short</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETerrill198012Short199929_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETerrill198012Short199929-18"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> but later challenged by biographers <a href="/wiki/Jung_Chang" title="Jung Chang">Jung Chang</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jon_Halliday" title="Jon Halliday">Jon Halliday</a>, and Alexander V. Pantsov and Steven I. Levine, who established that she was eighteen.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChangHalliday20057PantsovLevine201225_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChangHalliday20057PantsovLevine201225-19"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_wedding">The wedding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luo_Yixiu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The wedding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The wedding took place in 1908.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChangHalliday20057_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChangHalliday20057-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> According to a number of Mao's biographers, the ceremony would have likely followed traditional rural Hunanese custom. Thus, it probably would have begun with a feast in the groom's home on the day before the ceremony, to which friends and relatives were invited. The next day, the bride would have been dressed in red, with a red veil over her face, and carried by red <a href="/wiki/Palanquin" title="Palanquin" class="mw-redirect">palanquin</a> to the groom's family home. There, her veil would have been removed, and she would have been expected to express unhappiness and dissatisfaction with the groom by publicly insulting him.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETerrill198012PantsovLevine201226_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETerrill198012PantsovLevine201226-20"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> According to tradition, a display of <a href="/wiki/Fireworks" title="Fireworks">fireworks</a> would probably have taken place, before both bride and groom would have <a href="/wiki/Kowtow" title="Kowtow">kowtowed</a> to each guest, to the groom's <a href="/wiki/Ancestor_veneration_in_China" title="Ancestor veneration in China">ancestral altar</a>, to the spirits, and to one another, concluding the ceremony.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETerrill198012Short1999649PantsovLevine201226_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETerrill198012Short1999649PantsovLevine201226-21"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>If traditional practices were adhered to, feasting would have continued for two days, while guests would have given gifts, mainly of money, to the newlyweds.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPantsovLevine201226_14-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPantsovLevine201226-14"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> The wedding ceremony would have culminated with the guests entering the bridal chamber, where they would have made various sexual references and innuendos, led by a figure with his face painted black.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPantsovLevine201227_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPantsovLevine201227-22"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> In Chinese rural tradition, the bride was expected to show the bloodstains on the bed sheets from her wedding night to prove that her <a href="/wiki/Hymen" title="Hymen">hymen</a> had been broken during <a href="/wiki/Sexual_intercourse" title="Sexual intercourse">sexual intercourse</a>, and that she had therefore been a virgin.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPantsovLevine201227_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPantsovLevine201227-22"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Married_life">Married life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luo_Yixiu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Married life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Shaoshan_01.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Shaoshan_01.JPG/250px-Shaoshan_01.JPG" width="250" height="141" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Shaoshan_01.JPG/375px-Shaoshan_01.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Shaoshan_01.JPG/500px-Shaoshan_01.JPG 2x" data-file-width="3328" data-file-height="1872" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Shaoshan_01.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Mao's Shaoshanchong home in 2010; it was here that Luo lived after the wedding.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>According to what he told Snow, Mao refused to live with his wife and claimed that they had never consummated their marriage.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESnow1961145Short199929_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESnow1961145Short199929-23"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> Soon after the wedding, he ran away from home to live with an unemployed student in Shaoshan.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEShort199929PantsovLevine201227_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEShort199929PantsovLevine201227-24"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> There he spent much of his time reading, particularly historical works like <a href="/wiki/Sima_Qian" title="Sima Qian">Sima Qian</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Records_of_the_Grand_Historian" title="Records of the Grand Historian">Records of the Grand Historian</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/Ban_Gu" title="Ban Gu">Ban Gu</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Former_Han_Dynasty" title="History of the Former Han Dynasty" class="mw-redirect">History of the Former Han Dynasty</a>,</i> and political tracts like <a href="/wiki/Feng_Guifen" title="Feng Guifen">Feng Guifen</a>'s <i>Personal Protests from the Study of Jiao Bin</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEShort199929PantsovLevine201227_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEShort199929PantsovLevine201227-24"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Now considered part of the Mao family, Luo lived with Mao Yichang and Wen Qimei at their home, but was publicly humiliated by her husband's disappearance; some locals considered her to be Yichang's <a href="/wiki/Concubine" title="Concubine" class="mw-redirect">concubine</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEShort199929PantsovLevine201227_24-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEShort199929PantsovLevine201227-24"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> Luo Yixiu died of dysentery on February 11, 1910, the day after <a href="/wiki/Chinese_New_Year" title="Chinese New Year">Chinese New Year</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChangHalliday20057PantsovLevine201228.2C_589L.C3.BC2009.5Bhttp:.2F.2Fdangshi.people.com.cn.2FGB.2F144956.2F11272977.html_2.5D_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChangHalliday20057PantsovLevine201228.2C_589L.C3.BC2009.5Bhttp:.2F.2Fdangshi.people.com.cn.2FGB.2F144956.2F11272977.html_2.5D-25"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> Mao Zedong returned home; his father forgave him for his disobedience, and in the autumn of 1910 agreed to finance his son's studies at the Dongshan Higher Primary School, and so Mao left Shaoshanchong.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPantsovLevine201228_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPantsovLevine201228-26"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> When in 1936 Mao told Snow "I do not consider her my wife",<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESnow1961145_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESnow1961145-17"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> he made no mention of her death.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChangHalliday20057_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChangHalliday20057-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Luo Yixiu's grave is located on the mountain facing Mao Zedong's former residence in Shaoshanchong, a few steps away from the tomb of his parents.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHuLiu1996112_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHuLiu1996112-27"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luo_Yixiu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When Mao Zedong returned to Shaoshan in 1925 to organize a local peasant movement, he went to visit Luo Yixiu's relatives, including her father Luo Helou and his nephew Luo Shiquan (罗石泉). Luo Shiquan would join the Communist Party in the winter of that year and would remain a peasant activist until the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_Communist_Revolution" title="Chinese Communist Revolution">1949 revolution</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHuLiu1996112.E2.80.93113_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHuLiu1996112.E2.80.93113-28"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> Because Luo Yixiu had died without offspring, when the Mao lineage updated its <a href="/wiki/Genealogy_book" title="Genealogy book">genealogy book</a> in 1941, Mao Anlong (<span lang="hans" xml:lang="hans">毛岸龙</span>), who was Mao Zedong's third son with his second wife <a href="/wiki/Yang_Kaihui" title="Yang Kaihui">Yang Kaihui</a>, was listed as Luo's descendant.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHuLiu1996112_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHuLiu1996112-27"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> In 1950, Mao sent his eldest son <a href="/wiki/Mao_Anying" title="Mao Anying">Mao Anying</a> to Shaoshan and instructed him to visit Luo Shiquan.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHuLiu1996113_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHuLiu1996113-29"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> Mao also kept in contact with two men who had married Luo Yixiu's sisters, and met one of these men when he returned to Shaoshan in 1959 for the first time since the 1920s.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHuLiu1996113.E2.80.93114_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHuLiu1996113.E2.80.93114-30"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Influence_on_Mao">Influence on Mao</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luo_Yixiu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Influence on Mao">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <i><a href="/wiki/Mao:_A_Reinterpretation" title="Mao: A Reinterpretation">Mao: A Reinterpretation</a></i>, American historian <a href="/wiki/Lee_Feigon" title="Lee Feigon">Lee Feigon</a> argued that Mao's experience with arranged marriage inspired him to become "a vehement advocate of women's rights" in the late 1910s, as he began to write articles for the left-wing press criticizing the traditional Chinese family system and arguing that love, rather than societal or family pressures, should be the primary determinant in marriage.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFeigon200223_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFeigon200223-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> This idea had previously been expressed by journalist and sinologist <a href="/wiki/Clare_Hollingworth" title="Clare Hollingworth">Clare Hollingworth</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHollingworth198518.E2.80.9319_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHollingworth198518.E2.80.9319-31"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> In their biography <i><a href="/wiki/Mao:_The_Unknown_Story" title="Mao: The Unknown Story">Mao: The Unknown Story</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Jung_Chang" title="Jung Chang">Jung Chang</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jon_Halliday" title="Jon Halliday">Jon Halliday</a> agreed, stating that it was this experience with Luo that turned Mao into a "fierce opponent" of arranged marriage.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChangHalliday20057_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChangHalliday20057-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Mao would marry three more women over the course of his life: <a href="/wiki/Yang_Kaihui" title="Yang Kaihui">Yang Kaihui</a> in December 1920, <a href="/wiki/He_Zizhen" title="He Zizhen">He Zizhen</a> in May 1928, and <a href="/wiki/Jiang_Qing" title="Jiang Qing">Jiang Qing</a> in November 1939.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPantsovLevine2012589_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPantsovLevine2012589-32"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
