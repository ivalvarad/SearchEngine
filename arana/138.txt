<dt><b>October 1</b></dt>
</dl>
<div id="mp-tfa-img" style="float: left; margin: 0.5em 0.9em 0.4em 0em;">
<div class="thumbinner mp-thumb" style="background: transparent; border: none; padding: 0; max-width: 133px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Mae_La_refugee_camp.jpg" title="Burmese refugees in a camp in Thailand"><img alt="Burmese refugees in a camp in Thailand" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/8/8c/Mae_La_refugee_camp_TFA.jpg/133px-Mae_La_refugee_camp_TFA.jpg" width="133" height="100" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/8/8c/Mae_La_refugee_camp_TFA.jpg/200px-Mae_La_refugee_camp_TFA.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/8/8c/Mae_La_refugee_camp_TFA.jpg/266px-Mae_La_refugee_camp_TFA.jpg 2x" data-file-width="672" data-file-height="504" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption" style="padding: 0.25em 0; word-wrap: break-word;">Burmese refugees in a camp in Thailand</div>

<p>The <b><a href="/wiki/Gateway_Protection_Programme" title="Gateway Protection Programme">Gateway Protection Programme</a></b> is operated by the British government in partnership with the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_High_Commissioner_for_Refugees" title="United Nations High Commissioner for Refugees">United Nations High Commissioner for Refugees</a> (UNHCR) and co-funded by the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>, offering a legal route for a quota of UNHCR-identified refugees to settle in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>. It was proposed by the British Home Secretary, <a href="/wiki/David_Blunkett" title="David Blunkett">David Blunkett</a>, in October 2001, and its legal basis was established by the <a href="/wiki/Nationality,_Immigration_and_Asylum_Act_2002" title="Nationality, Immigration and Asylum Act 2002">Nationality, Immigration and Asylum Act 2002</a>. The programme launched in March 2004, initially with a quota of 500 and later 750 refugees per year, but the actual number of refugees resettled in most years has been fewer than the quota permitted. As of 2012, refugees of 12 different nationalities had been resettled, with the largest groups being <a href="/wiki/Refugees_of_Iraq" title="Refugees of Iraq">Iraqis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Demographics_of_the_Democratic_Republic_of_the_Congo" title="Demographics of the Democratic Republic of the Congo">Congolese</a>, <a href="/wiki/People_of_Ethiopia" title="People of Ethiopia">Ethiopians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Demographics_of_Burma" title="Demographics of Burma" class="mw-redirect">Burmese</a> and <a href="/wiki/Somalis" title="Somalis">Somalis</a>, and 18 of the UK's 434 local authorities had participated as resettlement locations. Evaluations of the programme have praised it as having a positive impact on the reception of refugees by local communities, but have also noted the difficulties these refugees have faced in securing employment. <dt><b>October 2</b></dt>
</dl>
<div id="mp-tfa-img" style="float: left; margin: 0.5em 0.9em 0.4em 0em;">
<div class="thumbinner mp-thumb" style="background: transparent; border: none; padding: 0; max-width: 133px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Capon_Chapel_Capon_Bridge_WV_2005_09_19_03.jpg" class="image" title="Capon Chapel"><img alt="Capon Chapel" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Capon_Chapel_Capon_Bridge_WV_2005_09_19_03.jpg/133px-Capon_Chapel_Capon_Bridge_WV_2005_09_19_03.jpg" width="133" height="100" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Capon_Chapel_Capon_Bridge_WV_2005_09_19_03.jpg/200px-Capon_Chapel_Capon_Bridge_WV_2005_09_19_03.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Capon_Chapel_Capon_Bridge_WV_2005_09_19_03.jpg/266px-Capon_Chapel_Capon_Bridge_WV_2005_09_19_03.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2288" data-file-height="1712" /></a></div>
</div>
<p><b><a href="/wiki/Capon_Chapel" title="Capon Chapel">Capon Chapel</a></b> is a mid-19th-century church near the town of <a href="/wiki/Capon_Bridge,_West_Virginia" title="Capon Bridge, West Virginia">Capon Bridge</a> in the US state of <a href="/wiki/West_Virginia" title="West Virginia">West Virginia</a>. A <a href="/wiki/Baptists" title="Baptists">Baptist</a> congregation began gathering at the site of the present-day church as early as 1756. The land belonged to William C. Nixon, a member of the <a href="/wiki/Virginia_House_of_Delegates" title="Virginia House of Delegates">Virginia House of Delegates</a>, and later to the extended family of Captain David Pugh of the <a href="/wiki/West_Virginia_House_of_Delegates" title="West Virginia House of Delegates">West Virginia House of Delegates</a>. Capon Chapel was used as a place of worship by Baptists until the late 19th or early 20th century, by the <a href="/wiki/Methodist_Episcopal_Church,_South" title="Methodist Episcopal Church, South">Southern Methodist Episcopal Church</a> for most of the 20th century, and in this century by the <a href="/wiki/United_Methodist_Church" title="United Methodist Church">United Methodist Church</a>. The church cemetery contains the remains of John Monroe (one of the first ministers at the site), Nixon, Pugh, <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a> veterans from the <a href="/wiki/Union_(American_Civil_War)" title="Union (American Civil War)">Union</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Confederate_States_of_America" title="Confederate States of America">Confederacy</a>, and free and enslaved <a href="/wiki/African_American" title="African American">African Americans</a>. Capon Chapel, along with its cemetery, was added to the <a href="/wiki/National_Register_of_Historic_Places" title="National Register of Historic Places">National Register of Historic Places</a> in 2012 in recognition of its rural religious architecture representative of the <a href="/wiki/Potomac_Highlands_of_West_Virginia" title="Potomac Highlands of West Virginia">Potomac Highlands</a> region, and for its service as a rural church in <a href="/wiki/Hampshire_County,_West_Virginia" title="Hampshire County, West Virginia">Hampshire County</a>. (<a href="/wiki/Capon_Chapel" title="Capon Chapel"><b>Full&#160;article...</b></a>)</p>

<dt><b>October 3</b></dt>

<div id="mp-tfa-img" style="float: left; margin: 0.5em 0.9em 0.4em 0em;"><a href="/wiki/File:Suillus_brevipes_98931.jpg" class="image" title="Suillus brevipes"><img alt="Suillus brevipes" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Suillus_brevipes_98931.jpg/133px-Suillus_brevipes_98931.jpg" width="133" height="103" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Suillus_brevipes_98931.jpg/200px-Suillus_brevipes_98931.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Suillus_brevipes_98931.jpg/266px-Suillus_brevipes_98931.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="794" /></a></div>
<p><i><b><a href="/wiki/Suillus_brevipes" title="Suillus brevipes">Suillus brevipes</a></b></i> is a species of <a href="/wiki/Fungus" title="Fungus">fungus</a> in the family <a href="/wiki/Boletaceae" title="Boletaceae">Boletaceae</a>. First described by American <a href="/wiki/Mycologist" title="Mycologist" class="mw-redirect">mycologists</a> in the late 19th century, it is commonly known as the short-stemmed slippery Jack. The <a href="/wiki/Mushroom" title="Mushroom">mushrooms</a> produced by the fungus are characterized by a chocolate to reddish-brown <a href="/wiki/Pileus_(mycology)" title="Pileus (mycology)">cap</a> covered with a sticky layer of slime, and a short whitish <a href="/wiki/Stipe_(mycology)" title="Stipe (mycology)">stalk</a> without a <a href="/wiki/Partial_veil" title="Partial veil">partial veil</a>. The cap can reach a diameter of about 10&#160;cm (3.9&#160;in), while the stalk is up to 6&#160;cm (2.4&#160;in) long and 2&#160;cm (0.8&#160;in) thick. Like other <a href="/wiki/Bolete" title="Bolete">bolete</a> mushrooms, <i>S.&#160;brevipes</i> produces <a href="/wiki/Basidiospore" title="Basidiospore">spores</a> in a vertically arranged layer of spongy tubes with openings that form a layer of small yellowish pores on the underside of the cap. The fungus grows on or near various species of two- and three-needled pines, especially <a href="/wiki/Lodgepole_pine" title="Lodgepole pine" class="mw-redirect">lodgepole</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ponderosa_pine" title="Ponderosa pine" class="mw-redirect">ponderosa pine</a>. It is found throughout North America, and has been <a href="/wiki/Introduced_species" title="Introduced species">introduced</a> to several other countries via transplanted pines. The mushrooms are slimy but <a href="/wiki/Edible_mushroom" title="Edible mushroom">edible</a>, and are good sources of the <a href="/wiki/Essential_fatty_acid" title="Essential fatty acid">essential</a> fatty acid <a href="/wiki/Linoleic_acid" title="Linoleic acid">linoleic acid</a>. (<a href="/wiki/Suillus_brevipes" title="Suillus brevipes"><b>Full&#160;article...</b></a>)</p>

<dt><b>October 4</b></dt>

<div id="mp-tfa-img" style="float: left; margin: 0.5em 0.9em 0.4em 0em;"><a href="/wiki/File:George_Formby_Sr,_1919.jpg" class="image" title="George Formby Sr in 1919"><img alt="George Formby Sr in 1919" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/b6/George_Formby_Sr%2C_1919.jpg/100px-George_Formby_Sr%2C_1919.jpg" width="100" height="144" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/b6/George_Formby_Sr%2C_1919.jpg/150px-George_Formby_Sr%2C_1919.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/b6/George_Formby_Sr%2C_1919.jpg/200px-George_Formby_Sr%2C_1919.jpg 2x" data-file-width="312" data-file-height="450" /></a></div>
<p><b><a href="/wiki/George_Formby_Sr" title="George Formby Sr">George Formby Sr</a></b> (1875–1921) was known as one of the greatest <a href="/wiki/Music_hall" title="Music hall">music hall</a> performers of the early 20th century. His comedy and songs played upon Lancashire stereotypes, and he was popular around Britain. His nickname, "The Wigan Nightingale", was coined because of the way he would use his <a href="/wiki/Bronchitis" title="Bronchitis">bronchial</a> cough as a comedic device. Born into poverty, he sang on street corners before starting a professional career in the 1890s, when he built up a following in Lancashire. He also developed a series of stage characters, including that of "John Willie", an accident-prone northerner. Formby had a successful recording career and made the transition from music hall to <a href="/wiki/Revue" title="Revue">revue</a> in 1916. He suffered from <a href="/wiki/Tuberculosis" title="Tuberculosis">tuberculosis</a>, and a stage accident in 1916 weakened him further; after he contracted <a href="/wiki/Influenza_A_virus_subtype_H1N1" title="Influenza A virus subtype H1N1">influenza</a> in the <a href="/wiki/1918_flu_pandemic" title="1918 flu pandemic">1918 pandemic</a>, he died in 1921 at the age of 45. Formby's act, and one of his costumes, inspired <a href="/wiki/Charlie_Chaplin" title="Charlie Chaplin">Charlie Chaplin</a> to create <a href="/wiki/The_Tramp" title="The Tramp">the Tramp</a>, his signature film character. Formby's son used parts of his father's act when starting his stage career and, once established, also changed his name to <a href="/wiki/George_Formby" title="George Formby">George Formby</a>; he went on to become the top British male star in box office takings between 1937 and 1943. (<a href="/wiki/George_Formby_Sr" title="George Formby Sr"><b>Full&#160;article...</b></a>)</p>

<dt><b>October 5</b></dt>

<div id="mp-tfa-img" style="float: left; margin: 0.5em 0.9em 0.4em 0em;"><a href="/wiki/File:Gert_20_September_1993.jpg" class="image" title="Gert on 20 September"><img alt="Gert on 20 September" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Gert_20_September_1993.jpg/120px-Gert_20_September_1993.jpg" width="120" height="130" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Gert_20_September_1993.jpg/180px-Gert_20_September_1993.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Gert_20_September_1993.jpg/240px-Gert_20_September_1993.jpg 2x" data-file-width="539" data-file-height="583" /></a></div>
<p><b><a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Gert_(1993)" title="Hurricane Gert (1993)">Hurricane Gert</a></b> was a large <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a> that caused extensive flooding throughout Central America and Mexico in September&#160;1993. It originated over the southwestern <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a> and briefly attained <a href="/wiki/Tropical_storm" title="Tropical storm" class="mw-redirect">tropical storm</a> strength before crossing <a href="/wiki/Nicaragua" title="Nicaragua">Nicaragua</a>, <a href="/wiki/Honduras" title="Honduras">Honduras</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Yucat%C3%A1n_Peninsula" title="Yucatán Peninsula">Yucatán Peninsula</a>. Once over warm water in the <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Campeche" title="Bay of Campeche">Bay of Campeche</a>, Gert grew into a <a href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_Hurricane_Scale#Category_2" title="Saffir–Simpson Hurricane Scale" class="mw-redirect">Category&#160;2 hurricane</a> and made <a href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" title="Landfall (meteorology)" class="mw-redirect">landfall</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Coast_of_Mexico" title="Gulf Coast of Mexico">Gulf Coast of Mexico</a>. The cyclone quickly weakened to a depression over the rugged terrain and eventually dissipated over the Pacific Ocean. Gert's broad wind circulation produced heavy rain across Central America, flooding property and crops. Although the highest winds occurred upon landfall in Mexico, the worst effects there were due to extreme rainfall of 31.41&#160;inches (798&#160;mm) over the mountains. Following the overflow of several rivers, expansive parts of the <a href="/wiki/La_Huasteca" title="La Huasteca">Huasteca region</a> became submerged, forcing tens of thousands to evacuate. Roads across the affected countries were severely disrupted, and thousands became homeless. The disaster killed 116&#160;people, left 16&#160;missing, and caused $170&#160;million in damage. (<a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Gert_(1993)" title="Hurricane Gert (1993)"><b>Full&#160;article...</b></a>)</p>

<dt><b>October 6</b></dt>
</dl>
<div id="mp-tfa-img" style="float: left; margin: 0.5em 0.9em 0.4em 0em;"><a href="/wiki/File:OG2874Waddy1945.jpg" class="image" title="John Lloyd Waddy"><img alt="John Lloyd Waddy" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/OG2874Waddy1945.jpg/100px-OG2874Waddy1945.jpg" width="100" height="144" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/OG2874Waddy1945.jpg/150px-OG2874Waddy1945.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/OG2874Waddy1945.jpg/200px-OG2874Waddy1945.jpg 2x" data-file-width="215" data-file-height="310" /></a></div>
<p><b><a href="/wiki/John_Lloyd_Waddy" title="John Lloyd Waddy">John Lloyd Waddy</a></b> (1916–1987) was a senior officer and aviator in the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Australian_Air_Force" title="Royal Australian Air Force">Royal Australian Air Force</a>, who later served as a member of the <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales_Legislative_Assembly" title="New South Wales Legislative Assembly">New South Wales Legislative Assembly</a> and <a href="/wiki/Minister_of_the_Crown" title="Minister of the Crown">Minister of the Crown</a>. As a fighter pilot during <a href="/wiki/Military_history_of_Australia_during_World_War_II" title="Military history of Australia during World War II">World War II</a>, he shot down fifteen enemy aircraft in the <a href="/wiki/Western_Desert_Campaign" title="Western Desert Campaign">Desert War</a>, becoming one of Australia's top-scoring <a href="/wiki/Flying_ace" title="Flying ace">aces</a> and earning the <a href="/wiki/Distinguished_Flying_Cross_(United_Kingdom)" title="Distinguished Flying Cross (United Kingdom)">Distinguished Flying Cross</a>. He went on to command <a href="/wiki/No._80_Squadron_RAAF" title="No. 80 Squadron RAAF">No.&#160;80 Squadron</a> in the <a href="/wiki/South_West_Pacific_theatre_of_World_War_II" title="South West Pacific theatre of World War II">South West Pacific</a>, where he was awarded the US <a href="/wiki/Air_Medal" title="Air Medal">Air Medal</a>. He was also one of eight senior pilots who took part in the "<a href="/wiki/Morotai_Mutiny" title="Morotai Mutiny">Morotai Mutiny</a>" of April 1945. He led the <a href="/wiki/Air_Force_Reserve_(Australia)" title="Air Force Reserve (Australia)">Air Force Reserve</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Group_captain" title="Group captain">group captain</a> in the early 1950s. Active in business and in veterans' groups, he was appointed an <a href="/wiki/Officer_of_the_Order_of_the_British_Empire" title="Officer of the Order of the British Empire" class="mw-redirect">Officer of the Order of the British Empire</a> in 1955. As the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_of_Australia" title="Liberal Party of Australia">Liberal</a> <a href="/wiki/Electoral_district_of_Kirribilli" title="Electoral district of Kirribilli">Member for Kirribilli</a> from 1962 to 1976, he held cabinet posts in the <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales_Parliament" title="New South Wales Parliament" class="mw-redirect">New South Wales Parliament</a>, including Minister for Child Welfare and Social Welfare (later Youth and Community Services), <a href="/wiki/Minister_for_Health_(New_South_Wales)" title="Minister for Health (New South Wales)">Minister for Health</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Minister_for_Police_and_Emergency_Services_(New_South_Wales)" title="Minister for Police and Emergency Services (New South Wales)">Minister for Police and Services</a>. (<a href="/wiki/John_Lloyd_Waddy" title="John Lloyd Waddy"><b>Full&#160;article...</b></a>)</p>

<dt><b>October 7</b></dt>
</dl>
<div id="mp-tfa-img" style="float: left; margin: 0.5em 0.9em 0.4em 0em;">
<div class="thumbinner mp-thumb" style="background: transparent; border: none; padding: 0; max-width: 120px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Hu_Zhengyans_seal.JPG" class="image" title="Hu Zhengyan's seal"><img alt="Hu Zhengyan's seal" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Hu_Zhengyans_seal.JPG/120px-Hu_Zhengyans_seal.JPG" width="120" height="123" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Hu_Zhengyans_seal.JPG/180px-Hu_Zhengyans_seal.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Hu_Zhengyans_seal.JPG/240px-Hu_Zhengyans_seal.JPG 2x" data-file-width="326" data-file-height="334" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption" style="padding: 0.25em 0; word-wrap: break-word;">Hu Zhengyan's seal</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><b><a href="/wiki/Hu_Zhengyan" title="Hu Zhengyan">Hu Zhengyan</a></b> (<abbr title="circa">c.</abbr> 1584&#160;– 1674) was a Chinese artist, printmaker and publisher. He worked in <a href="/wiki/Chinese_calligraphy" title="Chinese calligraphy">calligraphy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chinese_painting" title="Chinese painting">painting</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Seal_(East_Asia)" title="Seal (East Asia)">seal-carving</a> <i>(his own seal pictured)</i>, but was primarily a publisher, producing academic texts and records of his work. Hu lived in <a href="/wiki/Nanjing" title="Nanjing">Nanjing</a> during the transition from the <a href="/wiki/Ming_dynasty" title="Ming dynasty">Ming dynasty</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Qing_dynasty" title="Qing dynasty">Qing dynasty</a>. A Ming loyalist, he designed the <a href="/wiki/Hongguang_Emperor" title="Hongguang Emperor" class="mw-redirect">Hongguang Emperor</a>'s personal seal and was offered a position at the emperor's remnant court, but declined the post, and held only minor political offices. He largely retired from society after the emperor's capture and death in 1645. He owned and operated an academic publishing house called the Ten Bamboo Studio in Nanjing, where he practised various <a href="/wiki/Color_printing#China" title="Color printing">multi-colour printing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paper_embossing" title="Paper embossing">embossing</a> techniques. Hu's work pioneered new techniques in colour printmaking, producing delicate gradations of colour not previously achievable. He is best known for <i>The Ten Bamboo Studio Manual of Painting and Calligraphy</i>, an artist's primer which remained in print for around 200 years. (<a href="/wiki/Hu_Zhengyan" title="Hu Zhengyan"><b>Full&#160;article...</b></a>)</p>

<dt><b>October 8</b></dt>
</dl>
<div id="mp-tfa-img" style="float: left; margin: 0.5em 0.9em 0.4em 0em;"><a href="/wiki/File:Japanese_aircraft_carrier_Shinano.jpg" class="image" title="Shinano"><img alt="Shinano" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Japanese_aircraft_carrier_Shinano.jpg/133px-Japanese_aircraft_carrier_Shinano.jpg" width="133" height="68" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Japanese_aircraft_carrier_Shinano.jpg/200px-Japanese_aircraft_carrier_Shinano.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Japanese_aircraft_carrier_Shinano.jpg/266px-Japanese_aircraft_carrier_Shinano.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3839" data-file-height="1967" /></a></div>
<p><i><b><a href="/wiki/Japanese_aircraft_carrier_Shinano" title="Japanese aircraft carrier Shinano">Shinano</a></b></i> was an <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_carrier" title="Aircraft carrier">aircraft carrier</a> built by the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Japanese_Navy" title="Imperial Japanese Navy">Imperial Japanese Navy</a> during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, the largest one built up to that time. <a href="/wiki/Keel_laying" title="Keel laying">Laid down</a> in May 1940 as the third of the <a href="/wiki/Yamato-class_battleship" title="Yamato-class battleship"><i>Yamato</i>-class</a> <a href="/wiki/Battleship" title="Battleship">battleships</a>, the ship's partially complete hull was ordered to be converted to a carrier following Japan's disastrous loss of four fleet carriers at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Midway" title="Battle of Midway">Battle of Midway</a> in mid-1942. Her conversion was still incomplete in November 1944 when she was ordered to sail from the <a href="/wiki/Yokosuka_Naval_Arsenal" title="Yokosuka Naval Arsenal">Yokosuka Naval Arsenal</a> to <a href="/wiki/Kure,_Hiroshima" title="Kure, Hiroshima">Kure Naval Base</a> to complete her <a href="/wiki/Fitting_out" title="Fitting out" class="mw-redirect">fitting out</a> and to transfer a load of 50 <a href="/wiki/Yokosuka_MXY7_Ohka" title="Yokosuka MXY7 Ohka">Yokosuka MXY7 <i>Ohka</i></a> rocket-propelled <i><a href="/wiki/Kamikaze" title="Kamikaze">kamikaze</a></i> <a href="/wiki/Flying_bomb" title="Flying bomb">flying bombs</a>. Hastily dispatched with an inexperienced crew and serious design and construction flaws, the ship had inadequate pumps, no fire-control systems, and no carrier aircraft. She was sunk en route, just 10 days after <a href="/wiki/Ship_commissioning" title="Ship commissioning">commissioning</a>, on 29 November 1944, by four <a href="/wiki/Torpedo" title="Torpedo">torpedoes</a> from the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">US Navy</a> <a href="/wiki/Submarine" title="Submarine">submarine</a> <a href="/wiki/USS_Archerfish_(SS-311)" title="USS Archerfish (SS-311)"><i>Archerfish</i></a>. Over a thousand sailors and civilians were rescued, but some 1,435 were lost, including her captain. She remains the largest warship ever sunk by a submarine. (<a href="/wiki/Japanese_aircraft_carrier_Shinano" title="Japanese aircraft carrier Shinano"><b>Full&#160;article...</b></a>)</p>

<div id="mp-tfa-img" style="float: left; margin: 0.5em 0.9em 0.4em 0em;"><a href="/wiki/File:B_menziesii_gnangarra_19.jpg" class="image" title="Banksia menziesii"><img alt="Banksia menziesii" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/B_menziesii_gnangarra_19.jpg/133px-B_menziesii_gnangarra_19.jpg" width="133" height="100" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/B_menziesii_gnangarra_19.jpg/200px-B_menziesii_gnangarra_19.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/B_menziesii_gnangarra_19.jpg/266px-B_menziesii_gnangarra_19.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2576" data-file-height="1932" /></a></div>
<p><i><b><a href="/wiki/Banksia_menziesii" title="Banksia menziesii">Banksia menziesii</a></b></i>, commonly known as firewood banksia, is a <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> of flowering plant in the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Banksia" title="Banksia">Banksia</a></i>. It is a gnarled tree up to 10&#160;m (33&#160;ft) tall or, in its northern range, a spreading shrub. The serrated leaves are dull green with new growth a paler grey green. The autumn and winter <a href="/wiki/Inflorescence" title="Inflorescence">inflorescences</a> are often two-coloured red or pink and yellow. First described by the botanist <a href="/wiki/Robert_Brown_(botanist)" title="Robert Brown (botanist)">Robert Brown</a> in the early 19th century, no separate <a href="/wiki/Variety_(botany)" title="Variety (botany)">varieties</a> of <i>B. menziesii</i> are recognised. It is found in Western Australia, from the <a href="/wiki/Perth,_Western_Australia" title="Perth, Western Australia" class="mw-redirect">Perth</a> region north to the <a href="/wiki/Murchison_River_(Western_Australia)" title="Murchison River (Western Australia)">Murchison River</a>, and generally grows on sandy soils in <a href="/wiki/Scrubland" title="Scrubland" class="mw-redirect">scrubland</a> or low <a href="/wiki/Woodland" title="Woodland">woodland</a>. It provides food for an array of <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vertebrate" title="Vertebrate">vertebrate</a> animals; <a href="/wiki/Honeyeater" title="Honeyeater">honeyeaters</a> and other birds are prominent visitors. A relatively hardy plant, <i>B. menziesii</i> is commonly seen in gardens, <a href="/wiki/Tree_lawn" title="Tree lawn" class="mw-redirect">nature strips</a> and parks in Australian urban areas with <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_climate" title="Mediterranean climate">Mediterranean climates</a>, but its sensitivity to <a href="/wiki/Root_rot" title="Root rot">root rot</a> makes it short-lived in places with humid summers. It is widely used in the <a href="/wiki/Floriculture" title="Floriculture">cut flower industry</a> in Australia and overseas. (<a href="/wiki/Banksia_menziesii" title="Banksia menziesii"><b>Full&#160;article...</b></a>)</p>

<dt><b>October 10</b></dt>

<div id="mp-tfa-img" style="float: left; margin: 0.5em 0.9em 0.4em 0em;"><a href="/wiki/File:Washington_Street_(Marquette,_Michigan).jpg" class="image" title="Washington Street in 2012"><img alt="Washington Street in 2012" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Washington_Street_%28Marquette%2C_Michigan%29.jpg/100px-Washington_Street_%28Marquette%2C_Michigan%29.jpg" width="100" height="150" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Washington_Street_%28Marquette%2C_Michigan%29.jpg/150px-Washington_Street_%28Marquette%2C_Michigan%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Washington_Street_%28Marquette%2C_Michigan%29.jpg/200px-Washington_Street_%28Marquette%2C_Michigan%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2000" data-file-height="3008" /></a></div>
<p><b><a href="/wiki/U.S._Route_41_Business_(Marquette,_Michigan)" title="U.S. Route 41 Business (Marquette, Michigan)">Business US Highway&#160;41</a></b> was a <a href="/wiki/Michigan_State_Trunkline_Highway_System" title="Michigan State Trunkline Highway System">state trunkline highway</a> and <a href="/wiki/Business_loop" title="Business loop" class="mw-redirect">business loop</a> off <a href="/wiki/U.S._Route_41_in_Michigan" title="U.S. Route 41 in Michigan">US&#160;41</a>/<a href="/wiki/M-28_(Michigan_highway)" title="M-28 (Michigan highway)">M-28</a> in <a href="/wiki/Marquette,_Michigan" title="Marquette, Michigan">Marquette, Michigan</a>, along Washington and Front streets. These two streets originated with the founding of the city in the mid-19th century, and served the <a href="/wiki/Central_business_district" title="Central business district">downtown area</a>; the <a href="/wiki/Marquette_City_Hall" title="Marquette City Hall">original city hall</a> was built in 1895 on Washington Street. In 1910 the city started paving its streets, replacing <a href="/wiki/Plank_road" title="Plank road">wooden planks</a> with <a href="/wiki/Asphalt_concrete" title="Asphalt concrete">asphalt</a>; later that decade, Washington and Front streets were included in the state highway system. In 1926 they were included in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Numbered_Highway_System" title="United States Numbered Highway System" class="mw-redirect">United States Numbered Highway System</a>. When US&#160;41/M-28 was moved to bypass the downtown in 1963, Washington and Front streets were redesignated as a business loop. The highway was also co-designated Business M-28 on state maps between 1975 and 1981, mirroring a <a href="/wiki/M-28_Business_(Ishpeming-Negaunee,_Michigan)" title="M-28 Business (Ishpeming-Negaunee, Michigan)" class="mw-redirect">similar business loop designation</a> in the neighboring cities of <a href="/wiki/Ishpeming,_Michigan" title="Ishpeming, Michigan">Ishpeming</a> and <a href="/wiki/Negaunee,_Michigan" title="Negaunee, Michigan">Negaunee</a>. Jurisdiction over the two streets was transferred to the city as part of a highway swap that resulted in the <a href="/wiki/Decommissioned_highway" title="Decommissioned highway">decommissioning</a> of the trunkline in 2005. (<a href="/wiki/U.S._Route_41_Business_(Marquette,_Michigan)" title="U.S. Route 41 Business (Marquette, Michigan)"><b>Full&#160;article...</b></a>)</p>

<div id="mp-tfa-img" style="float: left; margin: 0.5em 0.9em 0.4em 0em;">
<div class="thumbinner mp-thumb" style="background: transparent; border: none; padding: 0; max-width: 120px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Propithecus_candidus_002.jpg" title="Silky sifaka"><img alt="Silky sifaka" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/ca/Propithecus_candidus_002_TFA.jpg/120px-Propithecus_candidus_002_TFA.jpg" width="120" height="120" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/ca/Propithecus_candidus_002_TFA.jpg/180px-Propithecus_candidus_002_TFA.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/ca/Propithecus_candidus_002_TFA.jpg/240px-Propithecus_candidus_002_TFA.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2528" data-file-height="2528" /></a></div>

<p>The <b><a href="/wiki/Silky_sifaka" title="Silky sifaka">silky sifaka</a></b> (<i>Propithecus candidus</i>) is a large <a href="/wiki/Lemur" title="Lemur">lemur</a> with silky white fur that lives in northeastern <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a>. It is listed by the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature">International Union for Conservation of Nature</a> as one of the world's <a href="/wiki/The_World%27s_25_Most_Endangered_Primates" title="The World's 25 Most Endangered Primates">25 most critically endangered primates</a>. The silky sifaka lives in groups of two to nine and spends most of its day feeding, resting, playing, grooming, and traveling. Like other eastern <a href="/wiki/Sifaka" title="Sifaka">sifakas</a>, it eats leaves and seeds, but also fruit, flowers, and even soil. As with other sifaka species, group members will groom, play with, carry, and nurse infants that are not their own. Adults vocalize frequently despite having only seven different calls. Like all lemurs, it relies strongly on <a href="/wiki/Olfaction" title="Olfaction">scent</a> for communication; males frequently <a href="/wiki/Territory_(animal)" title="Territory (animal)">scent-mark</a>. The species is found in the rainforests of northeastern Madagascar, with the remaining population in <a href="/wiki/Marojejy_National_Park" title="Marojejy National Park">Marojejy National Park</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anjanaharibe-Sud_Reserve" title="Anjanaharibe-Sud Reserve">Anjanaharibe-Sud Special Reserve</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Makira_Natural_Park" title="Makira Natural Park">Makira Forest Protected Area</a>, the Betaolana Corridor, and some unprotected forest fragments. Locals hunt the silky sifaka throughout its range. (<a href="/wiki/Silky_sifaka" title="Silky sifaka"><b>Full&#160;article...</b></a>)</p>

<dt><b>October 12</b></dt>

<p><b><a href="/wiki/Charles_Domery" title="Charles Domery">Charles Domery</a></b> (<a href="/wiki/Circa" title="Circa">c.</a>&#160;1778&#160;– after 1800) was a soldier with an <a href="/wiki/Polyphagia" title="Polyphagia">unusually large appetite</a>. Serving in the <a href="/wiki/Prussian_Army" title="Prussian Army">Prussian Army</a> during the <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_First_Coalition" title="War of the First Coalition">War of the First Coalition</a>, he deserted to the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolutionary_Army" title="French Revolutionary Army">French Revolutionary Army</a> in return for better <a href="/wiki/Military_rations" title="Military rations">rations</a>. He was voraciously hungry; while stationed near Paris, he was recorded as having eaten 174 cats in a year, and would eat 4 to 5 pounds (1.8 to 2.3&#160;kg) of grass each day if he could not find other food. During service on the French frigate <i><a href="/wiki/HMS_Donegal_(1798)" title="HMS Donegal (1798)">Hoche</a></i>, he attempted to eat the severed leg of a crew member hit by cannon fire, before it was wrestled from him. In 1799 <i>Hoche</i> was captured by British forces, and Domery and the crew were interned in <a href="/wiki/Liverpool" title="Liverpool">Liverpool</a>. Despite receiving ten times the rations of other inmates, he remained ravenous, eating the prison cat, at least 20 rats, and many prison candles. Domery's case was brought to the attention of the <a href="/wiki/Sick_and_Hurt_Commissioners" title="Sick and Hurt Commissioners">Sick and Hurt Commissioners</a>, who tested his eating capacity by feeding him four bottles of <a href="/wiki/Porter_(beer)" title="Porter (beer)">porter</a> and a total of 16 pounds (7.3&#160;kg) of raw cow's udder, raw beef and <a href="/wiki/Tallow" title="Tallow">tallow</a> candles, all of which he ate and drank in a single day without defecating, urinating, or vomiting. (<a href="/wiki/Charles_Domery" title="Charles Domery"><b>Full&#160;article...</b></a>)</p>

<dt><b>October 13</b></dt>

<div id="mp-tfa-img" style="float: left; margin: 0.5em 0.9em 0.4em 0em;">
<div class="thumbinner mp-thumb" style="background: transparent; border: none; padding: 0; max-width: 120px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Hastings_Station_-_geograph.org.uk_-_789720.jpg" class="image" title="Hastings railway station"><img alt="Hastings railway station" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Hastings_Station_-_geograph.org.uk_-_789720.jpg/120px-Hastings_Station_-_geograph.org.uk_-_789720.jpg" width="120" height="81" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Hastings_Station_-_geograph.org.uk_-_789720.jpg/180px-Hastings_Station_-_geograph.org.uk_-_789720.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Hastings_Station_-_geograph.org.uk_-_789720.jpg/240px-Hastings_Station_-_geograph.org.uk_-_789720.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="430" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption" style="padding: 0.25em 0; word-wrap: break-word;"><a href="/wiki/Hastings_railway_station" title="Hastings railway station">Hastings railway station</a></div>

<p>The <b><a href="/wiki/Hastings_Line" title="Hastings Line">Hastings Line</a></b> is a secondary railway line in <a href="/wiki/Kent" title="Kent">Kent</a> and <a href="/wiki/East_Sussex" title="East Sussex">East Sussex</a>, England, linking <a href="/wiki/Hastings_railway_station" title="Hastings railway station">Hastings</a> with the main town of <a href="/wiki/Tunbridge_Wells_railway_station" title="Tunbridge Wells railway station">Tunbridge Wells</a>, and from there into <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> via <a href="/wiki/Tonbridge_railway_station" title="Tonbridge railway station">Tonbridge</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sevenoaks_railway_station" title="Sevenoaks railway station">Sevenoaks</a>. Although the line primarily carries passengers, a <a href="/wiki/Gypsum" title="Gypsum">gypsum</a> mine served by the railway is a source of freight traffic. Passenger trains on the line are operated by <a href="/wiki/Southeastern_(train_operating_company)" title="Southeastern (train operating company)">Southeastern</a>. The railway was built by the <a href="/wiki/South_Eastern_Railway_(UK)" title="South Eastern Railway (UK)" class="mw-redirect">South Eastern Railway</a> in the early 1850s across the difficult terrain of the <a href="/wiki/High_Weald" title="High Weald" class="mw-redirect">High Weald</a>. Supervision was lax, and contractors skimped on the lining of the <a href="/wiki/Tunnel" title="Tunnel">tunnels</a>, causing deficiencies that showed up after the railway had opened. Rectifications included a restricted <a href="/wiki/Loading_gauge" title="Loading gauge">loading gauge</a> along the line, requiring the use of specially made <a href="/wiki/Rolling_stock" title="Rolling stock">rolling stock</a>. Served by <a href="/wiki/Steam_locomotive" title="Steam locomotive">steam locomotives</a> from the time of opening until the late 1950s, passenger services were then taken over by a fleet of <a href="/wiki/Diesel-electric_multiple_unit" title="Diesel-electric multiple unit" class="mw-redirect">diesel-electric multiple units</a> built to the line’s loading gauge. Freight was handled by <a href="/wiki/Diesel_locomotive" title="Diesel locomotive">diesel locomotives</a>, also built to fit the loading gauge. The diesel-electric units were in service until 1986, when the line was electrified and the most severely affected tunnels were <a href="/wiki/Single_track_(rail)" title="Single track (rail)" class="mw-redirect">singled</a>. <div id="mp-tfa-img" style="float: left; margin: 0.5em 0.9em 0.4em 0em;"><a href="/wiki/File:Mary_M._O%27Reilly_was_acting_director_of_the_Mints_and_Assay_Office_of_the_United_States_Department_of_the_Treasury.jpg" class="image" title="Mary M. <p><b><a href="/wiki/Mary_Margaret_O%27Reilly" title="Mary Margaret O'Reilly">Mary Margaret O'Reilly</a></b> (1865–1949) was the Assistant Director of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Mint" title="United States Mint">United States Bureau of the Mint</a>. One of the highest-ranking female civil servants of her time, the "sweetheart of the Treasury" often served as the Mint's acting director in the absence of the director beginning in 1916, and was formally made assistant director in 1924. O'Reilly lived her early life in <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>. She left school around age 14 to help support her widowed mother and her siblings, and worked in <a href="/wiki/Worcester,_Massachusetts" title="Worcester, Massachusetts">Worcester</a> for twenty years as a clerk. In 1904, O'Reilly succeeded in gaining a position at the Mint Bureau. Her rapid rise in the hierarchy was unusual for a woman at that time, and with many of the directors under whom she served having little knowledge of or interest in the bureau's operations, the task of running the Mint often fell to her. Beginning in 1933, O'Reilly served under her first female director, <a href="/wiki/Nellie_Tayloe_Ross" title="Nellie Tayloe Ross">Nellie Tayloe Ross</a>, and soon forged a strong bond with her. O'Reilly was so indispensable to the bureau's operations that President <a href="/wiki/Franklin_Delano_Roosevelt" title="Franklin Delano Roosevelt" class="mw-redirect">Franklin Delano Roosevelt</a> postponed her retirement, scheduled for 1935, to 1938. <p>"<b><a href="/wiki/Lisa_the_Vegetarian" title="Lisa the Vegetarian">Lisa the Vegetarian</a></b>" is the fifth episode of the <a href="/wiki/The_Simpsons_(season_7)" title="The Simpsons (season 7)">seventh season</a> of the animated series <i><a href="/wiki/The_Simpsons" title="The Simpsons">The Simpsons</a></i>, originally aired on the <a href="/wiki/Fox_Broadcasting_Company" title="Fox Broadcasting Company">Fox network</a> in the United States on October 15, 1995. Cohen">David X. Cohen</a>. It was watched by 14.6 million viewers and received positive reviews from television critics. <dt><b>October 16</b></dt>

<div id="mp-tfa-img" style="float: left; margin: 0.5em 0.9em 0.4em 0em;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Burning_of_the_Houses_of_Parliament.jpg" class="image" title="The Burning of the Houses of Parliament from the end of Abingdon Street; a coloured aquatint by an unknown artist"><img alt="The Burning of the Houses of Parliament from the end of Abingdon Street; a coloured aquatint by an unknown artist" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/The_Burning_of_the_Houses_of_Parliament.jpg/133px-The_Burning_of_the_Houses_of_Parliament.jpg" width="133" height="92" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/The_Burning_of_the_Houses_of_Parliament.jpg/200px-The_Burning_of_the_Houses_of_Parliament.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/The_Burning_of_the_Houses_of_Parliament.jpg/266px-The_Burning_of_the_Houses_of_Parliament.jpg 2x" data-file-width="705" data-file-height="486" /></a></div>
<p>On 16 October 1834 <b><a href="/wiki/Burning_of_Parliament" title="Burning of Parliament">a major fire</a></b> largely destroyed the <a href="/wiki/Palace_of_Westminster" title="Palace of Westminster">Palace of Westminster</a>, the medieval royal palace used as the home of the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Parliament of the United Kingdom">British parliament</a>. The conflagration was caused by the burning of small wooden <a href="/wiki/Tally_stick" title="Tally stick">tally sticks</a> that had been used as part of the accounting procedures of the <a href="/wiki/Exchequer" title="Exchequer">Exchequer</a> until 1826, which were being disposed of carelessly in the two furnaces under the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">House of Lords</a>. The blaze caused a chimney fire, initially under the floor of the Lords' chamber, then up through the walls before spreading rapidly throughout the complex. The fire lasted for most of the night and developed into the biggest conflagration to occur in London between the <a href="/wiki/Great_Fire_of_London" title="Great Fire of London">Great Fire</a> of 1666 and <a href="/wiki/The_Blitz" title="The Blitz">the Blitz</a> of the Second World War; large crowds were attracted to the spectacle. By the following morning a large part of the palace had been destroyed, although the actions of the <a href="/wiki/London_Fire_Brigade#History" title="London Fire Brigade">London Fire Engine Establishment</a> ensured that <a href="/wiki/Westminster_Hall" title="Westminster Hall" class="mw-redirect">Westminster Hall</a> and a few other parts of the old Houses of Parliament survived. In 1836 a design competition for a new palace was won by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Barry" title="Charles Barry">Charles Barry</a> who, in collaboration with <a href="/wiki/Augustus_Pugin" title="Augustus Pugin">Augustus Pugin</a>, incorporated the surviving buildings into the new complex. (<a href="/wiki/Burning_of_Parliament" title="Burning of Parliament"><b>Full&#160;article...</b></a>)</p>

<dt><b>October 17</b></dt>

<div id="mp-tfa-img" style="float: left; margin: 0.5em 0.9em 0.4em 0em;"><a href="/wiki/File:Roy_Kilner_1922.jpg" class="image" title="Roy Kilner"><img alt="Roy Kilner" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/3/36/Roy_Kilner_1922.jpg/100px-Roy_Kilner_1922.jpg" width="100" height="163" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/3/36/Roy_Kilner_1922.jpg/150px-Roy_Kilner_1922.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/3/36/Roy_Kilner_1922.jpg/200px-Roy_Kilner_1922.jpg 2x" data-file-width="564" data-file-height="920" /></a></div>
<p><b><a href="/wiki/Roy_Kilner" title="Roy Kilner">Roy Kilner</a></b> (1890–1928) was an English professional <a href="/wiki/Cricket" title="Cricket">cricketer</a> who played nine <a href="/wiki/Test_cricket" title="Test cricket">Test matches</a> for <a href="/wiki/England_cricket_team" title="England cricket team">England</a> between 1924 and 1926. He played for <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire_County_Cricket_Club" title="Yorkshire County Cricket Club">Yorkshire County Cricket Club</a> between 1911 and 1927, scoring 1,000 runs in a season ten times and <a href="/wiki/Take_wickets" title="Take wickets" class="mw-redirect">taking 100 wickets</a> in a season five times. He first played for Yorkshire as a <a href="/wiki/Batting_(cricket)" title="Batting (cricket)">batsman</a> before being wounded in the First World War. Returning in 1919 to a team short of <a href="/wiki/Bowler_(cricket)" title="Bowler (cricket)" class="mw-redirect">bowlers</a>, he developed into a proficient <a href="/wiki/Left-arm_orthodox_spin" title="Left-arm orthodox spin">left-arm spinner</a>. His aggressive batting and warm personality made him a popular player with both cricketers and spectators. First chosen for England in 1924, he was the second most successful bowler on the <a href="/wiki/English_cricket_team_in_Australia_in_1924%E2%80%9325" title="English cricket team in Australia in 1924–25">Ashes tour of 1924–25</a>. He was selected during the 1926 <a href="/wiki/The_Ashes" title="The Ashes">Ashes</a> series but dropped for the final Test. Kilner went on several coaching trips to India during English winters, and on one of these, in 1927–28, he contracted an illness; on his return to England, he died aged 37. His funeral was attended by over 100,000 people. (<a href="/wiki/Roy_Kilner" title="Roy Kilner"><b>Full&#160;article...</b></a>)</p>

<dt><b>October 18</b></dt>

<p><i><b><a href="/wiki/Deathrow_(video_game)" title="Deathrow (video game)">Deathrow</a></b></i> is a <a href="/wiki/Sports_video_game" title="Sports video game" class="mw-redirect">sports video game</a> developed by <a href="/wiki/Southend_Interactive" title="Southend Interactive">Southend Interactive</a> and published by <a href="/wiki/Ubisoft_Entertainment" title="Ubisoft Entertainment" class="mw-redirect">Ubisoft Entertainment</a>. It was released as an <a href="/wiki/Xbox_(console)" title="Xbox (console)">Xbox</a> <a href="/wiki/Video_game_console" title="Video game console">gaming system</a> exclusive in November 2002 in Europe and North America. Built on an in-house 3D <a href="/wiki/Game_engine" title="Game engine">game engine</a>, it was Southend's first full release. Supporting up to eight players, the game is based on the fictional <a href="/wiki/Extreme_sport" title="Extreme sport">extreme sport</a> Blitz, a futuristic full-contact hybrid of <a href="/wiki/Hockey" title="Hockey">hockey</a> and basketball played with a <a href="/wiki/Flying_disc" title="Flying disc">flying disc</a>. Teams can win on points or by knocking opposing players unconscious. The <a href="/wiki/Single-player" title="Single-player" class="mw-redirect">single-player</a> campaign is set in the 23rd century, when Blitz is a popular televised sport and the teams battle to scale the ranks and win the championship. The game's largely favorable reviews praised its fast-paced action and surround sound, but faulted its high difficulty curve, generic soundtrack, and lack of an online multiplayer mode. The game won the <i><a href="/wiki/TeamXbox" title="TeamXbox">TeamXbox</a></i> 2002 Breakthrough Game of the Year and the <a href="/wiki/IGN" title="IGN">IGN</a> 2002 Best Game Nobody Played awards. IGN later reported that a sequel would be unlikely due to the original's low revenue. Southend dissolved in 2013. (<a href="/wiki/Deathrow_(video_game)" title="Deathrow (video game)"><b>Full&#160;article...</b></a>)</p>

<dt><b>October 19</b></dt>
</dl>
<div id="mp-tfa-img" style="float: left; margin: 0.5em 0.9em 0.4em 0em;"><a href="/wiki/File:Banksia_verticillata_and_New_Holland_Honeyeater.JPG" class="image" title="Banksia verticillata"><img alt="Banksia verticillata" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Banksia_verticillata_and_New_Holland_Honeyeater.JPG/133px-Banksia_verticillata_and_New_Holland_Honeyeater.JPG" width="133" height="89" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Banksia_verticillata_and_New_Holland_Honeyeater.JPG/200px-Banksia_verticillata_and_New_Holland_Honeyeater.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Banksia_verticillata_and_New_Holland_Honeyeater.JPG/266px-Banksia_verticillata_and_New_Holland_Honeyeater.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1600" data-file-height="1067" /></a></div>
<p><i><b><a href="/wiki/Banksia_verticillata" title="Banksia verticillata">Banksia verticillata</a></b></i> or Albany banksia is a species of <a href="/wiki/Shrub" title="Shrub">shrub</a> of the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Banksia" title="Banksia">Banksia</a></i> in the <a href="/wiki/Proteaceae" title="Proteaceae">Proteaceae</a> family. It is native to the <a href="/wiki/Southwest_botanical_province" title="Southwest botanical province" class="mw-redirect">southwest</a> of <a href="/wiki/Western_Australia" title="Western Australia">Western Australia</a> and can reach up to 3&#160;m (10&#160;ft) in height, or even 5&#160;m (16&#160;ft) in sheltered areas; it is shorter in more exposed areas. This species has elliptic green leaves and large, bright golden yellow <a href="/wiki/Inflorescence" title="Inflorescence">flower spikes</a> that appear in summer and autumn. The <a href="/wiki/New_Holland_honeyeater" title="New Holland honeyeater">New Holland honeyeater</a> (<i>Phylidonyris novaehollandiae</i>) is the most prominent <a href="/wiki/Pollination" title="Pollination">pollinator</a> although several other species of <a href="/wiki/Honeyeater" title="Honeyeater">honeyeater</a>, as well as bees, visit the flower spikes. Listed as a vulnerable species by the Australian Government, it occurs in two <a href="/wiki/Disjunct_distribution" title="Disjunct distribution">disjunct</a> populations on <a href="/wiki/Granite" title="Granite">granite</a> outcrops along the south coast of <a href="/wiki/Western_Australia" title="Western Australia">Western Australia</a>, with the main population near <a href="/wiki/Albany,_Western_Australia" title="Albany, Western Australia">Albany</a> and a smaller population near <a href="/wiki/Walpole,_Western_Australia" title="Walpole, Western Australia">Walpole</a>. It is threatened by <a href="/wiki/Root_rot" title="Root rot">root rot</a> (specifically <i><a href="/wiki/Phytophthora_cinnamomi" title="Phytophthora cinnamomi">Phytophthora cinnamomi</a></i>) and aerial canker (<i><a href="/wiki/Zythiostroma" title="Zythiostroma">Zythiostroma</a></i>). <i>B. verticillata</i> is killed by bushfire, following which new plants regenerate from seed. Populations take over a decade to produce seed, and fire intervals of more than twenty years are needed to allow the <a href="/wiki/Canopy_seed_bank" title="Canopy seed bank">canopy seed bank</a> to accumulate. (<a href="/wiki/Banksia_verticillata" title="Banksia verticillata"><b>Full&#160;article...</b></a>)</p>

<dt><b>October 20</b></dt>
</dl>
<p><b><a href="/wiki/Luo_Yixiu" title="Luo Yixiu">Luo Yixiu</a></b> (1889–1910) was the first wife of the future Chinese <a href="/wiki/Communism" title="Communism">communist</a> revolutionary and political leader <a href="/wiki/Mao_Zedong" title="Mao Zedong">Mao Zedong</a>, to whom she was married from 1908 until her death. She came from a family of impoverished <a href="/wiki/Han_Chinese" title="Han Chinese">Han Chinese</a> landowners near <a href="/wiki/Shaoshan" title="Shaoshan">Shaoshan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hunan" title="Hunan">Hunan</a>, in south central China. Most of what is known about their marriage comes from Mao and appears in the 1936 book <i><a href="/wiki/Red_Star_Over_China" title="Red Star Over China">Red Star Over China</a></i> by the reporter <a href="/wiki/Edgar_Snow" title="Edgar Snow">Edgar Snow</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Arranged_marriage" title="Arranged marriage">marriage was arranged</a> by their fathers when Luo was eighteen and Mao was just fourteen. He later said that he was unhappy with the marriage, never consummating it and refusing to live with his wife. He moved out of the village to continue his studies elsewhere, eventually becoming a founding member of the <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_China" title="Communist Party of China">Communist Party of China</a>. Socially disgraced, Luo lived with Mao's parents for two years until she died of <a href="/wiki/Dysentery" title="Dysentery">dysentery</a>. Various biographers have suggested that this marriage affected Mao's later views, leading him to become a critic of arranged marriage and a vocal <a href="/wiki/Feminism" title="Feminism">feminist</a>. He would marry three more times, to <a href="/wiki/Yang_Kaihui" title="Yang Kaihui">Yang Kaihui</a>, <a href="/wiki/He_Zizhen" title="He Zizhen">He Zizhen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jiang_Qing" title="Jiang Qing">Jiang Qing</a>. (<a href="/wiki/Luo_Yixiu" title="Luo Yixiu"><b>Full&#160;article...</b></a>)</p>

<dt><b>October 21</b></dt>

<div id="mp-tfa-img" style="float: left; margin: 0.5em 0.9em 0.4em 0em;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sci-Fi_Dine-In_Theater_interior_by_hyku.jpg" class="image" title="The interior of the Sci-Fi Dine-In"><img alt="The interior of the Sci-Fi Dine-In" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Sci-Fi_Dine-In_Theater_interior_by_hyku.jpg/133px-Sci-Fi_Dine-In_Theater_interior_by_hyku.jpg" width="133" height="88" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Sci-Fi_Dine-In_Theater_interior_by_hyku.jpg/200px-Sci-Fi_Dine-In_Theater_interior_by_hyku.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Sci-Fi_Dine-In_Theater_interior_by_hyku.jpg/266px-Sci-Fi_Dine-In_Theater_interior_by_hyku.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4288" data-file-height="2848" /></a></div>
<p>The <b><a href="/wiki/Sci-Fi_Dine-In_Theater_Restaurant" title="Sci-Fi Dine-In Theater Restaurant">Sci-Fi Dine-In Theater Restaurant</a></b> is a <a href="/wiki/Theme_restaurant" title="Theme restaurant">theme restaurant</a> at <a href="/wiki/Disney%27s_Hollywood_Studios" title="Disney's Hollywood Studios">Disney's Hollywood Studios</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Amusement_park" title="Amusement park">theme park</a> at <a href="/wiki/Walt_Disney_World" title="Walt Disney World">Walt Disney World</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bay_Lake,_Florida" title="Bay Lake, Florida">Bay Lake, Florida</a>. The restaurant is modeled after a 1950s <a href="/wiki/Drive-in_theater" title="Drive-in theater">drive-in theater</a>. <a href="/wiki/Walt_Disney_Imagineering" title="Walt Disney Imagineering">Walt Disney Imagineering</a> designed the booths to resemble period <a href="/wiki/Convertible" title="Convertible">convertibles</a>. While eating, guests watch a large <a href="/wiki/Projection_screen" title="Projection screen">projection screen</a> displaying film clips from such films as <i><a href="/wiki/Frankenstein_Meets_the_Space_Monster" title="Frankenstein Meets the Space Monster">Frankenstein Meets the Space Monster</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Plan_9_from_Outer_Space" title="Plan 9 from Outer Space">Plan 9 from Outer Space</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Attack_of_the_50_Foot_Woman" title="Attack of the 50 Foot Woman">Attack of the 50 Foot Woman</a></i>. In 1991, the Sci-Fi Dine-In opened along with nineteen other new Walt Disney World attractions marking the complex's twentieth anniversary. By the following year, it had become the park's most popular restaurant. <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thai</a> movie theater operator <a href="/wiki/Major_Cineplex#EGV" title="Major Cineplex">EGV Entertainment</a> opened the EGV Drive-in Cafe in <a href="/wiki/Bangkok" title="Bangkok">Bangkok</a> in 2003, explicitly emulating the Sci-Fi Dine-In. <i><a href="/wiki/USA_Today" title="USA Today">USA Today</a></i>‍ '​s list of the best restaurants in American amusement parks ranks the Sci-Fi Dine-In fifteenth, but many reviewers rate it more highly for its atmosphere than for its cuisine. (<a href="/wiki/Sci-Fi_Dine-In_Theater_Restaurant" title="Sci-Fi Dine-In Theater Restaurant"><b>Full&#160;article...</b></a>)</p>

<dt><b>October 22</b></dt>

<div id="mp-tfa-img" style="float: left; margin: 0.5em 0.9em 0.4em 0em;"><a href="/wiki/File:Common_Blackbird.jpg" class="image" title="Turdus merula - Common blackbird with a grub"><img alt="Turdus merula - Common blackbird with a grub" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Common_Blackbird.jpg/100px-Common_Blackbird.jpg" width="100" height="67" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Common_Blackbird.jpg/150px-Common_Blackbird.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Common_Blackbird.jpg/200px-Common_Blackbird.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3105" data-file-height="2070" /></a></div>
<p>The <b><a href="/wiki/Common_blackbird" title="Common blackbird">common blackbird</a></b> (<i>Turdus merula</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> of <a href="/wiki/True_thrush" title="True thrush">true thrush</a>. It breeds in Europe, Asia, and North Africa, and has been <a href="/wiki/Introduced_species" title="Introduced species">introduced</a> to <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Uruguay" title="Uruguay">Uruguay</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Falkland_Islands" title="Falkland Islands">Falkland Islands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>. It has several <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> across its large range; a few of the Asian subspecies are sometimes considered to be full species. Depending on <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitude</a>, the common blackbird may be <a href="/wiki/Resident_bird" title="Resident bird" class="mw-redirect">resident</a>, partially <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, or fully migratory. The male of the <a href="/wiki/Subspecies#Nomenclature" title="Subspecies">nominate subspecies</a>, which is found throughout most of Europe, is all black except for a yellow eye-ring and <a href="/wiki/Beak" title="Beak">bill</a> and has a rich, melodious <a href="/wiki/Bird_vocalization" title="Bird vocalization">song</a>; the adult female and juvenile have mainly dark brown <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a>. The species breeds in woods and gardens, building a neat, mud-lined, cup-shaped nest. It is <a href="/wiki/Omnivorous" title="Omnivorous" class="mw-redirect">omnivorous</a>, eating a wide range of <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>, <a href="/wiki/Earthworm" title="Earthworm">earthworms</a>, <a href="/wiki/Berry" title="Berry">berries</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Fruit" title="Fruit">fruits</a>. This common and conspicuous bird has given rise to many literary and cultural references, frequently related to its song. (<a href="/wiki/Common_blackbird" title="Common blackbird"><b>Full&#160;article...</b></a>)</p>

<dt><b>October 23</b></dt>

<div id="mp-tfa-img" style="float: left; margin: 0.5em 0.9em 0.4em 0em;"><a href="/wiki/File:Quileute_Net_Fishing.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Quileute Net Fishing.jpg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Quileute_Net_Fishing.jpg/133px-Quileute_Net_Fishing.jpg" width="133" height="88" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Quileute_Net_Fishing.jpg/200px-Quileute_Net_Fishing.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Quileute_Net_Fishing.jpg/266px-Quileute_Net_Fishing.jpg 2x" data-file-width="643" data-file-height="426" /></a></div>
<p><i><b><a href="/wiki/United_States_v._Washington" title="United States v. Washington">United States v. Washington</a></b></i> was a 1974 case heard in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_District_Court_for_the_Western_District_of_Washington" title="United States District Court for the Western District of Washington">U.S. District Court for the Western District of Washington</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Court_of_Appeals_for_the_Ninth_Circuit" title="United States Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit">U.S. Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit</a>. It reaffirmed the reserved right of <a href="/wiki/Native_Americans_in_the_United_States" title="Native Americans in the United States">American Indian</a> tribes in the <a href="/wiki/Washington_(U.S._state)" title="Washington (U.S. state)" class="mw-redirect">State of Washington</a> to act alongside the state as co-managers of <a href="/wiki/Salmon" title="Salmon">salmon</a> and other fish, and to continue harvesting them in accordance with the various treaties that the United States had signed with the tribes. The tribes of Washington had ceded their land to the United States but had reserved the right to fish as they had always done <i>(pictured)</i>, including fishing at their traditional locations that were off the designated <a href="/wiki/Indian_reservation" title="Indian reservation">reservations</a>. After a long trial, the decision of federal judge <a href="/wiki/George_Hugo_Boldt" title="George Hugo Boldt">George Hugo Boldt</a> held that the tribes were entitled to half the fish harvest each year. In 1975 the Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals upheld Judge Boldt's ruling and the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Supreme_Court" title="U.S. Supreme Court" class="mw-redirect">U.S. Supreme Court</a> declined to hear the case. After the state refused to enforce the court order, Boldt ordered the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Coast_Guard" title="United States Coast Guard">Coast Guard</a> to enforce his rulings. In a later case, Justice <a href="/wiki/John_Paul_Stevens" title="John Paul Stevens">John Paul Stevens</a> summarized: "Both sides have a right, secured by treaty, to take a fair share of the available fish." (<a href="/wiki/United_States_v._Washington" title="United States v. Washington"><b>Full&#160;article...</b></a>)</p>

<dt><b>October 24</b></dt>

<p><b><a href="/wiki/Debora_Green" title="Debora Green">Debora Green</a></b> (b.&#160;1951) is an American <a href="/wiki/Physician" title="Physician">physician</a> who pleaded <a href="/wiki/No_contest" title="No contest" class="mw-redirect">no contest</a> to setting a 1995 fire that <a href="/wiki/Arson" title="Arson">burned down</a> her family's home and killed two of her children, and to trying to kill her husband, Michael Farrar, with poison. Their marriage had been tumultuous, and Farrar filed for divorce in July 1995. He soon fell violently ill, but his doctors could not pinpoint the source of his illness. Green began to drink heavily, even while supervising her children. In October the family home caught fire, and two of her children died in the blaze. Investigation showed that trails of <a href="/wiki/Fire_accelerant" title="Fire accelerant">accelerant</a> in the house led back to Green's bedroom, and that she had been putting <a href="/wiki/Ricin" title="Ricin">ricin</a> in Farrar's food. The trial was sensational, and covered heavily by news media, especially in the <a href="/wiki/Kansas" title="Kansas">Kansas</a>–<a href="/wiki/Missouri" title="Missouri">Missouri</a> area, where the crimes occurred. She was sentenced to forty years in prison. Crime writer <a href="/wiki/Ann_Rule" title="Ann Rule">Ann Rule</a> wrote about the case in her book <i>Bitter Harvest: A Woman's Fury, a Mother's Sacrifice</i>. Green has petitioned for a new trial twice in recent years, without success. (<a href="/wiki/Debora_Green" title="Debora Green"><b>Full&#160;article...</b></a>)</p>

<dt><b>October 25</b></dt>
</dl>
<div id="mp-tfa-img" style="float: left; margin: 0.5em 0.9em 0.4em 0em;"><a href="/wiki/File:1851-O_3CS_(obv).jpg" class="image" title="Three-cent silver, Type 1 obverse"><img alt="Three-cent silver, Type 1 obverse" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/1851-O_3CS_%28obv%29.jpg/100px-1851-O_3CS_%28obv%29.jpg" width="100" height="101" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/1851-O_3CS_%28obv%29.jpg/150px-1851-O_3CS_%28obv%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/1851-O_3CS_%28obv%29.jpg/200px-1851-O_3CS_%28obv%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="478" data-file-height="481" /></a></div>
<p>The <b><a href="/wiki/Three-cent_silver" title="Three-cent silver">three-cent silver</a></b> was struck by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Mint" title="United States Mint">Mint of the United States</a> for circulation from 1851 to 1872, and as a <a href="/wiki/Proof_coin" title="Proof coin" class="mw-redirect">proof coin</a> for collectors in 1873. A reduction of postage rates to three cents prompted Congress in 1851 to authorize the coin. At the time, profiteers were exporting and melting U.S. silver coins for their metal to trade for increasing amounts of gold in the wake of the <a href="/wiki/California_Gold_Rush" title="California Gold Rush">California Gold Rush</a>. The three-cent silver thwarted this scheme, as the first American coin with metal valued significantly less than its face value, and the first silver coin not usable as <a href="/wiki/Legal_tender" title="Legal tender">legal tender</a> in unlimited amounts. Designed by the Mint's Chief Engraver, <a href="/wiki/James_B._Longacre" title="James B. Longacre">James B. Longacre</a>, the coin saw heavy use until Congress protected other silver coins from profiteers in 1853 by reducing their silver content. The coin's place in commerce was lost with the economic chaos of the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">Civil War</a>, which led to hoarding of all gold and silver coins. After the <a href="/wiki/Three-cent_nickel" title="Three-cent nickel">three-cent piece in copper-nickel</a> emerged in 1865, the three-cent silver had a string of low mintages until its abolition by the <a href="/wiki/Coinage_Act_of_1873" title="Coinage Act of 1873">Coinage Act of 1873</a>. The series is not widely collected, and the pieces remain inexpensive relative to U.S. coins of similar scarcity. (<a href="/wiki/Three-cent_silver" title="Three-cent silver"><b>Full&#160;article...</b></a>)</p>

<dt><b>October 26</b></dt>

<div id="mp-tfa-img" style="float: left; margin: 0.5em 0.9em 0.4em 0em;"><a href="/wiki/File:Arthur_Lewis_Watkins_Sifton.jpg" class="image" title="Arthur Sifton"><img alt="Arthur Sifton" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Arthur_Lewis_Watkins_Sifton.jpg/100px-Arthur_Lewis_Watkins_Sifton.jpg" width="100" height="133" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Arthur_Lewis_Watkins_Sifton.jpg/150px-Arthur_Lewis_Watkins_Sifton.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Arthur_Lewis_Watkins_Sifton.jpg/200px-Arthur_Lewis_Watkins_Sifton.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3432" data-file-height="4557" /></a></div>
<p><b><a href="/wiki/Arthur_Sifton" title="Arthur Sifton">Arthur Sifton</a></b> (1858–1921) was the <a href="/wiki/List_of_premiers_of_Alberta" title="List of premiers of Alberta">second</a> <a href="/wiki/Premier_of_Alberta" title="Premier of Alberta">Premier of Alberta</a> from 1910 to 1917 and a minister in the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Canada" title="Government of Canada">Government of Canada</a> thereafter. When Alberta was created out of a portion of the Northwest Territories in 1905, Sifton became its first chief justice. In 1910 the government of Alberta Premier <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Cameron_Rutherford" title="Alexander Cameron Rutherford">Alexander Cameron Rutherford</a> was embroiled in the <a href="/wiki/Alberta_and_Great_Waterways_Railway_scandal" title="Alberta and Great Waterways Railway scandal">Alberta and Great Waterways Railway scandal</a>; Rutherford resigned, and the position was offered to Sifton. As premier, he failed to gain provincial control over natural resources, but succeeded in implementing some <a href="/wiki/Direct_democracy" title="Direct democracy">direct democracy</a> measures, leading to <a href="/wiki/Prohibition_in_Canada" title="Prohibition in Canada">prohibition</a> and the extension of the vote to women. During the <a href="/wiki/Conscription_crisis_of_1917" title="Conscription crisis of 1917" class="mw-redirect">conscription crisis of 1917</a>, Sifton supported the <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_of_Canada_(historical)" title="Conservative Party of Canada (historical)" class="mw-redirect">Conservative</a> Prime Minister, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Borden" title="Robert Borden">Robert Borden</a>, in his attempt to impose <a href="/wiki/Conscription" title="Conscription">conscription</a> to help win the <a href="/wiki/First_World_War" title="First World War" class="mw-redirect">First World War</a>. He backed the creation of a <a href="/wiki/Unionist_Party_(Canada)" title="Unionist Party (Canada)">Union</a> government composed of Conservatives and pro-conscription Liberals. In 1917 he left provincial politics and became a minister in this government. Over the next three and a half years he served briefly in four ministries and was a delegate to the <a href="/wiki/Paris_Peace_Conference,_1919" title="Paris Peace Conference, 1919">Paris Peace Conference of 1919</a>. (<a href="/wiki/Arthur_Sifton" title="Arthur Sifton"><b>Full&#160;article...</b></a>)</p>

<dt><b>October 27</b></dt>
</dl>
<div id="mp-tfa-img" style="float: left; margin: 0.5em 0.9em 0.4em 0em;"><a href="/wiki/File:Oerip_Soemohardjo_1993_Indonesia_stamp_crop.jpg" class="image" title="Oerip on a 1993 stamp"><img alt="Oerip on a 1993 stamp" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Oerip_Soemohardjo_1993_Indonesia_stamp_crop.jpg/100px-Oerip_Soemohardjo_1993_Indonesia_stamp_crop.jpg" width="100" height="117" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Oerip_Soemohardjo_1993_Indonesia_stamp_crop.jpg/150px-Oerip_Soemohardjo_1993_Indonesia_stamp_crop.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Oerip_Soemohardjo_1993_Indonesia_stamp_crop.jpg/200px-Oerip_Soemohardjo_1993_Indonesia_stamp_crop.jpg 2x" data-file-width="353" data-file-height="414" /></a></div>
<p><b><a href="/wiki/Oerip_Soemohardjo" title="Oerip Soemohardjo">Oerip Soemohardjo</a></b> (1893–1948) was an Indonesian general and the first chief of staff of the <a href="/wiki/Indonesian_National_Armed_Forces" title="Indonesian National Armed Forces">Indonesian National Armed Forces</a>. He trained in <a href="/wiki/Jakarta" title="Jakarta">Batavia</a> (modern-day Jakarta) to become a lieutenant in the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Netherlands_East_Indies_Army" title="Royal Netherlands East Indies Army">Royal Netherlands East Indies Army</a>, and after almost 25 years of service, was the highest-ranking <a href="/wiki/Native_Indonesian" title="Native Indonesian" class="mw-redirect">Native Indonesian</a> officer in the country. He resigned in 1938, but was recalled to active duty after <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a> <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Netherlands" title="Battle of the Netherlands">invaded the Netherlands</a> in May 1940. When the <a href="/wiki/Empire_of_Japan" title="Empire of Japan">Empire of Japan</a> <a href="/wiki/Japanese_occupation_of_Indonesia" title="Japanese occupation of Indonesia" class="mw-redirect">occupied Indonesia</a> less than two years later, he was detained in a prisoner-of-war camp for three and a half months. <a href="/wiki/Proclamation_of_Indonesian_Independence" title="Proclamation of Indonesian Independence">Indonesia proclaimed its independence</a> from the Netherlands in 1945; several months later, he was declared the chief of staff and interim leader of the newly formed army. Working to build a united force from the fractured former military groups in the country, he oversaw army development during the <a href="/wiki/Indonesian_National_Revolution" title="Indonesian National Revolution">Indonesian National Revolution</a> along with General <a href="/wiki/Sudirman" title="Sudirman">Sudirman</a>, the leader of the armed forces. He has received several awards from the Indonesian government, including the title <a href="/wiki/National_Hero_of_Indonesia" title="National Hero of Indonesia">National Hero of Indonesia</a> in 1964. (<a href="/wiki/Oerip_Soemohardjo" title="Oerip Soemohardjo"><b>Full&#160;article...</b></a>)</p>

<dt><b>October 28</b></dt>

<div id="mp-tfa-img" style="float: left; margin: 0.5em 0.9em 0.4em 0em;"><a href="/wiki/File:Mission_Concepcion_San_Antonio.JPG" class="image"><img alt="Mission Concepcion San Antonio.JPG" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Mission_Concepcion_San_Antonio.JPG/133px-Mission_Concepcion_San_Antonio.JPG" width="133" height="100" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Mission_Concepcion_San_Antonio.JPG/200px-Mission_Concepcion_San_Antonio.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Mission_Concepcion_San_Antonio.JPG/266px-Mission_Concepcion_San_Antonio.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2592" data-file-height="1944" /></a></div>
<p>The <b><a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Concepci%C3%B3n" title="Battle of Concepción">Battle of Concepción</a></b> was fought on October&#160;28, 1835, between <a href="/wiki/Centralist_Republic_of_Mexico" title="Centralist Republic of Mexico">Mexican</a> troops and <a href="/wiki/Texian" title="Texian" class="mw-redirect">Texian</a> insurgents on the grounds of <a href="/wiki/Mission_Concepci%C3%B3n" title="Mission Concepción" class="mw-redirect">Mission Concepción</a> <i>(pictured in 2010)</i>, 2 miles (3.2&#160;km) south of what is now <a href="/wiki/Downtown_San_Antonio" title="Downtown San Antonio">Downtown</a> <a href="/wiki/San_Antonio" title="San Antonio">San Antonio</a> in the U.S. state of Texas. The day before, <a href="/wiki/Stephen_F._Austin" title="Stephen F. Austin">Stephen F. Austin</a>, commander of the newly created <a href="/wiki/Texian_Army" title="Texian Army">Texian Army</a>, had sent <a href="/wiki/James_Bowie" title="James Bowie">James Bowie</a>, <a href="/wiki/James_Fannin" title="James Fannin">James Fannin</a>, and 90&#160;soldiers to find a defensible spot for the army to rest. After choosing a site near Mission Concepción, the scouting party camped for the night and sent a courier to notify Austin. Upon learning that the army was divided, General <a href="/wiki/Mart%C3%ADn_Perfecto_de_Cos" title="Martín Perfecto de Cos">Martín Perfecto de Cos</a> sent Colonel <a href="/wiki/Domingo_Ugartechea" title="Domingo Ugartechea">Domingo Ugartechea</a> with 275 soldiers to attack the scouting party. The Texians took cover in a horseshoe-shaped gully; their good defensive position, longer firing range, and better ammunition helped them to repel several attacks, and the Mexican soldiers retreated just 30 minutes before the remainder of the Texian Army arrived. Historians estimate that between 14 and 76 Mexican soldiers were killed, while only one Texian soldier died. (<a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Concepci%C3%B3n" title="Battle of Concepción"><b>Full&#160;article...</b></a>)</p>

<dt><b>October 29</b></dt>
</dl>
<div id="mp-tfa-img" style="float: left; margin: 0.5em 0.9em 0.4em 0em;"><a href="/wiki/File:Telescopium_IAU.svg" class="image"><img alt="Telescopium IAU.svg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Telescopium_IAU.svg/133px-Telescopium_IAU.svg.png" width="133" height="126" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Telescopium_IAU.svg/200px-Telescopium_IAU.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Telescopium_IAU.svg/266px-Telescopium_IAU.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="604" data-file-height="570" /></a></div>
<p><b><a href="/wiki/Telescopium" title="Telescopium">Telescopium</a></b> is a minor <a href="/wiki/Constellation" title="Constellation">constellation</a> in the southern <a href="/wiki/Celestial_sphere" title="Celestial sphere">celestial hemisphere</a>, one of twelve created in the 18th century by <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a> <a href="/wiki/Astronomer" title="Astronomer">astronomer</a> <a href="/wiki/Nicolas_Louis_de_Lacaille" title="Nicolas Louis de Lacaille" class="mw-redirect">Nicolas Louis de Lacaille</a> and one of several depicting scientific instruments. Its name is a <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latinized</a> form of the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Greek</a> word for <a href="/wiki/Telescope" title="Telescope">telescope</a>. Telescopium was later much reduced in size by <a href="/wiki/Francis_Baily" title="Francis Baily">Francis Baily</a> and <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Apthorp_Gould" title="Benjamin Apthorp Gould">Benjamin Gould</a>. The brightest star in the constellation is <a href="/wiki/Alpha_Telescopii" title="Alpha Telescopii">Alpha Telescopii</a>, a blue-white <a href="/wiki/Subgiant" title="Subgiant">subgiant</a> with an <a href="/wiki/Apparent_magnitude" title="Apparent magnitude">apparent magnitude</a> of 3.5, followed by the orange giant star <a href="/wiki/Zeta_Telescopii" title="Zeta Telescopii">Zeta Telescopii</a> at magnitude 4.1. <a href="/wiki/Eta_Telescopii" title="Eta Telescopii">Eta</a> and <a href="/wiki/PZ_Telescopii" title="PZ Telescopii">PZ Telescopii</a> are two young star systems with <a href="/wiki/Debris_disk" title="Debris disk">debris disks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brown_dwarf" title="Brown dwarf">brown dwarf</a> companions. Telescopium hosts two unusual stars with very little hydrogen that are likely to be the result of merged <a href="/wiki/White_dwarf" title="White dwarf">white dwarfs</a>: <a href="/wiki/HD_168476" title="HD 168476" class="mw-redirect">HD 168476</a>, also known as PV Telescopii, is a hot blue <a href="/wiki/Extreme_helium_star" title="Extreme helium star">extreme helium star</a>, while <a href="/wiki/RS_Telescopii" title="RS Telescopii">RS Telescopii</a> is an <a href="/wiki/R_Coronae_Borealis_variable" title="R Coronae Borealis variable">R Coronae Borealis variable</a>. <a href="/wiki/RR_Telescopii" title="RR Telescopii">RR Telescopii</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Cataclysmic_variable" title="Cataclysmic variable" class="mw-redirect">cataclysmic variable</a> that brightened as a <a href="/wiki/Nova" title="Nova">nova</a> to magnitude 6 in 1948. (<a href="/wiki/Telescopium" title="Telescopium"><b>Full&#160;article...</b></a>)</p>

<dt><b>October 30</b></dt>

<div id="mp-tfa-img" style="float: left; margin: 0.5em 0.9em 0.4em 0em;"><a href="/wiki/File:Freo_prison_WMAU_gnangarra-131.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Freo prison WMAU gnangarra-131.jpg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Freo_prison_WMAU_gnangarra-131.jpg/133px-Freo_prison_WMAU_gnangarra-131.jpg" width="133" height="88" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Freo_prison_WMAU_gnangarra-131.jpg/200px-Freo_prison_WMAU_gnangarra-131.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Freo_prison_WMAU_gnangarra-131.jpg/266px-Freo_prison_WMAU_gnangarra-131.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4928" data-file-height="3264" /></a></div>
<p><b><a href="/wiki/Fremantle_Prison" title="Fremantle Prison">Fremantle Prison</a></b> was built in <a href="/wiki/Fremantle" title="Fremantle">Fremantle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Western_Australia" title="Western Australia">Western Australia</a>, between 1851 and 1859 using <a href="/wiki/Convict_labour" title="Convict labour" class="mw-redirect">convict labour</a>. <a href="/wiki/Royal_Commission" title="Royal Commission">Royal Commissions</a> in 1898 and 1911 instigated some prison reforms, but after World War II, significant reforms lagged behind those occurring elsewhere in Australia and the world. Improvements in the late 1960s and early 1970s included an officer training school, social workers, welfare officers, and work release and community service programs. Punishments varied over the years, with <a href="/wiki/Flogging" title="Flogging" class="mw-redirect">flogging</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leg_irons" title="Leg irons" class="mw-redirect">leg irons</a> eventually replaced by lengthening of sentences and restriction from visitors or entertainment. More than 40 hangings were carried out at Fremantle Prison, which was Western Australia's only lawful place of execution between 1888 and 1984. There was a major riot in 1968; during <a href="/wiki/Fremantle_Prison_riot" title="Fremantle Prison riot">another in 1988</a>, guards were taken hostage, and fire damage totalled $1.8 million (in 1988 Australian dollars). The prison closed in 1991, replaced by the new maximum-security <a href="/wiki/Casuarina_Prison" title="Casuarina Prison">Casuarina Prison</a>. Since then, Fremantle Prison has become a tourist attraction and <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a>. (<a href="/wiki/Fremantle_Prison" title="Fremantle Prison"><b>Full&#160;article...</b></a>)</p>

<div id="mp-tfa-img" style="float: left; margin: 0.5em 0.9em 0.4em 0em;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bake_these_(pumpkins_in_Toronto).jpg" class="image"><img alt="Bake these (pumpkins in Toronto).jpg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Bake_these_%28pumpkins_in_Toronto%29.jpg/133px-Bake_these_%28pumpkins_in_Toronto%29.jpg" width="133" height="89" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Bake_these_%28pumpkins_in_Toronto%29.jpg/200px-Bake_these_%28pumpkins_in_Toronto%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Bake_these_%28pumpkins_in_Toronto%29.jpg/266px-Bake_these_%28pumpkins_in_Toronto%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3888" data-file-height="2592" /></a></div>
<p><i><b><a href="/wiki/Cucurbita" title="Cucurbita">Cucurbita</a></b></i> (Latin for <a href="/wiki/Gourd" title="Gourd">gourd</a>) is a <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> of <a href="/wiki/Vine" title="Vine">vines</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Cucurbitaceae" title="Cucurbitaceae">gourd family</a>, native to the <a href="/wiki/Andes" title="Andes">Andes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mesoamerica" title="Mesoamerica">Mesoamerica</a>. Five species are grown worldwide, variously known as squash, <a href="/wiki/Pumpkin" title="Pumpkin">pumpkin</a>, or gourd depending on species, <a href="/wiki/Variety_(botany)" title="Variety (botany)">variety</a>, and local parlance. The fruits have played a role in human culture for at least 2,000 years. First cultivated in the Americas before being brought to Europe by returning explorers, the plants remain an important food source. Most <i>Cucurbita</i> species are <a href="/wiki/Herbaceous_plant" title="Herbaceous plant">herbaceous</a> vines that grow several meters in length and have <a href="/wiki/Tendril" title="Tendril">tendrils</a>, but bush <a href="/wiki/Cultivar" title="Cultivar">cultivars</a> of <a href="/wiki/Cucurbita_pepo" title="Cucurbita pepo"><i>C.&#160;pepo</i></a> and <a href="/wiki/Cucurbita_maxima" title="Cucurbita maxima"><i>C.&#160;maxima</i></a> have also been developed. Many North and Central American species are visited by <a href="/wiki/Honey_bee" title="Honey bee">honey bees</a>, as well as specialist bees that pollinate only a single species. Most of the domesticated species can be considered <a href="/wiki/Winter_squash" title="Winter squash">winter squash</a>, since the full-grown fruits can be stored for months. Their extracts have many uses, including in <a href="/wiki/Cosmetics" title="Cosmetics">cosmetics</a> for dry and sensitive skin. The fruits are also good sources of vitamins and minerals in foods such as <a href="/wiki/Pumpkin_pie" title="Pumpkin pie">pumpkin pie</a>, biscuits, bread, desserts, puddings, beverages, and soups. (<a href="/wiki/Cucurbita" title="Cucurbita"><b>Full&#160;article...</b></a>)</p>
