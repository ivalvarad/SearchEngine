			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Arranged marriage</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
				</div>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;"><a href="/wiki/File:V.V.Pukirev_-_The_Arranged_Marriage.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/V.V.Pukirev_-_The_Arranged_Marriage.jpg/260px-V.V.Pukirev_-_The_Arranged_Marriage.jpg" width="260" height="335" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/V.V.Pukirev_-_The_Arranged_Marriage.jpg/390px-V.V.Pukirev_-_The_Arranged_Marriage.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/V.V.Pukirev_-_The_Arranged_Marriage.jpg/520px-V.V.Pukirev_-_The_Arranged_Marriage.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1011" data-file-height="1304" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:V.V.Pukirev_-_The_Arranged_Marriage.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Unequal marriage, a 19th-century painting by Russian artist <a href="/wiki/Pukirev" title="Pukirev" class="mw-redirect">Pukirev</a>. It depicts an arranged marriage where a young girl is forced to marry someone she doesn't want to.</div>

<p><b>Arranged marriage</b> is a type of marital union where the bride and groom are selected by a third party rather than by each other.<sup id="cite_ref-jo2008_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jo2008-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> It was common worldwide until the 18th century.<sup id="cite_ref-jo2008_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jo2008-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> In more recent times, arranged marriage is common in <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Voluntarism_and_Marriage_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Voluntarism_and_Marriage-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Somervill.2C_Barbara_2007_page_41-43_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Somervill.2C_Barbara_2007_page_41-43-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Latin_America" title="Latin America">Latin America</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Voluntarism_and_Marriage_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Voluntarism_and_Marriage-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> and parts of <a href="/wiki/East_Asia" title="East Asia">East Asia</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> elsewhere in developed countries, arranged marriage has continued in some <a href="/wiki/Monarchy" title="Monarchy">royal</a> families,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> parts of <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> among immigrant and minority ethnic groups.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> Other groups that practice this custom include the <a href="/wiki/Unification_Church" title="Unification Church">Unification Church</a>.</p>
<p>Arranged marriage should not be confused with the practice of <a href="/wiki/Forced_marriage" title="Forced marriage">forced marriage</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Vani_(custom)" title="Vani (custom)"><i>vani</i></a>. In an arranged marriage, while the meeting of the spouses is arranged by family members, relatives or friends, the spouses agree of their own free will to marry. By contrast, in a forced marriage, one or both spouses are coerced into the marriage - the union takes place without their freely given consent (under duress, threats, psychological pressure etc.).<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Arranged marriage differs from <i>autonomous marriage</i> - called <i>love marriage</i> in some parts of the world - where the individuals find and select their own spouses; arranged marriages, in contrast, are usually set up by the parents or an older family member. In some cases, arranged marriage involves a <a href="/wiki/Matchmaking" title="Matchmaking">matchmaker</a> such as priest or religious leader, <a href="/wiki/Marriage_websites" title="Marriage websites" class="mw-redirect">matrimonial site</a>, mutual friends or a trusted third party.</p>
<p>Arranged marriages vary in nature and in how much time passes between first introduction and <a href="/wiki/Engagement" title="Engagement">engagement</a>. In an "introduction only" arranged marriage, also known as quasi-arranged<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> marriages or assisted<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> marriages, the parents or guardians introduce a potential spouse. From that point on, it is up to the two individuals to develop the relationship and make a final choice. There is no set time period. This is increasingly common in Japan, parts of Latin America and Africa, South Asia and East Asia.</p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arranged_marriage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Arranged marriages were very common throughout the world until the 18th century.<sup id="cite_ref-jo2008_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jo2008-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> Typically, marriages everywhere were arranged by parents, grandparents or other relatives. Some historical exceptions are known, such as courtship and betrothal rituals during Renaissance period of Italy<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Gandharva_marriage" title="Gandharva marriage">Gandharva marriages</a> in Vedic period of India.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In China, arranged marriages (baoban hunyin, 包辦婚姻) - sometimes called blind marriages (manghun, 盲婚) - were the norm before the mid 20th century. A marriage was a negotiation and decision between parents and other older members of two families. The boy and girl, were typically told to get married, without a right to consent, even if they had never met each other until the wedding day.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Arranged marriages were the norm in Russia before the early 20th century, most of which were <a href="/wiki/Endogamous" title="Endogamous" class="mw-redirect">endogamous</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Until the first half of the 20th century, arranged marriages were common in migrant families in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-rs_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rs-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> They were sometimes called <i>picture-bride marriages</i> among Japanese American immigrants because the bride and groom knew each other only through the exchange of photographs before the day of their marriage. These marriages among immigrants were typically arranged by parents, or relatives from the country of their origin. As immigrants settled in and melted into a new culture, arranged marriages shifted first to quasi-arranged marriages where parents or friends made introductions and the couple met before the marriage; over time, the marriages among the descendants of these immigrants shifted to autonomous marriages driven by individual's choice, dating and courtship preferences, along with an increase in interracial marriages.<sup id="cite_ref-rs_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rs-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> Similar historical dynamics are claimed in other parts of the world.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Arranged marriages have declined in prosperous countries with social mobility, ascendancy of individualism and the nuclear family; nevertheless, arranged marriages are still seen in countries of Europe and North America, among royal families, aristocrats and minority religious groups such as in <a href="/wiki/Placement_marriage" title="Placement marriage">placement marriage</a> among Fundamentalist Mormon groups of the United States. In most other parts of the world, arranged marriages continue to varying degrees and increasingly in quasi-arranged form, along with autonomous marriages.<sup id="cite_ref-jo2008_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jo2008-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Comparison">Comparison</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arranged_marriage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Comparison">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Ambitious_Mother_and_the_Obliging_Clergyman.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/The_Ambitious_Mother_and_the_Obliging_Clergyman.jpg/250px-The_Ambitious_Mother_and_the_Obliging_Clergyman.jpg" width="250" height="174" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/The_Ambitious_Mother_and_the_Obliging_Clergyman.jpg/375px-The_Ambitious_Mother_and_the_Obliging_Clergyman.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/The_Ambitious_Mother_and_the_Obliging_Clergyman.jpg/500px-The_Ambitious_Mother_and_the_Obliging_Clergyman.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="445" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Ambitious_Mother_and_the_Obliging_Clergyman.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The Ambitious Mother and the Obliging Clergyman - a cartoon by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Dana_Gibson" title="Charles Dana Gibson">Charles Dana Gibson</a> caricaturing arranged marriages in early 20th century United States. A parent insists their daughter marry a man on grounds of wealth or aristocratic title, without considering the girl's wishes. The clergyman is caricatured officiating the marriage with a blindfold.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Marriages have been categorized into four groups in scholarly studies:<sup id="cite_ref-jo2008_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jo2008-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>parents or guardians select, the individuals are neither consulted nor have any say before the marriage (forced arranged marriage)</li>
<li>parents or guardians select, then the individuals are consulted, who consider and consent, and each individual has the power to refuse; sometimes, the individuals meet - in family setting or privately - before engagement and marriage as in <i><a href="/wiki/Shidduch" title="Shidduch">shidduch</a></i> custom among Orthodox Jews</li>
<li>individuals select, then parents or guardians are consulted, who consider and consent, and parents have the power to refuse</li>
<li>individuals select, the parents or guardians are neither consulted nor have any say before the marriage (autonomous marriage)</li>
</ul>
<p>Gary Lee and Lorene Stone suggest that most adult marriages in recent modern history, are some gradation between extreme example of either ideal arranged or ideal autonomous marriage, in part because marriage is a social institution.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> Similarly, Broude and Greene, after studying 142 cultures worldwide, have reported that 130 cultures have elements of arranged marriage.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Extreme examples of forced arranged marriage have been observed in some societies, particularly in child marriages of girls below age 12. Illustrations include <a href="/wiki/Vani_(custom)" title="Vani (custom)"><i>vani</i></a> which is currently seen in some tribal / rural parts of Pakistan, and <a href="/wiki/Shim-pua_marriage" title="Shim-pua marriage">Shim-pua marriage</a> in Taiwan before the 1970s (Tongyangxi in China).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arranged_marriage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are many kinds of arranged marriages, some of these are:<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Arranged exogamous marriage: is one where a third party finds and selects the bride and groom irrespective of their social, economic and cultural group.</li>
<li>Arranged endogamous marriage: is one where a third party finds and selects the bride and groom from a particular social, economic and cultural group.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Consanguinity" title="Consanguinity">Consanguineous</a> marriage: is a type of arranged endogamous marriage.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup> It is one where the bride and groom share a grandparent or near ancestor. Examples of these include first cousin marriages, uncle-niece marriages, second cousin marriages, and so on. The most common consanguineous marriages are first cousin marriages, followed by second cousin and uncle-niece marriages. This type of arranged marriages are most common in Muslim communities of the world. Between 25 to 40% of all marriages in parts of Saudi Arabia and Pakistan are first cousin marriages; while overall consanguineous arranged marriages exceed 65 to 80% in various regions of the Middle East, North Africa and Islamic Central Asia.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>The bride and groom in all of the above types of arranged marriages, usually do have the right to consent; if the bride or the groom or both do not have a right to consent, it is called a forced marriage.</p>
<p>Non-consanguineous arranged marriage is one where the bride and groom do not share a grandparent or near ancestor. This type of arranged marriages is common in Hindu and Buddhist South Asia, Southeast Asia, East Asia and Christian Latin America and sub-Saharan Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Consanguineous marriages are against the law in many parts of United States and Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup> While consanguineous arranged marriages are common and culturally preferred in Islamic countries and migrants from Muslim countries to other parts of the world, they are culturally forbidden or considered undesirable in most Christian, Hindu and Buddhist societies.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup> Consanguineous arranged marriages were common in Jewish communities before the 20th century, but have declined to less than 10% in modern times.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Causes_and_prevalence_of_arranged_marriage">Causes and prevalence of arranged marriage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arranged_marriage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Causes and prevalence of arranged marriage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Over human history through modern times, the practice of arranged marriages have been encouraged by a combination of factors such as the practice of child marriage,<sup id="cite_ref-Child_Marriages_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Child_Marriages-41"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup> late marriage, tradition,<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup> culture, religion, poverty and limited choice, disabilities,<sup id="cite_ref-Ghai.2C_A._2001_pages_26-37_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ghai.2C_A._2001_pages_26-37-44"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup> wealth and inheritance issues, politics, social and ethnic conflicts.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Child_marriage">Child marriage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arranged_marriage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Child marriage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Marriage_A-la-Mode_1,_The_Marriage_Settlement_-_William_Hogarth.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Marriage_A-la-Mode_1%2C_The_Marriage_Settlement_-_William_Hogarth.jpg/250px-Marriage_A-la-Mode_1%2C_The_Marriage_Settlement_-_William_Hogarth.jpg" width="250" height="190" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Marriage_A-la-Mode_1%2C_The_Marriage_Settlement_-_William_Hogarth.jpg/375px-Marriage_A-la-Mode_1%2C_The_Marriage_Settlement_-_William_Hogarth.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Marriage_A-la-Mode_1%2C_The_Marriage_Settlement_-_William_Hogarth.jpg/500px-Marriage_A-la-Mode_1%2C_The_Marriage_Settlement_-_William_Hogarth.jpg 2x" data-file-width="5557" data-file-height="4225" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Marriage_A-la-Mode_1,_The_Marriage_Settlement_-_William_Hogarth.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
"<a href="/wiki/Marriage_%C3%A0-la-mode_(Hogarth)" title="Marriage à-la-mode (Hogarth)">Marriage à-la-mode</a>" by <a href="/wiki/William_Hogarth" title="William Hogarth">William Hogarth</a>: a satire on arranged marriages and prediction of ensuing disaster</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Child marriage, particularly those below the age of 12, does not prepare or provide the individual much opportunity to make an informed, free choice about matrimony. These child marriages are implicitly arranged marriages.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup> In rural areas of sub-Saharan Africa, South Asia and Latin America, poverty and lack of options such as being able to attend school leave little choice to children other than be in early arranged marriages.<sup id="cite_ref-Child_Marriages_41-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Child_Marriages-41"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>According to Warner, in nations with the highest rates of child marriages, the marriage of the girl is almost always arranged by her parents or guardians.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup> The nations with the highest rates of arranged child marriages are: Niger, Chad, Mali, Bangladesh, Guinea, Central African Republic, Afghanistan, Yemen, and Pakistan. Arranged child marriages are also observed in parts of the Americas.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Poverty">Poverty</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arranged_marriage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Poverty">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In impoverished communities, every adult mouth to feed becomes a continuing burden. Arranging a marriage of a daughter, scholars say,<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52"><span>[</span>52<span>]</span></a></sup> is a means to reduce this burden. Poverty, thus, is a driver of arranged marriage.</p>
<p>This theory,<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53"><span>[</span>53<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54"><span>[</span>54<span>]</span></a></sup> is supported by the observed rapid drop in arranged marriages in fast growing economies of Asia. The benefit parents received from the contributions from their earning single daughters has been cited<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55"><span>[</span>55<span>]</span></a></sup> as a reason for their growing reluctance to see their daughters marry at too early an age.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Late_marriage">Late marriage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arranged_marriage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Late marriage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Late marriage, particularly past the age of 30, reduces the pool of available bachelors for autonomous marriages. Introductions and arranged marriages become a productive option.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56"><span>[</span>56<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>For example, in part due to economic prosperity, about 40% of modern Japanese women reach the age of 29 and have never been married. To assist late marriages, the traditional custom of arranged marriages called <a href="/wiki/Miai" title="Miai">Miai-kekkon</a> is re-emerging. It involves the prospective bride and groom, family, friends and a matchmaker (nakōdo, 仲人); the pair is selected by a process with the individuals and family involved (iegara, 家柄); and typically the couple meet three times, in public or private, before deciding if they want to get engaged.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57"><span>[</span>57<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58"><span>[</span>58<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hendry_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hendry-59"><span>[</span>59<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Limited_choices">Limited choices</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arranged_marriage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Limited choices">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Migrant minority ethnic populations have limited choice of partners, particularly when they are stereotyped, segregated or avoided by the majority population. This encourages <a href="/wiki/Homogamy_(sociology)" title="Homogamy (sociology)">homogamy</a> and arranged marriages within the ethnic group. Examples of this dynamic include Sikh marriages between 1910 to 1980 in Canada,<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60"><span>[</span>60<span>]</span></a></sup> homogamous quasi-arranged marriages between European descent South Africans,<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61"><span>[</span>61<span>]</span></a></sup> arranged marriages among Hasidic Jews,<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62"><span>[</span>62<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63"><span>[</span>63<span>]</span></a></sup> and arranged marriages among Japanese American immigrants before the 1960s, who would travel back to Japan, to marry the spouse arranged by the family, and then return married. In other cases, a girl from Japan would arrive in the United States as a <i>picture bride</i>, pre-arranged to marry the Japanese American man on arrival, whom she had never met.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64"><span>[</span>64<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_disabilities">Physical disabilities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arranged_marriage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Physical disabilities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Certain physical disabilities increase the likelihood of arranged, even forced marriages in some parts of the world.<sup id="cite_ref-Ghai.2C_A._2001_pages_26-37_44-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ghai.2C_A._2001_pages_26-37-44"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65"><span>[</span>65<span>]</span></a></sup> Okonjo says that a physical disability in a bride, and even more so, a groom is one of the reasons for early arranged marriages in Nigeria.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66"><span>[</span>66<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tradition">Tradition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arranged_marriage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Tradition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many cultures traditionally seek endogamous marriages. A prominent example of this practice is the <a href="/wiki/Hindu" title="Hindu">Hindu</a> culture where the bride and groom belong to the same caste, but are non-consanguineous, that is the bride and groom are not blood relatives nor extended family members. Other examples of cultures following the endogamous arranged marriage tradition include <a href="/wiki/Amish" title="Amish">Amish</a> people in United States,<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67"><span>[</span>67<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68"><span>[</span>68<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Orthodox_Judaism" title="Orthodox Judaism">Orthodox</a> Jews in Canada, the United States, Israel, and Western Europe,<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69"><span>[</span>69<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70"><span>[</span>70<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Arab_Christians" title="Arab Christians">Arab Christians</a><sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71"><span>[</span>71<span>]</span></a></sup> such as <a href="/wiki/Coptic_Christians" title="Coptic Christians" class="mw-redirect">Coptic Christians</a> in Egypt.<sup id="cite_ref-Somervill.2C_Barbara_2007_page_41-43_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Somervill.2C_Barbara_2007_page_41-43-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Arranged marriage is also the tradition of many Islamic nations of West Asia and North Africa, but with the difference that between 17% to majority of all marriages in these countries are also consanguineous marriages.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72"><span>[</span>72<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73"><span>[</span>73<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74"><span>[</span>74<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75"><span>[</span>75<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Endogamous non-consanguineous marriages limit the number of potential partners available, particularly when population size for the religion or caste or group is small; a limited marriagable pool makes locating potential partners challenging, and encourages arranged or quasi-arranged marriages.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76"><span>[</span>76<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77"><span>[</span>77<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The practice of endogamous consanguineous marriage, dramatically limits the marriagable pool; it inherently encourages marriages arranged according to tradition and birth. Over 1.3 billion people, predominantly of Islamic faith practice endogamous consanguineous arranged marriages.<sup id="cite_ref-hamamy_78-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hamamy-78"><span>[</span>78<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79"><span>[</span>79<span>]</span></a></sup> Consanguineous arranged marriages are presently also observed, though to a much lesser extent, in some ethnic groups of Africa, India, Indonesia, Polynesia and South America.<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80"><span>[</span>80<span>]</span></a></sup> In Pakistan and Saudi Arabia, majority (65%+) of all marriages are endogamous and consanguineous arranged marriages. More than 40% of all marriages are endogamous and consanguineous in Iraq, Iran, Jordan, Syria, Yemen, Kuwait, UAE, Oman, Sudan, Libya and Mauritania; and over 1 in 5 marriages in Turkey, Egypt, Algeria, regions of Nigeria, India and Malaysia with high Muslim populations are endogamous and consanguineous arranged marriages.<sup id="cite_ref-hamamy_78-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hamamy-78"><span>[</span>78<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81"><span>[</span>81<span>]</span></a></sup> Among these Islamic populations, arranged marriages include endogamous and non-consanguineous marriages, and therefore exceed the above observed rates of endogamous and consanguineous marriages.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Custom">Custom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arranged_marriage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Custom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The consequence of some customs is arranged marriage. For example, in rural and tribal parts of Pakistan and Afghanistan, disputes, unpaid debts in default and crimes such as murder are settled by a council of village elders, called <i>jirga</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-nyt2012_82-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt2012-82"><span>[</span>82<span>]</span></a></sup> A typical punishment for a crime committed by males involves requiring the guilty family to marry their virgin girl between 5 to 12 year old to the other family. This custom requires no consent from the girl, or even her parents. Such arranged child marriages are called <a href="/wiki/Vani_(custom)" title="Vani (custom)">vani (custom)</a>, swara and sak in different regional languages of Pakistan.<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83"><span>[</span>83<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hk2004_84-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hk2004-84"><span>[</span>84<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85"><span>[</span>85<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Another custom in certain Islamic nations,<sup id="cite_ref-86" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-86"><span>[</span>86<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-87" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-87"><span>[</span>87<span>]</span></a></sup> such as Pakistan, is <a href="/wiki/Watta_satta" title="Watta satta">watta satta</a>, where brother-sister pair of one family are swapped as spouses of brother-sister pair of another family. In other words, the wife is also the sister-in-law for the males in two families. This custom inherently leads to arranged form of marriage. About 30% of all marriages in western rural regions of Pakistan are by custom <i>watta-satta</i> marriages, and 75% of these Muslim marriages are between cousins and other blood relatives.<sup id="cite_ref-wb2007_88-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wb2007-88"><span>[</span>88<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-89" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-89"><span>[</span>89<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-90" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-90"><span>[</span>90<span>]</span></a></sup> Some immigrant families prefer customary practice of arranged marriage.<sup id="cite_ref-91" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-91"><span>[</span>91<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Politics">Politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arranged_marriage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Arranged marriages across feudal lords, city states and kingdoms, as a means of establishing political alliances, trade and peace were common in human history.<sup id="cite_ref-hendry_59-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hendry-59"><span>[</span>59<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-92" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-92"><span>[</span>92<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-93" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-93"><span>[</span>93<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wealth_and_inheritance_issues">Wealth and inheritance issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arranged_marriage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Wealth and inheritance issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Throughout most of human history, marriage has been a social institution that produced children and organized inheritance of property from one generation to next. Various cultures, particularly some wealthy royals and aristocratic families, arranged marriages in part to conserve or streamline the inheritance of their wealth.<sup id="cite_ref-94" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-94"><span>[</span>94<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Tongyangxi, also known as <a href="/wiki/Shim-pua_marriage" title="Shim-pua marriage">Shim-pua marriage</a> in Taiwanese - literally child or little daughter-in-law - was a tradition of arranged marriage, in which a poor family would arrange and marry a pre-adolescent daughter into a richer family as a servant.<sup id="cite_ref-95" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-95"><span>[</span>95<span>]</span></a></sup> The little girl provided slave-like free labour, and also the daughter-in-law to the adoptive family's son. This sort of arranged marriage, in theory, enabled the girl to escape poverty and wealthy family to get free labour and a daughter-in-law. Zhaozhui was a related custom by which a wealthy family that lacked an heir would arrange marriage of a boy child from another family. The boy would move in with the wealthy family, take on the surname of the new family, and marry the family's daughter. Such arranged marriages helped maintain inheritance bloodlines.<sup id="cite_ref-lyj_96-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lyj-96"><span>[</span>96<span>]</span></a></sup> Similar uxorilocal arranged marriages to preserve wealth inheritance were common in Korea, Japan and other parts of the world.<sup id="cite_ref-97" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-97"><span>[</span>97<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-98" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-98"><span>[</span>98<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-99" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-99"><span>[</span>99<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bride-wealth">Bride-wealth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arranged_marriage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Bride-wealth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In many cultures, particularly in parts of Africa and the Middle East, daughters are valuable on the marriage market, because the groom and his family must pay cash and property for the right to marry the daughter. This is termed as bride-wealth and locally, by various names such as Lobola and Wine Carrying.<sup id="cite_ref-100" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-100"><span>[</span>100<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-101" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-101"><span>[</span>101<span>]</span></a></sup> The bride-wealth is typically kept by the bride's family, after the marriage, and is a source of income to poor families. The brothers, father and male relatives of the bride typically take keen interest in arranging her marriage to a man who is willing to pay the most wealth in exchange for the right to marry her.<sup id="cite_ref-102" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-102"><span>[</span>102<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-103" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-103"><span>[</span>103<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">Religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arranged_marriage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Religion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some religious dominations recognize marriages only within the faith. Of the major religions of the world, only Islam forbids marriage of girls born to a devout parent to a man who does not belong to that religion. In other words, Islam forbids marriage of Muslim girls to non-Muslim men,<sup id="cite_ref-104" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-104"><span>[</span>104<span>]</span></a></sup> and the religious punishment for those who marry outside might be severe.<sup id="cite_ref-105" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-105"><span>[</span>105<span>]</span></a></sup> This is one of the motivations of arranged marriages in Islamic minority populations in Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-106" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-106"><span>[</span>106<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-107" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-107"><span>[</span>107<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversy">Controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arranged_marriage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Arranged marriages are actively debated between scholars. The questions debated include whether arranged marriages are being used to abuse international immigration system; whether arranged marriages inherently violate human rights, particularly women's rights;<sup id="cite_ref-Bunch.2C_Charlotte_1995_pages_15-16_108-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bunch.2C_Charlotte_1995_pages_15-16-108"><span>[</span>108<span>]</span></a></sup> whether they yield more stable marriages for raising children, the next generation;<sup id="cite_ref-109" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-109"><span>[</span>109<span>]</span></a></sup> and whether there is more or less loving, respectful relationship for the married couple.<sup id="cite_ref-nytimes2013_110-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes2013-110"><span>[</span>110<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sham_marriages">Sham marriages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arranged_marriage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Sham marriages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the United Kingdom, public discussion<sup id="cite_ref-rgch3_111-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rgch3-111"><span>[</span>111<span>]</span></a></sup> has questioned whether international arranged marriages are a sham, a convenient means to get residency and European citizenship to some male or female immigrants, who would otherwise be denied a visa to enter the country. These fears have been stoked by observed divorces once the minimum married residence period requirement is met. Ann Cryer has alleged examples of such abuse by West Asian Muslim families in her motion to the UK's House of Commons.<sup id="cite_ref-112" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-112"><span>[</span>112<span>]</span></a></sup> The United States has seen a similar controversy with sham arranged marriages.<sup id="cite_ref-113" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-113"><span>[</span>113<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-114" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-114"><span>[</span>114<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Arranged_marriages_and_human_rights">Arranged marriages and human rights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arranged_marriage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Arranged marriages and human rights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Various international organizations, including UNICEF, have campaigned for laws to ban arranged marriages of children, as well as forced arranged marriages.<sup id="cite_ref-115" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-115"><span>[</span>115<span>]</span></a></sup> Article 15 and 16 of <i>The Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Discrimination Against Women</i> (CEDAW) specifically cover marriage and family law, which support such as ban.<sup id="cite_ref-116" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-116"><span>[</span>116<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-117" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-117"><span>[</span>117<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Arranged marriages are a matter of debate and disagreements. Activists such as Charlotte Bunch suggest that marriages arranged by parents and other family members, typically assume heterosexual preference and involve emotional pressure; this drives some individuals into marriages that they consent under duress.<sup id="cite_ref-Bunch.2C_Charlotte_1995_pages_15-16_108-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bunch.2C_Charlotte_1995_pages_15-16-108"><span>[</span>108<span>]</span></a></sup> Bunch suggests that marriages should be autonomous.</p>
<p>In contrast, preventing arranged marriages may harm many individuals who want to get married and can benefit from parental participation in finding and selecting a mate. For example, Willoughby suggests<sup id="cite_ref-nytimes2013_110-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes2013-110"><span>[</span>110<span>]</span></a></sup> that arranged marriages work because they remove anxiety in process of finding Mr. and Ms. Right. Parents, families and friends provide an independent perspective when they participate in learning and evaluating the other person, past history, behavior, as well as the couple's mutual compatibility. Willoughby further suggests that parents and family provide more than input in the screening and selection process; often, they provide financial support for the wedding, housing, emotional support and other valuable resources for the couple as they navigate past the wedding into married life, and help raise their children.</p>
<p>Michael Rosenfeld says<sup id="cite_ref-nytimes2013_110-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes2013-110"><span>[</span>110<span>]</span></a></sup> that the differences between autonomous marriages and arranged marriages are empirically small; many people meet, date and choose to marry or cohabit with those who are similar in background, age, interests and social class they feel most similar to, screening factors most parents would have used for them anyway, according to Rosenfeld. Assuming the pool from which mates are screened and selected is large, Rosenfeld suggests that the differences between the two approaches to marriages are not as great as some imagine them to be.<sup id="cite_ref-nytimes2013_110-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes2013-110"><span>[</span>110<span>]</span></a></sup> Others<sup id="cite_ref-118" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-118"><span>[</span>118<span>]</span></a></sup> have expressed sentiments similar to Rosenfeld.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stability">Stability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arranged_marriage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Stability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Divorce rates have climbed in Europe and United States, with increase in autonomous marriage rates. The lowest divorce rates in the world are in cultures with high rates of arranged marriages such as Amish culture of United States (1%),<sup id="cite_ref-119" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-119"><span>[</span>119<span>]</span></a></sup> Hindus of India (3%),<sup id="cite_ref-nytimes2013_110-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes2013-110"><span>[</span>110<span>]</span></a></sup> and Ultra-Orthodox Jews of Israel (7%).<sup id="cite_ref-120" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-120"><span>[</span>120<span>]</span></a></sup> In contrast, over 50% of self-arranged marriages in many parts of Europe and United States end up in divorce.<sup id="cite_ref-121" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-121"><span>[</span>121<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-122" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-122"><span>[</span>122<span>]</span></a></sup> This has led scholars to ask if arranged marriages are more stable than autonomous marriages, and whether this stability matters. Others suggest that the low divorce rates may not reflect stability, rather it may reflect the difficulty in divorce process and social ostracism to the individuals, who choose to live in a dysfunctional marriage rather than face the consequences of a divorce.<sup id="cite_ref-nytimes2013_110-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes2013-110"><span>[</span>110<span>]</span></a></sup>Also, the perception of high divorse rates attributed to self-arranged marriages in the United States is being called into question<sup id="cite_ref-123" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-123"><span>[</span>123<span>]</span></a></sup> and no authoritative data is available to support the theory that Hindus of India continue to enjoy low divorse rates.</p>
<p>There is a difference in observed divorce rates between various types of arranged marriages. The divorce rates in Islamic countries with consanguineous arranged marriages such as Saudi Arabia, Turkey, Egypt, Qatar, Jordan is between 20% to 35%,<sup id="cite_ref-124" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-124"><span>[</span>124<span>]</span></a></sup> in contrast to less than 10% divorce rates in non-consanguineous arranged marriages among Amish people, Hindus and Orthodox Jews.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Love_and_respect_in_arranged_versus_autonomous_marital_life">Love and respect in arranged versus autonomous marital life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arranged_marriage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Love and respect in arranged versus autonomous marital life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Various small sample surveys have been done to ascertain if arranged marriages or autonomous marriages have a more satisfying married life. The results are mixed - some state marriage satisfaction is higher in autonomous marriages, others find no significant differences.<sup id="cite_ref-GuptaSingh1982_125-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GuptaSingh1982-125"><span>[</span>125<span>]</span></a></sup> Johnson and Bachan have questioned the small sample size and conclusions derived from them.<sup id="cite_ref-JohnsonBachan2013_126-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JohnsonBachan2013-126"><span>[</span>126<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Scholars<sup id="cite_ref-nytimes2013_110-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes2013-110"><span>[</span>110<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-127" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-127"><span>[</span>127<span>]</span></a></sup> ask whether love and respect in marital life is greater in arranged marriages than autonomous marriages. Epstein suggests that in many arranged marriages, love emerges over time. Neither autonomous nor arranged marriages offer any guarantees. Many arranged marriages also end up being cold and dysfunctional as well, with reports of abuse.<sup id="cite_ref-128" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-128"><span>[</span>128<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-129" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-129"><span>[</span>129<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-130" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-130"><span>[</span>130<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
