<p><b><a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">Science</a></b> is the methodical study of nature including testable <a href="/wiki/Scientific_theory" title="Scientific theory">explanations</a> and <a href="/wiki/Predictability" title="Predictability">predictions</a>. An older and closely related current meaning emerged from <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a>, whereby "science" referred to the body of reliable knowledge that is logically and <a href="/wiki/Reason" title="Reason">rationally</a> explained (<i>see <a href="/wiki/Science#History_and_etymology" title="Science">"History and etymology" section</a> below</i>).</p>
<p>Since <a href="/wiki/Classical_antiquity" title="Classical antiquity">classical antiquity</a>, science as a type of <a href="/wiki/Knowledge" title="Knowledge">knowledge</a> was closely linked to <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a>. In the <a href="/wiki/Western_World" title="Western World" class="mw-redirect">West</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Early_modern_period" title="Early modern period">early modern period</a>, the words "science" and "philosophy" were sometimes used interchangeably. Not until the 17th century did natural philosophy emerge as a separate branch of philosophy, which is today called "natural science". "Science" continued to denote reliable knowledge about a topic; it remains in use in modern terms such as <a href="/wiki/Library_science" title="Library science">library science</a> or <a href="/wiki/Political_science" title="Political science">political science</a>.</p>
<p>Today, the ever-evolving term "science" refers to the pursuit of knowledge, not the knowledge itself. It is often synonymous with "natural and physical science" and often restricted to those branches of study relating to the phenomena of the material universe and their laws. Although the term implies exclusion of pure mathematics, many university faculties include Mathematics Departments within their Faculty of Science. The dominant sense in ordinary use has a narrower use for the term "science." It developed as a part of science becoming a distinct enterprise of defining the "<a href="/wiki/Laws_of_nature" title="Laws of nature" class="mw-redirect">laws of nature</a>"; early examples include <a href="/wiki/Kepler" title="Kepler" class="mw-redirect">Kepler</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Kepler%27s_laws_of_planetary_motion" title="Kepler's laws of planetary motion">laws</a>, <a href="/wiki/Galileo" title="Galileo" class="mw-redirect">Galileo</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Law_of_falling_bodies" title="Law of falling bodies" class="mw-redirect">laws</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Newton" title="Isaac Newton">Newton</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Newton%27s_laws_of_motion" title="Newton's laws of motion">laws of motion</a>. In this period it became more common to refer to natural philosophy as "natural science." Over the course of the 19th century, the word "science" became increasingly associated with the <a href="/wiki/Scientific_method" title="Scientific method">disciplined study</a> of the natural world, including <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a>. This sometimes left the study of human thought and society in a linguistic limbo, which was resolved by classifying these areas of academic study as <a href="/wiki/Social_science" title="Social science">social science</a>. For example, psychology evolved from philosophy, and has grown into an area of study.</p>
<p>Currently, there are both "hard" (e.g. biological psychology) and "soft" science (e.g. social psychology) fields within the discipline. As a result, and as is consistent with the unfolding of the study of knowledge and development of methods to establish facts, each area of psychology employs a scientific method. Reflecting the evolution of the development of knowledge and established facts and the use of the scientific method, Psychology Departments in universities are found within: Faculty of Arts and Science, Faculty of Arts, and a Faculty of Science. Similarly, several other major areas of disciplined study and knowledge exist today under the general rubric of "science", such as <a href="/wiki/Formal_science" title="Formal science">formal science</a> and <a href="/wiki/Applied_science" title="Applied science">applied science</a>.</p>

<p><i><a href="/wiki/Paramecium" title="Paramecium">Paramecium aurelia</a></i>, the best known of all <a href="/wiki/Ciliate" title="Ciliate">ciliates</a>. The bubbles throughout the cell are <a href="/wiki/Vacuole" title="Vacuole">vacuoles</a>. The entire surface is covered in <a href="/wiki/Cilium" title="Cilium">cilia</a>, which are blurred by their rapid movement. Cilia are short, hair-like projections that help with <a href="/wiki/Aquatic_locomotion" title="Aquatic locomotion">locomotion</a>.</p>

<h2 style="font-family:sans-serif; font-size:100%; font-weight:bold; border: none; margin: 0; padding:0; padding-bottom:.1em; color:#069;"><span class="mw-headline" id="Selected_biography"><i>Selected biography</i></span></h2>
</div>
<div style="border: 1px solid #069; border-top-width: 1px; vertical-align: top;background: white;opacity: 1; color: black; text-align: left; margin: 0 0 10px; padding: 1em;padding-top: .3em;">
<p><b><a href="/wiki/Rosalind_Franklin" title="Rosalind Franklin">Rosalind Elsie Franklin</a></b> (25 July 1920 - 16 April 1958) was an <a href="/wiki/English_people" title="English people">English</a> <a href="/wiki/Chemist" title="Chemist">chemist</a> and <a href="/wiki/X-ray_crystallography" title="X-ray crystallography">X-ray crystallographer</a> who made critical contributions to the understanding of the fine molecular structures of <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a>, <a href="/wiki/RNA" title="RNA">RNA</a>, <a href="/wiki/Viruses" title="Viruses" class="mw-redirect">viruses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Graphite" title="Graphite">graphite</a>. Franklin is best known for her contribution to the discovery of the <a href="/wiki/DNA#Discovery_of_the_structure_of_DNA" title="DNA">structure of DNA</a> in 1953, while working at <a href="/wiki/King%27s_College_London" title="King's College London">King's College London</a> under the direction of physicist <a href="/wiki/Sir_John_Randall" title="Sir John Randall" class="mw-redirect">John Randall</a>. By the time the 1962 Nobel Prize for Physiology and Medicine was awarded to Crick, Watson, and her colleague Wilkins, she had been dead for 4 years. She subsequently became an icon in feminist literature.</p>
