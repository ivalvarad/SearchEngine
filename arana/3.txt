			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Hepatic portal system</h1>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Human_anatomy" title="Human anatomy" class="mw-redirect">human anatomy</a>, the <b>hepatic portal system</b> is the system of <a href="/wiki/Vein" title="Vein">veins</a> comprising the <a href="/wiki/Hepatic_portal_vein" title="Hepatic portal vein">hepatic portal vein</a> and its tributaries. It is also called the <b>portal venous system</b>, although it is not the only example of a <a href="/wiki/Portal_venous_system" title="Portal venous system">portal venous system</a>, and <b>splanchnic veins</b>, which is <i>not</i> <a href="/wiki/Synonymous" title="Synonymous" class="mw-redirect">synonymous</a> with <i>hepatic portal system</i> and is imprecise (as it means <i><a href="/wiki/Viscera" title="Viscera" class="mw-redirect">visceral</a> veins</i> and not necessarily the <i>veins of the <a href="/wiki/Abdominal" title="Abdominal" class="mw-redirect">abdominal</a> viscera</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Function">Function</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hepatic_portal_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Function">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <b>portal venous system</b> is responsible for directing blood from parts of the <a href="/wiki/Gastrointestinal_tract" title="Gastrointestinal tract" class="mw-redirect">gastrointestinal tract</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Liver" title="Liver">liver</a>. Substances absorbed in the small intestine travel first to the liver for processing before continuing to the heart. Not all of the <a href="/wiki/Gastrointestinal_tract" title="Gastrointestinal tract" class="mw-redirect">gastrointestinal tract</a> is part of this system. The system extends from about the lower portion of the <a href="/wiki/Esophagus" title="Esophagus">esophagus</a> to the upper part of the <a href="/wiki/Anal_canal" title="Anal canal">anal canal</a>. It also includes venous drainage from the <a href="/wiki/Spleen" title="Spleen">spleen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pancreas" title="Pancreas">pancreas</a>.</p>
<p>Many drugs that are absorbed through the <a href="/wiki/GI_tract" title="GI tract" class="mw-redirect">GI tract</a> are substantially metabolized by the liver before reaching general circulation. This is known as the <a href="/wiki/First_pass_effect" title="First pass effect">first pass effect</a>. As a consequence, certain drugs can only be taken via certain routes. For example, <a href="/wiki/Nitroglycerin" title="Nitroglycerin">nitroglycerin</a> cannot be swallowed because the liver would inactivate the medication, but it can be taken <a href="/wiki/Sublingual" title="Sublingual" class="mw-redirect">under the tongue</a> or transdermal (through the skin) and thus is absorbed in a way that bypasses the portal venous system. Inversely, <a href="/wiki/Dextromethorphan" title="Dextromethorphan">dextromethorphan</a>, a cough suppressor, is best taken orally because it needs to be metabolised by the liver into <a href="/wiki/Dextrorphan" title="Dextrorphan">dextrorphan</a> in order to be effective. This latter principle is that of most <a href="/wiki/Prodrugs" title="Prodrugs" class="mw-redirect">prodrugs</a>. The use of <a href="/wiki/Suppositories" title="Suppositories" class="mw-redirect">suppository</a> is a way to by-pass partially the <a href="/wiki/Portal_vein" title="Portal vein" class="mw-redirect">portal vein</a>: the upper 1/3 of the <a href="/wiki/Rectum" title="Rectum">rectum</a> is drained into the <a href="/wiki/Portal_vein" title="Portal vein" class="mw-redirect">portal vein</a> while the lower 2/3 are drained into the <a href="/wiki/Internal_iliac_vein" title="Internal iliac vein">internal iliac vein</a> that goes directly in the <a href="/wiki/Inferior_vena_cava" title="Inferior vena cava">inferior vena cava</a> (thus by-passing the liver).</p>
<p>Blood flow to the liver is unique in that it receives both oxygenated and (partially) deoxygenated blood. As a result, the <a href="/wiki/Partial_pressure" title="Partial pressure">partial gas pressure</a> of oxygen (pO<sub>2</sub>) and perfusion pressure of portal blood are lower than in other organs of the body. Blood passes from branches of the portal vein through cavities between "plates" of <a href="/wiki/Hepatocytes" title="Hepatocytes" class="mw-redirect">hepatocytes</a> called <a href="/wiki/Liver_sinusoid" title="Liver sinusoid">sinusoids</a>. Blood also flows from branches of the <a href="/wiki/Hepatic_artery" title="Hepatic artery" class="mw-redirect">hepatic artery</a> and mixes in the sinusoids to supply the hepatocytes with oxygen. This mixture <a href="/wiki/Percolation" title="Percolation">percolates</a> through the sinusoids and collects in a central vein which drains into the <a href="/wiki/Hepatic_vein" title="Hepatic vein">hepatic vein</a>. The hepatic vein subsequently drains into the <a href="/wiki/Inferior_vena_cava" title="Inferior vena cava">inferior vena cava</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Hepatic_artery" title="Hepatic artery" class="mw-redirect">hepatic artery</a> provides 30 to 40% of the oxygen to the liver, while only accounting for 25% of the total liver blood flow. The rest comes from the partially deoxygenated blood from the <a href="/wiki/Portal_vein" title="Portal vein" class="mw-redirect">portal vein</a>. Interestingly, the liver consumes about 20% of the total body oxygen when at rest. That is why the total liver blood flow is quite high, at about 1 litre a minute and up to two litres a minute. That is on average one fourth of the average <a href="/wiki/Cardiac_output" title="Cardiac output">cardiac output</a> at rest.</p>
<p>Large veins that are considered part of the <i>portal venous system</i> are the:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hepatic_portal_vein" title="Hepatic portal vein">Hepatic portal vein</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Splenic_vein" title="Splenic vein">Splenic vein</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Superior_mesenteric_vein" title="Superior mesenteric vein">Superior mesenteric vein</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Inferior_mesenteric_vein" title="Inferior mesenteric vein">Inferior mesenteric vein</a></li>
</ul>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Superior_mesenteric_vein" title="Superior mesenteric vein">superior mesenteric vein</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Splenic_vein" title="Splenic vein">splenic vein</a> come together to form the actual <a href="/wiki/Hepatic_portal_vein" title="Hepatic portal vein">hepatic portal vein</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Inferior_mesenteric_vein" title="Inferior mesenteric vein">inferior mesenteric vein</a> connects in the majority of people on the <a href="/wiki/Splenic_vein" title="Splenic vein">splenic vein</a>, but in some people, it is known to connect on the <a href="/wiki/Portal_vein" title="Portal vein" class="mw-redirect">portal vein</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Superior_mesenteric_vein" title="Superior mesenteric vein">superior mesenteric vein</a>.</p>
<p>Roughly, the portal venous system corresponds to areas supplied by the <a href="/wiki/Celiac_trunk" title="Celiac trunk" class="mw-redirect">celiac trunk</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Superior_mesenteric_artery" title="Superior mesenteric artery">superior mesenteric artery</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Inferior_mesenteric_artery" title="Inferior mesenteric artery">inferior mesenteric artery</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Clinical_relevance">Clinical relevance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hepatic_portal_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Clinical relevance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Portal_hypertension" title="Portal hypertension">Portal hypertension</a> is a condition in which the blood pressure of the portal venous system is too high. It is often the result of <a href="/wiki/Cirrhosis" title="Cirrhosis">cirrhosis</a> of the liver.</p>
