<p>In political and social sciences, <b>communism</b> (from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>communis</i> – common, universal)<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> is a social, political, and economic ideology and movement whose ultimate goal is the establishment of the <a href="/wiki/Communist_society" title="Communist society">communist society</a>, which is a socioeconomic order structured upon the <a href="/wiki/Common_ownership" title="Common ownership">common ownership</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Means_of_production" title="Means of production">means of production</a> and the absence of <a href="/wiki/Social_class" title="Social class">social classes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Money" title="Money">money</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/State_(polity)" title="State (polity)">state</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Communism includes a variety of schools of thought, which broadly include <a href="/wiki/Marxism" title="Marxism">Marxism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anarchism" title="Anarchism">anarchism</a> (<a href="/wiki/Anarchist_communism" title="Anarchist communism">anarchist communism</a>) and the political ideologies grouped around both. All these hold in common the analysis that the current order of society stems from its economic system, <a href="/wiki/Capitalism" title="Capitalism">capitalism</a>, that in this system, there are two major social classes: the <a href="/wiki/Proletariat" title="Proletariat">working class</a> – who must work to survive, and who make up a majority of society – and the <a href="/wiki/Bourgeoisie" title="Bourgeoisie">capitalist class</a> – a minority who derive profit from employing the proletariat, through private ownership of the <a href="/wiki/Means_of_production" title="Means of production">means of production</a> (the physical and institutional means with which commodities are produced and distributed), and that political, social and economic <a href="/wiki/Class_conflict" title="Class conflict">conflict between these two classes</a> will trigger a fundamental change in the economic system, and by extension a wide-ranging transformation of society. The primary element which will enable this transformation, according to this analysis, is the <a href="/wiki/Social_ownership" title="Social ownership" class="mw-redirect">social ownership</a> of the means of production.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_communism" title="History of communism">History of communism</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_communism">Early communism</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Primitive_communism" title="Primitive communism">Primitive communism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Religious_communism" title="Religious communism">Religious communism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Utopian_socialism" title="Utopian socialism">Utopian socialism</a></div>
<p>The origins of communism are debatable, and there are various historical groups, as well as theorists, whose beliefs have been subsequently described as communist. <a href="/wiki/List_of_German-language_philosophers" title="List of German-language philosophers">German philosopher</a> <a href="/wiki/Karl_Marx" title="Karl Marx">Karl Marx</a> saw <a href="/wiki/Primitive_communism" title="Primitive communism">primitive communism</a> as the original, <a href="/wiki/Hunter-gatherer" title="Hunter-gatherer">hunter-gatherer</a> state of humankind from which it arose. For Marx, only after humanity was capable of producing <a href="/wiki/Economic_surplus" title="Economic surplus">surplus</a>, did private property develop. The idea of a classless, egalitarian society first emerged in <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">Ancient Greece</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Pipes_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pipes-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">Plato</a>, writing in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Republic_(Plato)" title="The Republic (Plato)" class="mw-redirect">The Republic</a></i> around 380 BC, described it as a state where people shared all their property, wives, and children: "The private and individual is altogether banished from life and things which are by nature private, such as eyes and ears and hands, have become common, and in some way see and hear and act in common, and all men express praise and feel joy and sorrow on the same occasions."<sup id="cite_ref-Pipes_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pipes-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/History_of_Western_philosophy" title="History of Western philosophy" class="mw-redirect">history of Western thought</a>, certain elements of the idea of a society based on common ownership of property can be traced back to <a href="/wiki/Ancient_history" title="Ancient history">ancient times</a>. Examples include the <a href="/wiki/Spartacus" title="Spartacus">Spartacus</a> <a href="/wiki/Slave_rebellion" title="Slave rebellion">slave revolt</a> in Rome.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> The 5th-century <a href="/wiki/Mazdak" title="Mazdak">Mazdak</a> movement in <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Persia (Iran)</a> has been described as "communistic" for challenging the enormous privileges of the noble classes and the clergy, criticizing the institution of <a href="/wiki/Private_property" title="Private property">private property</a> and for striving for an egalitarian society.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>At one time or another, various small communist communities existed, generally under the inspiration of <a href="/wiki/Scripture" title="Scripture" class="mw-redirect">Scripture</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Lansford_pp24.E2.80.9325_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lansford_pp24.E2.80.9325-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">medieval</a> <a href="/wiki/Christian_Church" title="Christian Church">Christian church</a>, for example, some <a href="/wiki/Monasticism" title="Monasticism">monastic</a> communities and <a href="/wiki/Religious_order" title="Religious order">religious orders</a> shared their land and other property (see <i><a href="/wiki/Religious_communism" title="Religious communism">Religious</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Christian_communism" title="Christian communism">Christian communism</a></i>).</p>
<p>Communist thought has also been traced back to the work of 16th-century English writer <a href="/wiki/Thomas_More" title="Thomas More">Thomas More</a>. In his treatise <i><a href="/wiki/Utopia_(book)" title="Utopia (book)">Utopia</a></i> (1516), More portrayed a society based on <a href="/wiki/Common_ownership" title="Common ownership">common ownership</a> of property, whose rulers administered it through the application of reason. In the 17th century, communist thought surfaced again in England, where a <a href="/wiki/Puritan" title="Puritan" class="mw-redirect">Puritan</a> <a href="/wiki/Religious_denomination" title="Religious denomination">religious group</a> known as the "<a href="/wiki/Diggers" title="Diggers">Diggers</a>" advocated the abolition of private ownership of land.<sup id="cite_ref-diggers_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-diggers-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Eduard_Bernstein" title="Eduard Bernstein">Eduard Bernstein</a>, in his 1895 <i>Cromwell and Communism</i><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> argued that several groupings in the <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil War</a>, especially the <a href="/wiki/Diggers" title="Diggers">Diggers</a> espoused clear communistic, agrarian ideals, and that <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Cromwell" title="Oliver Cromwell">Oliver Cromwell</a>'s attitude to these groups was at best ambivalent and often hostile.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> Criticism of the idea of private property continued into the <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Enlightenment" title="Age of Enlightenment">Age of Enlightenment</a> of the 18th century, through such thinkers as <a href="/wiki/Jean_Jacques_Rousseau" title="Jean Jacques Rousseau" class="mw-redirect">Jean Jacques Rousseau</a> in France. Later, following the upheaval of the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French Revolution</a>, communism emerged as a political doctrine.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Various social reformers in the early 19th century founded communities based on common ownership. But unlike many previous communist communities, they replaced the religious emphasis with a rational and philanthropic basis.<sup id="cite_ref-britannica_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-britannica-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> Notable among them were <a href="/wiki/Robert_Owen" title="Robert Owen">Robert Owen</a>, who founded <a href="/wiki/New_Harmony,_Indiana" title="New Harmony, Indiana">New Harmony</a> in Indiana (1825), and <a href="/wiki/Charles_Fourier" title="Charles Fourier">Charles Fourier</a>, whose followers organized other settlements in the United States such as <a href="/wiki/Brook_Farm" title="Brook Farm">Brook Farm</a> (1841–47).<sup id="cite_ref-britannica_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-britannica-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> Later in the 19th century, <a href="/wiki/Karl_Marx" title="Karl Marx">Karl Marx</a> described these social reformers as "<a href="/wiki/Utopian_socialism" title="Utopian socialism">utopian socialists</a>" to contrast them with his program of "<a href="/wiki/Scientific_socialism" title="Scientific socialism">scientific socialism</a>" (a term coined by <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Engels" title="Friedrich Engels">Friedrich Engels</a>). Other writers described by Marx as "utopian socialists" included <a href="/wiki/Claude_Henri_de_Rouvroy,_comte_de_Saint-Simon" title="Claude Henri de Rouvroy, comte de Saint-Simon">Saint-Simon</a>.</p>
<p>In its modern form, communism grew out of the socialist movement of 19th-century Europe. As the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a> advanced, socialist critics blamed capitalism for the misery of the <a href="/wiki/Proletariat" title="Proletariat">proletariat</a>—a new class of urban factory workers who labored under often-hazardous conditions. Foremost among these critics were Marx and his associate Friedrich Engels. In 1848, Marx and Engels offered a new definition of communism and popularized the term in their famous pamphlet <i><a href="/wiki/The_Communist_Manifesto" title="The Communist Manifesto">The Communist Manifesto</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-britannica_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-britannica-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_communism">Modern communism</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Communism.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Communism.svg/220px-Communism.svg.png" width="220" height="113" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Communism.svg/330px-Communism.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Communism.svg/440px-Communism.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="863" data-file-height="443" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Communism.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Countries of the world now (red) or previously (orange) having nominally Marxist–Leninist governments.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/October_Revolution" title="October Revolution">1917 October Revolution</a> in Russia set the conditions for the rise to state power of Lenin's <a href="/wiki/Bolsheviks" title="Bolsheviks">Bolsheviks</a>, which was the first time any avowedly communist party reached that position. The revolution transferred power to the <a href="/wiki/All-Russian_Congress_of_Soviets" title="All-Russian Congress of Soviets">All-Russian Congress of Soviets</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-or_soviets_1_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-or_soviets_1-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-or_soviets_2_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-or_soviets_2-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-or_soviets_3_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-or_soviets_3-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> in which the Bolsheviks had a majority. The event generated a great deal of practical and theoretical debate within the Marxist movement. Marx predicted that socialism and communism would be built upon foundations laid by the most advanced capitalist development. Russia, however, was one of the poorest countries in Europe with an enormous, largely illiterate peasantry and a minority of industrial workers. Marx had explicitly stated that Russia might be able to skip the stage of bourgeois rule.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> Other socialists also believed that a <a href="/wiki/October_Revolution" title="October Revolution">Russian revolution</a> could be the precursor of workers' revolutions in the West.</p>
<p>The moderate <a href="/wiki/Menshevik" title="Menshevik" class="mw-redirect">Mensheviks</a> opposed Lenin's Bolshevik plan for <a href="/wiki/Communist_revolution" title="Communist revolution">socialist revolution</a> before capitalism was more fully developed. The Bolsheviks' successful rise to power was based upon the slogans such as "Peace, bread, and land" which tapped the massive public desire for an end to Russian involvement in the <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">First World War</a>, the peasants' demand for <a href="/wiki/Land_reform" title="Land reform">land reform</a>, and popular support for the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_(council)" title="Soviet (council)">Soviets</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Soviet_Union,_Lenin_(55).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Soviet_Union%2C_Lenin_%2855%29.jpg/170px-Soviet_Union%2C_Lenin_%2855%29.jpg" width="170" height="231" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Soviet_Union%2C_Lenin_%2855%29.jpg/255px-Soviet_Union%2C_Lenin_%2855%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/18/Soviet_Union%2C_Lenin_%2855%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="313" data-file-height="425" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Soviet_Union,_Lenin_(55).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Lenin" title="Vladimir Lenin">Vladimir Lenin</a> after his return to <a href="/wiki/Saint_Petersburg" title="Saint Petersburg">Petrograd</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Second_International" title="Second International">Second International</a> had dissolved in 1916 over national divisions, as the separate national parties that composed it did not maintain a unified front against the <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">war</a>, instead generally supporting their respective nation's role. Lenin thus created the <a href="/wiki/Third_International" title="Third International" class="mw-redirect">Third International</a> (Comintern) in 1919 and sent the <a href="/wiki/Twenty-one_Conditions" title="Twenty-one Conditions">Twenty-one Conditions</a>, which included <a href="/wiki/Democratic_centralism" title="Democratic centralism">democratic centralism</a>, to all European <a href="/wiki/Democratic_socialism" title="Democratic socialism">socialist parties</a> willing to adhere. In France, for example, the majority of the <a href="/wiki/French_Section_of_the_Workers%27_International" title="French Section of the Workers' International">French Section of the Workers' International</a> (SFIO) party split in 1921 to form the <a href="/wiki/French_Communist_Party" title="French Communist Party">French Section of the Communist International</a> (SFIC). Henceforth, the term "Communism" was applied to the objective of the parties founded under the umbrella of the Comintern. Their program called for the uniting of workers of the world for revolution, which would be followed by the establishment of a <a href="/wiki/Dictatorship_of_the_proletariat" title="Dictatorship of the proletariat">dictatorship of the proletariat</a> as well as the development of a <a href="/wiki/Socialist_economics" title="Socialist economics">socialist economy</a>.</p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Civil_War" title="Russian Civil War">Russian Civil War</a> (1918–1922), the Bolsheviks <a href="/wiki/Nationalization" title="Nationalization">nationalized</a> all productive property and imposed a policy named <a href="/wiki/War_communism" title="War communism">war communism</a>, which put factories and railroads under strict government control, collected and rationed food, and introduced some bourgeois management of industry. After three years of war and the 1921 <a href="/wiki/Kronstadt_rebellion" title="Kronstadt rebellion">Kronstadt rebellion</a>, Lenin declared the <a href="/wiki/New_Economic_Policy" title="New Economic Policy">New Economic Policy</a> (NEP) in 1921, which was to give a "limited place for a limited time to capitalism." The NEP lasted until 1928, when <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Stalin" title="Joseph Stalin">Joseph Stalin</a> achieved party leadership, and the introduction of the Five Year Plans spelled the end of it. Following the Russian Civil War, the Bolsheviks, in 1922, <a href="/wiki/History_of_Soviet_Russia_and_the_Soviet_Union_(1917%E2%80%931927)#Creation_of_the_USSR" title="History of Soviet Russia and the Soviet Union (1917–1927)" class="mw-redirect">formed the Union of Soviet Socialist Republics</a> (USSR), or Soviet Union, from the former <a href="/wiki/Russian_Empire" title="Russian Empire">Russian Empire</a>.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:162px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Lenin.gif" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/f1/Lenin.gif" width="160" height="120" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="160" data-file-height="120" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Lenin.gif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Lenin" title="Vladimir Lenin">Vladimir Lenin</a> giving a speech.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Following Lenin's democratic centralism, the Leninist parties were organized on a hierarchical basis, with active cells of members as the broad base; they were made up only of elite <a href="/wiki/Professional_revolutionaries" title="Professional revolutionaries" class="mw-redirect">cadres</a> approved by higher members of the party as being reliable and completely subject to <a href="/wiki/Party_discipline" title="Party discipline">party discipline</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Great_Purge" title="Great Purge">Great Purge</a> of 1937–1938 was Stalin's attempt to destroy any possible opposition within the Communist Party. In the <a href="/wiki/Moscow_Trials" title="Moscow Trials">Moscow Trials</a> many old Bolsheviks who had played prominent roles during the Russian Revolution of 1917, or in Lenin's Soviet government afterwards, including <a href="/wiki/Lev_Kamenev" title="Lev Kamenev">Kamenev</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grigory_Zinoviev" title="Grigory Zinoviev">Zinoviev</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alexei_Rykov" title="Alexei Rykov">Rykov</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nikolai_Bukharin" title="Nikolai Bukharin">Bukharin</a>, were accused, pleaded guilty, and executed.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Following <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, Marxist–Leninists consolidated power in <a href="/wiki/Central_Europe" title="Central Europe">Central</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Europe" title="Eastern Europe">Eastern Europe</a>, and in 1949, the <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_China" title="Communist Party of China">Communist Party of China</a> (CPC), led by <a href="/wiki/Mao_Zedong" title="Mao Zedong">Mao Zedong</a>, established the <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="People's Republic of China" class="mw-redirect">People's Republic of China</a>, which would follow its own ideological path of development following the <a href="/wiki/Sino-Soviet_split" title="Sino-Soviet split">Sino-Soviet split</a>. <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_Korea" title="North Korea">North Korea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Laos" title="Laos">Laos</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cambodia" title="Cambodia">Cambodia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Angola" title="Angola">Angola</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mozambique" title="Mozambique">Mozambique</a> and the countries that were part of the former <a href="/wiki/Socialist_Federal_Republic_of_Yugoslavia" title="Socialist Federal Republic of Yugoslavia">Yugoslavia</a> were among the other countries in the <a href="/wiki/Third_World" title="Third World">Third World</a> that adopted or imposed a government run by a Marxist–Leninist party at some point. By the early 1980s almost one-third of the world's population lived in states ruled by a self-proclaimed Marxist–Leninist party, including the former Soviet Union and the PRC.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cold_War">Cold War</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sputnik-stamp-ussr.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Sputnik-stamp-ussr.jpg/150px-Sputnik-stamp-ussr.jpg" width="150" height="217" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Sputnik-stamp-ussr.jpg/225px-Sputnik-stamp-ussr.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Sputnik-stamp-ussr.jpg/300px-Sputnik-stamp-ussr.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1267" data-file-height="1836" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sputnik-stamp-ussr.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
USSR postage stamp depicting the states ruled by self-proclaimed communist parties, launching the first artificial satellite <a href="/wiki/Sputnik_1" title="Sputnik 1">Sputnik 1</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Its leading role in the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" title="Second World War" class="mw-redirect">Second World War</a> saw the emergence of the Soviet Union as a superpower, with strong influence over Eastern Europe and parts of <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>. The European and Japanese empires were shattered and Communist parties played a leading role in many independence movements.</p>
<p>Marxist–Leninist governments modeled on the Soviet Union took power with Soviet assistance in <a href="/wiki/Bulgaria" title="Bulgaria">Bulgaria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Czechoslovakia" title="Czechoslovakia">Czechoslovakia</a>, <a href="/wiki/East_Germany" title="East Germany">East Germany</a>, <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a> and <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a>. A Marxist–Leninist government was also created under <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Tito" title="Joseph Tito" class="mw-redirect">Marshal Tito</a> in <a href="/wiki/Yugoslavia" title="Yugoslavia">Yugoslavia</a>, but Tito's independent policies led to the expulsion of <a href="/wiki/Socialist_Federal_Republic_of_Yugoslavia" title="Socialist Federal Republic of Yugoslavia">Yugoslavia</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Cominform" title="Cominform">Cominform</a>, which had replaced the <a href="/wiki/Comintern" title="Comintern" class="mw-redirect">Comintern</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Titoism" title="Titoism">Titoism</a> was branded "<a href="/wiki/Deviationism" title="Deviationism">deviationist</a>". <a href="/wiki/Albania" title="Albania">Albania</a> also became an independent Marxist–Leninist state after World War II.<sup id="cite_ref-communistalbania_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-communistalbania-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>By 1950, the <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_China" title="Communist Party of China">Chinese Marxist–Leninists</a> had taken over all of <a href="/wiki/Mainland_China" title="Mainland China">mainland China</a>. In the <a href="/wiki/Korean_War" title="Korean War">Korean War</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a>, communists fought for power in their countries against the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and its allies. With varying degrees of success, communists attempted to unite with <a href="/wiki/Nationalism" title="Nationalism">nationalist</a> and <a href="/wiki/Socialism" title="Socialism">socialist</a> forces against perceived <a href="/wiki/Western_world" title="Western world">Western</a> <a href="/wiki/Imperialism" title="Imperialism">imperialism</a> in these poor countries.</p>
<p>Communism was seen as a rival of and a threat to western capitalism for most of the 20th century.<sup id="cite_ref-Georgakas1992_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Georgakas1992-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> This rivalry peaked during the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a>, as the world's two remaining superpowers, the United States and the Soviet Union, polarized most of the world into two camps of nations. It supported the spread of their respective economic and political systems. As a result, the camps expanded their military capacity, stockpiled <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapon" title="Nuclear weapon">nuclear weapons</a>, and competed in space exploration.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dissolution_of_the_Soviet_Union">Dissolution of the Soviet Union</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote">Further information: <a href="/wiki/List_of_communist_parties" title="List of communist parties">List of communist parties</a>, <a href="/wiki/List_of_communist_and_anti-capitalist_parties_with_parliamentary_representation" title="List of communist and anti-capitalist parties with parliamentary representation" class="mw-redirect">List of communist and anti-capitalist parties with parliamentary representation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dissolution_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Dissolution of the Soviet Union">Dissolution of the Soviet Union</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Communist_Party_of_the_Russian_Federation_meeting_at_Manezhnaya_Square,_Moscow,_2011-12-18.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Communist_Party_of_the_Russian_Federation_meeting_at_Manezhnaya_Square%2C_Moscow%2C_2011-12-18.jpg/220px-Communist_Party_of_the_Russian_Federation_meeting_at_Manezhnaya_Square%2C_Moscow%2C_2011-12-18.jpg" width="220" height="120" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Communist_Party_of_the_Russian_Federation_meeting_at_Manezhnaya_Square%2C_Moscow%2C_2011-12-18.jpg/330px-Communist_Party_of_the_Russian_Federation_meeting_at_Manezhnaya_Square%2C_Moscow%2C_2011-12-18.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Communist_Party_of_the_Russian_Federation_meeting_at_Manezhnaya_Square%2C_Moscow%2C_2011-12-18.jpg/440px-Communist_Party_of_the_Russian_Federation_meeting_at_Manezhnaya_Square%2C_Moscow%2C_2011-12-18.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2667" data-file-height="1459" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Communist_Party_of_the_Russian_Federation_meeting_at_Manezhnaya_Square,_Moscow,_2011-12-18.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A demonstration of the <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_the_Russian_Federation" title="Communist Party of the Russian Federation">Communist Party of the Russian Federation</a>, Moscow, December 2011.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In 1985, <a href="/wiki/Mikhail_Gorbachev" title="Mikhail Gorbachev">Mikhail Gorbachev</a> became leader of the Soviet Union and relaxed central control, in accordance with reform policies of <a href="/wiki/Glasnost" title="Glasnost">glasnost</a> (openness) and <a href="/wiki/Perestroika" title="Perestroika">perestroika</a> (restructuring). The Soviet Union did not intervene as <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a>, <a href="/wiki/East_Germany" title="East Germany">East Germany</a>, <a href="/wiki/Czechoslovakia" title="Czechoslovakia">Czechoslovakia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bulgaria" title="Bulgaria">Bulgaria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a> all abandoned Marxist–Leninist rule by 1990. In 1991, the <a href="/wiki/Dissolution_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Dissolution of the Soviet Union">Soviet Union dissolved</a>.</p>
<p>At present, states controlled by Marxist–Leninist parties under a single-party system include the <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="People's Republic of China" class="mw-redirect">People's Republic of China</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a>, <a href="/wiki/Laos" title="Laos">Laos</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a>. <a href="/wiki/North_Korea" title="North Korea">North Korea</a> currently refers to its leading ideology as <a href="/wiki/Juche" title="Juche">Juche</a>, which is portrayed as a development of Marxism–Leninism. Communist parties, or their descendant parties, remain politically important in a number of other countries. The <a href="/wiki/South_African_Communist_Party" title="South African Communist Party">South African Communist Party</a> is a partner in the <a href="/wiki/African_National_Congress" title="African National Congress">African National Congress</a>-led government. <a href="/wiki/Communism_in_India" title="Communism in India" class="mw-redirect">In India</a>, communists lead the governments of three <a href="/wiki/States_and_territories_of_India" title="States and territories of India" class="mw-redirect">states</a>, with a combined population of more than 115 million. In <a href="/wiki/Nepal" title="Nepal">Nepal</a>, communists hold a majority in the <a href="/wiki/Nepalese_Constituent_Assembly" title="Nepalese Constituent Assembly" class="mw-redirect">parliament</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_Brazil" title="Communist Party of Brazil">Communist Party of Brazil</a> is a part of the parliamentary coalition led by the ruling democratic socialist Workers' Party and is represented in the <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_of_Brazil" title="Cabinet of Brazil">executive cabinet</a> of <a href="/wiki/Dilma_Rousseff" title="Dilma Rousseff">Dilma Rousseff</a>.</p>
<p>The People's Republic of China has reassessed many aspects of the Maoist legacy; it, along with Laos, Vietnam, and, to a lesser degree Cuba, has reduced state control of the economy in order to stimulate growth. <a href="/wiki/Chinese_economic_reform" title="Chinese economic reform">Chinese economic reforms</a> started in 1978 under the leadership of <a href="/wiki/Deng_Xiaoping" title="Deng Xiaoping">Deng Xiaoping</a>; since then, China has managed to bring down the poverty rate from 53% in the Mao era to just 6% in 2001.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> The People's Republic of China runs <a href="/wiki/Special_Economic_Zone" title="Special Economic Zone" class="mw-redirect">Special Economic Zones</a> dedicated to market-oriented enterprise, free from <a href="/wiki/Central_government" title="Central government">central government</a> control. Several other states run by self-proclaimed Marxist–Leninist parties have also attempted to implement market-based reforms, including Vietnam.</p>
<p>The ruling stratum of the Soviet Union was, by Trotskyism, held to be a bureaucratic <a href="/wiki/Caste" title="Caste">caste</a>, but not a new <a href="/wiki/Ruling_class" title="Ruling class">ruling class</a>, despite their political control.</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Marxism" title="Marxism">Marxism</a>, first developed by <a href="/wiki/Karl_Marx" title="Karl Marx">Karl Marx</a> and <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Engels" title="Friedrich Engels">Friedrich Engels</a>, has been the foremost ideology of the communist movement. Marxism considers itself to be the embodiment of <a href="/wiki/Scientific_socialism" title="Scientific socialism">scientific socialism</a>; rather than model an "ideal society" based on intellectuals' design, it is a non-idealist attempt at the understanding of society and history, through an analysis based in real life. Marxism does not see communism as a "state of affairs" to be established, but rather as the expression of a real movement, with parameters which are derived completely from real life and not based on any intelligent design.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> Marxism, therefore, does no blueprinting of a communist society; it only makes an analysis which concludes what will trigger its implementation, and discovers its fundamental characteristics based on the derivation of real life conditions.</p>
<p>At the root of Marxism is the <a href="/wiki/Materialist_conception_of_history" title="Materialist conception of history" class="mw-redirect">materialist conception of history</a>, known as <i>historical materialism</i> for short. It holds that the key characteristic of economic systems through history has been the <a href="/wiki/Mode_of_production" title="Mode of production">mode of production</a>, and that the change between modes of production has been triggered by <a href="/wiki/Class_struggle" title="Class struggle" class="mw-redirect">class struggle</a>. According to this analysis, the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a> ushered the world into a new mode of production: <a href="/wiki/Capitalist_mode_of_production_(Marxist_theory)" title="Capitalist mode of production (Marxist theory)">capitalism</a>. Before capitalism, certain working classes had ownership of instruments utilized in production. But because machinery was much more efficient, this property became worthless, and the mass majority of workers could only survive by selling their labor, working through making use of someone else's machinery, and therefore making someone else profit. Thus with capitalism, the world was divided between two major classes: the proletariat and the bourgeoisie.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> These classes are directly antagonistic: the bourgeoisie has private ownership of the means of production and earns a profit off <a href="/wiki/Surplus_value" title="Surplus value">surplus value</a>, which is generated by the proletariat, which has no ownership of the means of production and therefore no option but to sell its labor to the bourgeoisie.</p>
<p>Historical materialism goes on and says: the rising bourgeoisie within feudalism, through the furtherance of its own material interests, captured power and abolished, of all relations of private property, only the feudal privileges, and with this took out of existence the feudal ruling class. This was another of the keys behind the consolidation of capitalism as the new mode of production, which is the final expression of class and property relations, and also has led into a massive expansion of production. It is, therefore, only in capitalism that private property in itself can be abolished.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> The proletariat, similarly, will capture political power, abolish bourgeois property through the common ownership of the means of production, therefore abolishing the bourgeoisie, and ultimately abolishing the proletariat itself, and ushering the world into a new mode of production: communism. In between capitalism and communism there is the <a href="/wiki/Dictatorship_of_the_proletariat" title="Dictatorship of the proletariat">dictatorship of the proletariat</a>, a democratic state where the whole of the public authority is elected and recallable under the basis of <a href="/wiki/Universal_suffrage" title="Universal suffrage">universal suffrage</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> it is the defeat of the bourgeois state, but not yet of the capitalist mode of production, and at the same time the only element which places into the realm of possibility moving on from this mode of production.</p>
<p>An important concept in Marxism is socialization vs. nationalization. Nationalization is merely state ownership of property, whereas socialization is actual control and management of property by society. Marxism considers socialization its goal, and considers nationalization a tactical issue, with state ownership still being in the realm of the capitalist mode of production. In the words of Engels: "the transformation [...] into State-ownership does not do away with the capitalistic nature of the productive forces. [...] State-ownership of the productive forces is not the solution of the conflict, but concealed within it are the technical conditions that form the elements of that solution".<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> This has led some Marxist groups and tendencies to label states such as the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>, based on nationalization, as <a href="/wiki/State_capitalism" title="State capitalism">state capitalist</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-hetsa.org.au_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hetsa.org.au-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Leninism">Leninism</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Leninism" title="Leninism">Leninism</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Lenin_CL.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Lenin_CL.jpg/220px-Lenin_CL.jpg" width="220" height="326" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Lenin_CL.jpg/330px-Lenin_CL.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Lenin_CL.jpg/440px-Lenin_CL.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2320" data-file-height="3440" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Lenin_CL.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Lenin" title="Vladimir Lenin">Vladimir Lenin</a>, 1920.</div>

<blockquote class="toccolours" style="float:none; display:table;">
<div style="padding: 10px 15px 10px 15px;"><i>"We want to achieve a new and better order of society: in this new and better society there must be neither rich nor poor; all will have to work. Not a handful of rich people, but all the working people must enjoy the fruits of their common labour. Machines and other improvements must serve to ease the work of all and not to enable a few to grow rich at the expense of millions and tens of millions of people. This new and better society is called</i> socialist society<i>. The teachings about this society are called 'socialism'."</i><br />
<small>-Vladimir Lenin, <i><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.marxists.org/archive/lenin/works/1903/rp/1.htm">"To the Rural Poor" (1903)</a>;</i> Collected Works<i>, Vol 6, p. 366</i></small></div>
</blockquote>
<p><a href="/wiki/Leninism" title="Leninism">Leninism</a> is the revolutionary theories developed by <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Lenin" title="Vladimir Lenin">Vladimir Lenin</a>, including the organizational principles of <a href="/wiki/Democratic_centralism" title="Democratic centralism">democratic centralism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vanguardism" title="Vanguardism">Vanguardism</a> and the political theory of imperialism. Leninist theory postulates that, with the strongly determined will of the Bourgeoisie to establish <a href="/wiki/Imperialism" title="Imperialism">Imperialism</a>, socialism will not arise spontaneously through the natural decay of capitalism, and that workers by themselves, who may be more or less sedated by reactionary propaganda, are unable to effectively organize and develop socialist <a href="/wiki/Class_consciousness" title="Class consciousness">consciousness</a>, therefore requiring the leadership of a revolutionary vanguard organized on the basis of <a href="/wiki/Democratic_centralism" title="Democratic centralism">democratic centralism</a>. As a result, Leninism promotes a <a href="/wiki/Vanguard_party" title="Vanguard party" class="mw-redirect">Vanguard party</a> in order to lead the working-class and peasants in a revolution.</p>
<p>Although the creation of a <a href="/wiki/Vanguard_party" title="Vanguard party" class="mw-redirect">vanguard party</a> was outlined by Marx and Engels in Chapter II: "Proletarians and Communists" of <i>The Communist Manifesto</i><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, Lenin modified this position by changing the role of the vanguards to <a href="/wiki/Professional_revolutionary" title="Professional revolutionary" class="mw-redirect">professional revolutionaries</a>, who were to hold power post-revolution and direct the national economy and society in developing world socialism.</p>
<p>After disposing of the Bourgeois dictatorship through <a href="/wiki/Socialist_revolution" title="Socialist revolution" class="mw-redirect">socialist revolution</a>, Leninists seek to create a <a href="/wiki/Socialist_state" title="Socialist state">socialist state</a> in which the working class would be in power, which they see as being essential for laying the foundations for a transitional <a href="/wiki/Withering_away_of_the_state" title="Withering away of the state">withering of the state</a> towards communism (Stateless society). Instead, Leninism advocates the concept of <a href="/wiki/Democratic_centralism" title="Democratic centralism">democratic centralism</a> as a process to ensure the voicing of concern and disagreement and to refine policy. Generally, the purpose of democratic centralism is "diversity in ideas, unity in action."</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Marxism.E2.80.93Leninism.2C_Stalinism.2C_and_Trotskyism">Marxism–Leninism, Stalinism, and Trotskyism</span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Marxism.E2.80.93Leninism_and_Stalinism">Marxism–Leninism and Stalinism</span></h4>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Marxism%E2%80%93Leninism" title="Marxism–Leninism">Marxism–Leninism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stalinism" title="Stalinism">Stalinism</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:JStalin_Secretary_general_CCCP_1942_flipped.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/JStalin_Secretary_general_CCCP_1942_flipped.jpg/220px-JStalin_Secretary_general_CCCP_1942_flipped.jpg" width="220" height="304" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/JStalin_Secretary_general_CCCP_1942_flipped.jpg/330px-JStalin_Secretary_general_CCCP_1942_flipped.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/JStalin_Secretary_general_CCCP_1942_flipped.jpg/440px-JStalin_Secretary_general_CCCP_1942_flipped.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3000" data-file-height="4151" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

<p><a href="/wiki/Marxism%E2%80%93Leninism" title="Marxism–Leninism">Marxism–Leninism</a> is a political ideology developed by <a href="/wiki/Stalin" title="Stalin" class="mw-redirect">Stalin</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-made_by_stalin_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-made_by_stalin-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup> which according to its proponents is based in <a href="/wiki/Marxism" title="Marxism">Marxism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leninism" title="Leninism">Leninism</a>. The term describes the specific political ideology which Stalin implemented in the <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Communist Party of the Soviet Union">Communist Party of the Soviet Union</a> and, in a global scale, in the <a href="/wiki/Comintern" title="Comintern" class="mw-redirect">Comintern</a>. There is no definite agreement between historians of about whether Stalin actually followed the principles of Marx and Lenin.<sup id="cite_ref-stalin_follow_marx_lenin_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stalin_follow_marx_lenin-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup> It also contains aspects which, according to some, are deviations from Marxism, such as "<a href="/wiki/Socialism_in_one_country" title="Socialism in one country" class="mw-redirect">socialism in one country</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-sioc1_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sioc1-35"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-sioc2_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sioc2-36"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup> Marxism–Leninism was the ideology of the most clearly visible communist movement. As such, it is the most prominent ideology associated with communism.</p>
<p>Marxism–Leninism refers to the socioeconomic system and political ideology implemented by Stalin in the Soviet Union and later copied by other states based on the Soviet model (central planning, single-party state, etc.), whereas <a href="/wiki/Stalinism" title="Stalinism">Stalinism</a> refers to Stalin's style of governance (political repression, cult of personality, etc.); Marxism–Leninism stayed after <a href="/wiki/De-Stalinization" title="De-Stalinization">de-Stalinization</a>, Stalinism did not. However, the term "Stalinism" is sometimes used to refer to Marxism–Leninism, sometimes to avoid implying Marxism–Leninism is related to Marxism and Leninism.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Maoism" title="Maoism">Maoism</a> is a form of Marxism–Leninism associated with Chinese leader <a href="/wiki/Mao_Zedong" title="Mao Zedong">Mao Zedong</a>. After de-Stalinization, Marxism–Leninism was kept in the Soviet Union but certain <a href="/wiki/Anti-Revisionism" title="Anti-Revisionism" class="mw-redirect">anti-revisionist</a> tendencies, such as <a href="/wiki/Hoxhaism" title="Hoxhaism">Hoxhaism</a> and Maoism, argued that it was deviated from. Therefore, different policies were applied in Albania and China, which became more distanced from the Soviet Union.</p>
<p>Marxism–Leninism has been criticized by other communist and Marxist tendencies. They argue that Marxist–Leninist states did not establish socialism but rather <a href="/wiki/State_capitalism" title="State capitalism">state capitalism</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-hetsa.org.au_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hetsa.org.au-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup> The dictatorship of the proletariat, according to Marxism, represents the rule of the majority (democracy) rather than of one party, to the extent that co-founder of Marxism <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Engels" title="Friedrich Engels">Friedrich Engels</a> described its "specific form" as the <a href="/wiki/Republicanism" title="Republicanism">democratic republic</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup> Additionally, according to Engels, state property by itself is private property of capitalist nature<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup> unless the proletariat has control of political power, in which case it forms public property.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup> Whether the proletariat was actually in control of the Marxist–Leninist states is a matter of debate between Marxism–Leninism and other communist tendencies. To these tendencies, Marxism–Leninism is neither Marxism nor Leninism nor the union of both, but rather an artificial term created to justify Stalin's ideological distortion,<sup id="cite_ref-stalin_distortion_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stalin_distortion-40"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup> forced into the CPSU and Comintern. In the Soviet Union, this struggle against Marxism–Leninism was represented by <a href="/wiki/Trotskyism" title="Trotskyism">Trotskyism</a>, which describes itself as a Marxist and Leninist tendency.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Trotskyism">Trotskyism</span></h4>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Trotskyism" title="Trotskyism">Trotskyism</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Trotsky_militant.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/cd/Trotsky_militant.jpg/220px-Trotsky_militant.jpg" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/cd/Trotsky_militant.jpg/330px-Trotsky_militant.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/cd/Trotsky_militant.jpg/440px-Trotsky_militant.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="531" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Trotsky_militant.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Leon_Trotsky" title="Leon Trotsky">Leon Trotsky</a> reading <i><a href="/wiki/The_Militant" title="The Militant">The Militant</a></i>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Trotskyism is a Marxist and Leninist tendency that was developed by <a href="/wiki/Leon_Trotsky" title="Leon Trotsky">Leon Trotsky</a>, opposed to Marxism–Leninism. It supports the theory of <a href="/wiki/Permanent_revolution" title="Permanent revolution">permanent revolution</a> and <a href="/wiki/World_revolution" title="World revolution">world revolution</a> instead of the <a href="/wiki/Two_stage_theory" title="Two stage theory" class="mw-redirect">two stage theory</a> and <a href="/wiki/Socialism_in_one_country" title="Socialism in one country" class="mw-redirect">socialism in one country</a>. It supported <a href="/wiki/Proletarian_internationalism" title="Proletarian internationalism">proletarian internationalism</a> and another Communist revolution in the Soviet Union, which Trotsky claimed had become a "<a href="/wiki/Degenerated_worker%27s_state" title="Degenerated worker's state" class="mw-redirect">degenerated worker's state</a>" under the leadership of Stalin, rather than the <a href="/wiki/Dictatorship_of_the_proletariat" title="Dictatorship of the proletariat">dictatorship of the proletariat</a>, in which class relations had re-emerged in a new form.</p>
<p>Trotsky and his supporters, struggling against Stalin for power in the Soviet Union, organized into the <i><a href="/wiki/Left_Opposition" title="Left Opposition">Left Opposition</a></i> and their platform became known as Trotskyism. Stalin eventually succeeded in gaining control of the Soviet regime and Trotskyist attempts to remove Stalin from power resulted in Trotsky's exile from the Soviet Union in 1929. Trotsky later founded the <a href="/wiki/Fourth_International" title="Fourth International">Fourth International</a>, a Trotskyist rival to the <a href="/wiki/Comintern" title="Comintern" class="mw-redirect">Comintern</a>, in 1938.</p>
<p>Trotsky's politics differed sharply from those of Stalin and Mao, most importantly in declaring the need for an international proletarian revolution (rather than socialism in one country) and unwavering support for a true dictatorship of the proletariat based on democratic principles.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Libertarian_Marxism">Libertarian Marxism</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Libertarian_Marxism" title="Libertarian Marxism">Libertarian Marxism</a></div>
<p>Libertarian Marxism refers to a broad scope of economic and political philosophies that emphasize the <a href="/wiki/Anti-authoritarian" title="Anti-authoritarian" class="mw-redirect">anti-authoritarian</a> aspects of <a href="/wiki/Marxism" title="Marxism">Marxism</a>. Early currents of libertarian Marxism, known as <a href="/wiki/Left_communism" title="Left communism">left communism</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup> emerged in opposition to <a href="/wiki/Marxism%E2%80%93Leninism" title="Marxism–Leninism">Marxism–Leninism</a><sup id="cite_ref-Non-Leninist_Marxism_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Non-Leninist_Marxism-42"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup> and its derivatives, such as <a href="/wiki/Stalinism" title="Stalinism">Stalinism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maoism" title="Maoism">Maoism</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Trotskyism" title="Trotskyism">Trotskyism</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup> Libertarian Marxism is also critical of <a href="/wiki/Reformist" title="Reformist" class="mw-redirect">reformist</a> positions, such as those held by <a href="/wiki/Social_democrats" title="Social democrats" class="mw-redirect">social democrats</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup> Libertarian Marxist currents often draw from Marx and Engels' later works, specifically the <i><a href="/wiki/Grundrisse" title="Grundrisse">Grundrisse</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Civil_War_in_France" title="The Civil War in France">The Civil War in France</a></i>;<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup> emphasizing the Marxist belief in the ability of the <a href="/wiki/Working_class" title="Working class">working class</a> to forge its own destiny without the need for a revolutionary party or <a href="/wiki/State_(polity)" title="State (polity)">state</a> to mediate or aid its liberation.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup> Along with <a href="/wiki/Anarchism" title="Anarchism">anarchism</a>, Libertarian Marxism is one of the main currents of <a href="/wiki/Libertarian_socialism" title="Libertarian socialism">libertarian socialism</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Libertarian Marxism includes such currents as <a href="/wiki/Luxemburgism" title="Luxemburgism">Luxemburgism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Council_communism" title="Council communism">council communism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Left_communism" title="Left communism">left communism</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Socialisme_ou_Barbarie" title="Socialisme ou Barbarie">Socialisme ou Barbarie</a></i>, the <a href="/wiki/Johnson-Forest_tendency" title="Johnson-Forest tendency" class="mw-redirect">Johnson-Forest tendency</a>, <a href="/wiki/World_socialism" title="World socialism" class="mw-redirect">world socialism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lettrism" title="Lettrism">Lettrism</a>/<a href="/wiki/Situationist_International" title="Situationist International">Situationism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Operaismo" title="Operaismo" class="mw-redirect">operaismo</a>/<a href="/wiki/Autonomism" title="Autonomism">autonomism</a>, and <a href="/wiki/New_Left" title="New Left">New Left</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup> Libertarian Marxism has often had a strong influence on both <a href="/wiki/Post-left_anarchism" title="Post-left anarchism" class="mw-redirect">post-left</a> and <a href="/wiki/Social_anarchism" title="Social anarchism">social anarchists</a>. Notable theorists of libertarian Marxism have included <a href="/wiki/Anton_Pannekoek" title="Anton Pannekoek" class="mw-redirect">Anton Pannekoek</a>, <a href="/wiki/Raya_Dunayevskaya" title="Raya Dunayevskaya">Raya Dunayevskaya</a>, <a href="/wiki/CLR_James" title="CLR James" class="mw-redirect">CLR James</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Negri" title="Antonio Negri">Antonio Negri</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cornelius_Castoriadis" title="Cornelius Castoriadis">Cornelius Castoriadis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Brinton" title="Maurice Brinton" class="mw-redirect">Maurice Brinton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guy_Debord" title="Guy Debord">Guy Debord</a>, <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Gu%C3%A9rin" title="Daniel Guérin">Daniel Guérin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ernesto_Screpanti" title="Ernesto Screpanti">Ernesto Screpanti</a> and <a href="/wiki/Raoul_Vaneigem" title="Raoul Vaneigem">Raoul Vaneigem</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Council_communism">Council communism</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Council_communism" title="Council communism">Council communism</a></div>
<p>Council communism is a <a href="/wiki/Far-left" title="Far-left" class="mw-redirect">far-left</a> movement originating in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> in the 1920s. Its primary organization was the <a href="/wiki/Communist_Workers_Party_of_Germany" title="Communist Workers Party of Germany" class="mw-redirect">Communist Workers Party of Germany</a> (KAPD). Council communism continues today as a theoretical and activist position within both left-wing Marxism and <a href="/wiki/Libertarian_socialism" title="Libertarian socialism">libertarian socialism</a>.</p>
<p>The central argument of council communism, in contrast to those of <a href="/wiki/Social_democracy" title="Social democracy">social democracy</a> and Leninist communism, is that democratic <a href="/wiki/Workers%27_councils" title="Workers' councils" class="mw-redirect">workers' councils</a> arising in the factories and municipalities are the natural form of working class organization and governmental power. This view is opposed to both the <a href="/wiki/Reformist" title="Reformist" class="mw-redirect">reformist</a> and the Leninist <a href="/wiki/Ideology" title="Ideology">ideologies</a>, with their stress on, respectively, parliaments and <a href="/wiki/New_institutionalism" title="New institutionalism">institutional</a> government (i.e., by applying social reforms, on the one hand, and <a href="/wiki/Vanguard_party" title="Vanguard party" class="mw-redirect">vanguard parties</a> and participative <a href="/wiki/Democratic_centralism" title="Democratic centralism">democratic centralism</a> on the other).</p>
<p>The core principle of council communism is that the government and the <a href="/wiki/Economic_system" title="Economic system">economy</a> should be managed by <a href="/wiki/Workers%27_councils" title="Workers' councils" class="mw-redirect">workers' councils</a> composed of delegates elected at workplaces and <a href="/wiki/Recall_election" title="Recall election">recallable</a> at any moment. As such, council communists oppose <a href="/wiki/Planned_economy" title="Planned economy">state-run</a> <a href="/wiki/Authoritarianism" title="Authoritarianism">authoritarian</a> "<a href="/wiki/State_socialism" title="State socialism">State socialism</a>"/"<a href="/wiki/State_capitalism" title="State capitalism">State capitalism</a>". They also oppose the idea of a "revolutionary party", since council communists believe that a revolution led by a party will necessarily produce a party dictatorship. Council communists support a worker's democracy, which they want to produce through a federation of workers' councils.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Left_communism">Left communism</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Left_communism" title="Left communism">Left communism</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Rosa_Luxemburg.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Rosa_Luxemburg.jpg/220px-Rosa_Luxemburg.jpg" width="220" height="330" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Rosa_Luxemburg.jpg/330px-Rosa_Luxemburg.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Rosa_Luxemburg.jpg/440px-Rosa_Luxemburg.jpg 2x" data-file-width="497" data-file-height="746" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Rosa_Luxemburg.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Rosa_Luxemburg" title="Rosa Luxemburg">Rosa Luxemburg</a>, inspiration of left communism.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Left communism is the range of communist viewpoints held by the communist left,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (February 2015)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> which criticizes the political ideas of the <a href="/wiki/Bolshevik" title="Bolshevik" class="mw-redirect">Bolsheviks</a> at certain periods, from a position that is asserted to be more authentically Marxist and proletarian than the views of Leninism held by the <a href="/wiki/Comintern" title="Comintern" class="mw-redirect">Communist International</a> after its first and during its second congress.<sup id="cite_ref-Non-Leninist_Marxism_42-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Non-Leninist_Marxism-42"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Left communists see themselves to the left of Leninists (whom they tend to see as 'left of capital', not socialists), <a href="/wiki/Anarchist_communism" title="Anarchist communism">anarchist communists</a> (some of whom they consider internationalist socialists) as well as some other revolutionary socialist tendencies (for example <a href="/wiki/De_Leonism" title="De Leonism">De Leonists</a>, who they tend to see as being internationalist socialists only in limited instances).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Although she died before left communism became a distinct tendency, <a href="/wiki/Rosa_Luxemburg" title="Rosa Luxemburg">Rosa Luxemburg</a> has heavily influenced most left communists, both politically and theoretically. Proponents of left communism have included <a href="/wiki/Amadeo_Bordiga" title="Amadeo Bordiga">Amadeo Bordiga</a>, <a href="/wiki/Herman_Gorter" title="Herman Gorter">Herman Gorter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antonie_Pannekoek" title="Antonie Pannekoek">Anton Pannekoek</a>, <a href="/wiki/Otto_R%C3%BChle" title="Otto Rühle">Otto Rühle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Karl_Korsch" title="Karl Korsch">Karl Korsch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sylvia_Pankhurst" title="Sylvia Pankhurst">Sylvia Pankhurst</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paul_Mattick" title="Paul Mattick">Paul Mattick</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-Marxist_communism">Non-Marxist communism</span></h2>
<p>The dominant forms of communism are based on Marxism, but non-Marxist versions of communism (such as <a href="/wiki/Christian_communism" title="Christian communism">Christian communism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anarchist_communism" title="Anarchist communism">anarchist communism</a>) also exist.</p>

<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Anarchist_communism" title="Anarchist communism">Anarchist communism</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Kropotkin2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Kropotkin2.jpg/220px-Kropotkin2.jpg" width="220" height="282" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Kropotkin2.jpg/330px-Kropotkin2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/00/Kropotkin2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="422" data-file-height="541" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Kropotkin2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Peter_Kropotkin" title="Peter Kropotkin">Peter Kropotkin</a>, main theorist of <a href="/wiki/Anarcho-communism" title="Anarcho-communism" class="mw-redirect">anarcho-communism</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Anarchist communism (also known as libertarian communism) is a theory of <a href="/wiki/Anarchism" title="Anarchism">anarchism</a> which advocates the abolition of the <a href="/wiki/State_(polity)" title="State (polity)">state</a>, <a href="/wiki/Private_property" title="Private property">private property</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Capitalism" title="Capitalism">capitalism</a> in favor of <a href="/wiki/Common_ownership" title="Common ownership">common ownership</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Means_of_production" title="Means of production">means of production</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Mayne_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mayne-49"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Direct_democracy" title="Direct democracy">direct democracy</a> and a horizontal network of <a href="/wiki/Voluntary_association" title="Voluntary association">voluntary associations</a> and <a href="/wiki/Workers%27_council" title="Workers' council">workers' councils</a> with production and consumption based on the guiding principle: "<a href="/wiki/From_each_according_to_his_ability,_to_each_according_to_his_need" title="From each according to his ability, to each according to his need">from each according to his ability, to each according to his need</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52"><span>[</span>52<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Anarcho-communism differs from Marxism rejecting its view about the need for a State Socialism phase before building communism. The main anarcho-communist theorist <a href="/wiki/Peter_Kropotkin" title="Peter Kropotkin">Peter Kropotkin</a> argued "that a revolutionary society should "transform itself immediately into a communist society,", that is, should go immediately into what Marx had regarded as the "more advanced," completed, phase of communism."<sup id="cite_ref-theanarchistlibrary.org_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-theanarchistlibrary.org-53"><span>[</span>53<span>]</span></a></sup> In this way it tries to avoid the reappearance of "class divisions and the need for a state to oversee everything".<sup id="cite_ref-theanarchistlibrary.org_53-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-theanarchistlibrary.org-53"><span>[</span>53<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Some forms of anarchist communism such as <a href="/wiki/Insurrectionary_anarchism" title="Insurrectionary anarchism">insurrectionary anarchism</a> are <a href="/wiki/Egoist_anarchism" title="Egoist anarchism">egoist</a> and strongly influenced by radical <a href="/wiki/Individualism" title="Individualism">individualism</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54"><span>[</span>54<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-creativenothing_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-creativenothing-55"><span>[</span>55<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-56"><span>[</span>56<span>]</span></a></sup> believing that anarchist communism does not require a communitarian nature at all. Most anarcho-communists view anarcho-communism as a way of reconciling the opposition between the individual and society.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57"><span>[</span>57<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58"><span>[</span>58<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59"><span>[</span>59<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>To date in human history, the best known examples of an <i>anarchist communist</i> society, established around the ideas as they exist today, that received worldwide attention and knowledge in the historical canon, are the anarchist territories during the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Revolution" title="Spanish Revolution" class="mw-redirect">Spanish Revolution</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Free_Territory" title="Free Territory">Free Territory</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Revolution_(1917)" title="Russian Revolution (1917)" class="mw-redirect">Russian Revolution</a>. Through the efforts and influence of the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Anarchists" title="Spanish Anarchists" class="mw-redirect">Spanish Anarchists</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Revolution" title="Spanish Revolution" class="mw-redirect">Spanish Revolution</a> within the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Civil_War" title="Spanish Civil War">Spanish Civil War</a>, starting in 1936 anarchist communism existed in most of <a href="/wiki/Aragon" title="Aragon">Aragon</a>, parts of the <a href="/wiki/Levante,_Spain" title="Levante, Spain">Levante</a> and <a href="/wiki/Andalusia" title="Andalusia">Andalusia</a>, as well as in the stronghold of <a href="/wiki/Anarchist_Catalonia" title="Anarchist Catalonia" class="mw-redirect">Anarchist Catalonia</a> before being brutally crushed by the combined forces of <a href="/wiki/Francoism" title="Francoism" class="mw-redirect">the authoritarian regime that won the war</a>, <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Hitler</a>, <a href="/wiki/Benito_Mussolini" title="Benito Mussolini">Mussolini</a>, Spanish Communist Party repression (backed by the USSR) as well as economic and armaments blockades from the capitalist countries and the Spanish Republic itself. During the Russian Revolution, anarchists such as <a href="/wiki/Nestor_Makhno" title="Nestor Makhno">Nestor Makhno</a> worked to create and defend—through the <a href="/wiki/Revolutionary_Insurrectionary_Army_of_Ukraine" title="Revolutionary Insurrectionary Army of Ukraine">Revolutionary Insurrectionary Army of Ukraine</a>—anarchist communism in the <a href="/wiki/Free_Territory" title="Free Territory">Free Territory</a> of the Ukraine from 1919 before being conquered by the Bolsheviks in 1921.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Christian_communism">Christian communism</span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Christian_communism" title="Christian communism">Christian communism</a> is a form of <a href="/wiki/Religious_communism" title="Religious communism">religious communism</a> centred on <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a>. It is a theological and political theory based upon the view that the <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">teachings of Jesus Christ</a> urge Christians to support communism as the ideal <a href="/wiki/Social_structure" title="Social structure">social system</a>. Christian communists trace the origins of their practice to teachings in the <a href="/wiki/New_Testament" title="New Testament">New Testament</a>, such as the <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_the_Apostles" title="Acts of the Apostles">Acts of the Apostles</a> at chapter 2 and verses 42, 44 and 45:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p><sup>42</sup> <i>And they continued steadfastly in the apostles' doctrine and in fellowship <a href="/wiki/..." title="..." class="mw-redirect">...</a></i> <sup>44</sup> <i>And all that believed were together, and had all things in common;</i> <sup>45</sup> <i>And sold their possessions and goods, and parted them to all men, as every man had need.</i></p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— <a href="/wiki/Authorized_King_James_Version" title="Authorized King James Version" class="mw-redirect">King James Version</a></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>Christian communism can be seen as a radical form of <a href="/wiki/Christian_socialism" title="Christian socialism">Christian socialism</a>. Also, because many Christian communists have formed independent stateless communes in the past, there is a link between Christian communism and <a href="/wiki/Christian_anarchism" title="Christian anarchism">Christian anarchism</a>. Christian communists may not agree with various parts of <a href="/wiki/Marxism" title="Marxism">Marxism</a>, but they share some political goals of Marxists, for example replacing <a href="/wiki/Capitalism" title="Capitalism">capitalism</a> with <a href="/wiki/Socialism" title="Socialism">socialism</a>, which should in turn be followed by communism at a later point in the future. However, Christian communists sometimes disagree with Marxists (and particularly with <a href="/wiki/Leninism" title="Leninism">Leninists</a>) on the way a socialist or communist society should be organized.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Anti-communism" title="Anti-communism">Anti-communism</a></div>
<div class="hatnote">See also <a href="/wiki/Criticisms_of_Marxism" title="Criticisms of Marxism">Criticisms of Marxism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Criticisms_of_socialism" title="Criticisms of socialism">Criticisms of socialism</a> for a discussion of objections to socialism in general.</div>
<p>Some people have criticized socialism and by extension communism, stating that the two systems would <a href="/wiki/Economic_calculation_problem" title="Economic calculation problem">distort or remove price signals</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Mises_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mises-60"><span>[</span>60<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hayek_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hayek-61"><span>[</span>61<span>]</span></a></sup> slow or stagnate technological advance,<sup id="cite_ref-milton_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-milton-62"><span>[</span>62<span>]</span></a></sup> reduce incentives,<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63"><span>[</span>63<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64"><span>[</span>64<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65"><span>[</span>65<span>]</span></a></sup> and reduce prosperity,<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66"><span>[</span>66<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67"><span>[</span>67<span>]</span></a></sup> as well as on the grounds of its feasibility<sup id="cite_ref-Mises_60-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mises-60"><span>[</span>60<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hayek_61-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hayek-61"><span>[</span>61<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-milton_62-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-milton-62"><span>[</span>62<span>]</span></a></sup> and its social and political effects.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68"><span>[</span>68<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69"><span>[</span>69<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70"><span>[</span>70<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71"><span>[</span>71<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Self_72-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Self-72"><span>[</span>72<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Criticism of communism can be divided into two broad categories: those concerning themselves with the practical aspects of 20th century <a href="/wiki/Communist_state" title="Communist state">Communist states</a>, and those concerning themselves with communist principles and theory.</p>
<p>The categories of criticisms mentioned above are discussed in separate articles:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Criticisms_of_communist_party_rule" title="Criticisms of communist party rule">Criticisms of communist party rule</a> is an article dealing with criticisms of the practical policies implemented by 20th century governments claiming to follow the ideology of <a href="/wiki/Marxism%E2%80%93Leninism" title="Marxism–Leninism">Marxism–Leninism</a> (these are usually called "Communist states" in the West).</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Criticisms_of_Marxism" title="Criticisms of Marxism">Criticisms of Marxism</a> is an article dealing with criticisms of Marxist theory itself.</li>
</ul>
