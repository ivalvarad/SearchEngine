<p><b>Mathieu Kérékou</b> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_French" title="Help:IPA for French">[ma.tjø ke.ʁe.ku]</a></span> (2 September 1933 – 14 October 2015) was <a href="/wiki/President_of_Benin" title="President of Benin" class="mw-redirect">President of Benin</a> from 1972 to 1991 and again from 1996 to 2006. After seizing power in a military coup, he ruled the country for 17 years, for most of that time under an officially <a href="/wiki/Marxist%E2%80%93Leninist" title="Marxist–Leninist" class="mw-redirect">Marxist–Leninist</a> ideology, before he was stripped of his powers by the National Conference of 1990. He was defeated in the <a href="/wiki/Beninese_presidential_election,_1991" title="Beninese presidential election, 1991">1991 presidential election</a>, but was returned to the presidency in the <a href="/wiki/Beninese_presidential_election,_1996" title="Beninese presidential election, 1996">1996 election</a> and controversially <a href="/wiki/Beninese_presidential_election,_2001" title="Beninese presidential election, 2001">re-elected in 2001</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Military_background">Military background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mathieu_K%C3%A9r%C3%A9kou&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Military background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kérékou was born in 1933 in <a href="/wiki/Kouarfa" title="Kouarfa">Kouarfa</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Pouvoir_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pouvoir-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> in north-west <a href="/wiki/French_Dahomey" title="French Dahomey">French Dahomey</a>. After having studied at military schools in modern-day Mali and Senegal,<sup id="cite_ref-Pouvoir_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pouvoir-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> Kérékou served in the military. Following independence, from 1961 to 1963 he was an <i><a href="/wiki/Aide-de-camp" title="Aide-de-camp">aide-de-camp</a></i> to Dahomeyan President <a href="/wiki/Hubert_Maga" title="Hubert Maga">Hubert Maga</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-Profiles_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Profiles-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> following <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Kouand%C3%A9t%C3%A9" title="Maurice Kouandété">Maurice Kouandété</a>'s seizure of power in December 1967, Kérékou, who was his cousin,<sup id="cite_ref-Profiles_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Profiles-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Amuwo_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Amuwo-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> was made chairman of the Military Revolutionary Council.<sup id="cite_ref-Profiles_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Profiles-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> After Kérékou attended French military schools from 1968 to 1970,<sup id="cite_ref-Profiles_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Profiles-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Maga made him a major, deputy chief of staff, and commander of the Ouidah paratroop unit.<sup id="cite_ref-Profiles_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Profiles-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Amuwo_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Amuwo-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="1972_coup_and_single-party_rule">1972 coup and single-party rule</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mathieu_K%C3%A9r%C3%A9kou&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 1972 coup and single-party rule">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kérékou seized power in <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Dahomey" title="Republic of Dahomey">Dahomey</a> in a military coup on 26 October 1972,<sup id="cite_ref-Pouvoir_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pouvoir-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> ending a system of government in which three members of a presidential council were to rotate power (earlier in the year Maga had handed over power to <a href="/wiki/Justin_Ahomadegb%C3%A9" title="Justin Ahomadegbé" class="mw-redirect">Justin Ahomadegbé</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-Decalo_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Decalo-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>During his first two years in power, Kérékou expressed only nationalism and said that the country's revolution would not "burden itself by copying foreign ideology ... We do not want communism or capitalism or socialism. We have our own Dahomean social and cultural system." On 30 November 1974, however, he announced the adoption of <a href="/wiki/Marxism-Leninism" title="Marxism-Leninism" class="mw-redirect">Marxism-Leninism</a> by the state.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> The country was renamed from the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Dahomey" title="Republic of Dahomey">Republic of Dahomey</a> to the <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_Benin" title="People's Republic of Benin">People's Republic of Benin</a> a year later; the banks and <a href="/wiki/Petroleum_industry" title="Petroleum industry">petroleum industry</a> were nationalized. The <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Revolutionary_Party_of_Benin" title="People's Revolutionary Party of Benin">People's Revolutionary Party of Benin</a> (<i>Parti de la révolution populaire du Bénin</i>, PRPB) was established as the sole ruling party. In 1980, Kérékou was elected president by the Revolutionary National Assembly; he retired from the army in 1987.<sup id="cite_ref-Onadipe_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Onadipe-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>It has been suggested that Kérékou's move to Marxism-Leninism was motivated mainly by pragmatic considerations, and that Kérékou himself was not actually a leftist radical; the new ideology offered a means of legitimization, a way of distinguishing the new regime from those that had preceded it, and was based on broader unifying principles than the politics of ethnicity. Kérékou's regime initially included officers from both the north and south of the country, but as the years passed the northerners (like Kérékou himself) became clearly dominant, undermining the idea that the regime was not based in ethnicity.<sup id="cite_ref-Decalo_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Decalo-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> By officially adopting Marxism-Leninism, Kérékou may also have wanted to win the support of the country's leftists.<sup id="cite_ref-Allen_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Allen-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Kérékou's regime was rigid and vigorous in pursuing its newly adopted ideological goals from the mid-1970s to the late 1970s. Beginning in the late 1970s, the regime jettisoned much of its radicalism and settled onto a more moderately socialist course as Kérékou consolidated his personal control.<sup id="cite_ref-Myth_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Myth-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Kérékou survived numerous attempts to oust him, including an invasion of the port city of <a href="/wiki/Cotonou" title="Cotonou">Cotonou</a> by mercenaries contracted by a group of exiled Beninese political rivals in January 1977,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> as well as two coup attempts in 1988.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>It was hoped that the nationalizations of the 1970s would help develop the economy, but it remained in a very poor condition, with the state sector being plagued by inefficiency and corruption. Kérékou began reversing course in the early 1980s, closing down numerous state-run companies and attempting to attract foreign investment.<sup id="cite_ref-Decalo_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Decalo-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> He also accepted an <a href="/wiki/International_Monetary_Fund" title="International Monetary Fund">IMF</a> structural readjustment programme in 1989, agreeing to austerity measures that severely cut state expenditure.<sup id="cite_ref-Decalo_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Decalo-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Allen_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Allen-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> The economic situation continued to worsen during the 1980s, provoking widespread unrest in 1989. A student strike began in January of that year; subsequently strikes among various elements of society increased in frequency and the nature of their demands grew broader: whereas initially they had focused on economic issues such as salary arrears, this progressed to include demands for political reform.<sup id="cite_ref-Allen_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Allen-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transition_to_democracy">Transition to democracy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mathieu_K%C3%A9r%C3%A9kou&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Transition to democracy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the period of reforms towards multiparty democracy in Africa at the beginning of the 1990s, Benin moved onto this path early, with Kérékou being forced to make concessions to popular discontent. Benin's early and relatively smooth transition may be attributed to the particularly dismal economic situation in the country, which seemed to preclude any alternative.<sup id="cite_ref-Decalo_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Decalo-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> In the midst of increasing unrest, Kérékou was re-elected as president by the National Assembly in August 1989,<sup id="cite_ref-Onadipe_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Onadipe-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> but in December 1989 Marxism-Leninism was dropped as the state ideology,<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> and a national conference was held in February 1990. The conference turned out to be hostile to Kérékou and declared its own sovereignty; despite the objections of some of his officers to this turn of events, Kérékou did not act against the conference,<sup id="cite_ref-Decalo_4-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Decalo-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> although he labelled the conference's declaration of sovereignty a "civilian coup". During the transition that followed, Kérékou remained president but lost most of his power.<sup id="cite_ref-Allen_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Allen-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>During the 1990 National Conference, which was nationally televised, Kérékou spoke to the <a href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_Cotonou" title="Archbishop of Cotonou" class="mw-redirect">Archbishop of Cotonou</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Isidor_de_Souza&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Isidor de Souza (page does not exist)">Isidor de Souza</a>, confessing guilt and begging forgiveness for the flaws of his regime. An observer described it as a "remarkable piece of political theater", full of cultural symbolism and significance; in effect, Kérékou was seeking forgiveness from his people. Such a gesture, so unusual for the African autocrats of the time, could have fatally weakened Kérékou's political standing, but he performed the gesture in such a way that, far from ending his political career, it instead served to symbolically redeem him and facilitate his political rehabilitation, while also "securing him immunity from prosecution". Kérékou shrewdly utilized the timing and setting: "Culturally as well as theologically it was impossible to refuse forgiveness on these terms."<sup id="cite_ref-Myth_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Myth-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/World_Bank" title="World Bank">World Bank</a> economist <a href="/wiki/Nic%C3%A9phore_Soglo" title="Nicéphore Soglo">Nicéphore Soglo</a>, chosen as prime minister by the conference, took office in March, and a new constitution was approved in a December 1990 referendum. Multi-party elections were held in March 1991, which Kérékou lost, obtaining only about 32% of the vote in the second round against Prime Minister Soglo;<sup id="cite_ref-Elections_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Elections-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> while he won very large vote percentages in the north, in the rest of the country he found little support.<sup id="cite_ref-Decalo_4-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Decalo-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Kérékou was thus the first mainland African president to lose power through a popular election.<sup id="cite_ref-Decalo_4-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Decalo-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> He apologized for "deplorable and regrettable incidents" that occurred during his rule.<sup id="cite_ref-Onadipe_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Onadipe-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>After losing the election in March 1991, Kérékou left the political scene and "withdrew to total silence", another move that was interpreted as penitential.<sup id="cite_ref-Myth_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Myth-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Election_as_President.2C_1996">Election as President, 1996</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mathieu_K%C3%A9r%C3%A9kou&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Election as President, 1996">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kérékou reclaimed the presidency in the March 1996 election. Soglo's economic reforms and his alleged dictatorial tendencies had caused his popularity to suffer.<sup id="cite_ref-Onadipe_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Onadipe-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Although Kérékou received fewer votes than Soglo in the first round, he then defeated Soglo in the second round, taking 52.5% of the vote.<sup id="cite_ref-Amuwo_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Amuwo-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Elections_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Elections-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> Kérékou was backed in the second round by third place candidate <a href="/wiki/Adrien_Houngb%C3%A9dji" title="Adrien Houngbédji">Adrien Houngbédji</a> and fourth place candidate <a href="/wiki/Bruno_Amoussou" title="Bruno Amoussou">Bruno Amoussou</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-Amuwo_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Amuwo-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> as in 1991, Kérékou received very strong support from northern voters,<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> but he also improved his performance in the south.<sup id="cite_ref-Amuwo_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Amuwo-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Soglo alleged fraud, but this was rejected by the Constitutional Court, which confirmed Kérékou's victory.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> When taking the oath of office, Kérékou left out a portion that referred to the "spirits of the ancestors" because he had become a born-again Christian after his defeat by Soglo. He was subsequently forced to retake the oath including the reference to spirits.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Disputed_re-election.2C_2001">Disputed re-election, 2001</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mathieu_K%C3%A9r%C3%A9kou&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Disputed re-election, 2001">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Mathieu_K%C3%A9r%C3%A9kou_2006Feb10.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Mathieu_K%C3%A9r%C3%A9kou_2006Feb10.JPG/220px-Mathieu_K%C3%A9r%C3%A9kou_2006Feb10.JPG" width="220" height="232" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Mathieu_K%C3%A9r%C3%A9kou_2006Feb10.JPG/330px-Mathieu_K%C3%A9r%C3%A9kou_2006Feb10.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Mathieu_K%C3%A9r%C3%A9kou_2006Feb10.JPG/440px-Mathieu_K%C3%A9r%C3%A9kou_2006Feb10.JPG 2x" data-file-width="924" data-file-height="974" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Mathieu_K%C3%A9r%C3%A9kou_2006Feb10.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Mathieu Kérékou in 2006</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Kérékou was re-elected for a second five-year term in the <a href="/wiki/Benin_presidential_election,_2001" title="Benin presidential election, 2001" class="mw-redirect">March 2001 presidential election</a> under controversial circumstances. In the first round he took 45.4% of the vote; Soglo, who took second place, and parliament speaker Houngbédji, who took third, both refused to participate in the second round, alleging fraud and saying that they did not want to legitimize the vote by participating in it. This left the fourth place finisher, Amoussou, to face Kérékou in the run-off, and Kérékou easily won with 83.6% of the vote.<sup id="cite_ref-Elections_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Elections-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> It was subsequently discovered that the American corporation <a href="/wiki/Titan_Corporation" title="Titan Corporation">Titan</a> gave more than two million dollars to Kérékou's re-election campaign as a bribe.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>During Kérékou's second period in office his government followed a liberal economic path. The period also saw Benin take part in international peacekeeping missions in other African states.<sup id="cite_ref-irin_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-irin-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Kérékou was barred from running again in 2006 on two counts. The constitution not only limited the president to two terms, but also required that presidential candidates be younger than 70 (he turned 70 in 2003, through his second term). Kérékou said in July 2005 that he would not attempt to amend the constitution to allow him to run for a third term. "If you don't leave power," he said, "power will leave you."<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> There was, however, speculation that he had wanted it to be changed, but faced too much opposition.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>On 5 March 2006, <a href="/wiki/Benin_presidential_election,_2006" title="Benin presidential election, 2006" class="mw-redirect">voters went to the polls</a> to decide who would succeed Kérékou as President of Benin. <a href="/wiki/Yayi_Boni" title="Yayi Boni" class="mw-redirect">Yayi Boni</a> defeated Adrien Houngbédji in a run-off vote on 19 March,<sup id="cite_ref-Elections_13-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Elections-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> and Kérékou left office at the end of his term, at midnight on 6 April 2006.<sup id="cite_ref-irin_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-irin-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion_and_symbolism">Religion and symbolism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mathieu_K%C3%A9r%C3%A9kou&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Religion and symbolism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kérékou allegedly converted to <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a> in 1980 while on a visit to Libya, and changed his first name to Ahmed,<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> but he later returned to the use of the name Mathieu. This alleged conversion may have been designed to please the Libyan leader <a href="/wiki/Muammar_Gaddafi" title="Muammar Gaddafi">Muammar Gaddafi</a> and obtain financial and military support.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> Alternatively, the conversion story may have been a rumor planted by some of his opponents in order to destabilize his regime. He subsequently became a <a href="/wiki/Born-again_Christian" title="Born-again Christian" class="mw-redirect">born-again Christian</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-magical_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-magical-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> Some <a href="/wiki/West_African_Vodun" title="West African Vodun">Vodun</a> believers in Benin regarded him as having magical powers, explaining his ability to survive repeated coup attempts during his military rule.<sup id="cite_ref-magical_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-magical-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Nicknamed "the chameleon" from an early point in his career,<sup id="cite_ref-Business_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Business-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> Kérékou's motto was "the branch will not break in the arms of the chameleon".<sup id="cite_ref-Pouvoir_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pouvoir-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Myth_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Myth-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> The nickname and motto he adopted were full of cultural symbolism, articulating and projecting his power and ability. Unlike some past rulers who had adopted animal symbolism intending to project a violent, warlike sense of power, Kérékou's symbolic animal suggested skill and cleverness; his motto suggested that he would keep the branch from breaking, but implicitly warned of what could happen to "the branch" if it was not "in the arms of the chameleon"—political chaos.<sup id="cite_ref-Myth_8-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Myth-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> To some, his nickname seemed particularly apt as he successfully adapted himself to a new political climate and neoliberal economic policies in the 1990s.<sup id="cite_ref-Business_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Business-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>He used the campaign slogan, "Experience in the service of youth."<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Retirement_and_death">Retirement and death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mathieu_K%C3%A9r%C3%A9kou&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Retirement and death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After leaving office in 2006, Kérékou stayed out of politics and spent time at his homes in Cotonou and <a href="/wiki/Natitingou" title="Natitingou">Natitingou</a> in northwestern Benin, his native region. He suffered a health crisis in 2014 and was taken to Paris for treatment. Although he recovered, he continued to suffer health problems, and he died in Benin on 14 October 2015 at the age of 82.<sup id="cite_ref-RFI_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RFI-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> His death was announced in a statement by President <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Boni_Yayi" title="Thomas Boni Yayi">Thomas Boni Yayi</a>. No cause of death was stated.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> A week of national mourning was declared.<sup id="cite_ref-RFI_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RFI-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
