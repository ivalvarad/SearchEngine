<p><b>Nagaland</b> <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span><span title="/ɑː/ 'a' in 'father'">ɑː</span><span title="'g' in 'guy'">ɡ</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/æ/ short 'a' in 'bad'">æ</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span></span>/</a></span></span> is a state in <a href="/wiki/Northeast_India" title="Northeast India">Northeast India</a>. It borders the state of <a href="/wiki/Assam" title="Assam">Assam</a> to the west, <a href="/wiki/Arunachal_Pradesh" title="Arunachal Pradesh">Arunachal Pradesh</a> and part of <a href="/wiki/Assam" title="Assam">Assam</a> to the north, <a href="/wiki/Burma" title="Burma" class="mw-redirect">Burma</a> to the east and <a href="/wiki/Manipur" title="Manipur">Manipur</a> to the south. The state capital is <a href="/wiki/Kohima" title="Kohima">Kohima</a>, and the largest city is <a href="/wiki/Dimapur" title="Dimapur">Dimapur</a>. It has an area of 16,579 square kilometres (6,401&#160;sq&#160;mi) with a population of 1,980,602 per the <a href="/wiki/2011_Census_of_India" title="2011 Census of India">2011 Census of India</a>, making it one of the smallest states of India.<sup id="cite_ref-coiprov_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-coiprov-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The state is inhabited by 16 major tribes - <a href="/wiki/Ao_Naga" title="Ao Naga">Ao</a>, <a href="/wiki/Angami_Naga" title="Angami Naga">Angami</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chang_Naga" title="Chang Naga">Chang</a>, <a href="/wiki/Konyak_people" title="Konyak people">Konyak</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lotha_Naga" title="Lotha Naga">Lotha</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sumi_Naga" title="Sumi Naga">Sumi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chakhesang_Naga" title="Chakhesang Naga">Chakhesang</a>, <a href="/wiki/Khiamniungan_people" title="Khiamniungan people">Khiamniungan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dimasa_people" title="Dimasa people">Dimasa Kachari</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phom_Naga" title="Phom Naga">Phom</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rengma_Naga" title="Rengma Naga">Rengma</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sangtam_Naga" title="Sangtam Naga">Sangtam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yimchunger" title="Yimchunger">Yimchunger</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thadou_people" title="Thadou people">Thadou</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kuki_people" title="Kuki people">Kuki</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zeme_Naga" title="Zeme Naga">Zeme</a>-<a href="/wiki/Liangmai_Naga" title="Liangmai Naga">Liangmai</a> (Zeliang) and <a href="/wiki/Pochury_Naga" title="Pochury Naga">Pochury</a> as well as <a href="/wiki/List_of_Naga_tribes" title="List of Naga tribes">a number of sub-tribes</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-pn_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pn-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Each tribe is unique in character with its own distinct customs, language and dress.<sup id="cite_ref-undp_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-undp-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Two threads common to all, are language and religion - English is in predominant use. Nagaland is one of three states in India where the population is mostly <a href="/wiki/Christian" title="Christian">Christian</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-censusreligion_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-censusreligion-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Nagaland became the 16th state of <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> on 1 December 1963. Agriculture is the most important economic activity and the principal crops include rice, corn, <a href="/wiki/Millet" title="Millet">millets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pulse_(legume)" title="Pulse (legume)">pulses</a>, tobacco, <a href="/wiki/Oilseeds" title="Oilseeds" class="mw-redirect">oilseeds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sugarcane" title="Sugarcane">sugarcane</a>, potatoes, and <a href="/wiki/Fiber" title="Fiber">fibers</a>. Other significant economic activity includes <a href="/wiki/Forestry" title="Forestry">forestry</a>, tourism, insurance, real estate, and miscellaneous <a href="/wiki/Putting-out_system#Cottage_industry" title="Putting-out system">cottage industries</a>. The state has experienced insurgency as well as inter-ethnic conflict since the 1950s. The violence and insecurity have long limited Nagaland's economic development, because it had to commit its scarce resources on law, order and security.<sup id="cite_ref-cc1_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cc1-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-cc2_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cc2-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> In the last 15 years, the state has seen less violence and annual economic growth rates nearing 10% on a compounded basis, one of the fastest in the region.<sup id="cite_ref-ibef_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ibef-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The state is mostly mountainous except those areas bordering Assam valley. <a href="/wiki/Mount_Saramati" title="Mount Saramati">Mount Saramati</a> is the highest peak with a height of 3,840 metres and its range forms a natural barrier between Nagaland and Burma.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> It lies between the parallels of 98-degrees and 96-degrees east longitude and 26.6-degrees and 27.4-degrees latitude north of the equator. The state is home to a rich variety of flora and fauna; it has been suggested as the "falcon capital of the world".<sup id="cite_ref-amur_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-amur-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nagaland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Naga_people" title="Naga people">Naga people</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Angami_Naga_1875.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Angami_Naga_1875.JPG/200px-Angami_Naga_1875.JPG" width="200" height="280" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Angami_Naga_1875.JPG/300px-Angami_Naga_1875.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Angami_Naga_1875.JPG/400px-Angami_Naga_1875.JPG 2x" data-file-width="894" data-file-height="1251" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Angami_Naga_1875.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A sketch of Angami Naga tribesman from 1875.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The ancient history of the Nagas is unclear. Some anthropologists suggest Nagas belong to the <a href="/wiki/Mongoloid_race" title="Mongoloid race" class="mw-redirect">Mongoloid race</a>, and different tribes migrated at different times, each settling in the north-eastern part of present India and establishing their respective sovereign mountain terrains and village-states. There are no records of whether they came from the northern Mongolian region, southeast Asia or southwest China, except that their origins are from the east of India and that historic records show the present day Naga people settled before the arrival of the <a href="/wiki/Ahom_kingdom" title="Ahom kingdom">Ahoms</a> in 1228 AD.<sup id="cite_ref-undp_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-undp-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-cc1_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cc1-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The origin of the word ‘Naga' is also sketchy.<sup id="cite_ref-undp_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-undp-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> A popularly accepted, but controversial view is that it originated from the Burmese word ‘Naka’, meaning people with earrings. Others suggest it means pierced noses.<sup id="cite_ref-Inato_cultural_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Inato_cultural-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Before the arrival of European colonialism in South Asia, there had been many wars, persecution and raids from <a href="/wiki/Burma" title="Burma" class="mw-redirect">Burma</a> on Naga tribes, <a href="/wiki/Meitei_people" title="Meitei people">Meitei people</a> and others in India's northeast. The invaders came for "head hunting" and to seek wealth and captives from these tribes and ethnic groups. When the British inquired Burmese guides about the people living in northern Himalayas, they were told ‘Naka’. This was recorded as ‘Naga’, and has been in use thereafter.<sup id="cite_ref-undp_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-undp-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-cc2_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cc2-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>With the arrival of <a href="/wiki/British_East_India_Company" title="British East India Company" class="mw-redirect">British East India Company</a> in the early 19th century, followed by the <a href="/wiki/British_Raj" title="British Raj">British Raj</a>, Britain expanded its domain over entire South Asia including the Naga Hills. The first Europeans to enter the hills were Captains Jenkins and Pemberton in 1832. The early contact with the Naga tribes were of suspicion and conflict. The colonial interests in Assam, such as tea estates and other trading posts suffered from raids from tribes who were known for their bravery and "head hunting" practices. To put an end to these raids, the British troops recorded 10 military expeditions between 1839 and 1850.<sup id="cite_ref-undp_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-undp-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> In February 1851, at the bloody battle at Kikrüma, numerous people died both on the British side as well as the Kikrüma Naga tribe side; in days after the battle, intertribal warfare followed that led to more bloodshed. After that war, the British first adopted a policy of respect and non-interference with Naga tribes. This policy failed. Over 1851 to 1865, Naga tribes continued to raid the British in Assam. The British India Government, fresh from the shocks of <a href="/wiki/Indian_Rebellion_of_1857" title="Indian Rebellion of 1857">1857 Indian rebellion</a>, reviewed its governance structure throughout South Asia including its northeastern region. In 1866, the British India administration reached the historic step in Nagaland's modern history, by establishing a post at Samaguting with the explicit goal of ending intertribal warfare and tribal raids on property and personnel.<sup id="cite_ref-cc1_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cc1-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-cc2_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cc2-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> In 1869, Captain Butler was appointed to lead and consolidate the British presence in the Nagaland hills. In 1878, the headquarters were transferred to Kohima - creating a city that remains to this day an important center of administration, commerce and culture for Nagaland.<sup id="cite_ref-undp_3-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-undp-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>On 4 October 1879, GH Damant (M.A.C.S), a British political agent, went to Khonoma with some troops, where he was shot dead along with 35 of his team.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> Kohima was next attacked and the stockade looted. This violence led to a determined effort by the British Raj, to return and respond. The subsequent defeat of Khonoma marked the end of serious and persistent hostility in the Naga Hills.<sup id="cite_ref-undp_3-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-undp-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Between 1880 and 1922, the British administration consolidated their position over a large area of the Naga Hills and integrated it into its Assam operations. The British administration enforced the Rupee as the currency for economic activity and a system of structured tribal government that was very different than historic social governance practices.<sup id="cite_ref-cc1_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cc1-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> These developments triggered profound social changes among the Naga people.</p>

<p>In parallel, since mid 19th century, Christian missionaries from the United States and Europe, stationed in India,<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1974_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1974-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> reached out into Nagaland and neighboring states, playing their role in converting Nagaland's Naga tribes from <a href="/wiki/Animism" title="Animism">Animism</a> to Christianity.<sup id="cite_ref-cc1_6-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cc1-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="20th_century">20th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nagaland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 20th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Kohima" title="Battle of Kohima">Battle of Kohima</a></div>
<p>In 1944, the Japanese, invading through Burma, attempted to thrust into India through Kohima. The population was evacuated. British and Indian soldiers <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Kohima" title="Battle of Kohima">defended the area of Kohima</a> and were relieved by British and Punjabis in June 1944, having lost many of their original force. The Japanese lost half their numbers, many through starvation, and were forced to withdraw through Burma.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Road_to_statehood">Road to statehood</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nagaland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Road to statehood">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1929, a Memorandum was submitted to the Simon Statutory Commission, requesting that the Nagas be exempt from reforms and new taxes proposed in British India, should be left alone to determine their own future.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> This Naga Memorandum stated,</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Before the British Government conquered our country in 1879-80, we were living in a state of intermittent warfare with the Assamese of the Assam valley to the North and West of our country and Manipuris to the South. They never conquered us nor were we subjected to their rules. On the other hand, we were always a terror to these people. Our country within the administered area consists of more than eight regions quite different from one another, with quite different languages which cannot be understood by each other, and there are more regions outside the administered area which are not known at present. We have no unity among us and it is only the British Government that is holding us together now. Our education is poor. (...) Our country is poor and it does not pay for any administration. Therefore if it is continued to be placed under Reformed Scheme, we are afraid new and heavy taxes will have to be imposed on us, and when we cannot pay, then all lands have to be sold and in long run we shall have no share in the land of our birth and life will not be worth living then. Though our land at present is within the British territory, Government have always recognized our private rights in it, but if we are forced to enter the council the majority of whose number is sure to belong to other districts, we also have much fear the introduction of foreign laws and customs to supersede our own customary laws which we now enjoy.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— Naga Memorandum to Simon Commission, British India, 1929<sup id="cite_ref-sks_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sks-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>From 1929 to 1935, the understanding of sovereignty by Nagas was ‘self-rule’ based on traditional territorial definition. From 1935 to 1945, Nagas were merely asking for autonomy within Assam. In response to the Naga memorandum to Simon Commission, the British House of Commons decreed that the Naga Hills ought to be kept outside the purview of the New Constitution; the Government of India Act, 1935 and ordered Naga areas as Excluded Area; meaning outside the administration of British India government. Thereafter from April 1, 1937, it was brought under the direct administration of the Crown through Her Majesty’s representative; the Governor of Assam province.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The Naga Memorandum submitted by the Naga Club (which later became the Naga National Council) to the Simon Commission explicitly stated, 'to leave us alone to determine ourselves as in ancient times.'<sup id="cite_ref-sks_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sks-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> In February 1946, the Naga Club officially took shape into a unified Naga National Council in Wokha. In June 1946, the Naga National Council submitted a four-point memorandum to officials discussing the independence of India from British colonial rule. The memorandum strongly protested against the grouping of Assam with Bengal and asserted that Naga Hills should be constitutionally included in an autonomous Assam, in a free India, with local autonomy, due safeguards and separate electorate for the Naga tribes.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Jawaharlal_Nehru" title="Jawaharlal Nehru">Jawaharlal Nehru</a> replied to the memorandum and welcomed the Nagas to join the Union of India promising local autonomy and safeguards. On April 9, 1946, the Naga National Council (NNC) submitted a memorandum to the British Cabinet Mission during its visit to Delhi. The crux of the memorandum stated that: “Naga future would not be bound by any arbitrary decision of the British Government and no recommendation would be accepted without consultation”.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In June 1946, the NNC submitted a four-point memorandum signed by T. Sakhrie; the then Secretary of NNC, to the still-visiting British Cabinet Mission. The memorandum stated that:<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> 1. The NNC stands for the solidarity of all Naga tribes, including those in un-administered areas; 2. The Council protests against the grouping of Assam with Bengal; 3. The Naga Hills should be constitutionally included in an autonomous Assam, in a free India, with local autonomy and due safeguards for the interests of the Nagas; 4. The Naga tribes should have a separate electorate.</p>
<p>On August 1, 1946, Nehru, President of the <a href="/wiki/Indian_National_Congress_Party" title="Indian National Congress Party" class="mw-redirect">Indian National Congress Party</a> in his reply to the memorandum, appealed to the Nagas to join the Union of India promising local autonomy and safeguards in a wide ranging areas of administration.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> It was after 1946 only that the Nagas had asserted their inalienable right to be a separate nation and an absolute right to live independently.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>After the <a href="/wiki/Indian_independence_movement" title="Indian independence movement">independence of India</a> in 1947, the area remained a part of the province of Assam. Nationalist activities arose amongst a section of the Nagas. <a href="/wiki/Angami_Zapu_Phizo" title="Angami Zapu Phizo">Phizo</a>-led <a href="/wiki/Naga_National_Council" title="Naga National Council">Naga National Council</a> and demanded a political union of their ancestral and native groups. The movement led to a series of violent incidents, that damaged government and civil infrastructure, attacked government officials and civilians. The union government sent the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Army" title="Indian Army">Indian Army</a> in 1955, to restore order. In 1957, an agreement was reached between Naga leaders and the Indian government, creating a single separate region of the Naga Hills. The Tuensang frontier was united with this single political region, Naga Hills Tuensang Area (NHTA),<sup id="cite_ref-Nagaland_Legislative_Assembly_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nagaland_Legislative_Assembly-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> and it became a <a href="/wiki/Union_territory" title="Union territory">Union territory</a> directly administered by the Central government with a large degree of autonomy. This was not satisfactory to the tribes, however, and agitation with violence increased across the state – including attacks on army and government institutions, banks, as well as non-payment of taxes. In July 1960, following discussion between <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_India" title="Prime Minister of India">Prime Minister</a> Nehru and the leaders of the Naga People Convention (NPC), a 16-point agreement was arrived at whereby the Government of India recognised the formation of Nagaland as a full-fledged state within the Union of India.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Accordingly, the territory was placed under the Nagaland Transitional Provisions Regulation, 1961<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> which provided for an Interim body consisting of 45 members to be elected by various tribes according to the customs, traditions and usage of the respective tribes. Subsequently, Nagaland attained statehood with the enactment of the state of Nagaland Act in 1962<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> by the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_India" title="Parliament of India" class="mw-redirect">Parliament</a>. The interim body was dissolved on 30 November 1963 and the state of Nagaland was formally inaugurated on 1 December 1963 and <a href="/wiki/Kohima" title="Kohima">Kohima</a> was declared as the state capital. After elections in January 1964, the first democratically elected <a href="/wiki/Nagaland_Legislative_Assembly" title="Nagaland Legislative Assembly">Nagaland Legislative Assembly</a> was constituted on 11 February 1964.<sup id="cite_ref-Nagaland_Legislative_Assembly_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nagaland_Legislative_Assembly-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The rebel activity continued, in the form of banditry and attacks, motivated more by tribal rivalry and personal vendetta than by political aspiration. Cease-fires were announced and negotiations continued, but this did little to stop the violence. In March 1975, direct presidential rule was imposed by the then Prime Minister Indira Gandhi on the state. In November 1975, the leaders of largest rebellion groups agreed to lay down their arms and accept the Indian constitution, a small group did not agree and continued their insurgent activity.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> The Nagaland Baptist Church Council played an important role by initiating peace efforts in 1960s.<sup id="cite_ref-undp_3-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-undp-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> This took concrete and positive shape during its Convention in early 1964. It formed the Nagaland Peace Council in 1972. However, these efforts have not completely ended the inter-factional violence. In 2012, the state's leaders approached Indian central government to seek a political means for a lasting peace within the state.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Over the 5-year period of 2009 to 2013, between 0 to 11 civilians died per year in Nagaland from rebellion related activity (or less than 1 death per 100,000 people), and between 3 to 55 militants deaths per year in inter-factional killings (or between 0 and 3 deaths per 100,000 people).<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> The world's average annual death rate from intentional violence, in recent years, has been 7.9 per 100,000 people.<sup id="cite_ref-gede_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gede-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> The most recent Nagaland Legislative Assembly election took place on 23 February 2013 to elect the Members of the Legislative Assembly (MLA) from each of the 60 Assembly Constituencies in the state. Nagaland People's Front was elected to power with 37 seats.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Battle_of_Kohima">Battle of Kohima</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nagaland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Battle of Kohima">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Tennis_Court" title="Battle of the Tennis Court">Battle of the Tennis Court</a></div>
<p>In 1944 during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, along with <a href="/wiki/Manipur" title="Manipur">Manipur</a>, British and Indian troops in Nagaland successfully repelled Japanese troops in <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Kohima" title="Battle of Kohima">Battle of Kohima</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> The battle was fought from 4 April to 22 June 1944 from the town of Kohima, coordinated with action from Imphal, Manipur.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>There is the World War II Cemetery, and the War Museum, in honour of those who lost their lives during World War II during the fighting between British Empire and Japanese troops. Nearly 4,000 British Empire troops lost their lives, along with 3,000 Japanese. Many of those who lost their lives were Naga people, particularly of Angami tribe. Near the memorial is the <a href="/wiki/Mary_Help_of_Christians_Cathedral" title="Mary Help of Christians Cathedral">Kohima Cathedral</a>, on Aradura hill, built with funds from the families and friends of deceased Japanese soldiers. Prayers are held in Kohima for peace and in memory of the fallen of both sides of the battle.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_rituals">Historical rituals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nagaland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Historical rituals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Historically, Naga tribes celebrated two main rituals. These were feasting and head hunting.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Head_hunting">Head hunting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nagaland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Head hunting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Head hunting, a male activity, would involve separating men from their women before, during and after coming back from an expedition. The women, as a cultural practice, would encourage men to undertake head-hunting as a prerequisite to marriage. The men would go on an expedition against other tribes or neighboring kingdoms, and kill to score number of heads they were able to hunt. A successful head hunter would be conferred a right to ornaments. The practice of head hunting was banned in 19th century India, and is no longer practiced among Naga people.<sup id="cite_ref-undp_3-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-undp-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Feasts_of_Merit">Feasts of Merit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nagaland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Feasts of Merit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The Feasts of Merit reflected the splendor and celebration of Naga life.<sup id="cite_ref-cc1_6-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cc1-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Only married men could give such Feasts, and his wife took a prominent and honored place during the ritual which emphasized male-female cooperation and interdependence. His wife brewed the beer which he offered to the guests. The event displayed various ceremonies and festivities organized by the sponsor. The Feast given by a wealthier tribes person would be more extravagant.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup> He would typically invite everyone from the tribe. This event bestowed honor to the couple from the tribe. After the Feast, the tribe would give the man and his wife rights to ornaments equally.<sup id="cite_ref-cc1_6-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cc1-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nagaland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Nagaland is largely a mountainous state. The <a href="/wiki/Naga_Hills" title="Naga Hills">Naga Hills</a> rise from the <a href="/wiki/Brahmaputra_Valley" title="Brahmaputra Valley">Brahmaputra Valley</a> in Assam to about 2,000 feet (610&#160;m) and rise further to the southeast, as high as 6,000 feet (1,800&#160;m). Mount Saramati at an elevation of 12,601.70 feet (3,841.00&#160;m) is the state's highest peak; this is where the Naga Hills merge with the <a href="/wiki/Patkai_Range" title="Patkai Range" class="mw-redirect">Patkai Range</a> in which form the boundary with Burma. Rivers such as the Doyang and Diphu to the north, the Barak river in the southwest, dissect the entire state. 20 percent of the total land area of the state is covered with wooded forest, a haven for flora and fauna. The evergreen tropical and the sub tropical forests are found in strategic pockets in the state.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nagaland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Nagaland has a largely monsoon climate with high humidity levels. Annual rainfall averages around 70–100 inches (1,800–2,500&#160;mm), concentrated in the months of May to September. Temperatures range from 70&#160;°F (21&#160;°C) to 104&#160;°F (40&#160;°C). In winter, temperatures do not generally drop below 39&#160;°F (4&#160;°C), but frost is common at high elevations. The state enjoys a salubrious climate. Summer is the shortest season in the state that lasts for only a few months. The temperature during the summer season remains between 16&#160;°C (61&#160;°F) to 31&#160;°C (88&#160;°F). Winter makes an early arrival and bitter cold and dry weather strikes certain regions of the state. The maximum average temperature recorded in the winter season is 24&#160;°C (75&#160;°F). Strong north-west winds blow across the state during the months of February and March.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Flora_and_fauna">Flora and fauna</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nagaland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Flora and fauna">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

About a million <a href="/wiki/Amur_falcon" title="Amur falcon">Amur falcons</a> roost in Nagaland.<sup id="cite_ref-amur_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-amur-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> That is about 50 falcons per square kilometer.</div>

<p>About one-sixth of Nagaland is covered by tropical and sub-tropical evergreen forests—including palms, bamboo, rattan as well as timber and <a href="/wiki/Mahogany" title="Mahogany">mahogany</a> forests. While some forest areas have been cleared for <a href="/wiki/Jhum_cultivation" title="Jhum cultivation" class="mw-redirect">jhum cultivation</a>, many scrub forests, high grass, reeds; secondary dogs, pangolins, porcupines, elephants, leopards, bears, many species of monkeys, <a href="/wiki/Sambar_deer" title="Sambar deer">sambar</a>, harts, oxen, and buffaloes thrive across the state's forests. The <a href="/wiki/Great_Indian_hornbill" title="Great Indian hornbill" class="mw-redirect">great Indian hornbill</a> is one of the most famous birds found in the state. <a href="/wiki/Tragopan_blythii" title="Tragopan blythii" class="mw-redirect">Blyth's tragopan</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Vulnerable_species" title="Vulnerable species">vulnerable species</a> of pheasant, is the state bird of Nagaland. It is sighted in <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mount_Japf%C3%BC&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Mount Japfü (page does not exist)">Mount Japfü</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dz%C3%BCkou_Valley" title="Dzükou Valley">Dzükou Valley</a> of Kohima district, Satoi range in Zunheboto district and Pfütsero in Phek district. Of the mere 2500 tragopans sighted in the world, Dzükou valley is the natural habitat of more than 1,000.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Gayal" title="Gayal">Mithun</a> (a semi domesticated <a href="/wiki/Gaur" title="Gaur">gaur</a>) found only in the north-eastern states of India, is the state animal of Nagaland and has been adopted as the official seal of the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Nagaland" title="Government of Nagaland">Government of Nagaland</a>. It is ritually the most valued species in the state. To conserve and protect this animal in the northeast, the National Research Centre on Mithun (NRCM) was established by the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Council_of_Agricultural_Research" title="Indian Council of Agricultural Research">Indian Council of Agricultural Research</a> (ICAR) in 1988.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Geology">Geology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nagaland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Geology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Several preliminary studies indicate significant recoverable reserves of petroleum and natural gas.<sup id="cite_ref-pn_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pn-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><br /></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Demographics">Demographics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nagaland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Demographics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The population of Nagaland is nearly two million people, of which 1.04 million are males and 0.95 million females.<sup id="cite_ref-undp_3-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-undp-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Among its various districts, Dimapur has the largest population (379769), followed by Kohima (270063). The least populated district is Longleng (50593). 75% of the population lives in the rural areas. As of 2013, about 10% of rural population is below the poverty line; among the people living in urban areas 4.3% of them are below the poverty line.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p><br />
Nagaland has more language diversity than any other state in India<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. Naga people speak over 36 different languages and dialects, mostly unintelligible with each other<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. Per Grierson's classification system, Naga languages can be grouped into Western, Central and Eastern Naga Groups. The Western Group includes among others Angami, Chokri<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2001_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2001-46"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup> and Kheza. The Central Naga group includes Ao, Lotha and Sangtam, whereas Eastern Group includes Konyak and Chang.<sup id="cite_ref-bbk_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbk-47"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup> In addition, there are Naga-Bodo group illustrated by Mikir language, and Kuki group of languages illustrated by Sopvama (also called Mao Naga) and Luppa languages. These belong mostly<sup id="cite_ref-cc1_6-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cc1-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> to the <a href="/wiki/Sino-Tibetan_languages" title="Sino-Tibetan languages">Sino-Tibetan language family</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup> Shafer came up with his own classification system for languages found in and around Nagaland.<sup id="cite_ref-bbk_47-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbk-47"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup> Each tribe has one or more dialects that are unintelligible to others.</p>
<p>In 1967, the Nagaland Assembly proclaimed English as the official language of Nagaland and it is the medium for education in Nagaland. Other than English, <a href="/wiki/Nagamese" title="Nagamese" class="mw-redirect">Nagamese</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Creole_language" title="Creole language">creole language</a> form of Indo-Aryan <a href="/wiki/Assamese_language" title="Assamese language">Assamese</a>, is a widely spoken language.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup> Every tribe has its own <a href="/wiki/Mother_tongue" title="Mother tongue" class="mw-redirect">mother tongue</a> but communicates with other tribes in Nagamese or English. However, English is the predominant spoken and written language in Nagaland.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The major languages spoken as per 2001 census are Ao (257,500), Konyak (248,002), Lotha (168,356), Angami (131,737), Phom (122,454), Yimchungre (92,092), Sema (92,884), Sangtam (84,150), Chakru (83,506), Chang (62,347), Zeliang (61,492), Rengma (58,590), Kheza (40,362), Khiemnungan (37,752), Chakhesang (9,544), Assamese (16,183), Bengali (58,890), Hindi (56,981) etc.</p>

<p>The state's population is 1.978 million, out of which 87.92% are Christians.<sup id="cite_ref-Indian_Census_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Indian_Census-51"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-vj_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vj-52"><span>[</span>52<span>]</span></a></sup> The census of 2011 recorded the state's Christian population at 17,39,651, making it, with <a href="/wiki/Meghalaya" title="Meghalaya">Meghalaya</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mizoram" title="Mizoram">Mizoram</a> one of the three Christian-majority states in India. The state has a very high church attendance rate in both urban and rural areas. Huge churches dominate the skylines of Kohima, Dimapur, and Mokokchung.</p>
<p>Nagaland is known as "<i>the only predominantly Baptist state in the world</i>."<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53"><span>[</span>53<span>]</span></a></sup> Among Christians, <a href="/wiki/Baptists" title="Baptists">Baptists</a> have constituted more than 75% of the state's population, thus making it more Baptist (on a percentage basis) than <a href="/wiki/Mississippi" title="Mississippi">Mississippi</a> in the southern United States, where 55% of the population is Baptist, and <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a> which is 51% Baptist.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54"><span>[</span>54<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55"><span>[</span>55<span>]</span></a></sup> Roman Catholics, <a href="/wiki/Revivalism" title="Revivalism" class="mw-redirect">Revivalists</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pentecostalism" title="Pentecostalism">Pentecostals</a> are the other <a href="/wiki/Christian_denomination" title="Christian denomination">Christian denomination</a> numbers. Catholics are found in significant numbers in parts of Wokha district and Kohima district as also in the urban areas of Kohima and Dimapur.</p>
<p>Christianity arrived in Nagaland in early 19th century. The American Baptist Naga mission grew out of Assam mission in 1836. Miles Bronson, Nathan Brown and others in Christian missionaries working out of Jaipur, to bring Christianity into Indian subcontinent, saw the opportunity for gaining converts since India's northeast was principally Animist and folk religion driven. Along with other tribal regions of the northeast, Nagaland people accepted Christianity.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1974_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1974-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> However, the conversions have been marked by high rates of re-denomination ever since. After having converted to Christianity, people do not feel bound to any one sect, switch affiliation between a variety of denominations. According to a 2007 report,<sup id="cite_ref-vj_52-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vj-52"><span>[</span>52<span>]</span></a></sup> breakaway churches are constantly being established alongside older sects. These new Christian churches differ from older ones in terms of their liturgical traditions and methods of worship. The younger churches exhibit a more vocally explicit form of worship. The Constitution of India grants all citizens, including Nagaland people, a freedom to leave, change or adopt any religion and its new sects.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hinduism" title="Hinduism">Hinduism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a> are also found in Nagaland. They are minority religions in the state, at 8.75% and 2.47% of the population respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-Indian_Census_51-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Indian_Census-51"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>An ancient indigenous religion known as the Heraka is followed by 4,168 people belonging to the Zeliangrong tribe living in Nagaland.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56"><span>[</span>56<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Rani_Gaidinliu#Life_in_Independent_India" title="Rani Gaidinliu">Rani Gaidinliu</a> was a freedom fighter who struggled for the revival of the traditional Naga religion of animism or <i><a href="/wiki/Heraka" title="Heraka" class="mw-redirect">Heraka</a></i>. Today, 94% of the Kuki tribe people living in Nagaland are Christian.</p>

<p>The governor is the constitutional head of state, representative of the <a href="/wiki/President_of_India" title="President of India">President of India</a>. He possesses largely ceremonial responsibilities apart from law and order responsibilities.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Legislative_Assembly_of_Nagaland" title="Legislative Assembly of Nagaland" class="mw-redirect">Legislative Assembly of Nagaland</a> (<a href="/wiki/Vidhan_Sabha" title="Vidhan Sabha">Vidhan Sabha</a>) is the real executive and legislative body of the state. The 60-member Vidhan Sabha – all elected members of legislature – forms the government executive and is led by the <a href="/wiki/Chief_Minister_of_Nagaland" title="Chief Minister of Nagaland" class="mw-redirect">Chief minister</a>. Unlike most states in India, Nagaland has been granted a great degree of state autonomy, as well as special powers and autonomy for Naga tribes to conduct their own affairs. Each tribe has a hierarchy of councils at the village, range, and tribal levels dealing with local disputes.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Elections">Elections</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nagaland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Elections">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Alliance_of_Nagaland" title="Democratic Alliance of Nagaland">Democratic Alliance of Nagaland</a> (DAN) is a state level coalition of political parties. It headed the government with the <a href="/wiki/Bharatiya_Janata_Party" title="Bharatiya Janata Party">Bharatiya Janata Party</a> (BJP) and <a href="/wiki/Janata_Dal_(United)" title="Janata Dal (United)">Janata Dal (United)</a> (JDU). It was formed in 2003 after the Nagaland Legislative Assembly election, with the <a href="/wiki/Naga_People%27s_Front" title="Naga People's Front">Naga People's Front</a> (NPF), and the BJP.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57"><span>[</span>57<span>]</span></a></sup> The alliance has been in power in Nagaland since 2003.<sup id="cite_ref-India_Today_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-India_Today-58"><span>[</span>58<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nagaland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Gross State Domestic Product (GSDP) of Nagaland was about <span class="nowrap"><a href="/wiki/Indian_rupee" title="Indian rupee"><span title="INR">₹</span></a>12065 <a href="/wiki/Crore" title="Crore">crore</a></span> (US$1.8&#160;billion) in 2011-12.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59"><span>[</span>59<span>]</span></a></sup> Nagaland's GSDP grew at 9.9% compounded annually for a decade, thus more than doubling the per capita income.<sup id="cite_ref-ibef_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ibef-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Nagaland has a high literacy rate of 80.1 per cent. Majority of the population in the state speaks English, which is the official language of the state. The state offers technical and medical education.<sup id="cite_ref-ibef_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ibef-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> Nevertheless, agriculture and forestry contribute majority of Nagaland's Gross Domestic Product. The state is rich in mineral resources such as coal, limestone, iron, nickel, cobalt, chromium, and marble.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60"><span>[</span>60<span>]</span></a></sup> Nagaland has a recoverable reserve of limestone of 1,000 million tonnes plus a large untapped resource of marble and handicraft stone.</p>

<p>Most of state's population, about 68 per cent of the total, depends on rural cultivation. The main crops of the state are rice, millet, maize, and pulses. Cash crops, like sugarcane and potato, are also grown in some parts. Plantation crops such as premium coffee, cardamom, and tea are grown in hilly areas in small quantities, but a large growth potential. Most people cultivate rice as it is the main staple diet of the people. About 80% of the cropped area is dedicated to rice. Oilseeds is another, higher income crop gaining ground in Nagaland. The farm productivity for all crops is low, compared to other Indian states, suggesting significant opportunity for farmer income increase. Currently the Jhum to Terraced cultivation ratio is 4:3; where Jhum is local name for cut-and-burn shift farming. Jhum farming is ancient, causes a lot of pollution and soil damage, yet accounts for majority of farmed area. The state does not produce enough food, and depends on trade of food from others states of India.<sup id="cite_ref-pn_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pn-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Forestry is also an important source of income. Cottage industries such as weaving, woodwork, and pottery are also an important source of revenue.</p>
<p>Tourism has a lot of potential, but largely limited due to insurgency and concern of violence over the last five decades. Nagaland's gross state domestic product for 2004 is estimated at $1.4 billion in current prices.</p>
<p>The state generates 87.98 MU compared to a demand for 242.88 MU. This deficit requires Nagaland to buy power. The state has significant hydroelectric potential, which if realized could make the state a power surplus state. In terms of power distribution, every village and town, and almost every household has an electricity connection; but, this infrastructure is not effective given the power shortage in the state.<sup id="cite_ref-pn_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pn-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Hornbill_Festival_of_Nagaland">Hornbill Festival of Nagaland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nagaland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Hornbill Festival of Nagaland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hornbill_Festival" title="Hornbill Festival">Hornbill Festival</a></div>
<div class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Tourism_in_North_East_India" title="Tourism in North East India">Tourism in North East India</a></div>
<p>Hornbill Festival<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63"><span>[</span>63<span>]</span></a></sup> was launched by the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Nagaland" title="Government of Nagaland">Government of Nagaland</a> in December 2000 to encourage intertribal interaction and to promote cultural heritage of the state. Organized by the State Tourism Department and Art &amp; Culture Department. Hornbill Festival showcases a mélange of cultural displays under one roof. This festival takes place between 1 to 7 December every year.</p>
<p>It is held at Naga Heritage Village, Kisama which is about 12&#160;km from Kohima. All the tribes of Nagaland take part in this festival. The aim of the festival is to revive and protect the rich culture of Nagaland and display its history, culture and traditions.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64"><span>[</span>64<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The festival is named after the <a href="/wiki/Great_Indian_hornbill" title="Great Indian hornbill" class="mw-redirect">hornbill</a> bird, which is displayed in folklores in most of the states tribes. The week-long festival unites one and all in Nagaland and people enjoy the colourful performances, crafts, sports, food fairs, games and ceremonies. Traditional arts which include paintings, wood carvings, and sculptures are also on display. Festival highlights include Traditional Naga Morungs Exhibition and sale of arts and crafts, food stalls, herbal medicine stalls, shows and sales, cultural medley – songs and dances, fashion shows, beauty contest, traditional archery, naga wrestling, indigenous games and musical concerts. Additional attractions include the Konyak fire eating demonstration, pork-fat eating competitions, the Hornbill Literature Festival (including the Hutton Lectures), Hornbill Global Film Fest, Hornbill Ball, Choral Panorama, North East India Drum Ensemble, Naga king chilli eating competition, Hornbill National Rock Contest,<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65"><span>[</span>65<span>]</span></a></sup> Hornbill International Motor Rally and WW-II Vintage Car Rally.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66"><span>[</span>66<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67"><span>[</span>67<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transportation_infrastructure">Transportation infrastructure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nagaland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Transportation infrastructure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The railway network in the state is minimal. Broad gauge lines run 7.98 miles (12.84&#160;km), National Highway roads 227.0 miles (365.3&#160;km), and state roads 680.1 miles (1,094.5&#160;km). Road is the backbone of Nagaland's transportation network. The state also has over 15,000&#160;km of surfaced roads, but these are not satisfactorily maintained given the weather damage. In terms of population served for each kilometer of surfaced road, Nagaland is the second best state in the region after Arunachal Pradesh.<sup id="cite_ref-pn_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pn-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_Resources">Natural Resources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nagaland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Natural Resources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After a gap of almost 20 years, Nagaland state Chief Minister, T. R. Zeliang launched the resumption of oil exploration in Changpang and Tsori areas, under Wokha district in July 2014. The exploration will be carried out by the Metropolitan Oil &amp; Gas Pvt. Ltd. Zeliang has alleged failures and disputed payments made to the state made by previous explorer, the state owned <a href="/wiki/Oil_and_Natural_Gas_Corporation" title="Oil and Natural Gas Corporation">Oil and Natural Gas Corporation</a> (ONGC).<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68"><span>[</span>68<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nagaland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Music_of_Nagaland" title="Music of Nagaland">Music of Nagaland</a></div>
<p>The sixteen main tribes of Nagaland are <a href="/wiki/Angami_Naga" title="Angami Naga">Angami</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ao_(people)" title="Ao (people)" class="mw-redirect">Ao</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chakhesang" title="Chakhesang" class="mw-redirect">Chakhesang</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chang_Naga" title="Chang Naga">Chang</a>, Dimasa Kachari, <a href="/wiki/Khiamniungan" title="Khiamniungan" class="mw-redirect">Khiamniungan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Konyak_people" title="Konyak people">Konyak</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lotha_Naga" title="Lotha Naga">Lotha</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phom_Naga" title="Phom Naga">Phom</a>, Pochury, <a href="/wiki/Rengma" title="Rengma" class="mw-redirect">Rengma</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sangtam_Naga" title="Sangtam Naga">Sangtam</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sumi_Naga)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Sumi Naga) (page does not exist)">Sumi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yimchunger" title="Yimchunger">Yimchunger</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kuki_people" title="Kuki people">Kuki</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zeliangrong" title="Zeliangrong">Zeliang</a>. The Konyaks, Angamis, Aos, Lothas, and Sumis are the largest Naga tribes; there are several smaller tribes as well (see <a href="/wiki/List_of_Naga_tribes" title="List of Naga tribes">List of Naga tribes</a>). Tribe and clan traditions and loyalties play an important part in the life of Nagas. Weaving is a traditional art handed down through generations in Nagaland. Each of the tribe has its own unique designs and colours, producing <a href="/wiki/Naga_shawl" title="Naga shawl">shawls</a>, shoulder bags, decorative spears, table mats, wood carvings, and bamboo works. Among many tribes the design of the shawl denotes the social status of the wearer. Some of the more known shawls include <i>Tsungkotepsu</i> and <i>Rongsu</i> of the Ao tribe; <i>Sutam, Ethasu, Longpensu</i> of the Lothas; <i>Supong</i> of the Sangtams, <i>Rongkhim</i> and <i>Tsungrem Khim</i> of the Yimchungers; the Angami <i>Lohe</i> shawls with thick embroidered animal motifs etc.</p>
<p>Folk songs and dances are essential ingredients of the traditional Naga culture. The oral tradition is kept alive through the media of folk tales and songs. Naga folks songs are both romantic and historical, with songs narrating entire stories of famous ancestors and incidents. There are also seasonal songs which describe various activities done in a particular agricultural season. Tribal dances of the Nagas give an insight into the inborn Naga reticence of the people. War dances and other dances belonging to distinctive Naga tribes are a major art form in Nagaland.</p>
