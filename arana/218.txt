<p>The <b>Portal Bridge</b> is a railroad bridge over the <a href="/wiki/Hackensack_River" title="Hackensack River">Hackensack River</a> in <a href="/wiki/Gateway_Region" title="Gateway Region">northeastern New Jersey</a>, <a href="/wiki/USA" title="USA" class="mw-redirect">USA</a>, just west of <a href="/wiki/Secaucus_Junction" title="Secaucus Junction">Secaucus Junction</a>. It is a two-track, moveable swing-span between the towns of <a href="/wiki/Kearny,_New_Jersey" title="Kearny, New Jersey">Kearny</a> and <a href="/wiki/Secaucus" title="Secaucus" class="mw-redirect">Secaucus</a> owned and operated by <a href="/wiki/Amtrak" title="Amtrak">Amtrak</a> as part of the <a href="/wiki/Northeast_Corridor" title="Northeast Corridor">Northeast Corridor</a> and also used by <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey_Transit_Rail_Operations" title="New Jersey Transit Rail Operations">New Jersey Transit</a>. As of 2015<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Portal_Bridge&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, it is considered the busiest train span in the Western Hemisphere,<sup id="cite_ref-mcgeehan2014_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mcgeehan2014-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> carrying between 150,000 and 200,000 passengers per day.<sup id="cite_ref-mcgeehan2014_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mcgeehan2014-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-porter2014_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-porter2014-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-maag2015_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-maag2015-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Originally opened in 1910,<sup id="cite_ref-maag2015_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-maag2015-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> the bridge was built by <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_Railroad" title="Pennsylvania Railroad">Pennsylvania Railroad</a> in conjunction with service to the newly constructed <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_Station_(New_York_City)" title="Pennsylvania Station (New York City)">Pennsylvania Station</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>.</p>
<p>The bridge is considered obsolete and under review for replacement. Currently, the bridge limits train speeds to 60&#160;mph<sup id="cite_ref-mcgeehan2014_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mcgeehan2014-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-porter2014_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-porter2014-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-maag2015_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-maag2015-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> and is so low that it often has to be opened to allow commercial boats to pass underneath it causing more delays.<sup id="cite_ref-porter2014_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-porter2014-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-maag2015_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-maag2015-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> For these reasons, it has been called the <a href="/wiki/Achilles%27_heel" title="Achilles' heel">Achilles' heel</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Northeast_Corridor" title="Northeast Corridor">Northeast Corridor</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-porter2014_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-porter2014-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Existing design and engineering plans for the bridge's replacement call for two fixed bridges. <sup id="cite_ref-mcgeehan2014_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mcgeehan2014-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> Work on the replacement bridges has stalled due to lack of funding and as of 2014 funding for the $940 million to build it had not been identified.<sup id="cite_ref-mcgeehan2014_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mcgeehan2014-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-porter2014_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-porter2014-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design_and_construction">Design and construction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Portal_Bridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Design and construction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Portal Bridge is a 961 feet (293&#160;m) steel structure with masonry abutments. The bridge consists of a 300 feet (91&#160;m) through-truss swing span and six (three on each side of the center span) 110-foot-long open-deck girder approach spans.<sup id="cite_ref-NTSB1997_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NTSB1997-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">(p8)</sup> The bridge itself is partially made of wood.<sup id="cite_ref-mcgeehan2014_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mcgeehan2014-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> The bridge's lowest beams are just 23 feet (7.0&#160;m) above the surface of the <a href="/wiki/Hackensack_River" title="Hackensack River">Hackensack River</a> at <a href="/wiki/High_tide" title="High tide" class="mw-redirect">high tide</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-mcgeehan2014_1-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mcgeehan2014-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>When closed to river traffic, the bridge bears upon six wedge blocks. Two blocks are at each end of the bridge while two more sit adjacent to the center of the bridge. After the wedges are withdrawn the center-bearing supports the structure as the bridge is opened and returns it to its closed position once the river traffic has passed through one or both of the navigation channels.</p>
<p>Construction of the bridge was begun in August 1905, and the bridge was placed in service on November 27, 1910,<sup id="cite_ref-NTSB1997_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NTSB1997-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">(p8)</sup> based on bridge designs from the 1840s.<sup id="cite_ref-maag2015_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-maag2015-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> The bridge was designed to last 100 years.<sup id="cite_ref-maag2015_3-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-maag2015-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Overhead catenary to supply power to electric locomotives was installed in the 1930s.<sup id="cite_ref-NTSB1997_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NTSB1997-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">(p8)</sup> Some of the bridge machinery was updated in 1931. Minor repairs were made in the 1970s, and major repairs to structural mechanical and electrical equipment were completed as part of Amtrak's Northeast Corridor Improvement Project between 1982 and 1984 by William F. Hegarty, Inc. of East Hanover, NJ.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operation">Operation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Portal_Bridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Operation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rail_service">Rail service</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Portal_Bridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Rail service">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Rail traffic is carried over the <a href="/wiki/Swing_bridge" title="Swing bridge">swing bridge</a> when it is closed. Rail service is currently at capacity, having grown from 40,000 daily passengers in 2005<sup id="cite_ref-mcgeehan2005_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mcgeehan2005-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> to 150,000 to 200,000 daily passengers in 2015<sup id="cite_ref-porter2014_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-porter2014-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-maag2015_3-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-maag2015-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> on approximately 450 daily trains<sup id="cite_ref-mcgeehan2014_1-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mcgeehan2014-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> for <a href="/wiki/Amtrak" title="Amtrak">Amtrak</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey_Transit_Rail_Operations" title="New Jersey Transit Rail Operations">New Jersey Transit</a>.</p>
<p>As of 2011<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Portal_Bridge&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, Amtrak operated some 103 scheduled trains in both directions over this segment of the <a href="/wiki/Northeast_Corridor" title="Northeast Corridor">Northeast Corridor</a> between <a href="/wiki/Newark_Penn_Station" title="Newark Penn Station" class="mw-redirect">Newark Penn Station</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_York_Penn_Station" title="New York Penn Station" class="mw-redirect">New York Penn Station</a>. Four of <a href="/wiki/NJ_Transit" title="NJ Transit" class="mw-redirect">NJ Transit</a>’s rail lines (<a href="/wiki/Northeast_Corridor_Line" title="Northeast Corridor Line">Northeast Corridor Line</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_Jersey_Coast_Line" title="North Jersey Coast Line">North Jersey Coast Line</a>, <a href="/wiki/Morris_and_Essex_Lines" title="Morris and Essex Lines" class="mw-redirect">Morris and Essex Lines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Montclair-Boonton_Line" title="Montclair-Boonton Line">Montclair-Boonton Line</a>) with 393 trains each weekday in both directions use the bridge.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="River_traffic">River traffic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Portal_Bridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: River traffic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>River traffic along the <a href="/wiki/Hackensack_River" title="Hackensack River">Hackensack River</a> can flow under the <a href="/wiki/Swing_bridge" title="Swing bridge">swing bridge</a> when it is open. Bridge schedules exempt the Portal Draw from opening weekdays 6 am to 10 am and 4 pm to 8 pm, during peak commuter travel periods over the bridge. The bridge opens on signal at other times.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>As of 2015<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Portal_Bridge&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the only regularly scheduled commercial traffic on the river is a barge full of sludge<sup id="cite_ref-maag2015_3-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-maag2015-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> traveling from <a href="/wiki/Bergen_County,_New_Jersey" title="Bergen County, New Jersey">Bergen County</a> Utilities Authority complex in <a href="/wiki/Little_Ferry,_New_Jersey" title="Little Ferry, New Jersey">Little Ferry</a> to a sewage treatment plant in <a href="/wiki/Newark,_New_Jersey" title="Newark, New Jersey">Newark</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-mcgeehan2014_1-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mcgeehan2014-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> For the last four months of 2014, of the 90 times the bridge was opened, 75 were to provide service to the sludge barge.<sup id="cite_ref-maag2015_3-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-maag2015-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> The bridge has caused numerous delays in train service in order to allow for river service, though Amtrak does not keep specific records of delays.<sup id="cite_ref-maag2015_3-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-maag2015-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Operational_issues">Operational issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Portal_Bridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Operational issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The bridge requires millions of dollars of yearly maintenance.<sup id="cite_ref-mcgeehan2005_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mcgeehan2005-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> According to several officials, the bridge is considered a "choke-point" which reduces the potential speed and capacity of the line. These officials include <a href="/wiki/Bob_Menendez" title="Bob Menendez">U.S. Sen. Bob Menendez (NJ)</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-porter2014_2-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-porter2014-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Drew Galloway, <a href="/wiki/Amtrak" title="Amtrak">Amtrak</a> Assistant V.P. of Planning and Development<sup id="cite_ref-mcgeehan2014_1-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mcgeehan2014-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> and the chief of Planning and Performance for the <a href="/wiki/Northeast_Corridor" title="Northeast Corridor">Northeast Corridor</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-porter2014_2-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-porter2014-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey_Transit" title="New Jersey Transit">New Jersey Transit</a> Executive Director Richard Sarles.<sup id="cite_ref-mcgeehan2005_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mcgeehan2005-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-philly_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-philly-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Accidents_and_incidents">Accidents and incidents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Portal_Bridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Accidents and incidents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1996_derailment">1996 derailment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Portal_Bridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 1996 derailment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The bridge was site of a <a href="/wiki/Derailment" title="Derailment">derailment</a> on November 23, 1996<sup id="cite_ref-NTSB1997_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NTSB1997-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> when the <a href="/wiki/Swing_bridge" title="Swing bridge">swing bridge</a> failed to close properly. <a href="/wiki/Amtrak" title="Amtrak">Amtrak</a>'s Fast Mail Train No. 12, with twelve passenger and mail coaches pulled by two locomotives on a Washington-to-Boston run with 88 passengers and 20 crew members, derailed as it reached the bridge. It sideswiped an oncoming passenger train, but continued across the bridge, prevented from plunging through the trestles into the river by guide rails that parallel the main tracks. Then its twin locomotives, a baggage car, and three passenger coaches plunged over an embankment.<sup id="cite_ref-NTSB1997_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NTSB1997-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">(pp1–5)</sup> There were no deaths; thirty-four people were hospitalized.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> The reason for the derailment was that a rail was 5 inches higher than it supposed to be, and acted as a ramp.<sup id="cite_ref-NTSB1997_4-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NTSB1997-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Gulliver2014_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gulliver2014-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> As a result of the derailment, the maximum speed on the bridge was lowered to 60&#160;mph,<sup id="cite_ref-mcgeehan2014_1-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mcgeehan2014-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> helping to make the bridge a choke point for the entire <a href="/wiki/Northeast_Corridor" title="Northeast Corridor">Northeast Corridor</a>. The cost of the derailment was estimated at $3.6 Million<sup id="cite_ref-NTSB1997_4-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NTSB1997-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">(p8)</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fires">Fires</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Portal_Bridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Fires">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On May 13, 2005, the bridge caught on fire. <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey_Transit" title="New Jersey Transit">NJ Transit</a> engineers believe that the 13kV overhead electrical wires overheated, sending shards of metal towards the <a href="/wiki/Creosote" title="Creosote">creosote</a>-covered wooden fenders at the base of the bridge.<sup id="cite_ref-mcgeehan2005fire_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mcgeehan2005fire-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> The immediate result of the fire was to block all traffic until the next morning.<sup id="cite_ref-mcgeehan2005_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mcgeehan2005-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> The cost of the incident was $5M.<sup id="cite_ref-Belsen2008_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Belsen2008-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> On August 4, 2014, the bridge caught on fire,. interrupting rail traffic for half an hour.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Replacement">Replacement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Portal_Bridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Replacement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In December 2008, the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Railroad_Administration" title="Federal Railroad Administration">Federal Railroad Administration</a> approved a $1.34 billion project to replace the Portal Bridge with two new bridges—a three-track bridge to the north, and a two-track bridge to the south.<sup id="cite_ref-Belsen2008_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Belsen2008-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> The new bridges were scheduled to be completed in 2017, at which time the Portal Bridge was to be dismantled.</p>
<p>In course of design work the number of tracks on the north bridge has been reduced from three to two.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Design work progressed in 2009 and 2010. In 2009, New Jersey applied for $38.5 million in funding for the replacement from the <a href="/wiki/American_Recovery_and_Reinvestment_Act_of_2009" title="American Recovery and Reinvestment Act of 2009">American Recovery and Reinvestment Act of 2009</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> On January 28, 2010, the federal funds were released as a <a href="/wiki/Transportation_Investment_Generating_Economic_Recovery" title="Transportation Investment Generating Economic Recovery">TIGER grant</a><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> as part of a larger package of $112M for the entire <a href="/wiki/Northeast_Corridor" title="Northeast Corridor">Northeast Corridor</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Frassinelli2010_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Frassinelli2010-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> The $38.5 million in federal funds were intended for final design for the new bridge.<sup id="cite_ref-Frassinelli2010_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Frassinelli2010-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The beginning of construction of a replacement, expected to follow design work and begin in 2010, did not occur. The original timeline for the project called for construction of the new bridge to begin in 2010, with the bridge replacement to be complete by 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> However, partly due to cancellation of the <a href="/wiki/Access_to_the_Region%27s_Core" title="Access to the Region's Core">Access to the Region's Core</a> project in 2010, as well as funding issues, this original plan was reduced to a single two-track bridge constructed north of the current bridge with room for a new bridge south of the current bridge left open to follow.<sup id="cite_ref-mcgeehan2014_1-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mcgeehan2014-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In 2014, design work for the new Portal Bridge North had been completed.<sup id="cite_ref-mcgeehan2014_1-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mcgeehan2014-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> The proposed Portal Bridge North would be over 50 feet (15&#160;m) tall, would not be a swinging bridge, and would allow train speeds of at least 90&#160;mph.<sup id="cite_ref-porter2014_2-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-porter2014-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> The new bridge would also be a part of the <a href="/wiki/Amtrak" title="Amtrak">Amtrak</a> <a href="/wiki/Gateway_Project" title="Gateway Project">Gateway Project</a>—itself a partial replacement of the <a href="/wiki/Access_to_the_Region%27s_Core" title="Access to the Region's Core">Access to the Region's Core</a>—estimated to cost $13.5 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-GatewayPDF_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GatewayPDF-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Progress on the Portal Bridge North is stalled due to funding. In April 2011, Amtrak applied for federal funding of $570 million for construction, with New Jersey expected to commit $150 million.<sup id="cite_ref-Amstrakseeks_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Amstrakseeks-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-AMTKseeking_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMTKseeking-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> As of 2015<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Portal_Bridge&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, however, the project is lacking $940 million in funding.<sup id="cite_ref-mcgeehan2014_1-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mcgeehan2014-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-porter2014_2-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-porter2014-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
