			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Portal venous system</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
				</div>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="hatnote">This article is about the portal venous systems in general.  For the system involving the liver, see <a href="/wiki/Hepatic_portal_system" title="Hepatic portal system">Hepatic portal system</a>.</div>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Portal_venous_system.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Portal_venous_system.png/220px-Portal_venous_system.png" width="220" height="166" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Portal_venous_system.png/330px-Portal_venous_system.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Portal_venous_system.png/440px-Portal_venous_system.png 2x" data-file-width="714" data-file-height="538" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Portal_venous_system.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
General diagram of a portal venous system, for example, this occurs in the <a href="/wiki/Hypophyseal_portal_system" title="Hypophyseal portal system">hypophyseal portal system</a> between the <a href="/wiki/Hypothalamus" title="Hypothalamus">hypothalamus</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Anterior_pituitary_gland" title="Anterior pituitary gland" class="mw-redirect">anterior pituitary gland</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Circulatory_system" title="Circulatory system">circulatory system</a> of animals, a <b>portal venous system</b> occurs when a <a href="/wiki/Capillary_bed" title="Capillary bed" class="mw-redirect">capillary bed</a> pools into another capillary bed through <a href="/wiki/Vein" title="Vein">veins</a>, without first going through the heart. Both capillary beds and the <a href="/wiki/Blood_vessel" title="Blood vessel">blood vessels</a> that connect them are considered part of the portal venous system.</p>
<p>They are relatively uncommon as the majority of capillary beds drain into veins which then drain into the <a href="/wiki/Heart" title="Heart">heart</a>, not into another capillary bed. Portal venous systems are considered venous because the blood vessels that join the two capillary beds are either <a href="/wiki/Vein" title="Vein">veins</a> or <a href="/wiki/Venule" title="Venule">venules</a>.</p>
<p>Examples of such systems include the <a href="/wiki/Hepatic_portal_system" title="Hepatic portal system">hepatic portal system</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Hypophyseal_portal_system" title="Hypophyseal portal system">hypophyseal portal system</a> and (in non-mammals) the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Renal_portal_system&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Renal portal system (page does not exist)">renal portal system</a>. Unqualified, "portal venous system" often refers to the hepatic portal system. For this reason, "portal vein" most commonly refers to the <a href="/wiki/Hepatic_portal_vein" title="Hepatic portal vein">hepatic portal vein</a>.</p>
<p>The functional significance of such a system is that it transports products of one region directly to another region in relatively high concentrations. If the heart was involved in the blood circulation between those two regions, those products would be spread around the rest of the body.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_humans">In humans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Portal_venous_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: In humans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The final common pathway for transport of venous blood from spleen, pancreas, gallbladder and the abdominal portion of the gastrointestinal tract (with the exception of the inferior part of the anal canal) is through the hepatic portal vein. The portal vein is formed by the union of the superior mesenteric vein and the splenic vein posterior to the neck of the pancreas at the level of vertebra body L1. Ascending towards the liver, the portal vein passes posterior to the superior part of the duodenum and enters the right margin of the lesser omentum. It is anterior to the omental foramen and posterior to both the bile duct, which is slightly to the right, and the hepatic artery proper, which is slightly to the left. On approaching the liver, the portal vein divides into right and left branches which enter the liver parenchyma. It gives tributaries: the right and left gastric veins, the cystic vein and the para-umbilical veins.</p>
