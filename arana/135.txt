<p><i><b>Deathrow</b></i> is a <a href="/wiki/Sports_video_game" title="Sports video game" class="mw-redirect">sports video game</a> developed by <a href="/wiki/Southend_Interactive" title="Southend Interactive">Southend Interactive</a> and published by <a href="/wiki/Ubisoft_Entertainment" title="Ubisoft Entertainment" class="mw-redirect">Ubisoft Entertainment</a>. It was released as an <a href="/wiki/Xbox_(console)" title="Xbox (console)">Xbox</a> <a href="/wiki/Video_game_console" title="Video game console">gaming system</a> exclusive on 18 October 2002 in Europe and four days later in North America. <i>Deathrow</i>‍‍ '​‍s development began in May 1999 as an online <a href="/wiki/Personal_computer" title="Personal computer">PC</a> (player computer) game. In early 2001, Southend transitioned to an Xbox console release, which let the team use <a href="/wiki/Pixel_shaders" title="Pixel shaders" class="mw-redirect">pixel shaders</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bump_map" title="Bump map" class="mw-redirect">bump mapped</a> textures, and <a href="/wiki/Specular_lighting" title="Specular lighting" class="mw-redirect">specular lighting</a>. <i>Deathrow</i> was built on an in-house 3D <a href="/wiki/Game_engine" title="Game engine">game engine</a> and was Southend's first full release.</p>
<p>The game is based on the fictional <a href="/wiki/Extreme_sport" title="Extreme sport">extreme sport</a> Blitz, a <a href="/wiki/Futuristic" title="Futuristic" class="mw-redirect">futuristic</a> <a href="/wiki/Full-contact" title="Full-contact" class="mw-redirect">full-contact</a> hybrid of <a href="/wiki/Hockey" title="Hockey">hockey</a> and basketball played with a <a href="/wiki/Flying_disc" title="Flying disc">flying disc</a>. Two teams of four players attempt to move the disc through their opponent's goal, and teams can either win on points or by knocking out their opposing team. There are 150 individual characters across 18 thematic teams. The <a href="/wiki/Single-player" title="Single-player" class="mw-redirect">single-player</a> campaign's plot is set in the 23rd century, when Blitz is a popular, televised sport and the teams battle to scale the ranks and win the championship. Players earn credits from their goals scored, opponent knockouts, and crowd-pleasing to be spent on player upgrades, bets, and new teammates. The game also supports <a href="/wiki/Split_screen_(computer_graphics)" title="Split screen (computer graphics)">split-screen</a> and <a href="/wiki/System_Link" title="System Link">System Link</a> multiplayer for up to eight players across up to eight Xbox consoles.</p>
<p><i>Deathrow</i> received largely favorable reviews. Reviewers praised the game's fast-paced action, and surround sound, but complained of its high difficulty curve, generic soundtrack, and lack of online multiplayer. Some critics felt the game's use of profanity was excessive, while others thought it was a highlight. Reviewers considered the game's concept and mechanics similar to other series, specifically <i><a href="/wiki/Speedball_(video_game)" title="Speedball (video game)">Speedball</a></i>. <a href="/wiki/IGN" title="IGN">IGN</a> and <i><a href="/wiki/TeamXbox" title="TeamXbox">TeamXbox</a></i> both named <i>Deathrow</i> an Editor's Choice. The game won the <i>TeamXbox</i> 2002 Breakthrough Game of the Year award and the IGN 2002 Best Game Nobody Played. IGN later reported that a sequel would be unlikely due to the original's low revenue. Southend dissolved in 2013.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gameplay">Gameplay</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deathrow_(video_game)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Gameplay">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Deathrow</i> is set in the year 2219,<sup id="cite_ref-Complex_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Complex-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> where Blitz is the world's most popular sport.<sup id="cite_ref-IGN:_Nobody_Played_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGN:_Nobody_Played-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Players attempt to toss a disc through a hoop while avoiding <a href="/wiki/Full-contact" title="Full-contact" class="mw-redirect">full-contact</a> from their opponents, including punches, kicks, throws, and stomps.<sup id="cite_ref-IGN_review_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGN_review-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Like a futuristic <a href="/wiki/Rugby_football" title="Rugby football">rugby</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-EGM_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EGM-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> the sport combines elements of <a href="/wiki/Hockey" title="Hockey">hockey</a>, basketball,<sup id="cite_ref-OXM_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OXM-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> and full-contact <a href="/wiki/American_football" title="American football">American football</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IGN_review_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGN_review-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Critics compared the game's mechanics to <i><a href="/wiki/Discs_of_Tron" title="Discs of Tron">Discs of Tron</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Mortal_Kombat" title="Mortal Kombat">Mortal Kombat</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-IGN:_Nobody_Played_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGN:_Nobody_Played-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Blood_Bowl" title="Blood Bowl">Blood Bowl</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-IGN:_fresh_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGN:_fresh-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Final_Fantasy_X" title="Final Fantasy X">Final Fantasy X</a></i>‍ '​s <a href="/wiki/Blitzball_(Final_Fantasy)" title="Blitzball (Final Fantasy)" class="mw-redirect">Blitzball</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-EGM_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EGM-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Blades_of_Steel" title="Blades of Steel">Blades of Steel</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-1UP.com_review_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1UP.com_review-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/Speedball_(video_game)" title="Speedball (video game)">Speedball</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-OXM_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OXM-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-EGM_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EGM-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Edge_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Edge-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> and its aesthetic to that of <i><a href="/wiki/Blade_Runner" title="Blade Runner">Blade Runner</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-EGM_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EGM-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> The game's premise was also compared to the 1975 film <i><a href="/wiki/Rollerball_(1975_film)" title="Rollerball (1975 film)">Rollerball</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-GS_review_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GS_review-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-GS:_E3_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GS:_E3-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>There are four rounds in a match of Blitz,<sup id="cite_ref-IGN_review_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGN_review-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> where two teams of four <a href="/wiki/Artificial_intelligence_(video_games)" title="Artificial intelligence (video games)">computer</a> or human players<sup id="cite_ref-OXM_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OXM-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> score points for each energized Blitz disc thrown through their opponent's hoop, which is eight feet off the ground.<sup id="cite_ref-IGN_review_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGN_review-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Players pass and travel with the disc across the arenas,<sup id="cite_ref-IGN_review_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGN_review-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> and the game continues without pause between points scored.<sup id="cite_ref-TX_review_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TX_review-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> The team with the most points at the end of a match wins. Players can choose to brawl when not scoring points. Fighting depletes character health, depending on who takes the blows. Once his health is completely depleted, a character is removed from the game, and teams with all players knocked out are disqualified (though players can be substituted between rounds).<sup id="cite_ref-IGN_review_3-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGN_review-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> If a player tends towards belligerence, the game's artificial intelligence will compensate and exact revenge for its teammates.<sup id="cite_ref-TX:_movies_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TX:_movies-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Friendly_fire" title="Friendly fire">Friendly fire</a>, where teammates can intentionally or inadvertently hurt each other with attacks meant for their opponents, is permitted.<sup id="cite_ref-IGN_review_3-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGN_review-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>There are 150 unique players on 18 teams,<sup id="cite_ref-IGN_review_3-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGN_review-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> each with thematic personalities in appearance, play style, and profanity.<sup id="cite_ref-OXM_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OXM-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Teams also vary in skill set, and attributes such as defense, speed, strength, and teamwork.<sup id="cite_ref-IGN_review_3-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGN_review-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Examples include the Sea Cats (fast all-female team with European accents), the Marines (who wear <a href="/wiki/Battledress" title="Battledress">camouflaged garb</a> and use <a href="/wiki/Military_jargon" title="Military jargon" class="mw-redirect">military jargon</a>), the Demons (high strength attribute with demonic language),<sup id="cite_ref-IGN_review_3-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGN_review-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> and the Black Dragons (ninjas with high agility and combat skills).<sup id="cite_ref-IGN:_fresh_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGN:_fresh-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Teams battle in 32 arenas—one half with traditional, open-style stadiums, and the other half with environmental obstacles, such as the <a href="/wiki/Underground_mining" title="Underground mining" class="mw-redirect">underground mines</a>. Minor <a href="/wiki/Power-ups" title="Power-ups" class="mw-redirect">power-ups</a> including health, credits, and skill augments for individual players regenerate regularly on the field.<sup id="cite_ref-IGN_review_3-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGN_review-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Players earn credits for knocking out opponents, scoring points, and impressing the crowd with violence and skill. The credits can be used towards player enhancements such as <a href="/wiki/Black_market" title="Black market">black market</a> <a href="/wiki/Performance-enhancing_drugs" title="Performance-enhancing drugs">performance-enhancing drugs</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IGN_review_3-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGN_review-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> A crowd meter displays audience support,<sup id="cite_ref-IGN_review_3-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGN_review-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> which boosts the player's team abilities when filled.<sup id="cite_ref-GameSpy_review_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GameSpy_review-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/IGN" title="IGN">IGN</a> found the game to heavily rely on teamwork. <a href="/wiki/Artificial_intelligence_(video_games)" title="Artificial intelligence (video games)">Computer players</a> on teams rated with low teamwork will not take initiative to pursue the disc or to help teammates in need. This attribute can be raised over the course of a game. Players can call plays including physical offense, fast offense, neutral, defense, and goal defense.<sup id="cite_ref-IGN_review_3-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGN_review-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The game supports <a href="/wiki/Single-player" title="Single-player" class="mw-redirect">single-player</a>, four-player <a href="/wiki/Local_multiplayer" title="Local multiplayer" class="mw-redirect">local multiplayer</a>, and <a href="/wiki/System_Link" title="System Link">System Link</a><sup id="cite_ref-EGM_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EGM-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> with up to eight players across up to eight Xbox consoles, but does not support <a href="/wiki/Xbox_Live" title="Xbox Live">Xbox Live</a> <a href="/wiki/Online_play" title="Online play" class="mw-redirect">online play</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IGN_review_3-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGN_review-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-GameSpy_review_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GameSpy_review-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>Deathrow</i> has a futuristic <a href="/wiki/Electronic_dance_music" title="Electronic dance music">electronic dance music</a> soundtrack<sup id="cite_ref-IGN:_impressions_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGN:_impressions-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> and over 3,000 words of voice acting.<sup id="cite_ref-GS:_E3_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GS:_E3-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> It also supports <a href="/wiki/5.1_surround_sound" title="5.1 surround sound">5.1 surround sound</a> and customized soundtracks.<sup id="cite_ref-IGN_review_3-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGN_review-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> The game is <a href="/wiki/Backwards_compatible" title="Backwards compatible" class="mw-redirect">backwards compatible</a> <a href="/wiki/List_of_Xbox_games_compatible_with_Xbox_360" title="List of Xbox games compatible with Xbox 360">with the Xbox 360</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Complex_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Complex-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Controls">Controls</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deathrow_(video_game)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Controls">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Players use the Xbox controller's left <a href="/wiki/Analog_stick" title="Analog stick">analog stick</a> to move the character, the right stick to <a href="/wiki/Strafing_(gaming)" title="Strafing (gaming)">strafe</a>, and the main buttons to jump, block, punch, and kick.<sup id="cite_ref-IGN_review_3-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGN_review-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> When on offense, the latter two functions become "pass" and "shoot".<sup id="cite_ref-TX:_interview_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TX:_interview-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> Players switch between characters and taunt with the white and black buttons, respectively, and <a href="/wiki/Play-calling" title="Play-calling" class="mw-redirect">call plays</a> with the directional pad. The left trigger modifies an existing action, such as running into dives, slide-tackles, and grabs, and the right trigger orients the camera towards the objective (either the disc or the goal, depending on the team in possession). This camera control is designed for precision when diving for the disc or shooting on goal. A character in possession of the disc will show a trajectory line of their potential shot or pass, which is altered by player movement, breath, and physical contact. Players charge the disc by holding the shooting button, whereby the disc turns greener as the shot grows more powerful.<sup id="cite_ref-IGN_review_3-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGN_review-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> A fully charged shot called a Deathrow will incapacitate any player it hits,<sup id="cite_ref-IGN_review_3-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGN_review-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-GS_review_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GS_review-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> while overcharged shots electrify and stun the carrier.<sup id="cite_ref-IGN_review_3-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGN_review-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Before each game and single-player Conquest, players choose between Sports and Action camera views.<sup id="cite_ref-IGN_review_3-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGN_review-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Action view is a trailing third-person shot similar to looking over the player-character's shoulder, while Sports view is a spectator perspective similar that of a televised basketball game.<sup id="cite_ref-NYT_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> Enclosed arenas are inaccessible when using Sports view. The camera view cannot be changed once single-player begins, so Conquest mode players in Sports view will not see the arenas they unlock. The Action view camera swings wildly and can be pulled back slightly in the menus.<sup id="cite_ref-IGN_review_3-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGN_review-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Campaign">Campaign</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deathrow_(video_game)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Campaign">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The in-game story of Blitz begins in 2197 as a Los Angeles gang sport used to find recruits. Over 20 years later, the illegal sport is picked up for broadcast by the Prime Network, who forms the Blitz Disc Association (BDA) and plans for the first Blitz competition with exhibition games and prize money. Through exhibition games and prize money, 13 teams of humans with various competitive augmentations are chosen to compete.<sup id="cite_ref-TX:_preview_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TX:_preview-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Conquest, the single-player tournament career mode, pits the player's team against the ranked hierarchy en route to the championship. Up to three additional human players can join in the single-player. Players initially choose between four teams, though 13 total are unlockable. Teams begin with four players with no alternates for substitution, and fight their way from fourth place in the Rookie Division to first place against each team in between. Players can continue to take challenges within the division before irrevocably moving on to the next division.<sup id="cite_ref-IGN_review_3-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGN_review-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The player's team receives randomized, team-specific textual messages in between games, including offers for <a href="/wiki/Free_agent" title="Free agent">free agent</a> offers, training sessions, drugs, bets on the player's performance, and events including accidental gifts from the player's manager, threats from the team's owner,<sup id="cite_ref-IGN_review_3-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGN_review-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Organized_crime" title="Organized crime">organized crime</a> extortions.<sup id="cite_ref-OXM_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OXM-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Players can buy character attribute increases with their credits. Single-player progress unlocks concept art and game assets external to the game, as well as new teams, players, and arenas. Each of the unlockable 13 teams has six unlockable players (for a total of 10 players on each), and five additional legacy teams are limited to four players apiece. Multi-disc and "Extreme" <a href="/wiki/Difficulty_level" title="Difficulty level" class="mw-redirect">difficulty</a> gameplay options are also unlockable.<sup id="cite_ref-IGN_review_3-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGN_review-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deathrow_(video_game)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Deathrow</i> was developed by <a href="/wiki/Southend_Interactive" title="Southend Interactive">Southend Interactive</a> and produced by <a href="/wiki/Ubisoft_Entertainment" title="Ubisoft Entertainment" class="mw-redirect">Ubisoft Entertainment</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-TX:_preview_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TX:_preview-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> Five friends opened Southend in <a href="/wiki/Malm%C3%B6,_Sweden" title="Malmö, Sweden" class="mw-redirect">Malmö, Sweden</a> in 1998 to fulfill their childhood ambitions to make video games.<sup id="cite_ref-TX:_interview_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TX:_interview-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> Southend began <i>Deathrow</i> development in May 1999 and expected the game to be an online <a href="/wiki/Personal_computer" title="Personal computer">PC</a> video game<sup id="cite_ref-TX:_preview_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TX:_preview-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> tentatively titled <i>Blitz Disc Arena</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-TX:_interview_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TX:_interview-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> The idea for the sports game descended from a combination of <i><a href="/wiki/Speedball_2" title="Speedball 2" class="mw-redirect">Speedball 2</a></i>, the <i><a href="/wiki/Quake_(series)" title="Quake (series)">Quake</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/Unreal_(series)" title="Unreal (series)"><i>Unreal</i> series</a>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Tekken" title="Tekken">Tekken</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-TX:_interview_15-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TX:_interview-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> Southend's nine-person team received Xbox <a href="/wiki/Development_kits" title="Development kits" class="mw-redirect">development kits</a> in June 2000<sup id="cite_ref-TX:_preview_17-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TX:_preview-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> and decided to move the game to console in early 2001.<sup id="cite_ref-TX:_interview_15-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TX:_interview-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> According to Southend animator Rodrigo Cespedes in a 2002 <i><a href="/wiki/TeamXbox" title="TeamXbox">TeamXbox</a></i> interview, "Xbox was the only console that would allow [them] to produce the game as it was originally envisioned", adding that <a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">Microsoft</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ubisoft" title="Ubisoft">Ubisoft</a> encouraged the mature direction with emphasis on blood, brutality, and profanity.<sup id="cite_ref-TX:_interview_15-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TX:_interview-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> Thus they began to port the game to the console for its feature capabilities, including <a href="/wiki/Vertex_shaders" title="Vertex shaders" class="mw-redirect">vertex</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pixel_shaders" title="Pixel shaders" class="mw-redirect">pixel shaders</a> for <a href="/wiki/Bump_map" title="Bump map" class="mw-redirect">bump mapped</a> environmental textures and character animations, <a href="/wiki/Specular_highlight" title="Specular highlight">specular lighting</a>, and bumped reflection mapping.<sup id="cite_ref-TX:_preview_17-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TX:_preview-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The game was developed on an in-house 3D <a href="/wiki/Game_engine" title="Game engine">game engine</a> under construction for multiple years.<sup id="cite_ref-TX:_preview_17-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TX:_preview-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> Each character is made of over 7000 polygons and 55 bones, making for players with facial expressions, over 800 animations, and a capacity to blink.<sup id="cite_ref-IGN_review_3-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGN_review-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Character faces can additionally express emotions like happiness or anger, and feelings of pain.<sup id="cite_ref-TX:_preview_17-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TX:_preview-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> The move to Xbox led to greater variation in the team personalities. The artists drew many options for each team and the developers chose from the lot. <i>Deathrow</i> was designed for the Action camera view, but Sports view was introduced to expand the game's appeal.<sup id="cite_ref-TX:_interview_15-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TX:_interview-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><i>Deathrow</i> was displayed at Ubisoft's <a href="/wiki/E3_2002" title="E3 2002" class="mw-redirect">E3 2002</a> booth,<sup id="cite_ref-GS:_E3_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GS:_E3-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> and was released on 18 October 2002 in Europe,<sup id="cite_ref-Eurogamer:_release_date_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Eurogamer:_release_date-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> and on 22 October 2002 in the United States<sup id="cite_ref-IGN:_Dream_sequels_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGN:_Dream_sequels-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> as an Xbox exclusive.<sup id="cite_ref-IGN_review_3-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGN_review-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-OXM_5-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OXM-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> The game did not include Xbox Live online multiplayer for want of development time.<sup id="cite_ref-IGN_review_3-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGN_review-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-TX:_interview_15-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TX:_interview-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> At the time of release, Southend had no plans to release <a href="/wiki/Downloadable_content" title="Downloadable content">downloadable content</a>, though they implemented a method to do so.<sup id="cite_ref-IGN_review_3-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGN_review-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>note 1<span>]</span></a></sup> The game was Southend's first full release.<sup id="cite_ref-TX:_preview_17-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TX:_preview-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p><i>Deathrow</i> received "generally favorable" reviews, according to video game <a href="/wiki/Review_aggregator" title="Review aggregator">review aggregator</a> <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Metacritic_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Metacritic-22"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> IGN and <i>TeamXbox</i> both named <i>Deathrow</i> an Editor's Choice.<sup id="cite_ref-IGN_review_3-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGN_review-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-TX_review_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TX_review-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BW_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BW-24"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> The game won <i>TeamXbox</i>‍‍ '​‍s 2002 Breakthrough Game of the Year award<sup id="cite_ref-TeamXbox:_Breakthrough_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TeamXbox:_Breakthrough-25"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> and IGN's 2002 Best Xbox Game Nobody Played.<sup id="cite_ref-IGN:_Nobody_Played_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGN:_Nobody_Played-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> It was also runner-up for their Xbox action game of the year.<sup id="cite_ref-IGN:_Action_runner-up_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGN:_Action_runner-up-26"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> IGN felt that of the year's praiseworthy yet unappreciated games, <i>Deathrow</i> alone "truly blew [them] away".<sup id="cite_ref-IGN:_Nobody_Played_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGN:_Nobody_Played-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Critics praised the game's fast, chaotic action<sup id="cite_ref-IGN:_Nobody_Played_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGN:_Nobody_Played-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-OXM_5-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OXM-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-TX_review_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TX_review-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NYT_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> and use of surround sound.<sup id="cite_ref-IGN_review_3-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGN_review-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-TX_review_11-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TX_review-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> The reviewers bemoaned its high difficulty curve,<sup id="cite_ref-OXM_5-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OXM-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-EGM_4-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EGM-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> generic soundtrack,<sup id="cite_ref-IGN_review_3-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGN_review-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-OXM_5-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OXM-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> and lack of online multiplayer.<sup id="cite_ref-IGN_review_3-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGN_review-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-GS_review_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GS_review-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-TX_review_11-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TX_review-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-TX:_vault_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TX:_vault-27"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> Some reviewers thought the game used profanity excessively,<sup id="cite_ref-IGN:_Nobody_Played_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGN:_Nobody_Played-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-EGM_4-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EGM-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-OXM_5-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OXM-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-GS_review_9-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GS_review-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Pittsburgh_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pittsburgh-28"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> while others considered it a highlight.<sup id="cite_ref-IGN_review_3-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGN_review-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-TX_review_11-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TX_review-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>David Hodgson of <i><a href="/wiki/Electronic_Gaming_Monthly" title="Electronic Gaming Monthly">Electronic Gaming Monthly</a></i> (<i>EGM</i>) found <i>Deathrow</i>‍‍ '​‍s European origins apparent as "awkward, over-the-top expletives in obnoxious American accents" were paired with rugby. Hodgson said that <i>Deathrow</i> struggled to show grittiness in a very shiny environment. He added that the game suffered from immoderate violence, frustrating fighting sequences, lack of online play, and "steep learning curve". Hodgson compared the core mechanics to a "mini-game masquerading as sports entertainment".<sup id="cite_ref-EGM_4-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EGM-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> William Racer of the <i><a href="/wiki/Official_Xbox_Magazine" title="Official Xbox Magazine">Official Xbox Magazine</a></i> (<i>OXM</i>) praised the fast-paced nature of the game and its eye for detail, and complained about the camera angles and difficulty. He placed the game in a lineage of invented sports from a dystopian future and found the game more entertaining than the rest. Racer also found the music generic, and the voice acting good.<sup id="cite_ref-OXM_5-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OXM-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Eric Bush of <i>TeamXbox</i> complimented the computer opponents's artificial intelligence and said that they put up a challenge.<sup id="cite_ref-TX_review_11-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TX_review-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/GameSpy" title="GameSpy">GameSpy</a></i>‍‍ '​‍s Osborne appreciated the game's small details like the streak trailing the disc through the air.<sup id="cite_ref-GameSpy_review_13-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GameSpy_review-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> IGN's Kaiser Hwang called the arena lighting effects, <a href="/wiki/Bump_mapping" title="Bump mapping">bump mapping</a>, and textures the best since <i><a href="/wiki/Halo:_Combat_Evolved" title="Halo: Combat Evolved">Halo: Combat Evolved</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-IGN:_impressions_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGN:_impressions-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/1UP.com" title="1UP.com">1UP.com</a>, <i>OXM</i>‍‍ '​‍s Racer, and IGN's Douglass Perry and David Clayman recommended <i>Deathrow</i> as a <a href="/wiki/Party_game" title="Party game">party game</a>, with the IGN staff specifically recommending the game with System Link.<sup id="cite_ref-OXM_5-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OXM-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-IGN:_Life_of_the_party_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGN:_Life_of_the_party-29"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/GameSpot" title="GameSpot">GameSpot</a></i>‍‍ '​‍s <a href="/wiki/Greg_Kasavin" title="Greg Kasavin">Greg Kasavin</a> spoke highly of the tight controls and accessible gameplay in spite of a larger learning curve.<sup id="cite_ref-GS_review_9-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GS_review-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> IGN's Goldstein described the controls as "relatively simple" and easy to understand within a single game,<sup id="cite_ref-IGN_review_3-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGN_review-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> and Scott Osborne of <i>GameSpy</i> found the controls awkward but easily learned.<sup id="cite_ref-GameSpy_review_13-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GameSpy_review-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> In comparison, Charles Herold of <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> and a friend could not figure out Blitz's rules for 20 minutes, feeling "too macho" to do the tutorial.<sup id="cite_ref-NYT_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_Post-Gazette" title="Pittsburgh Post-Gazette">Pittsburgh Post-Gazette</a></i>‍‍ '​‍s Jonathan Silver thought the gameplay was too frenzied, like "<i><a href="/wiki/NHL_Hitz" title="NHL Hitz">NHL Hitz</a></i> on <a href="/wiki/Steroids" title="Steroids" class="mw-redirect">steroids</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-Pittsburgh_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pittsburgh-28"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> Hilary Goldstein of IGN's only complaint about the controls was the camera's looseness. She noted the game's "serious attitude" and "very gritty view of sports", and similar to hockey, felt that the non-disc action was "one of the nicest aspects" of the game. She praised the graphics and environments, surround sound, the array of unlockables, the single-player, and its replay value, but bemoaned the lack of options to change between camera views, the Action view in general, and the indistinguishability between players. Goldstein regarded <i>Deathrow</i>‍ '​s profanity as the "best use of endless cursing in a game... ever".<sup id="cite_ref-IGN_review_3-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGN_review-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Herold of <i>The New York Times</i> noted violence's centrality to the game and figured that the game's age restrictions were likely due to the "savage profanities", which he felt gave the game personality unlike other sports video games. He added that the game's frantic speed kept him too consumed to curse at the game himself.<sup id="cite_ref-NYT_16-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>The Atlanta Journal-Constitution</i>‍‍ '​‍s Troy Oxford also connected the profanity to the game's <span style="padding-right:.2em;">"</span><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Software_Rating_Board#Ratings" title="Entertainment Software Rating Board">'M' rating</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-Atlanta_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Atlanta-30"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Edge_(magazine)" title="Edge (magazine)">Edge</a></i> referred to <i>Deathrow</i> as a substance-less and "contrived <a href="/wiki/Video_game_clone" title="Video game clone">clone</a>" of the 1990 <i>Speedball 2</i>, which used a ball instead of a disc.<sup id="cite_ref-Edge_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Edge-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> David Hodgson of <i>EGM</i> similarly praised the 1990 title in comparison.<sup id="cite_ref-EGM_4-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EGM-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> William Racer of <i>OXM</i> did not mind the two games' similarities and added that "you might as well copy from the best".<sup id="cite_ref-OXM_5-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OXM-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> While Kasavin of <i>GameSpot</i> thought the theme was tired,<sup id="cite_ref-GS_review_9-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GS_review-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>The Atlanta Journal-Constitution</i>‍‍ '​‍s Oxford wrote that the game felt "fresh".<sup id="cite_ref-Atlanta_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Atlanta-30"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> Reflecting on the release year, IGN director Peer Schneider said that games like <i>Deathrow</i> showed the games industry's ability to make new, high-caliber franchises.<sup id="cite_ref-BW_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BW-24"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> Two IGN staffers predicted the game to be a <a href="/wiki/Sleeper_hit" title="Sleeper hit">sleeper hit</a>:<sup id="cite_ref-IGN_review_3-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGN_review-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-IGN:_impressions_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGN:_impressions-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> one noted the sparse press compared to the game's quality,<sup id="cite_ref-IGN_review_3-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGN_review-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> and the other explained that Ubisoft was busy promoting bigger titles such as <i><a href="/wiki/Tom_Clancy%27s_Splinter_Cell_(video_game)" title="Tom Clancy's Splinter Cell (video game)">Tom Clancy's Splinter Cell</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Dragon%27s_Lair_3D:_Return_to_the_Lair" title="Dragon's Lair 3D: Return to the Lair">Dragon's Lair 3D</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Rayman" title="Rayman">Rayman</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-IGN:_impressions_14-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGN:_impressions-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deathrow_(video_game)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2006, <i>TeamXbox</i>‍‍ '​‍s Matthew Fisher determined that the game aged well.<sup id="cite_ref-TX:_vault_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TX:_vault-27"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> In 2012, <i><a href="/wiki/Complex_(magazine)" title="Complex (magazine)">Complex Gaming</a></i> listed <i>Deathrow</i> 13th on its 15 Most Violent Sports Video Games.<sup id="cite_ref-Complex_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Complex-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> Scottish developer <a href="/wiki/Ludometrics" title="Ludometrics">Ludometrics</a> described their 2014 video game <i><a href="/wiki/Bodycheck_(video_game)" title="Bodycheck (video game)" class="mw-redirect">Bodycheck</a></i> as a spiritual successor to <i>Deathrow</i>, <i>Speedball 2</i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Skateball" title="Skateball">Skateball</a></i>, though the game is set in the medieval past instead of the ultraviolent future.<sup id="cite_ref-Scotsman:_Ludometrics_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Scotsman:_Ludometrics-31"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Around the time of launch, Southend was interested in producing a sequel. In March 2004, IGN listed <i>Deathrow 2</i> as one of its five desired Xbox sequels, specifically for Xbox Live online play support. IGN placed its chances at a 90% likelihood.<sup id="cite_ref-IGN:_Dream_sequels_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGN:_Dream_sequels-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> IGN reported a month later that despite interest from Southend, <a href="/wiki/Ubisoft" title="Ubisoft">Ubisoft</a> would be unlikely to release a forthcoming <i>Deathrow</i> sequel due to the original's low revenue.<sup id="cite_ref-IGN:_No_sequel_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGN:_No_sequel-32"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> Southend separated from its Swedish IT consulting firm parent company, Tacet Holding AB, and became a fully independent company in April 2013. With it, Southend CEO Fredrik Brönjemark announced that "now is the right time for Southend to manage its own destiny and to invest in its own products", of which <i>Deathrow</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Ilomilo" title="Ilomilo">ilomilo</a></i> were examples.<sup id="cite_ref-GamesIndustry.biz:_independent_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GamesIndustry.biz:_independent-33"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup> Southend closed in June 2013 when its full 24-person staff was hired into <a href="/wiki/Ubisoft_Massive" title="Ubisoft Massive">Ubisoft Massive</a>, another Swedish developer.<sup id="cite_ref-GamesIndustry.biz:_closure_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GamesIndustry.biz:_closure-34"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
