<p><b>George David Thompson</b> (20 March 1899 – 26 June 1965) was an American investment banker, industrialist, and modern art collector, based in Pittsburgh.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=G._David_Thompson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>George David Thompson was born in <a href="/wiki/Newark,_Ohio" title="Newark, Ohio">Newark, Ohio</a> in 1899,<sup id="cite_ref-MetMuseum_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MetMuseum-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> and grew up in Indiana, going to high school in high school in <a href="/wiki/Peru,_Indiana" title="Peru, Indiana">Peru, Indiana</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-MetMuseum_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MetMuseum-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> He gave up on "a promising career as a singer",<sup id="cite_ref-PittsburghQuarterly_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PittsburghQuarterly-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> and instead obtained an engineering degree from the <a href="/wiki/Carnegie_Institute_of_Technology" title="Carnegie Institute of Technology" class="mw-redirect">Carnegie Institute of Technology</a> in 1920.<sup id="cite_ref-MetMuseum_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MetMuseum-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=G._David_Thompson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>He worked in New York City as an investment banker.<sup id="cite_ref-PittsburghQuarterly_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PittsburghQuarterly-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> In 1933, Thompson became a financier, co-founding Thompson and Taylor, which took control of a number of steelmakers in the Great Depression, including the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pittsburgh_Spring_Steel_Company&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Pittsburgh Spring Steel Company (page does not exist)">Pittsburgh Spring Steel Company</a> and the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pittsburgh_Steel_Foundry_Company&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Pittsburgh Steel Foundry Company (page does not exist)">Pittsburgh Steel Foundry Company</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-MetMuseum_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MetMuseum-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> In 1945, he was in charge of four steel companies.<sup id="cite_ref-PittsburghQuarterly_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PittsburghQuarterly-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/David_Rockefeller" title="David Rockefeller">David Rockefeller</a> called him, "a hard taskmaster and a tough negotiator".<sup id="cite_ref-PittsburghQuarterly_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PittsburghQuarterly-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Art_collector">Art collector</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=G._David_Thompson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Art collector">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1928, he made his first serious purchase, a <a href="/wiki/Paul_Klee" title="Paul Klee">Paul Klee</a> painting.<sup id="cite_ref-PittsburghQuarterly_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PittsburghQuarterly-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Jean_Metzinger,_c.1913,_Le_Fumeur_(Man_with_a_Pipe),_129.7_x_96.68_cm,_Carnegie_Museum_of_Art.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/25/Jean_Metzinger%2C_c.1913%2C_Le_Fumeur_%28Man_with_a_Pipe%29%2C_129.7_x_96.68_cm%2C_Carnegie_Museum_of_Art.jpg/220px-Jean_Metzinger%2C_c.1913%2C_Le_Fumeur_%28Man_with_a_Pipe%29%2C_129.7_x_96.68_cm%2C_Carnegie_Museum_of_Art.jpg" width="220" height="294" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/25/Jean_Metzinger%2C_c.1913%2C_Le_Fumeur_%28Man_with_a_Pipe%29%2C_129.7_x_96.68_cm%2C_Carnegie_Museum_of_Art.jpg/330px-Jean_Metzinger%2C_c.1913%2C_Le_Fumeur_%28Man_with_a_Pipe%29%2C_129.7_x_96.68_cm%2C_Carnegie_Museum_of_Art.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/25/Jean_Metzinger%2C_c.1913%2C_Le_Fumeur_%28Man_with_a_Pipe%29%2C_129.7_x_96.68_cm%2C_Carnegie_Museum_of_Art.jpg/440px-Jean_Metzinger%2C_c.1913%2C_Le_Fumeur_%28Man_with_a_Pipe%29%2C_129.7_x_96.68_cm%2C_Carnegie_Museum_of_Art.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1619" data-file-height="2160" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Jean_Metzinger,_c.1913,_Le_Fumeur_(Man_with_a_Pipe),_129.7_x_96.68_cm,_Carnegie_Museum_of_Art.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Le_Fumeur" title="Le Fumeur">Le Fumeur</a> by <a href="/wiki/Jean_Metzinger" title="Jean Metzinger">Jean Metzinger</a></div>

<p>In 1953, Thompson gave <a href="/wiki/Le_Fumeur" title="Le Fumeur">Le Fumeur</a> by <a href="/wiki/Jean_Metzinger" title="Jean Metzinger">Jean Metzinger</a> to the Carnegie Museum of Art, Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania.</p>
<p>By the age of 60, Thompson had acquired at least 600 works of modern art.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Thompson had wanted his collection to stay in Pittsburgh, and had that happened, "the arts world of Pittsburgh would have been a different place", according to the Pittsburgh Quarterly, which estimated his collection as being worth US$350 million in 2006.<sup id="cite_ref-PittsburghQuarterly_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PittsburghQuarterly-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> However, in 1959 Pittsburgh's <a href="/wiki/Carnegie_Museum_of_Art" title="Carnegie Museum of Art">Carnegie Museum of Art</a> rejected his offer, which was on condition that a building bearing his name was constructed to house the collection.<sup id="cite_ref-PittsburghQuarterly_2-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PittsburghQuarterly-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>According to the <i><a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_Quarterly" title="Pittsburgh Quarterly">Pittsburgh Quarterly</a></i>, this "decision not to build a Thompson building clearly made <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Beyeler" title="Ernst Beyeler">Beyeler</a>'s fortune, and ironically, it is Beyeler who has a museum containing his collection and bearing his name in <a href="/wiki/Basel" title="Basel">Basel</a>, Switzerland."<sup id="cite_ref-PittsburghQuarterly_2-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PittsburghQuarterly-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In 1960, the <a href="/wiki/Kunstsammlung_Nordrhein-Westfalen" title="Kunstsammlung Nordrhein-Westfalen">Kunstsammlung Nordrhein-Westfalen</a> in <a href="/wiki/D%C3%BCsseldorf" title="Düsseldorf">Düsseldorf</a> was established with the purchase of 88 works by <a href="/wiki/Paul_Klee" title="Paul Klee">Paul Klee</a> from Thompson's colllection, brokered by Basel art dealer <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Beyeler" title="Ernst Beyeler">Ernst Beyeler</a>. In the early 1960s, he sold his entire <a href="/wiki/Alberto_Giacometti" title="Alberto Giacometti">Alberto Giacometti</a> collection of 70 works to the Swiss art dealer <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Beyeler" title="Ernst Beyeler">Ernst Beyeler</a>, and it was divided between the <a href="/wiki/Kunsthaus_Z%C3%BCrich" title="Kunsthaus Zürich">Kunsthaus Zürich</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Basel_Kunstmuseum" title="Basel Kunstmuseum" class="mw-redirect">Basel Kunstmuseum</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Kunstmuseum_Winterthur" title="Kunstmuseum Winterthur">Kunstmuseum Winterthur</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-PittsburghQuarterly_2-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PittsburghQuarterly-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In May 1961, New York's <a href="/wiki/Solomon_R._Guggenheim_Museum" title="Solomon R. Guggenheim Museum">Solomon R. Guggenheim Museum</a> held an exhibition entitled <i>One Hundred Paintings from the G. David Thompson Collection</i>, with works by Cézanne, Monet, Degas, Josef Albers, Adja Yunkers, Braque, Klee, Legér, Matisse, Miró, Mondrian, Schwitters, and Wols, with Picasso being the most represented, with 12 works.<sup id="cite_ref-CMOA_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CMOA-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Thompson gave Pittsburgh's <a href="/wiki/Carnegie_Museum_of_Art" title="Carnegie Museum of Art">Carnegie Museum of Art</a> more than 100 artworks, including paintings by Adolph Gottlieb, Josef Albers, Jean Dubuffet, Willem de Kooning, Alberto Burri, Jean Metzinger, Carlo Carrà, Francis Picabia, David Smith, Henry Moore, Marino Marini, Isamu Noguchi, Jean-Desiré-Gustave Courbet, Adolphe Monticelli, James Lambdin, David Blythe, and John Kane.<sup id="cite_ref-CMOA_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CMOA-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>He gave New York's <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_Modern_Art" title="Museum of Modern Art">Museum of Modern Art</a> (MoMA) works by <a href="/wiki/Claes_Oldenburg" title="Claes Oldenburg">Claes Oldenburg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Victor_Vasarely" title="Victor Vasarely">Victor Vasarely</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jules_Olitski" title="Jules Olitski">Jules Olitski</a>, <a href="/wiki/Henry_Moore" title="Henry Moore">Henry Moore</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pablo_Picasso" title="Pablo Picasso">Pablo Picasso</a>'s early masterpiece <i>Two Nudes</i> (1906).<sup id="cite_ref-CMOA_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CMOA-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Thompson was elected a MoMA trustee in December 1960, having already served on MoMA's collections committee, and held both posts until his death.<sup id="cite_ref-CMOA_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CMOA-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>After his death, <a href="/wiki/Parke-Bernet_Galleries" title="Parke-Bernet Galleries" class="mw-redirect">Parke-Bernet Galleries</a> in New York in March 1966 auctioned 90 paintings and 18 sculptures, in what they described as "beyond question the most important collection of 20th-century art ever to be offered at auction at one time".<sup id="cite_ref-PPG_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PPG-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=G._David_Thompson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Thompson and his wife Helene<sup id="cite_ref-NYtimes_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYtimes-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> lived at <i>Stone's Throw</i>, 4554 Brownsville Road, in <a href="/wiki/Whitehall,_Allegheny_County,_Pennsylvania" title="Whitehall, Allegheny County, Pennsylvania">Whitehall</a> in suburban Pittsburgh .<sup id="cite_ref-MetMuseum_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MetMuseum-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-CMOA_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CMOA-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-PPG_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PPG-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>His son G. David Thompson Jr died in 1958, and two <a href="/wiki/Henry_Moore" title="Henry Moore">Henry Moore</a> sculptures, one to <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a>, the other to the Carnegie Museum of Art, were given in his memory.<sup id="cite_ref-PittsburghQuarterly_2-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PittsburghQuarterly-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> He also donated works by Alberto Burri, Franz Kline, Robert Motherwell, Karel Appel, and Victor Vasarely in his honour.<sup id="cite_ref-CMOA_4-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CMOA-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Helene Thompson died in September 1982.<sup id="cite_ref-NYtimes_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYtimes-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
