<p><b>Alberto Giacometti</b> (<small>Italian pronunciation:&#160;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Italian" title="Help:IPA for Italian">[alˈbɛrto dʒakoˈmetti]</a></span>; 10 October 1901 – 11 January 1966) was an Italian Swiss sculptor, painter, draughtsman and printmaker. He was born in the canton <a href="/wiki/Graub%C3%BCnden" title="Graubünden">Graubünden</a>'s southerly alpine valley <a href="/wiki/Val_Bregaglia" title="Val Bregaglia">Val Bregaglia</a>, as the eldest of four children to <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Giacometti" title="Giovanni Giacometti">Giovanni Giacometti</a>, a well-known <a href="/wiki/Post-Impressionism" title="Post-Impressionism">post-Impressionist</a> painter. Coming from an artistic background, he was interested in art from an early age.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alberto_Giacometti&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Giacometti was born in <a href="/wiki/Borgonovo" title="Borgonovo">Borgonovo</a>, now part of the Swiss municipality of <a href="/wiki/Bregaglia" title="Bregaglia">Bregaglia</a>, near the Italian border. He was a descendant of Protestant refugees escaping the <a href="/wiki/Italian_Inquisition" title="Italian Inquisition" class="mw-redirect">inquisition</a>. Alberto attended the <a href="/wiki/Geneva" title="Geneva">Geneva</a> School of <a href="/wiki/Fine_art" title="Fine art">Fine Arts</a>. His brothers <a href="/wiki/Diego_Giacometti" title="Diego Giacometti">Diego</a> (1902–85) and <a href="/wiki/Bruno_Giacometti" title="Bruno Giacometti">Bruno</a> (1907–2012) would go on to become artists as well. Additionally, <a href="/wiki/Zaccaria_Giacometti" title="Zaccaria Giacometti">Zaccaria Giacometti</a>, later professor of constitutional law and chancellor of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Zurich" title="University of Zurich">University of Zurich</a> grew up together with them, having been orphaned at the age of 12 in 1905.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In 1922 he moved to Paris to study under the sculptor <a href="/wiki/Antoine_Bourdelle" title="Antoine Bourdelle">Antoine Bourdelle</a>, an associate of <a href="/wiki/Auguste_Rodin" title="Auguste Rodin">Rodin</a>. It was there that Giacometti experimented with <a href="/wiki/Cubism" title="Cubism">cubism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Surrealism" title="Surrealism">surrealism</a> and came to be regarded as one of the leading surrealist sculptors. Among his associates were <a href="/wiki/Joan_Mir%C3%B3" title="Joan Miró">Miró</a>, <a href="/wiki/Max_Ernst" title="Max Ernst">Max Ernst</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pablo_Picasso" title="Pablo Picasso">Picasso</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bror_Hjorth" title="Bror Hjorth">Bror Hjorth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Balthus" title="Balthus">Balthus</a>.</p>
<p>Between 1936 and 1940, Giacometti concentrated his sculpting on the human head, focusing on the sitter's gaze. He preferred models he was close to, his sister and the artist <a href="/wiki/Isabel_Rawsthorne" title="Isabel Rawsthorne" class="mw-redirect">Isabel Rawsthorne</a> (then known as Isabel Delmer). This was followed by a phase in which his statues of Isabel became stretched out; her limbs elongated.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Obsessed with creating his sculptures exactly as he envisioned through his unique view of reality, he often carved until they were as thin as nails and reduced to the size of a pack of cigarettes, much to his consternation. A friend of his once said that if Giacometti decided to sculpt you, "he would make your head look like the blade of a knife". After his marriage to Annette Arm in 1946 his tiny sculptures became larger, but the larger they grew, the thinner they became. Giacometti said that the final result represented the sensation he felt when he looked at a woman.</p>
<p>His paintings underwent a parallel procedure. The figures appear isolated and severely attenuated, as the result of continuous reworking. Subjects were frequently revisited: one of his favorite models was his younger brother <a href="/wiki/Diego_Giacometti" title="Diego Giacometti">Diego Giacometti</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> A third brother, <a href="/wiki/Bruno_Giacometti" title="Bruno Giacometti">Bruno Giacometti</a>, was a noted architect.</p>

<p>In 1958 Giacometti was asked to create a monumental sculpture for the <a href="/wiki/Chase_Manhattan_Bank" title="Chase Manhattan Bank" class="mw-redirect">Chase Manhattan Bank</a> building in New York, which was beginning construction. Although he had for many years "harbored an ambition to create work for a public square",<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> he "had never set foot in New York, and knew nothing about life in a rapidly evolving metropolis. Nor had he ever laid eyes on an actual <a href="/wiki/Skyscraper" title="Skyscraper">skyscraper</a>", according to his biographer James Lord.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Giacometti's work on the project resulted in the four figures of standing women—his largest sculptures—entitled <i>Grande femme debout</i> I through IV (1960). The commission was never completed, however, because Giacometti was unsatisfied by the relationship between the sculpture and the site, and abandoned the project.</p>
<p>In 1962, Giacometti was awarded the grand prize for sculpture at the <a href="/wiki/Venice_Biennale" title="Venice Biennale">Venice Biennale</a>, and the award brought with it worldwide fame. Even when he had achieved popularity and his work was in demand, he still reworked models, often destroying them or setting them aside to be returned to years later. The prints produced by Giacometti are often overlooked but the catalogue raisonné, <i>Giacometti – The Complete Graphics and 15 Drawings by Herbert Lust</i> (Tudor 1970), comments on their impact and gives details of the number of copies of each print. Some of his most important images were in editions of only 30 and many were described as rare in 1970.</p>
<p>In his later years Giacometti's works were shown in a number of large exhibitions throughout Europe. Riding a wave of international popularity, and despite his declining health, he travelled to the United States in 1965 for an exhibition of his works at the <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_Modern_Art" title="Museum of Modern Art">Museum of Modern Art</a> in New York. As his last work he prepared the text for the book <i>Paris sans fin</i>, a sequence of 150 <a href="/wiki/Lithographs" title="Lithographs" class="mw-redirect">lithographs</a> containing memories of all the places where he had lived.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alberto_Giacometti&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Giacometti died in 1966 of heart disease (<a href="/wiki/Pericarditis" title="Pericarditis">pericarditis</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Chronic_bronchitis" title="Chronic bronchitis" class="mw-redirect">chronic bronchitis</a> at the Kantonsspital in <a href="/wiki/Chur" title="Chur">Chur</a>, Switzerland. His body was returned to his birthplace in Borgonovo, where he was interred close to his parents. In May 2007 the executor of his widow's estate, former French foreign minister <a href="/wiki/Roland_Dumas" title="Roland Dumas">Roland Dumas</a>, was convicted of illegally selling Giacometti's works to a top auctioneer, Jacques Tajan, who was also convicted. Both were ordered to pay €850,000 to the Alberto and Annette Giacometti Foundation.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>Regarding Giacometti's sculptural technique and according to the <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Museum_of_Art" title="Metropolitan Museum of Art">Metropolitan Museum of Art</a>: "The rough, eroded, heavily worked surfaces of Three Men Walking (II), 1949, typify his technique. Reduced, as they are, to their very core, these figures evoke lone trees in winter that have lost their foliage. Within this style, Giacometti would rarely deviate from the three themes that preoccupied him—the walking man; the standing, nude woman; and the bust—or all three, combined in various groupings<i>.</i>"<sup id="cite_ref-Metropolitan_Museum_of_Art_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Metropolitan_Museum_of_Art-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In a letter to <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Matisse" title="Pierre Matisse">Pierre Matisse</a>, Giacometti wrote: "Figures were never a compact mass but like a transparent construction".<sup id="cite_ref-MOMA_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MOMA-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> In the letter, Giacometti writes about how he looked back at the realist, classical busts of his youth with nostalgia, and tells the story of the existential crisis which precipitated the style he became known for.</p>
<p>"[I rediscovered] the wish to make compositions with figures. For this I had to make (quickly I thought; in passing), one or two studies from nature, just enough to understand the construction of a head, of a whole figure, and in 1935 I took a model. This study should take, I thought, two weeks and then I could realize my compositions...I worked with the model all day from 1935 to 1940...Nothing was as I imagined. A head, became for me an object completely unknown and without dimensions."<sup id="cite_ref-MOMA_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MOMA-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Since Giacometti achieved exquisite realism with facility when he was executing busts in his early adolescence, Giacometti's difficulty in re-approaching the figure as an adult is generally understood as a sign of existential struggle for meaning, rather than as a technical deficit.</p>
<p>Giacometti was a key player in the <a href="/wiki/Surrealist" title="Surrealist" class="mw-redirect">Surrealist</a> art movement, but his work resists easy categorization. Some describe it as formalist, others argue it is expressionist or otherwise having to do with what <a href="/wiki/Deleuze" title="Deleuze" class="mw-redirect">Deleuze</a> calls "blocs of sensation" (as in Deleuze's analysis of <a href="/wiki/Francis_Bacon_(artist)" title="Francis Bacon (artist)">Francis Bacon</a>). Even after his excommunication from the Surrealist group, while the intention of his sculpting was usually imitation, the end products were an expression of his emotional response to the subject. He attempted to create renditions of his models the way he saw them, and the way he thought they ought to be seen. He once said that he was sculpting not the human figure but "the shadow that is cast".</p>
<p>Scholar <a href="/wiki/William_Barrett_(philosopher)" title="William Barrett (philosopher)">William Barrett</a> in <i>Irrational Man: A Study in Existential Philosophy</i> (1962), argues that the attenuated forms of Giacometti's figures reflect the view of 20th century <a href="/wiki/Modernism" title="Modernism">modernism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Existentialism" title="Existentialism">existentialism</a> that modern life is increasingly empty and devoid of meaning. "All the sculptures of today, like those of the past, will end one day in pieces...So it is important to fashion ones work carefully in its smallest recess and charge every particle of matter with life."</p>
<p>A new exhibition in Paris, since September 2011, shows how Giacometti strongly drew his inspiration for his work from <a href="/wiki/Etruscan_art" title="Etruscan art">Etruscan art</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alberto_Giacometti&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Exhibitions">Exhibitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alberto_Giacometti&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Exhibitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Giacometti's work has been the subject of numerous solo exhibitions including most recently <a href="/wiki/Pera_Museum" title="Pera Museum">Pera Museum</a>, Istanbul (2015) <a href="/wiki/Pushkin_Museum" title="Pushkin Museum">Pushkin Museum</a>, Moscow (2008); “The Studio of Alberto Giacometti: Collection of the Fondation Alberto et Annette Giacometti”, <a href="/wiki/Centre_Pompidou" title="Centre Pompidou" class="mw-redirect">Centre Pompidou</a>, Paris (2007–2008); Kunsthal Rotterdam (2008); <a href="/wiki/Fondation_Beyeler" title="Fondation Beyeler" class="mw-redirect">Fondation Beyeler</a>, Basel (2009), Buenos Aires (2012); and Kunsthalle Hamburg (2013).</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/National_Portrait_Gallery,_London" title="National Portrait Gallery, London">National Portrait Gallery, London</a>'s first solo exhibition of Giacometti's work, <i>Pure Presence</i> opened to five star reviews on 13 October 2015 (to January 10, 2016, in honour of the fiftieth anniversary of the artist's death).<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> <sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Art_Foundations">Art Foundations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alberto_Giacometti&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Art Foundations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Fondation_Alberto_et_Annette_Giacometti" title="Fondation Alberto et Annette Giacometti">Fondation Alberto et Annette Giacometti</a>, having received a bequest from Alberto Giacometti's widow Annette, holds a collection of circa 5,000 works, frequently displayed around the world through exhibitions and long-term loans. A public interest institution, the Foundation was created in 2003 and aims at promoting, disseminating, preserving and protecting Alberto Giacometti's work.</p>
<p>The <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.giacometti-stiftung.ch/">Alberto Giacometti-Stiftung</a> established in Zürich in 1965, holds a smaller collection of works acquired from the collection of the Pittsburgh industrialist <a href="/wiki/G._David_Thompson" title="G. David Thompson">G. David Thompson</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_sales">Notable sales</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alberto_Giacometti&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Notable sales">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In November 2000 a Giacometti bronze, <i>Grande Femme Debout I</i>, sold for <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">$</a>14.3 million.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>Grande Femme Debout II</i> was bought by the <a href="/wiki/Gagosian_Gallery" title="Gagosian Gallery">Gagosian Gallery</a> for $27.4 million at <a href="/wiki/Christie%27s" title="Christie's">Christie's</a> auction in New York City on May 6, 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/L%27Homme_qui_marche_I" title="L'Homme qui marche I">L'Homme qui marche I</a></i>, a life-sized bronze sculpture of a man, became one of the most expensive works of art and the most expensive sculpture ever sold at auction on February 2, 2010, when it sold for £65 million (US$104.3 million) at <a href="/wiki/Sotheby%27s" title="Sotheby's">Sotheby's</a>, London.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Grande_t%C3%AAte_mince" title="Grande tête mince">Grande tête mince</a></i>, a large bronze bust, sold for $53.3 million just three months later.</p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/L%27Homme_au_doigt" title="L'Homme au doigt">L'Homme au doigt</a></i> (<i>Pointing Man</i>) sold for $126 million (£81314455.32), or $141.3 million with fees, in Christie's May 11, 2015 Looking Forward to the Past sale in New York, a record for a sculpture at auction. The work had been in the same private collection for 45 years.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_legacy">Other legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alberto_Giacometti&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Other legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Giacometti created the monument on the grave of <a href="/wiki/Gerda_Taro" title="Gerda Taro">Gerda Taro</a> at <a href="/wiki/P%C3%A8re_Lachaise_Cemetery" title="Père Lachaise Cemetery">Père Lachaise Cemetery</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In 2001 he was included in the <a href="/wiki/Painting_the_Century:_101_Portrait_Masterpieces_1900%E2%80%932000" title="Painting the Century: 101 Portrait Masterpieces 1900–2000">Painting the Century: 101 Portrait Masterpieces 1900–2000</a> exhibition held at the <a href="/wiki/National_Portrait_Gallery_(England)" title="National Portrait Gallery (England)" class="mw-redirect">National Portrait Gallery</a>, London.</p>
<p>Giacometti and his sculpture <i>L'Homme qui marche I</i> appear on the current <a href="/wiki/Banknotes_of_Switzerland" title="Banknotes of Switzerland" class="mw-redirect">100 Swiss Franc banknote</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>According to a lecture by <a href="/wiki/Michael_Peppiatt" title="Michael Peppiatt">Michael Peppiatt</a> at Cambridge University on July 8, 2010, Giacometti, who had a friendship with author/playwright <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Beckett" title="Samuel Beckett">Samuel Beckett</a>, created a tree for the <a href="/wiki/Theatrical_scenery" title="Theatrical scenery">set</a> of a 1961 Paris production of <i><a href="/wiki/Waiting_For_Godot" title="Waiting For Godot" class="mw-redirect">Waiting For Godot</a></i>.</p>
