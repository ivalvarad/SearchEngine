<p><b>Welcome to Wikipedia!</b></p>
<p>You have probably already been using Wikipedia for some time, maybe you also have been doing some editing of existing articles - maybe you have been doing a lot of editing of other peoples’ articles but now you are ready to start your own article from scratch. Jump in. <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Be_bold" title="Wikipedia:Be bold">Be Bold</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Introduction">Introduction</span></h2>
<p>This guide will help you to start your first encyclopedia article and keep you inside the guard rails. We will explain some of the DOs and DON'Ts, then show you how to create an article. When you're ready to start writing, consider using the <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Article_wizard" title="Wikipedia:Article wizard">Article Wizard</a> to help you create the article – it will walk you through these steps.</p>
<p>Here are some tips that may help you along the way:</p>
<ol>
<li>You can only create a new article directly once you've <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:SIGNUP" title="Wikipedia:SIGNUP" class="mw-redirect">registered an account</a> – you need only choose a username and password. If you don't want to register for an account, registered and unregistered users alike may submit their article for review and publishing by others at the <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:AfC" title="Wikipedia:AfC" class="mw-redirect">Articles for Creation</a> project.</li>
<li>Wikipedia covers certain kinds of subjects and not others. Wikipedia requires that subjects for articles be <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Notability" title="Wikipedia:Notability">"worthy of notice"</a>; this concept of notability serves to avoid the indiscriminate inclusion of new topics. If the topic is likely to be suitable for an encyclopedia, go ahead; if you're unsure, or the article is about you or something you are closely connected to, you can ask questions at the Wikipedia <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Teahouse" title="Wikipedia:Teahouse">Teahouse</a>.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Special:Search" title="Special:Search">Search Wikipedia first</a></b> in case an article <i>already exists</i> on the subject, perhaps under a different title. If the article already exists, feel free to practice by making any constructive edits to improve it.</li>
<li><b>Practice first.</b> Before starting, try editing existing articles to get a feel for writing and for using Wikipedia's mark-up language – we recommend that you first take a tour through the <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Tutorial" title="Wikipedia:Tutorial">Wikipedia tutorial</a> or review <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Contributing_to_Wikipedia" title="Wikipedia:Contributing to Wikipedia">contributing to Wikipedia</a> to learn editing basics.</li>
<li><b>Gather references</b> both to use as source(s) of the information you will include and to demonstrate the <b><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:N" title="Wikipedia:N" class="mw-redirect">notability</a></b> of your article's subject matter. Wikipedia requires the use of <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:IRS" title="Wikipedia:IRS" class="mw-redirect">reliable published sources</a>. Sources, particularly internet sources, should also be stable, that is they should be high enough quality that they will continue to exist for future generations. There are many places to find reliable sources, including your local library, but if internet-based sources are to be used, start with <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://books.google.com/bkshp?hl=en&amp;tab=wp">books</a> and <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.google.com/search?hl=en&amp;gl=us&amp;tbm=nws&amp;q=a#hl=en&amp;safe=off&amp;gl=us&amp;tbs=ar:1&amp;tbm=nws&amp;sclient=psy-ab&amp;q=%22your+search+here%22&amp;oq=%22your+search+here%22&amp;gs_l=serp.3...13840.16191.2.16495.2.2.0.0.0.0.75.150.2.2.0...0.0...1c.gI5pLKtnlBw&amp;pbx=1&amp;bav=on.2,or.r_gc.r_pw.r_qf.&amp;fp=eaf35048cd590c78&amp;biw=1550&amp;bih=1201">news archive</a> searches rather than a simple web search. Extra care should be taken to make sure that articles on <b><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:BLP" title="Wikipedia:BLP" class="mw-redirect">living persons</a></b> have reliable sources – articles about living people without reliable sources may be deleted, especially if they include negative or controversial content. References to blogs, personal websites, <a href="/wiki/Facebook" title="Facebook">Facebook</a> and <a href="/wiki/YouTube" title="YouTube">YouTube</a> are unsuitable and material obtained from them may be challenged and removed.</li>
<li><b>Consider requesting feedback.</b> You can request feedback on articles you would like to create in a number of places, including the <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Talk_page" title="Wikipedia:Talk page" class="mw-redirect">talk page</a> of a related <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:WikiProject" title="Wikipedia:WikiProject">WikiProject</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:TEAHOUSE" title="Wikipedia:TEAHOUSE" class="mw-redirect">Teahouse</a>.</li>
<li><b>Consider creating the article first in your user space.</b> As a registered user, you have your own <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:USER" title="Wikipedia:USER" class="mw-redirect">user space</a>. You can start the new article there, on a <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:SUBP" title="Wikipedia:SUBP" class="mw-redirect">subpage</a>; you can get it in shape, take your time, ask other editors to help work on it, and only move it into the <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:MAINSPACE" title="Wikipedia:MAINSPACE" class="mw-redirect">"live" part of Wikipedia</a> once it is ready to go. To create your own subpage, see <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Subpages#How_to_create_user_subpages" title="Wikipedia:Subpages">here</a>. When the new article is "ready for prime time", you can <a href="/wiki/Help:Moving_a_page" title="Help:Moving a page" class="mw-redirect">move</a> it into the main article space. (Notes. The <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Article_wizard" title="Wikipedia:Article wizard">Article Wizard</a> has an option to create these kinds of draft pages. Even in user space, unacceptable articles (see below) are liable to be nominated for deletion.)</li>
</ol>
<dl>
<dd><i>Remember that if the article is not acceptable, it will be <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:CSD" title="Wikipedia:CSD" class="mw-redirect">deleted quickly</a>. Wikipedia has a <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:NPP" title="Wikipedia:NPP" class="mw-redirect">new pages patrol</a> division where people check new articles shortly after creation.</i>
<ul>
<li><b>Articles that do not meet <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:N" title="Wikipedia:N" class="mw-redirect">notability guidelines</a> and do not cite <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:RS" title="Wikipedia:RS" class="mw-redirect">reliable published sources</a> are likely to be deleted.</b></li>
<li><b>Do not create</b> pages about <b><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Autobiography" title="Wikipedia:Autobiography">yourself</a></b>, your <b><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:FAQ/Organizations" title="Wikipedia:FAQ/Organizations">company</a></b>, your <b><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:COI" title="Wikipedia:COI" class="mw-redirect">band</a></b>, or your <b><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:COI" title="Wikipedia:COI" class="mw-redirect">friends</a></b>; pages that <b><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Spam" title="Wikipedia:Spam">advertise</a></b>; <b><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:OR" title="Wikipedia:OR" class="mw-redirect">personal essays</a></b>; or other articles you would <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:NOT" title="Wikipedia:NOT" class="mw-redirect">not find in an encyclopedia</a>.</li>
<li><b>Be careful</b> about the following: <b><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Plagiarism" title="Wikipedia:Plagiarism">copying things</a></b>, <b>controversial</b> material, extremely <b>short articles</b>, and <b>local-interest articles</b>.</li>
</ul>
</dd>
</dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Search_for_an_existing_article">Search for an existing article</span></h2>
<p>Wikipedia already has 4,991,428 articles. Before creating an article, try to make sure there is not already an article on the same topic, perhaps under a slightly different name. <a href="/wiki/Help:Searching" title="Help:Searching">Search</a> for the article, and review Wikipedia's <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Article_titles" title="Wikipedia:Article titles">article titling policy</a> before creating your first article. If an article on your topic already exists, but you think people might look for it under some different name or spelling, learn how to <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Redirect" title="Wikipedia:Redirect">create <i>redirects</i> to alternative titles</a>; adding needed redirects is a good way to help Wikipedia. Also, remember to check the article's <a href="/wiki/Special:Log/delete" title="Special:Log/delete">deletion log</a> in order to avoid creating an article that has already been deleted. (In some cases, the topic may be suitable even if deleted in the past; the past deletion may have been because it was a copyright violation, did not explain the importance of the topic, or on other grounds addressed to the writing rather that the topic's suitability.)</p>
<p>If a search does not find the topic, consider broadening your search to find existing articles that might include the subject of your article. For example, if you want to write an article about a band member, you might search for the band and then add information about your subject as a <a href="/wiki/Help:Section" title="Help:Section">section</a> within that broader article.</p>

<p>Gather sources for the information you will be writing about. To be worthy of inclusion in an encyclopedia, a subject must be sufficiently <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:N" title="Wikipedia:N" class="mw-redirect">notable</a>, and that notability must be <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:V" title="Wikipedia:V" class="mw-redirect">verifiable</a> through <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:REF" title="Wikipedia:REF" class="mw-redirect">citations</a> to <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:RS" title="Wikipedia:RS" class="mw-redirect">reliable sources</a>.</p>
<p>As noted, the sources you use must be <u>reliable</u>; that is, they must be sources that exercise some form of editorial control and have some reputation for fact checking and accuracy. Print sources (and web-based versions of those sources) tend to be the most reliable, though some web-only sources may also be reliable. Examples might include (but are not limited to) books published by major publishing houses, newspapers, magazines, peer-reviewed scholarly journals, websites of any of the above, and other websites that meet the same requirements as a reputable print-based source.</p>
<p>In general, sources with <b>no</b> editorial control are not reliable. These include (but are not limited to) books published by vanity presses, self-published 'zines', blogs, web forums, usenet discussions, personal social media, fan sites, vanity websites that permit the creation of self-promotional articles, and other similar venues. If anyone at all can post information without anyone else checking that information, it is probably not reliable.</p>
<p>To put it simply, if there are reliable sources (such as newspapers, journals, or books) with extensive information published over an extended period about a subject, then that subject is notable and you must cite such sources as part of the process of creating (or expanding) the Wikipedia article. If you cannot find such <i>reliable sources</i> that provide extensive and comprehensive information about your proposed subject, then the subject is not notable or verifiable and almost certainly will be deleted. So your first job is to <b>go find references</b> to cite.</p>
<p>There are many places to find reliable sources, including your local library, but if internet-based sources are to be used, start with <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://books.google.com/bkshp?hl=en&amp;tab=wp">books</a> and <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.google.com/search?hl=en&amp;gl=us&amp;tbm=nws&amp;q=a#hl=en&amp;safe=off&amp;gl=us&amp;tbs=ar:1&amp;tbm=nws&amp;sclient=psy-ab&amp;q=%22your+search+here%22&amp;oq=%22your+search+here%22&amp;gs_l=serp.3...13840.16191.2.16495.2.2.0.0.0.0.75.150.2.2.0...0.0...1c.gI5pLKtnlBw&amp;pbx=1&amp;bav=on.2,or.r_gc.r_pw.r_qf.&amp;fp=eaf35048cd590c78&amp;biw=1550&amp;bih=1201">news archive</a> searches rather than a web search.</p>
<p>Once you have references for your article, you can learn to place the references into the article by reading <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Referencing_for_beginners" title="Wikipedia:Referencing for beginners" class="mw-redirect">Wikipedia:Referencing for beginners</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">Wikipedia:Citing sources</a>. Do not worry too much about formatting citations properly. It would be great if you did that, but the main thing is to <b>get references into the article</b>, even if they are not perfectly formatted.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Things_to_avoid">Things to avoid</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main pages: <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:What_Wikipedia_is_not" title="Wikipedia:What Wikipedia is not">Wikipedia:What Wikipedia is not</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Avoiding_common_mistakes" title="Wikipedia:Avoiding common mistakes">Wikipedia:Avoiding common mistakes</a></div>
<dl>
<dt>Articles about yourself, your friends, your website, a band you're in, your teacher, a word you made up, or a story you wrote&#160;</dt>
<dd>If you <i>are</i> worthy of inclusion in the encyclopedia, let someone else add an article for you. Putting your friends in an encyclopedia may seem like a nice surprise or an amusing joke, but articles like this are likely to be <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Articles_for_deletion" title="Wikipedia:Articles for deletion">removed</a>. In this process, feelings may be hurt and you may be <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Blocking_Policy" title="Wikipedia:Blocking Policy" class="mw-redirect">blocked</a> from editing if you repeatedly make attempts to re-create the article. These things can be avoided by a little forethought on your part. The article may remain if you have enough humility to make it neutral and you really are notable, but even then it's best to submit a draft for approval and <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:CONSENSUS" title="Wikipedia:CONSENSUS" class="mw-redirect">consensus</a> of the community instead of just posting it up, since unconscious biases may still exist of which you may not be aware.</dd>
<dt>Non-notable topics&#160;</dt>
<dd>People frequently add pages to Wikipedia without considering <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Notability" title="Wikipedia:Notability">whether the topic is really notable enough</a> to go into an encyclopedia. Because Wikipedia does not have the space limitations of paper-based encyclopedias, our <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Notability" title="Wikipedia:Notability">notability</a> policies and guidelines allow a wide range of articles – however, they do not allow <i>every</i> topic to be included. A particularly common special case of this is pages about people, companies, or groups of people, that do not substantiate the notability or importance of their subject with reliable sources, so we have decided that such pages may be speedily deleted under our <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:SPEEDY" title="Wikipedia:SPEEDY" class="mw-redirect">WP:SPEEDY</a> policy. This can offend – so <i>please</i> consider whether your chosen topic is notable enough for Wikipedia, and then substantiate the notability or importance of your subject by citing those reliable sources in the process of creating your article. <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:NOT" title="Wikipedia:NOT" class="mw-redirect">Wikipedia is not</a> a directory of everything in existence.</dd>
<dt>Advertising&#160;</dt>
<dd>Please do not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Spam" title="Wikipedia:Spam">try to promote your product or business</a>. Please do not insert external links to your commercial website unless a neutral party would judge that the link truly belongs in the article; we do have articles about products like <a href="/wiki/Kleenex" title="Kleenex">Kleenex</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sharpie_(marker)" title="Sharpie (marker)">Sharpies</a>, or notable businesses such as <a href="/wiki/McDonald%27s" title="McDonald's">McDonald's</a>, but if you are writing about a product or business be sure you write from a <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Neutral_point_of_view" title="Wikipedia:Neutral point of view">neutral point of view</a>, that you have no <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:COI" title="Wikipedia:COI" class="mw-redirect">conflict of interest</a>, and that you are able to find references in <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:RS" title="Wikipedia:RS" class="mw-redirect">reliable sources</a> that are independent from the subject you are writing about.</dd>
<dt>Personal essays or original research&#160;</dt>
<dd>Wikipedia surveys <i>existing</i> human knowledge; it is not a place to publish new work. Do not write articles that present your own <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research" title="Wikipedia:No original research">original theories, opinions, or insights,</a> <i>even if</i> you can support them by reference to accepted work. A common mistake is to present a novel synthesis of ideas in an article. Remember, just because both Fact A and Fact B are true does not mean that A caused B, or vice versa (<a href="/wiki/Fallacies" title="Fallacies" class="mw-redirect">fallacies</a>). If the synthesis or causation is true, locate and cite <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:RS" title="Wikipedia:RS" class="mw-redirect">reliable sources</a> that report the connection.</dd>
<dt>A single sentence or only a website link</dt>
<dd>Articles need to have real content of their own.</dd>
<dt>Attacks on a person or organization&#160;</dt>
<dd>Material that violates our <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:BLP" title="Wikipedia:BLP" class="mw-redirect">biographies of living persons</a> policy or is intended to threaten, defame, or harass its subject or another entity is not permitted. Unsourced negative information, especially in articles about living people, is quickly removed, and <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Attack_page" title="Wikipedia:Attack page">attack pages</a> may be deleted immediately.</dd>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="And_be_careful_about...">And be careful about...</span></h2>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:30%; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 1em; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="background-color: #dddddd; color: black; text-align: center; font-size: larger; font-weight: bold;">Copyright</div>
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>As a general rule, <b>do not copy-paste text from other websites</b>. <small>(There are a few limited exceptions, and a few words as part of a properly <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:CITE" title="Wikipedia:CITE" class="mw-redirect">cited</a> and clearly attributed quotation is OK.)</small></p>
</div>
</div>
<div style="text-align: left;"><cite>– <i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Copy-paste" title="Wikipedia:Copy-paste" class="mw-redirect">Wikipedia:Copy-paste</a></i></cite></div>
</div>
<dl>
<dt>Copying things. Do not violate copyrights</dt>
<dd>Never copy and paste text into a Wikipedia article unless it is a relatively short quotation, placed in quotation marks, and cited using an <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Cite_sources" title="Wikipedia:Cite sources" class="mw-redirect">inline citation</a>. Even material that you are <i>sure</i> is in the <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Public_domain" title="Wikipedia:Public domain">public domain</a> must be attributed to the source, or the result, while not a copyright violation, is <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:PLAGIARISM" title="Wikipedia:PLAGIARISM" class="mw-redirect">plagiarism</a>. Also note that most web pages <b>are not</b> in the public domain and most song <a href="/wiki/Lyrics" title="Lyrics">lyrics</a> <b>are not</b> either. In fact, most things published after 1923, and almost everything written since <a href="/wiki/January_1" title="January 1">January 1</a>, <a href="/wiki/1978" title="1978">1978</a> are automatically under <a href="/wiki/Copyright" title="Copyright">copyright</a> <i>even if they have no copyright notice or © symbol.</i> If you think what you are contributing is in the public domain, <i>say where you got it,</i> either in the article or on the discussion page, and on the discussion page give the reason why you think it is in the public domain (e.g. "It was published in 1895..."). For more information, see <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Copyrights" title="Wikipedia:Copyrights">Wikipedia:Copyrights</a> (which includes instructions for verifying permission to copy previously published text) and <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:NFC#Text" title="Wikipedia:NFC" class="mw-redirect">our non-free content guidelines for text</a>. Finally, please note that superficial modification of material, such as minor rewording, is insufficient to avoid plagiarism and copyright violations. See <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Close_paraphrasing" title="Wikipedia:Close paraphrasing">Wikipedia:Close paraphrasing</a>.</dd>
</dl>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:30%; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 1em; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="background-color: #dddddd; color: black; text-align: center; font-size: larger; font-weight: bold;">Good sources</div>
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>1. have a reputation for reliability: they are <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:RS" title="Wikipedia:RS" class="mw-redirect">reliable sources</a><br />
2. are independent of the subject<br />
3. are <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:V" title="Wikipedia:V" class="mw-redirect">verifiable</a> by other editors</p>
</div>
</div>
</div>
<dl>
<dt>Good research and citing your sources</dt>
<dd>Articles written out of thin air may be better than nothing, but they are hard to <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">verify</a>, which is an important part of building a trusted reference work. Please research with the <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Reliable_sources" title="Wikipedia:Reliable sources" class="mw-redirect">best sources available</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Cite_sources" title="Wikipedia:Cite sources" class="mw-redirect">cite them</a> properly. Doing this, along with not copying text, will help avoid any possibility of <a href="/wiki/Plagiarism" title="Plagiarism">plagiarism</a>. We welcome <i>good</i> short articles, called "<a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Stub" title="Wikipedia:Stub">stubs</a>", that can serve as launching pads from which others can take off – stubs can be relatively short, a few sentences, but should provide some useful information. If you do not have enough material to write a good stub, you probably should not create an article. At the end of a stub, you should include a "stub template" like this: {{stub}}. (Other Wikipedians will appreciate it if you use a more specific stub template, like {{art-stub}}. See the <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:WikiProject_Stub_sorting/Stub_types" title="Wikipedia:WikiProject Stub sorting/Stub types">list of stub types</a> for a list of all specific stub templates.) Stubs help track articles that need expansion.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Articles about <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:BLP" title="Wikipedia:BLP" class="mw-redirect">living persons</a></dt>
<dd>Articles written about living persons must be referenced so that they can be <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:V" title="Wikipedia:V" class="mw-redirect">verified</a>. Biographies about living subjects that lack sources may be deleted.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Advocacy and controversial material</dt>
<dd>Please do not write articles that advocate one particular viewpoint on politics, religion, or anything else. Understand what we mean by a <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Neutral_point_of_view" title="Wikipedia:Neutral point of view">neutral point of view</a> before tackling this sort of topic.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Articles that contain different definitions of the topic</dt>
<dd>Articles are primarily about what something <b>is</b>, <i>not</i> any term(s). If the article is <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Wikipedia_is_not_a_dictionary" title="Wikipedia:Wikipedia is not a dictionary">just about a word or phrase</a> and especially if there are very different ways that a term is used, it usually belongs in <a href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Main_Page" class="extiw" title="wiktionary:Main Page">Wiktionary</a>. Instead, try to write a good short first paragraph that <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:GOODDEF" title="Wikipedia:GOODDEF" class="mw-redirect">defines</a> one <i>subject</i> as well as some more material to go with it.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Organization</dt>
<dd>Make sure there are incoming links to the new article from other Wikipedia articles (click "What links here" in the toolbox) and that the new article is included in at least one appropriate category (see <a href="/wiki/Help:Category" title="Help:Category">help:category</a>). Otherwise it will be difficult for readers to find the article.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Local-interest articles</dt>
<dd>These are articles about places like schools, or streets that are of interest to a relatively small number of people such as alumni or people who live nearby. <i>There is no consensus</i> about such articles, but some will challenge them if they include nothing that shows how the place is special and different from tens of thousands of similar places. Photographs add interest. Try to give <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Places_of_local_interest" title="Wikipedia:Places of local interest">local-interest articles</a> local colour. <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Third-party_sources" title="Wikipedia:Third-party sources">Third-party sources</a> are the only way to prove that the subject you are writing about is <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:N" title="Wikipedia:N" class="mw-redirect">notable</a>.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Breaking news events</dt>
<dd>While Wikipedia accepts articles about notable recent events, articles about breaking news events with no enduring notability are <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:NOTNEWS" title="Wikipedia:NOTNEWS" class="mw-redirect">not appropriate for our project</a>. Consider writing such articles on our sister project <a href="/wiki/Wikinews" title="Wikinews">Wikinews</a>. See <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Notability_(events)" title="Wikipedia:Notability (events)">Wikipedia:Notability (events)</a> for further information.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Editing on the wrong page</dt>
<dd>If you're trying to create a new page, you'll start with a completely empty edit box. If you see text in the editing box that is filled with words you didn't write (for example, the contents of this page), you're accidentally editing a pre-existing page. Don't save your changes. See <a href="#How_to_create_a_page">#How to create a page</a> below, and start over.</dd>
</dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Are_you_closely_connected_to_the_article_topic.3F">Are you closely connected to the article topic?</span></h2>
<p>Wikipedia is the encyclopedia that anyone can edit, but there are special guidelines for editors who are paid or sponsored. These guidelines are intended to prevent biased articles, and maintain the public's trust that content in Wikipedia is impartial and has been added in good faith. (See Wikipedia's <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Conflict_of_interest" title="Wikipedia:Conflict of interest">conflict of interest (COI)</a> guideline.)</p>
<p>The official guidelines are that editors <b>must be volunteers.</b> That means Wikipedia discourages editing articles about individuals, companies, organizations, products/services, or political causes that pay you directly or indirectly. This includes in-house PR departments and marketing departments, other company employees, <a href="/wiki/Public_relations" title="Public relations">public relations</a> firms and publicists, social media consultants, and <a href="/wiki/Online_reputation_management" title="Online reputation management" class="mw-redirect">online reputation management</a> consultants. However, Wikipedia recognizes the large volume of good faith contributions by people who have some affiliation to the articles they work on.</p>
<p>Here are some ground rules. If you break these rules, your edits are likely to be reverted, and the article(s) and your other edits may get extra scrutiny from other Wikipedia editors. Your account may also be blocked.</p>
<table class="wikitable">
<tr>
<th>Things to avoid</th>
<th>Things to be careful about</th>
<th>Great ways to contribute</th>
</tr>
<tr>
<td width="30%" style="vertical-align:top;">
<ul>
<li>Don't add promotional language</li>
<li>Don't remove negative/critical text from an article</li>
<li>Don't make a "group" account for multiple people to share</li>
<li>Don't neglect to disclose your affiliation on the article's talk page</li>
</ul>
</td>
<td width="30%" style="vertical-align:top;">
<ul>
<li>Maintain a neutral, objective tone in any content you add or edit</li>
<li>Cite secondary sources (e.g., a major media article) for any new statements you add – even if you are confident a statement is true (e.g., it is about your work), only say it if it has been restated already in a secondary source.</li>
</ul>
</td>
<td width="40%" style="vertical-align:top;">
<ul>
<li>Make minor edits/corrections to articles (e.g., typos, fixing links, adding references to new secondary sources)</li>
<li>If you are biased, suggest new article text or edits on the <i><a href="/wiki/Help:Using_talk_pages" title="Help:Using talk pages">article talk page</a></i> (not on the main article page).</li>
<li>Disclose your relationship to the client/topic.</li>
<li>Edit using personal accounts.</li>
<li>Recruit help: Seek out a sponsor (volunteer editor) who has worked on similar articles, or submit ideas for article topics via <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:RA" title="Wikipedia:RA" class="mw-redirect">Requested articles</a>.</li>
</ul>
</td>
</tr>
</table>
<p>Note that this has to do only with conflict of interest. Editors are encouraged to write on topics related to their expertise: e.g., a NASA staffperson might write about planets, or an academic researcher might write about their field. Also, <a class="external text" href="http://outreach.wikimedia.org/wiki/Wikipedian_in_Residence">Wikipedians-in-residence</a> or other interns who are paid, hosted or otherwise sponsored by a scientific or cultural institution can upload content and write articles in partnership with curators, indirectly providing positive branding for their hosts.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="How_to_create_a_page">How to create a page</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote">For a technical guide to the mechanics of page creation, see <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:How_to_create_a_page" title="Wikipedia:How to create a page">Wikipedia:How to create a page</a>.</div>
<p>Only <a href="/wiki/Special:UserLogin" title="Special:UserLogin">logged in</a> users can create an article. If you have written an article but have no account you can submit your article for review and publishing at <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:AFC" title="Wikipedia:AFC" class="mw-redirect">Articles for Creation</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Title_for_your_new_article">Title for your new article</span></h3>
<p>In the search box below, type the title of your article, then click Go. If the Search page reports "You may create the page" followed by the article name in red, then you can click the red article name to start editing your article.</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Resolving_clashes">Resolving clashes</span></h4>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main page: <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Disambiguation" title="Wikipedia:Disambiguation">Wikipedia:Disambiguation</a></div>
<p>Sometimes there is already an article under your chosen title, but on a different topic. In this case, the titles must be distinguished from one another. The process of distinguishing identical or similar titles with distinct meanings is called "disambiguation". This can be confusing for newcomers: if necessary, create your page as a <a href="/wiki/Help:Userspace_draft" title="Help:Userspace draft">userspace draft</a>, then ask at the <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:New_contributors%27_help_page" title="Wikipedia:New contributors' help page">new contributors' help page</a> or <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:HD" title="Wikipedia:HD" class="mw-redirect">help desk</a> for help on sorting out the disambiguation.</p>
<p>There are three main ways disambiguation is done, depending on how many topics there are and whether there's one which is much more important than the rest:</p>
<ul>
<li><b>Disambiguation page</b>. For example, when you enter the word <i>"<a href="/wiki/Salsa" title="Salsa" class="mw-disambig">Salsa</a>"</i>, you will be brought to a page known as a <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:DAB" title="Wikipedia:DAB" class="mw-redirect">disambiguation page</a> that will list all the subjects named <i>"Salsa"</i>, including <a href="/wiki/Salsa_(sauce)" title="Salsa (sauce)">Salsa (sauce)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Salsa_music" title="Salsa music">Salsa music</a>, and several other meanings of the word Salsa. Please only create disambiguation pages for existing article clashes, not for prospective articles.</li>
<li><b>Primary usage</b>. In some cases, the title by itself will be used for the most common meaning of the term (the "primary usage"), and all other uses will be found on a disambiguation page. For example, when you enter <a href="/wiki/Mouse" title="Mouse">Mouse</a>, you will be brought to the article about the animal. All other uses, such as <a href="/wiki/Mouse_(computing)" title="Mouse (computing)">Mouse (computing)</a>, are listed on the disambiguation page.</li>
<li><b>Just two topics</b>. Or in some cases, if there are only two meanings of a title, the plain title will be used for one meaning (the more common one), and a message on the top of that page, known as a <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:HATNOTE" title="Wikipedia:HATNOTE" class="mw-redirect">hatnote</a>, will be used to direct readers to the other. For example, if you enter <a href="/wiki/Avocado" title="Avocado">Avocado</a>, you will be brought to the article about the plant. A hatnote will direct you to a town called <a href="/wiki/Avocado,_California" title="Avocado, California">Avocado, California</a>.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Entering_references">Entering references</span></h3>
<p>The very first thing you should write in your article is a list of the source(s) for your information. For now, just enter them like this (and they will automatically turn into links):</p>
<dl>
<dd>(1) <a rel="nofollow" class="external free" href="http://www.nytimes.com/2007/04/12/books/12vonnegut.html">http://www.nytimes.com/2007/04/12/books/12vonnegut.html</a></dd>
<dd>(2) <a rel="nofollow" class="external free" href="http://www-03.ibm.com/ibm/history/exhibits/space/space_shuttle.html">http://www-03.ibm.com/ibm/history/exhibits/space/space_shuttle.html</a></dd>
</dl>
<p>Later, you'll <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Tutorial/Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Tutorial/Citing sources">learn how to format them</a> to appear as footnotes.</p>
<p>If you know that it will take you a few edits to list references properly, put the template <b><code>{{<a href="/wiki/Template:Newpage" title="Template:Newpage" class="mw-redirect">newpage</a>}}</code></b> on top of the page to signify to other editors that you are working on it. Even better is to create your article in a <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:User_page" title="Wikipedia:User page" class="mw-redirect">subpage</a> of your user page, take as long as you need to make it a good article, then move it to the main article space. You can create your personal sandbox for article development by clicking <a href="/wiki/Special:MyPage/sandbox" title="Special:MyPage/sandbox">this link</a>. However, even in user space articles on unacceptable topics are liable to be nominated for deletion.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Categorizing">Categorizing</span></h3>
<p>Every article should be in one or more Wikipedia categories. Often the easiest way to find relevant categories is to think of a similar topic that has an article and visit it to see the categories it is placed in. Otherwise, you may go to <a href="/wiki/Category:Articles" title="Category:Articles">Category:Articles</a> and click on any relevant subcategories, then keep following relevant subcategory links as far as you can. Follow all relevant subcategory chains, and add a category declaration, written [[Category:your category]], at the bottom of your article for each relevant category that has no relevant subcategories of its own.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Final_step">Final step</span></h3>
<p>After you have entered your article, click Show preview to check for errors, then click Save page.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="And_then_what.3F">And then what?</span></h2>
<p>Now that you have created the page, there are still several things you can do.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Keep_making_improvements">Keep making improvements</span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:FINISH" title="Wikipedia:FINISH" class="mw-redirect">Wikipedia is not finished</a>. Generally, an article is nowhere near being completed the moment it is created. There is a long way to go. In fact, it may take you several edits just to get it started.</p>
<p>If you have so much interest in the article you just created, you may learn more about it in the future, and accordingly have more to add. This may be later today, tomorrow, or several months from now. Any time – go ahead.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Improve_formatting">Improve formatting</span></h3>
<p>To format your article correctly (and expand it, and possibly even make it <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Featured_articles" title="Wikipedia:Featured articles">featured</a>!), see</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Tutorial" title="Wikipedia:Tutorial">Wikipedia:Tutorial</a> to learn how to format your article</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Writing_better_articles" title="Wikipedia:Writing better articles">Wikipedia:Writing better articles</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:The_perfect_article" title="Wikipedia:The perfect article">Wikipedia:The perfect article</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Lead_section" title="Wikipedia:Lead section" class="mw-redirect">Wikipedia:Lead section</a></li>
</ul>
<p>Others can freely contribute to the article when it has been saved. The creator does not have special rights to control the later content. See <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Ownership_of_articles" title="Wikipedia:Ownership of articles" class="mw-redirect">Wikipedia:Ownership of articles</a>.</p>
<p>Also, before you get frustrated or offended about the way others modify or remove your contributions, see <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Don%27t_be_ashamed" title="Wikipedia:Don't be ashamed">Wikipedia:Don't be ashamed</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Avoid_orphans">Avoid orphans</span></h3>
<p>An <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Orphan" title="Wikipedia:Orphan">orphaned article</a> is an article that has few or no other articles linking to it. The main problem with an orphan is that it'll be unknown to others, and may get fewer readers if it is not de-orphaned.</p>
<p>Most new articles are orphans from the moment they are created, but you can work to change that. This will involve editing one or more <i>other</i> articles. Try searching Wikipedia for other pages referring to the subject of your article, then turn those references into links by adding double brackets to either side: "[[" and "]]". If another article has a word or phrase that has the same meaning as your new article, but not expressed in the same words as the title, you can link that word or phrase as follows: "[[title of your new article|word or phrase found in other article]]." Or in certain cases, you could create that word or phrase as a redirect to your new article.</p>
<p>One of the first things you want to do after creating a new article is to provide links to it so it will not be an orphan. You can do that right away, or if you find that exhausting, you can wait a while, provided that you keep the task in mind.</p>
<p>See <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Drawing_attention_to_new_pages" title="Wikipedia:Drawing attention to new pages" class="mw-redirect">Wikipedia:Drawing attention to new pages</a> to learn how to get others to see your new articles.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Add_to_a_disambiguation_page">Add to a disambiguation page</span></h3>
<p>If the term is ambiguous (meaning there are multiple pages using that or a similar title), see if there is a <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:DAB" title="Wikipedia:DAB" class="mw-redirect">disambiguation page</a> for articles bearing that title. If so, add it to that page.</p>

<li>The best places to ask for assistance is at the <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:TH/Q" title="Wikipedia:TH/Q" class="mw-redirect">Teahouse</a> and at the main <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:HD" title="Wikipedia:HD" class="mw-redirect">Help desk</a>.</li>
<li><span class="noprint plainlinks"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Special:MyTalk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=new&amp;preload=Help:Contents/helpmepreload&amp;preloadtitle=Help+me%21">Click here to ask for help on your talk page</a></span>, a volunteer will visit you there shortly!</li>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Read_a_traditional_encyclopedia">Read a traditional encyclopedia</span></h3>
<p>Try to read traditional paper encyclopedia articles to get the layout, style, tone, and other elements of encyclopedic content. It is suggested that if you plan to write articles for an encyclopedia, you have some background knowledge in formal writing as well as about the topic at hand. A composition class in your high school or college is recommended before you start writing encyclopedia articles.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/World_Book" title="World Book" class="mw-redirect">World Book</a> is a good place to start. The goal of Wikipedia is to create an up-to-the-moment encyclopedia on every notable subject imaginable. Pretend that your article will be published in a paper encyclopedia.</p>
