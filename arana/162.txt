<p><b>Jiang Qing</b><sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>N 1<span>]</span></a></sup> (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">江青</span>; <a href="/wiki/Wade%E2%80%93Giles" title="Wade–Giles">Wade–Giles</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-wadegile" xml:lang="zh-Latn-wadegile">Chiang Ch'ing</span></i>, also known as <b>Madame Mao</b>; March 19, 1914&#160;– May 14, 1991) was a Chinese actress and a major political figure during the <a href="/wiki/Cultural_Revolution" title="Cultural Revolution">Cultural Revolution</a> (1966–76). She was the fourth wife of <a href="/wiki/Mao_Zedong" title="Mao Zedong">Mao Zedong</a>, the Chairman of the <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_China" title="Communist Party of China">Communist Party of China</a>. She used the <a href="/wiki/Stage_name" title="Stage name">stage name</a> <b>Lan Ping</b> (<span lang="zh-hans" xml:lang="zh-hans">蓝苹</span>) during her acting career, and was known by many other names (see <a href="/wiki/Jiang_Qing#Names_of_Jiang_Qing" title="Jiang Qing">"names" section below</a>). She married Mao in <a href="/wiki/Yan%27an" title="Yan'an">Yan'an</a> in November 1938 and served as the inaugural "<a href="/wiki/First_Lady" title="First Lady">First Lady</a>" of the People's Republic of China. Jiang Qing was best known for playing a major role in the Cultural Revolution and for forming the radical political alliance known as the "<a href="/wiki/Gang_of_Four" title="Gang of Four">Gang of Four</a>".</p>
<p>Jiang Qing served as Mao's personal secretary in the 1940s and was head of the Film Section of the Communist Party's Propaganda Department in the 1950s. She served as an important emissary for Mao in the early stages of the Cultural Revolution. In 1966 she was appointed deputy director of the <a href="/wiki/Central_Cultural_Revolution_Group" title="Central Cultural Revolution Group" class="mw-redirect">Central Cultural Revolution Group</a>. She collaborated with <a href="/wiki/Lin_Biao" title="Lin Biao">Lin Biao</a> to advance Mao's unique brand of Communist ideology as well as Mao's <a href="/wiki/Cult_of_personality" title="Cult of personality">cult of personality</a>. At the height of the Cultural Revolution, Jiang Qing held significant influence in the affairs of state, particularly in the realm of culture and the arts, and was idolized in propaganda posters as the "Great Flagbearer of the <a href="/wiki/Proletariat" title="Proletariat">Proletarian</a> Revolution". In 1969, Jiang gained a seat on the <a href="/wiki/Politburo_of_the_Communist_Party_of_China" title="Politburo of the Communist Party of China">Politburo</a>.</p>
<p>Before Mao's death, the Gang of Four maintained control of many of China's political institutions, including the media and propaganda. However, Jiang Qing, deriving most of her political legitimacy from Mao, often found herself at odds with other top leaders. Mao's death in 1976 dealt a significant blow to Jiang Qing's political fortunes. She was arrested in October 1976 by <a href="/wiki/Hua_Guofeng" title="Hua Guofeng">Hua Guofeng</a> and his allies, and was subsequently condemned by party authorities. Since then, Jiang Qing has been officially branded as having been part of the "Lin Biao and Jiang Qing Counter-Revolutionary Clique" (林彪江青反革命集团), to which most of the blame for the damage and devastation caused by the Cultural Revolution was assigned. Though initially sentenced to death, her sentence was commuted to life imprisonment in 1983. After being released for medical treatment, Jiang Qing committed suicide in May 1991.<sup id="cite_ref-Landsberger_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Landsberger-2"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jiang_Qing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Jiang Qing was born in <a href="/wiki/Zhucheng" title="Zhucheng">Zhucheng</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shandong" title="Shandong">Shandong</a> province on March 19, 1914. Her birth name was <b>Lǐ Shūméng</b> (<span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">李淑蒙</span>). She was the only child of Li Dewen (<span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">李德文</span>), a carpenter, and his subsidiary wife, or <a href="/wiki/Concubine" title="Concubine" class="mw-redirect">concubine</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (September 2014)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> Her father ran his own carpentry and cabinet making shop. After Jiang's parents had a violent argument, her mother found work as a domestic servant<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> (some accounts cite that Jiang's mother also worked as a prostitute) and separated from her husband.<sup id="cite_ref-Fox1_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fox1-5"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>When Jiang enrolled in elementary school, she took the name Lĭ Yúnhè (<span lang="zh-hans" xml:lang="zh-hans">李云鹤</span>), meaning "Crane in the Clouds", by which she was known for much of her early life. Due to her socioeconomic status and the fact that she was an illegitimate child, she was looked down upon by her schoolmates and she and her mother moved in with her maternal grandparents when she started middle school.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> In 1926, when she was 12 years old, her father died. Jiang's mother relocated them to <a href="/wiki/Tianjin" title="Tianjin">Tianjin</a> where she worked as a child laborer in a cigarette factory for several months. Two years later, Jiang and her mother settled in <a href="/wiki/Jinan" title="Jinan">Jinan</a>. The following summer, she entered an experimental theater and drama school. Her talent brought her to the attention of administrators who selected her to join a drama club in Beijing where she advanced her acting skills. She returned to Jinan in May 1931 and married Pei Minglun, the wealthy son of a businessman. The marriage was an unhappy one and they soon divorced.</p>
<p>From July 1931 to April 1933, Jiang attended <a href="/wiki/Qingdao_University" title="Qingdao University">Qingdao University</a> in <a href="/wiki/Qingdao" title="Qingdao">Qingdao</a>. She met Yu Qiwei, a biology student three years her senior, who was an underground member of the Communist Party Propaganda Department. By 1932, they had fallen in love and were living together. She joined the "Communist Cultural Front," a circle of artists, writers, and actors, and performed in <i><a href="/wiki/Put_Down_Your_Whip" title="Put Down Your Whip">Put Down Your Whip</a></i>, a renowned popular play about a woman who escapes from Japanese-occupied north-eastern China and performs in the streets to survive. In February 1933, Jiang took the oath of the Chinese Communist Party with Yu at her side, and she was appointed member of the Chinese Communist Party youth wing. Yu was arrested in April the same year and Jiang was subsequently shunned by his family. She fled to her parents' home in Shanghai and returned to the drama school in Jinan, where she was warmly received. Through friendships she had previously established, she received an introduction to attend Shanghai University for the summer where she also taught some general literacy classes. In October, she rejoined the Communist Youth League, and at the same time, began participating in an amateur drama troupe.</p>
<p>In September 1934, Jiang Qing was arrested and jailed for her political activities in Shanghai, but was released three months later, in December of the same year. She then traveled to Beijing where she reunited with Yu Qiwei who had just been released following his prison sentence, and the two began living together again.</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:1934_Jiang_Qing_movie_shot.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/1934_Jiang_Qing_movie_shot.jpg/220px-1934_Jiang_Qing_movie_shot.jpg" width="220" height="167" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/1934_Jiang_Qing_movie_shot.jpg/330px-1934_Jiang_Qing_movie_shot.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/1934_Jiang_Qing_movie_shot.jpg/440px-1934_Jiang_Qing_movie_shot.jpg 2x" data-file-width="499" data-file-height="379" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:1934_Jiang_Qing_movie_shot.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Jiang Qing in a 1935 <a href="/wiki/Film_poster" title="Film poster">film poster</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Jiang Qing returned to Shanghai in March 1935, and became a professional actress, adopting the stage name "Lán Píng" (meaning "Blue Apple", Chinese: 蓝苹). She appeared in numerous films and plays, including <i>God of Liberty</i>, <i>The Scenery of City</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Blood_on_Wolf_Mountain" title="Blood on Wolf Mountain">Blood on Wolf Mountain</a></i> and <i>Old Mr. Wang</i>. In Ibsen's play <i><a href="/wiki/A_Doll%27s_House" title="A Doll's House">A Doll's House</a></i>, Jiang Qing played the role of Nora.</p>
<p>With her career established, she became involved with actor/director Tang Na, with whom she appeared in <i><a href="/wiki/Scenes_of_City_Life" title="Scenes of City Life">Scenes of City Life</a></i> and <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Statue_of_Liberty_(Tang_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="The Statue of Liberty (Tang film) (page does not exist)">The Statue of Liberty</a></i>. They were married in <a href="/wiki/Hangzhou" title="Hangzhou">Hangzhou</a> in March 1936, however he soon discovered she was continuing her relationship with Yu Qiwei. The scandal became public knowledge and he made two suicide attempts before their divorce became final. In 1937, Jiang joined the <a href="/wiki/Lianhua_Film_Company" title="Lianhua Film Company">Lianhua Film Company</a> and starred in the film <i>Big Thunderstorm</i>. She reportedly had an affair with director, Zhang Min, however she denied it in her autobiographical writings.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Flight_to_Yan.27an">Flight to Yan'an</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jiang_Qing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Flight to Yan'an">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Young_Jiang_Qing_and_Mao6.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Young_Jiang_Qing_and_Mao6.jpg/220px-Young_Jiang_Qing_and_Mao6.jpg" width="220" height="136" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Young_Jiang_Qing_and_Mao6.jpg/330px-Young_Jiang_Qing_and_Mao6.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/06/Young_Jiang_Qing_and_Mao6.jpg 2x" data-file-width="357" data-file-height="220" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Young_Jiang_Qing_and_Mao6.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Mao_Zedong" title="Mao Zedong">Mao</a> and Jiang Qing writing together in <a href="/wiki/Yan%27an" title="Yan'an">Yan'an</a>, 1938</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Following the <a href="/wiki/Marco_Polo_Bridge_Incident" title="Marco Polo Bridge Incident">Marco Polo Bridge Incident</a> on July 7, 1937 and the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Shanghai" title="Battle of Shanghai">Japanese invasion of Shanghai</a> which destroyed most of its movie industry, Jiang left her celebrity life on the stage behind. She went first to <a href="/wiki/Xi%27an" title="Xi'an">Xi'an</a>, then to the Chinese Communist headquarters in <a href="/wiki/Yan%27an" title="Yan'an">Yan'an</a> to "join the revolution" and the war to resist the Japanese invasion. In November, she enrolled in the "Anti-Japanese Military and Political University" (Marxist–Leninist Institute) for study. The Lu Xun Academy of Arts was newly founded in Yan'an on April 10, 1938, and Jiang Qing became a drama department instructor, teaching and performing in college plays and <a href="/wiki/Chinese_opera" title="Chinese opera">operas</a>.</p>
<p>Shortly after arriving in Yan'an, Jiang became involved with <a href="/wiki/Mao_Zedong" title="Mao Zedong">Mao Zedong</a>. Some communist leaders were scandalized by the relationship once it became public. At 45, Mao was nearly twice Jiang's age, and Jiang had lived a highly bourgeois lifestyle before coming to Yan'an. Mao was still married to <a href="/wiki/He_Zizhen" title="He Zizhen">He Zizhen</a>, a lifelong Communist who had previously completed the <a href="/wiki/Long_March" title="Long March">Long March</a> with him, and with whom Mao had five children. Eventually, Mao arranged a compromise with the other leaders of the CCP: Mao was granted a divorce and permitted to marry Jiang, but she was required to stay out of public politics for thirty years. Jiang abided by this agreement. However, thirty years later, at the beginning of the Cultural Revolution, Jiang became active in politics.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Young_Jiang_Qing_and_Mao4.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Young_Jiang_Qing_and_Mao4.jpg/220px-Young_Jiang_Qing_and_Mao4.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Young_Jiang_Qing_and_Mao4.jpg/330px-Young_Jiang_Qing_and_Mao4.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Young_Jiang_Qing_and_Mao4.jpg/440px-Young_Jiang_Qing_and_Mao4.jpg 2x" data-file-width="797" data-file-height="531" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Young_Jiang_Qing_and_Mao4.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Li_Na_(daughter_of_Mao_Zedong)" title="Li Na (daughter of Mao Zedong)">Li Na</a>, Jiang Qing and Mao in Yan'an in 1943</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>On November 28, 1938, Jiang and Mao married in a small private ceremony following approval by the Party's Central Committee. Because Mao's marriage to He had not yet ended, Jiang was reportedly made to sign a marital contract which stipulated that she would not appear in public with Mao as her escort. Jiang and Mao's only child together, a daughter named <a href="/wiki/Li_Na_(daughter_of_Mao_Zedong)" title="Li Na (daughter of Mao Zedong)">Li Na</a>, was born in 1940.</p>

<p>After the founding of the People's Republic of China in 1949, Jiang became the nation's first lady. She worked as Director of Film in the Central Propaganda Department, and as a member of the Ministry of Culture steering committee for the film industry. An uproar in 1950 led to the investigation of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Life_of_Wu_Xun" title="The Life of Wu Xun">The Life of Wu Xun</a></i>, a film about a 19th-century beggar who raised money to educate the poor. Jiang supported criticism of the film for celebrating counter-revolutionary ideas.</p>
<p>Following the <a href="/wiki/Great_Leap_Forward" title="Great Leap Forward">Great Leap Forward</a> (1958–1961), Mao was highly criticized within the CCP, and turned to Jiang, among others, to support him and persecute his enemies. Taking advantage of the power given to her by Mao, Jiang began by reforming the Chinese theatre. She led an initiative for reforming modern opera in 1963 that resulted in the "eight model revolutionary operas" established at the Peking Opera. This initiative and others strictly defined permitted works of drama, music, dance, and other arts, including outright bans of unapproved works.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Cultural_Revolution">The Cultural Revolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jiang_Qing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The Cultural Revolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Backed by her husband, she was appointed deputy director of the Central Cultural Revolution Group in 1966 and emerged as a serious political figure in the summer of that year. She became a member of the <a href="/wiki/Politburo_of_the_Communist_Party_of_China" title="Politburo of the Communist Party of China">Politburo</a> in 1969. By then she had established a close political working relationship with the other members of what later became known as the <a href="/wiki/Gang_of_Four_(China)" title="Gang of Four (China)" class="mw-redirect">Gang of Four</a>—<a href="/wiki/Zhang_Chunqiao" title="Zhang Chunqiao">Zhang Chunqiao</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yao_Wenyuan" title="Yao Wenyuan">Yao Wenyuan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wang_Hongwen" title="Wang Hongwen">Wang Hongwen</a>. She was one of the most powerful and controversial figures in China during Mao's last years.</p>
<p>During this period, Mao Zedong galvanized students and young workers as his Red Guards to attack what he termed as revisionists in the party. Mao told them the revolution was in danger and that they must do all they could to stop the emergence of a privileged class in China. He argued this is what had happened in the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> under <a href="/wiki/Khrushchev" title="Khrushchev" class="mw-redirect">Khrushchev</a>.</p>
<p>With time, Jiang began playing an increasingly active political role in the movement. She took part in most important Party and government activities. She was supported by a radical coterie, dubbed, by Mao himself, the Gang of Four. Although a prominent member of the <a href="/wiki/Cultural_Revolution_Group" title="Cultural Revolution Group">Central Cultural Revolution Group</a> and a major player in Chinese politics from 1966 to 1976, she essentially remained on the sidelines.<sup id="cite_ref-Landsberger_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Landsberger-2"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The initial storm of the Cultural Revolution came to an end when <a href="/wiki/Liu_Shaoqi" title="Liu Shaoqi">Liu Shaoqi</a> was forced from all his posts on October 13, 1968. Lin Biao now became Mao's designated successor. Chairman Mao now gave his support to the <a href="/wiki/Gang_of_Four" title="Gang of Four">Gang of Four</a>: Jiang Qing, <a href="/wiki/Wang_Hongwen" title="Wang Hongwen">Wang Hongwen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yao_Wenyuan" title="Yao Wenyuan">Yao Wenyuan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zhang_Chunqiao" title="Zhang Chunqiao">Zhang Chunqiao</a>. These four radicals occupied powerful positions in the Politburo after the Tenth Party Congress of 1973.</p>
<p>Jiang Qing also directed operas and ballets with communist and revolutionary content as part of an effort to transform China's culture. She dominated the Chinese arts, and in particular attempted to reform the <a href="/wiki/Beijing_opera" title="Beijing opera" class="mw-redirect">Beijing Opera</a>. She developed a new form of art called the <a href="/wiki/Eight_model_plays" title="Eight model plays" class="mw-redirect">Eight model plays</a> which depicted the world in simple, binary terms: the positive characters ("good guys") were predominantly farmers, workers and revolutionary soldiers, whilst the negative characters ("bad guys") were landlords and anti-revolutionaries. The negative characters, in contrast to their proletarian foils who performed boldly centre stage, were identifiable by their darker make-up and relegation to the outskirts of the stage until direct conflict with a positive character.<sup id="cite_ref-Landsberger_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Landsberger-2"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> Critics would argue that her influence on art was too restrictive, because she replaced nearly all earlier works of art with revolutionary Maoist works.</p>
<p>Jiang Qing first collaborated with then second-in-charge <a href="/wiki/Lin_Biao" title="Lin Biao">Lin Biao</a>, but after Lin Biao's death in 1971, she turned against him publicly in the <a href="/wiki/Criticize_Lin,_Criticize_Confucius" title="Criticize Lin, Criticize Confucius">Criticize Lin, Criticize Confucius</a> Campaign. By the mid 1970s, Jiang Qing also spearheaded the campaign against Deng Xiaoping (afterwards saying that this was inspired by Mao). The Chinese public became intensely discontented at this time and chose to blame Jiang Qing, a more accessible and easier target than Chairman Mao. By 1973, although unreported due to it being a personal matter, Mao and his wife Jiang had separated:</p>
<dl>
<dd>"It was reported that Mao Tsetung and Chiang Ching were separated in 1973. Most people, however, did not know this. Hence Chiang Ching was still able to use her position as Mao's wife to deceive people. Because of her relations to Mao, it was particularly difficult for the Party to deal with her."<sup id="cite_ref-the_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-the-7"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>Jiang Qing's hobbies included photography, playing cards, and watching foreign movies, especially <i><a href="/wiki/Gone_with_the_Wind_(film)" title="Gone with the Wind (film)">Gone with the Wind</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-chang_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chang-8"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> When touring a troupe of young girls excelling in marksmanship, she "discovered" <a href="/wiki/Joan_Chen" title="Joan Chen">Joan Chen</a>, then 14 years old, launching Joan's career as a Chinese and then international actress.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>She developed severe <a href="/wiki/Hypochondriasis" title="Hypochondriasis">hypochondriasis</a> and erratic nerves.<sup id="cite_ref-chang_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chang-8"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> She required two <a href="/wiki/Sedative" title="Sedative">sedatives</a> over the course of a day and three sleeping pills to fall asleep. Staff were assigned to chase away birds and <a href="/wiki/Cicada" title="Cicada">cicadas</a> from her Imperial Fishing Villa. She ordered house servants to cut down on noise by removing their shoes and preventing clothes from rustling. Mild temperature differences bothered her; thermostats were always set to 21.5&#160;°C (70.7&#160;°F) in winter and 26&#160;°C (78.8&#160;°F) in summer.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_persecution_of_enemies">Political persecution of enemies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jiang_Qing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Political persecution of enemies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Jiang Qing incited radical youths organized as <a href="/wiki/Red_Guards_(China)" title="Red Guards (China)">Red Guards</a> against other senior political leaders and government officials, including Liu Shaoqi, the <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="President of the People's Republic of China">President</a> at the time, and <a href="/wiki/Deng_Xiaoping" title="Deng Xiaoping">Deng Xiaoping</a>, the Deputy Premier. Internally divided into factions both to the "left" and "right" of Jiang Qing and Mao, not all Red Guards were friendly to Jiang Qing.</p>
<p>Jiang's rivalry with, and personal dislike of, <a href="/wiki/Zhou_Enlai" title="Zhou Enlai">Zhou Enlai</a> led Jiang to hurt Zhou where he was most vulnerable. In 1968 Jiang had Zhou's adopted son (Sun Yang) and daughter (<a href="/wiki/Sun_Weishi" title="Sun Weishi">Sun Weishi</a>) tortured and murdered by Maoist <a href="/wiki/Red_Guards_(China)" title="Red Guards (China)">Red Guards</a>. Sun Yang was murdered in the basement of <a href="/wiki/Renmin_University" title="Renmin University" class="mw-redirect">Renmin University</a>. After Sun Weishi died following seven months of torture in a secret prison (at Jiang's direction), Jiang made sure that Sun's body was cremated and disposed of so that no autopsy could be performed, and so that Sun's family could not have her ashes. In 1968 Jiang forced Zhou to sign an arrest warrant for his own brother.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> In 1973 and 1974, Jiang directed the "Criticize Lin, Criticize Confucius" campaign against premier Zhou because Zhou was viewed as one of Jiang's primary political opponents. In 1975, Jiang initiated a campaign named "Criticizing Song Jiang, Evaluating the Water Margin", which encouraged the use of Zhou as an example of a political loser. After Zhou Enlai died in 1976, Jiang initiated the "Five Nos" campaign in order to discourage and prohibit any public mourning for Zhou.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death_of_Mao_Zedong">Death of Mao Zedong</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jiang_Qing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Death of Mao Zedong">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Jiang_Qing_arts_poster.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/5/59/Jiang_Qing_arts_poster.jpg/220px-Jiang_Qing_arts_poster.jpg" width="220" height="148" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/5/59/Jiang_Qing_arts_poster.jpg/330px-Jiang_Qing_arts_poster.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/5/59/Jiang_Qing_arts_poster.jpg/440px-Jiang_Qing_arts_poster.jpg 2x" data-file-width="487" data-file-height="328" /></a>

Poster showing Jiang Qing promoting the fine arts during the <a href="/wiki/Cultural_Revolution" title="Cultural Revolution">Cultural Revolution</a> while holding Mao's <i><a href="/wiki/The_Little_Red_Book" title="The Little Red Book" class="mw-redirect">Little Red Book</a></i>. The slogan reads: "The invincible thoughts of Mao Zedong illuminate the stages of revolutionary art!"</div>

<p>On September 5, 1976, Mao's failing health turned critical when he suffered a <a href="/wiki/Heart_attack" title="Heart attack" class="mw-redirect">heart attack</a>, far more serious than his previous two earlier in the year. Upon being contacted by <a href="/wiki/Hua_Guofeng" title="Hua Guofeng">Hua Guofeng</a>, Jiang Qing returned from her trip to the countryside and spent only a few moments in the hospital's Building 202, where Mao was being treated. Later she returned to her own residence in the Spring Lotus Chamber.</p>
<p>On the afternoon of September 7, Mao's condition took a turn for the worse. Mao had just fallen asleep and needed to rest, but Jiang Qing insisted on rubbing his back and moving his limbs, and she sprinkled powder on his body. The medical team protested that the dust from the powder was not good for his lungs, but she instructed the nurses on duty to follow her example later.</p>
<p>The next morning, September 8, she went again. This time she wanted the medical staff to change Mao's sleeping position, claiming that he had been lying too long on his left side. The doctor on duty objected, explaining that Mao could breathe only on his left side. Jiang ordered him move Mao nonetheless. As a result, Mao's breathing stopped and his face turned blue. Jiang Qing left the room while the medical staff put Mao on a respirator and performed emergency cardiopulmonary resuscitation.</p>
<p>Eventually, Mao was revived and Hua Guofeng urged Jiang Qing not to interfere further with the doctors' work. However, nearly all of Mao's organs failed and he fell into a coma by the end of that day. With Mao beyond recovery and unwilling to further prolong his suffering, Chinese government decided to disconnect Mao's life support mechanism.</p>
<p>Mao's death occurred just after midnight at 00:10 hours on September 9, 1976. Mao's chosen successor, <a href="/wiki/Hua_Guofeng" title="Hua Guofeng">Hua Guofeng</a>, became the chairman of his funeral committee. It was believed Hua was a compromise candidate between the free-marketeers and the party orthodox. Some argue this may have been due to his ambivalence and his low-key profile, particularly compared to <a href="/wiki/Deng_Xiaoping" title="Deng Xiaoping">Deng Xiaoping</a>, the preferred candidate of the market-oriented factions. The party apparatus, under orders from Jiang Qing and Zhang Chunqiao, wrote a eulogy affirming Mao's achievements in order to justify their claims to power.</p>
<p>By this time state media was effectively under the control of the Gang of Four. State newspapers continued to denounce Deng shortly after Mao's death. Jiang Qing was especially paranoid of Deng's influence on national affairs, whereas she considered Hua Guofeng a mere nuisance. In numerous documents published in the 1980s it was claimed that Jiang Qing was conspiring to make herself the new Chairman of the Communist Party.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Downfall_and_death">Downfall and death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jiang_Qing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Downfall and death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1976_coup">1976 coup</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jiang_Qing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 1976 coup">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Jiang Qing showed few signs of sorrow during the days following Mao's death. It was uncertain who controlled the Communist Party's central organs during this transition period. <a href="/wiki/Hua_Guofeng" title="Hua Guofeng">Hua Guofeng</a>, as Mao's designated successor, held the titular power as the acting Chairman of the Communist Party and as Premier. However, Hua was not very influential. Some sources indicate that Mao mentioned Jiang Qing before his death in a note to Hua Guofeng, telling him to "go and consult her" if he runs into problems (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">有事找江青</span>).<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Jiang Qing believed that upholding the status quo, where she was one of the highest-ranked members of the central authorities, would mean that she would effectively hold onto power. In addition, she believed that her status as Mao's widow would make it difficult for her to be removed. She continued to invoke Mao's name in her major decisions, and acted as first-in-charge.</p>
<p>Her political ambitions and lack of respect for most of the elder revolutionaries within the Central Committee became notorious. Her support within the Central Committee was dwindling, and her public approval was dismal. Ye Jianying, a renowned general, met in private with Hua Guofeng and Wang Dongxing, commander of a secret service-like organization called the <a href="/wiki/8341_Special_Regiment" title="8341 Special Regiment" class="mw-redirect">8341 Special Regiment</a>. They determined that Jiang Qing and her associates must be removed by force in order to restore stability.</p>
<p>On the morning of October 6, 1976, Jiang Qing came to Mao's former residence in <a href="/wiki/Zhongnanhai" title="Zhongnanhai">Zhongnanhai</a>, gathered her close aides and Mao's former personal aides in a "Study Mao's Work" session. According to Du Xiuxian, her photographer, Jiang Qing remarked that she knew people within the Central Committee were plotting against her.</p>
<p>After the session, Jiang Qing took several aides to <a href="/wiki/Jingshan_Park" title="Jingshan Park">Jingshan Park</a> to pick apples. In the evening, Jiang Qing, Zhang Chunqiao, Wang Hongwen, and Yao Wenyuan were arrested and kept in the lower level of Zhongnanhai. According to Zhang Yaoci, who carried out the arrest, Jiang Qing did not say much when she was arrested.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> In a <a href="/wiki/Bloodless_coup" title="Bloodless coup" class="mw-redirect">bloodless coup</a>, the Gang of Four was charged with attempts to seize power by organizing militant coups in Shanghai and Beijing, subverting the government, counter-revolutionary activity, and treason.</p>
<p>After her arrest, Jiang Qing was sent to the Qincheng Prison and detained for five years. In both official and civilian accounts of the period, the fall of the Gang was met with celebrations all over China. Indeed, Jiang Qing's role in the Cultural Revolution was perceived by the public to be largely negative, and the Gang of Four was a convenient <a href="/wiki/Scapegoat" title="Scapegoat">scapegoat</a> for the ten years of political and social turmoil. Her role during the Cultural Revolution is still a subject of historical debate.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Trial">Trial</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jiang_Qing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Trial">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Jiang_Qing_at_trial.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/8/83/Jiang_Qing_at_trial.jpg/220px-Jiang_Qing_at_trial.jpg" width="220" height="135" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/8/83/Jiang_Qing_at_trial.jpg/330px-Jiang_Qing_at_trial.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/8/83/Jiang_Qing_at_trial.jpg/440px-Jiang_Qing_at_trial.jpg 2x" data-file-width="500" data-file-height="307" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Jiang_Qing_at_trial.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Jiang Qing at her trial in 1980</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In 1980, the trials of the <a href="/wiki/Gang_of_Four" title="Gang of Four">Gang of Four</a> began. The trials were televised nationwide.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Naming_conventions_(geographic_names)" title="Wikipedia:Naming conventions (geographic names)"><span title="The geographic scope near this tag is ambiguous. (September 2014)">where?</span></a></i>]</sup> By showing the way the Gang of Four was tried, <a href="/wiki/Deng_Xiaoping" title="Deng Xiaoping">Deng Xiaoping</a> wanted the people to realize that a new era had begun.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Portions of the 20,000-word indictment were printed in China's press before the trial started; they accused the defendants of a host of heinous crimes that took place during the Cultural Revolution. The charges specify that 727,420 Chinese were "persecuted" during that period, and that 34,274 died, though the often vague indictment did not specify exactly how. Among the chief victims: one-time Chief of State <a href="/wiki/Liu_Shaoqi" title="Liu Shaoqi">Liu Shaoqi</a>, whose widow <a href="/wiki/Wang_Guangmei" title="Wang Guangmei">Wang Guangmei</a>, herself imprisoned during the Cultural Revolution for 12 years, attended the trial as an observer.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The indictment described two plots by the "Jiang Qing-Lin Biao Counter-revolutionary Clique" to seize power. Jiang Qing was not accused of conspiring with Lin Biao, or with other members of the Gang of Four who allegedly planned an armed rebellion to "usurp power" in 1976, when Mao was close to death. Instead, the charges against her focused on her systematic persecution of creative artists during the Cultural Revolution. Amongst other things, she was accused of hiring 40 people in Shanghai to disguise themselves as Red Guards and ransack the homes of writers and performers. The apparent purpose was said to find and destroy letters, photos and other potentially damaging materials on Jiang Qing's early career in Shanghai, which she wanted to keep secret.</p>
<p>Despite the seriousness of the accusations against her, Jiang Qing appeared unrepentant. She had not confessed her guilt, something that the Chinese press emphasized to show her bad attitude. There had been reports that she planned to defend herself by cloaking herself in Mao's mantle, saying that she did only what he approved. As the trial got under way, Jiang Qing dismissed her assigned lawyers, deciding instead to represent herself. During her public trials at the "Special Court", Jiang Qing was the only member of the Gang of Four who argued on her own behalf. The defense's argument was that she obeyed the orders of Chairman <a href="/wiki/Mao_Zedong" title="Mao Zedong">Mao Zedong</a> at all times. Jiang Qing maintained that all she had done was to defend Chairman Mao. It was at this trial that Jiang Qing made the famous quote: "<i>I was Chairman Mao's dog. I bit whomever he asked me to bite.</i>" (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">我是主席的一条狗，主席要我咬谁就咬谁。</span>).<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-modern_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-modern-16"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> The official records of the trial have not been released.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jiang_Qing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Jiang Qing was sentenced to death with a two-year reprieve in 1981. By 1983, her death sentence was commuted to life imprisonment. During this time, she made several requests to visit Mao Zedong's embalmed body in Beijing, but they were turned down. When the Tiananmen Square protests occurred, Jiang believed that the student activists were liberals rather than Maoists, but she blamed them on Deng Xiaoping, writing that "He let in all those Western ideas!"</p>
<p>While in prison, Jiang Qing was diagnosed with throat cancer, but she refused an operation. She was eventually released, on medical grounds, in 1991. At the hospital, Jiang Qing used the name Lǐ Rùnqīng (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">李润青</span>). She committed suicide on May 14, 1991, aged 77, by hanging herself in a bathroom of her hospital. She penned a suicide note reading "Today the revolution has been stolen by the revisionist clique of Deng, <a href="/wiki/Peng_Zhen" title="Peng Zhen">Peng Zhen</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Yang_Shangkun" title="Yang Shangkun">Yang Shangkun</a>. Chairman Mao exterminated Liu Shaoqi, but not Deng, and the result of this omission is that unending evils have been unleashed on the Chinese people and nation. Chairman, your student and fighter is coming to see you!".<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> Her suicide occurred two days short of the 25th anniversary of the <a href="/wiki/Cultural_Revolution" title="Cultural Revolution">Cultural Revolution</a>.</p>
<p>She wished for her remains to be buried in her home province of <a href="/wiki/Shandong" title="Shandong">Shandong</a>, but in consideration of possible future vandalism to her tomb, the state decided to have her remains moved to a safer common cemetery in Beijing.<sup id="cite_ref-duowei_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-duowei-18"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> Jiang Qing is buried in Futian Cemetery in the western hills of Beijing. Her grave is marked by a tall white stone inscribed with her school name, not the name by which she was famously known, which reads: "Tomb of Late Mother, Li Yunhe, 1914–1991" (先母李云鹤之墓，一九一四年至一九九一年).<sup id="cite_ref-duowei_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-duowei-18"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Names_of_Jiang_Qing">Names of Jiang Qing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jiang_Qing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Names of Jiang Qing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are several reasons for Jiang Qing's large repertoire of names. A large part of it has to do with the fluctuating period she lived in. At the time of her birth, many female children never received given names or formal education.</p>
<p>Her father named her Li Jinhai because he wanted a son, but this was altered after her birth to Li Shumeng. She enrolled in school under a more dignified name, Li Yunhe,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (September 2014)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and simply changed it for convenience to Li He.</p>
<p>As was customary for Chinese actors during that time (and for some, until the present-day), she chose a stage name, which was used in all the plays and films that credited her roles. Lan Ping was the name she was known by within Chinese film circles and a name she came to identify with.</p>
<p>It is unclear when she changed her name to Jiang Qing, but it probably occurred before her arrival in <a href="/wiki/Yan%27an" title="Yan'an">Yan'an</a>. It is believed that the character "Qing" was chosen because it related to the concept of Blue ("Lan"). There is some evidence that the name signified her status as a Communist and a severance from her "bourgeois" past.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> She also used Li Jin to pen a number of articles she wrote during the Cultural Revolution.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Eventually, to protect her identity, she used Li Runqing when she was hospitalized after being released from prison. She was buried under her tombstone which bore the name "Li Yunhe".<sup id="cite_ref-Duowei:_Jiang_Qing.27s_gravesite_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Duowei:_Jiang_Qing.27s_gravesite-21"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
