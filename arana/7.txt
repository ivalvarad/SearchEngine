<div class="hatnote">"Portal vein" redirects here. For a vein that connects two systems of capillary beds, see <a href="/wiki/Portal_venous_system" title="Portal venous system">portal venous system</a>.</div>
<p>The <b>hepatic portal vein</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Blood_vessel" title="Blood vessel">blood vessel</a> that conducts blood from the <a href="/wiki/Gastrointestinal_tract" title="Gastrointestinal tract" class="mw-redirect">gastrointestinal tract</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spleen" title="Spleen">spleen</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Liver" title="Liver">liver</a>. This blood is rich in nutrients that have been extracted from food, and the liver processes these nutrients; it also filters toxins that may have been ingested with the food. 75% of total liver blood flow is through the hepatic portal vein, with the remainder coming from the <a href="/wiki/Hepatic_artery_proper" title="Hepatic artery proper">hepatic artery proper</a>. The blood leaves the liver to the heart in the <a href="/wiki/Hepatic_veins" title="Hepatic veins" class="mw-redirect">hepatic veins</a>.</p>
<p>The hepatic portal vein is not a true <a href="/wiki/Vein" title="Vein">vein</a>, because it conducts blood to <a href="/wiki/Capillary" title="Capillary">capillary</a> beds in the liver and not directly to the heart. It is a major component of the <a href="/wiki/Hepatic_portal_system" title="Hepatic portal system">hepatic portal system</a>, one of only two <a href="/wiki/Portal_venous_system" title="Portal venous system">portal venous systems</a> in the body – with the <a href="/wiki/Hypophyseal_portal_system" title="Hypophyseal portal system">hypophyseal portal system</a> being the other.</p>
<p>The hepatic portal vein is usually formed by the confluence of the <a href="/wiki/Superior_mesenteric_vein" title="Superior mesenteric vein">superior mesenteric</a> and <a href="/wiki/Splenic_vein" title="Splenic vein">splenic veins</a> and also receives blood from the <a href="/wiki/Inferior_mesenteric_vein" title="Inferior mesenteric vein">inferior mesenteric</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gastric_vein_(disambiguation)" title="Gastric vein (disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect">gastric</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cystic_vein" title="Cystic vein">cystic veins</a>.</p>
<p>Conditions involving the hepatic portal vein cause considerable illness and death. An important example of such a condition is elevated <a href="/wiki/Blood_pressure" title="Blood pressure">blood pressure</a> in the hepatic portal vein. This condition, called <a href="/wiki/Portal_hypertension" title="Portal hypertension">portal hypertension</a>, is a major complication of <a href="/wiki/Cirrhosis" title="Cirrhosis">cirrhosis</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hepatic_portal_vein&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="wikitable" style="float:right; border-collapse:collapse" border="1" cellpadding="3">

<p>Measuring approximately 8 cm (3 inches) in adults,<sup id="cite_ref-isbn0-7817-7174-9_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-isbn0-7817-7174-9-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> the hepatic portal vein is located in the <a href="/wiki/Right_upper_quadrant" title="Right upper quadrant" class="mw-redirect">right upper quadrant</a> of the abdomen, originating behind the neck of the <a href="/wiki/Pancreas" title="Pancreas">pancreas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-phtn_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-phtn-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In most individuals, the hepatic portal vein is formed by the union of the <a href="/wiki/Superior_mesenteric_vein" title="Superior mesenteric vein">superior mesenteric vein</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Splenic_vein" title="Splenic vein">splenic vein</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-isbn1-55009-364-9_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-isbn1-55009-364-9-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> For this reason, the hepatic portal vein is occasionally called the <i><b>splenic-mesenteric confluence</b></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-phtn_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-phtn-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Occasionally, the hepatic portal vein also directly communicates with the <a href="/wiki/Inferior_mesenteric_vein" title="Inferior mesenteric vein">inferior mesenteric vein</a>, although this is highly variable. Other tributaries of the hepatic portal vein include the <a href="/wiki/Cystic_vein" title="Cystic vein">cystic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gastric_veins" title="Gastric veins" class="mw-redirect">gastric veins</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-grays_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-grays-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Immediately before reaching the liver, the portal vein divides into right and left. It ramifies further, forming smaller venous branches and ultimately portal venules. Each portal venule courses alongside a hepatic arteriole and the two vessels form the vascular components of the <a href="/wiki/Portal_triad" title="Portal triad">portal triad</a>. These vessels ultimately empty into the <a href="/wiki/Hepatic_sinusoid" title="Hepatic sinusoid" class="mw-redirect">hepatic sinusoids</a> to supply blood to the liver.<sup id="cite_ref-grays_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-grays-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Portacaval_anastomoses">Portacaval anastomoses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hepatic_portal_vein&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Portacaval anastomoses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Portal_venous_system" title="Portal venous system">portal venous system</a> has several <a href="/wiki/Anastomoses" title="Anastomoses" class="mw-redirect">anastomoses</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Systemic_venous_system" title="Systemic venous system">systemic venous system</a>. In cases of <a href="/wiki/Portal_hypertension" title="Portal hypertension">portal hypertension</a> these anastamoses may become engorged, dilated, or varicosed and subsequently rupture.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Accessory_hepatic_portal_veins">Accessory hepatic portal veins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hepatic_portal_vein&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Accessory hepatic portal veins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Accessory hepatic portal veins are those veins that drain directly into the liver without joining the hepatic portal vein. These include the <a href="/wiki/Paraumbilical_vein" title="Paraumbilical vein">paraumbilical veins</a> as well as veins of the <a href="/wiki/Lesser_omentum" title="Lesser omentum">lesser omentum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Falciform_ligament" title="Falciform ligament">falciform ligament</a>, and those draining the <a href="/wiki/Gallbladder" title="Gallbladder">gallbladder</a> wall.<sup id="cite_ref-phtn_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-phtn-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Function">Function</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hepatic_portal_vein&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Function">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The hepatic portal vein and <a href="/wiki/Hepatic_arteries" title="Hepatic arteries" class="mw-redirect">hepatic arteries</a> form the liver's dual blood supply. Approximately 75% of hepatic blood flow is derived from the hepatic portal vein, while the remainder is from the hepatic arteries.<sup id="cite_ref-phtn_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-phtn-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Unlike most veins, the hepatic portal vein does not drain into the <a href="/wiki/Heart" title="Heart">heart</a>. Rather, it is part of a <a href="/wiki/Portal_venous_system" title="Portal venous system">portal venous system</a> that delivers venous blood into another <a href="/wiki/Capillary_system" title="Capillary system" class="mw-redirect">capillary system</a>, namely the <a href="/wiki/Hepatic_sinusoid" title="Hepatic sinusoid" class="mw-redirect">hepatic sinusoids</a> of the liver. In carrying venous blood from the gastrointestinal tract to the liver, the hepatic portal vein accomplishes two tasks; namely, it supplies the liver with metabolic substrates and it ensures that substances ingested are first processed by the liver before reaching the <a href="/wiki/Systemic_circulation" title="Systemic circulation" class="mw-redirect">systemic circulation</a>. This accomplishes two things. First, possible toxins that may be ingested can be detoxified by the hepatocytes before they are released into the systemic circulation. Second, the liver is the first organ to absorb nutrients just taken in by the intestines. After draining into the liver sinusoids, blood from the liver is drained by the <a href="/wiki/Hepatic_vein" title="Hepatic vein">hepatic vein</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Clinical_significance">Clinical significance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hepatic_portal_vein&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Clinical significance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Portal_hypertension">Portal hypertension</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hepatic_portal_vein&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Portal hypertension">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Portal_hypertension" title="Portal hypertension">Portal hypertension</a></div>
<p>Increased <a href="/wiki/Blood_pressure" title="Blood pressure">blood pressure</a> in the portal vein, called <a href="/wiki/Portal_hypertension" title="Portal hypertension">portal hypertension</a>, is a major complication of liver disease, most commonly <a href="/wiki/Cirrhosis" title="Cirrhosis">cirrhosis</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-isbn0-632-05582-0_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-isbn0-632-05582-0-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Stigmata of portal hypertension include those of chronic liver disease: <a href="/wiki/Ascites" title="Ascites">ascites</a>, <a href="/wiki/Esophageal_varices" title="Esophageal varices">esophageal varices</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spider_nevi" title="Spider nevi" class="mw-redirect">spider nevi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Caput_medusae" title="Caput medusae">caput medusae</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Palmar_erythema" title="Palmar erythema">palmar erythema</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-isbn1-85996-164-9_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-isbn1-85996-164-9-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pylephlebitis">Pylephlebitis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hepatic_portal_vein&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Pylephlebitis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Pylephlebitis" title="Pylephlebitis">Pylephlebitis</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Pylephlebitis" title="Pylephlebitis">Pylephlebitis</a> is infection of the hepatic portal vein, usually arising from an infectious intraabdominal process such as <a href="/wiki/Diverticulosis" title="Diverticulosis">diverticulosis</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-pmid8589130_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid8589130-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-pmid8488411_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid8488411-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
