			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">General Electric GE90</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">

<p>The <b>General Electric GE90</b> is a family of <a href="/wiki/High-bypass_turbofan_engine" title="High-bypass turbofan engine" class="mw-redirect">high-bypass</a> <a href="/wiki/Turbofan" title="Turbofan">turbofan</a> <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_engine" title="Aircraft engine">aircraft engines</a> built by <a href="/wiki/GE_Aviation" title="GE Aviation">GE Aviation</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Boeing_777" title="Boeing 777">Boeing 777</a>, with thrust ratings ranging from 74,000 to 115,000&#160;lbf (330 to 510&#160;kN). It entered service in November 1995. Currently the world’s largest turbofan engine, it is one of three options for the 777-200, -200ER, and -300, and the exclusive engine of the -200LR, -300ER, and 777F.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design_and_development">Design and development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Electric_GE90&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Design and development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:GE90-Airflow.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/GE90-Airflow.jpg/220px-GE90-Airflow.jpg" width="220" height="137" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/GE90-Airflow.jpg/330px-GE90-Airflow.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/56/GE90-Airflow.jpg 2x" data-file-width="349" data-file-height="217" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:GE90-Airflow.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A 1998 <a href="/wiki/Computational_Fluid_Dynamics" title="Computational Fluid Dynamics" class="mw-redirect">CFD simulation</a> of airflow through the engine</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The GE90 was launched in 1990.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> GE Aviation is associated with <a href="/wiki/Snecma" title="Snecma">Snecma</a> (France), <a href="/wiki/IHI_Corporation" title="IHI Corporation">IHI</a> (Japan) and <a href="/wiki/Avio_S.p.A." title="Avio S.p.A." class="mw-redirect">Avio</a> (Italy). Developed from the 1970s <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> <a href="/wiki/Energy_Efficient_Engine" title="Energy Efficient Engine">Energy Efficient Engine</a>, the 10-stage high pressure compressor develops a pressure ratio of 23:1 (an industry record) and is driven by a 2-stage, air-cooled, HP turbine. A 3-stage intermediate pressure compressor, situated directly behind the fan, supercharges the core. The fan/IPC is driven by a 6-stage low pressure turbine.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ge-747-N747GE-020918-03.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Ge-747-N747GE-020918-03.jpg/220px-Ge-747-N747GE-020918-03.jpg" width="220" height="129" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Ge-747-N747GE-020918-03.jpg/330px-Ge-747-N747GE-020918-03.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Ge-747-N747GE-020918-03.jpg/440px-Ge-747-N747GE-020918-03.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1229" data-file-height="721" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ge-747-N747GE-020918-03.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The higher-thrust GE90-115B mounted on GE's Boeing 747 aircraft during a flight test campaign.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The higher-thrust variants, GE90-110B1 and -115B, have a different architecture from the earlier marks of GE90, with one stage removed from the HP compressor (probably from the rear, to increase <a href="/w/index.php?title=Core_size&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Core size (page does not exist)">core size</a>), with an extra stage added to the IP compressor to maintain/increase overall pressure ratio to achieve a net increase in core flow. General Electric performed a similar re-staging exercise when they upgraded the <a href="/wiki/CF6" title="CF6" class="mw-redirect">CF6</a> from the -6 to the higher thrust -50. However, this thrust growth route is expensive, since all the downstream components (e.g. turbines) must be larger (in flow capacity). As a result GE sought (and received) sole engine supplier status with the -115B on the Boeing 777-300ER and the 777-200F that is usually de-rated to 110. The fan is an advanced, larger diameter unit made from <a href="/wiki/Composite_material" title="Composite material">composite materials</a> and is the first production engine to feature swept rotor blades. Although the larger fan in itself would produce a higher static thrust, an increase in core size and, thereby <a href="/w/index.php?title=Core_power&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Core power (page does not exist)">core power</a>, was required to improve the net thrust at normal flight speeds.</p>
<p>The GE90-115B is powerful enough to fully operate GE's <a href="/wiki/Boeing_747" title="Boeing 747">Boeing 747</a> testbed on its own power, an attribute demonstrated during a flight test.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operational_history">Operational history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Electric_GE90&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Operational history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Air_Canada_GE90_Boeing_777-200LR.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Air_Canada_GE90_Boeing_777-200LR.jpg/220px-Air_Canada_GE90_Boeing_777-200LR.jpg" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Air_Canada_GE90_Boeing_777-200LR.jpg/330px-Air_Canada_GE90_Boeing_777-200LR.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Air_Canada_GE90_Boeing_777-200LR.jpg/440px-Air_Canada_GE90_Boeing_777-200LR.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3240" data-file-height="4320" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Air_Canada_GE90_Boeing_777-200LR.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A GE90-110B1 mounted on an <a href="/wiki/Air_Canada" title="Air Canada">Air Canada</a> <a href="/wiki/Boeing_777-200LR" title="Boeing 777-200LR" class="mw-redirect">Boeing 777-200LR</a> inflight over <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The first General Electric-powered Boeing 777 was delivered to British Airways on November 12, 1995;<sup id="cite_ref-eden115_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eden115-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> the aircraft, with two GE90-77Bs, entered service five days later.<sup id="cite_ref-norris143_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-norris143-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Initial service was affected by <a href="/wiki/Gearbox" title="Gearbox" class="mw-redirect">gearbox</a> bearing wear issues, which caused the airline to temporarily withdraw its 777 fleet from <a href="/wiki/Transatlantic_flight" title="Transatlantic flight">transatlantic</a> service in 1997.<sup id="cite_ref-norris143_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-norris143-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> British Airways' aircraft returned to full service later that year,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> and General Electric subsequently announced engine upgrades.</p>
<p>Due to the expense of producing the higher-thrust GE-90 variants, the GE90-115B is the sole engine available on the Boeing 777-300ER. The ultra-long range Boeing 777-200LR is normally fitted with the GE90-110B1 but can also take the -115B. The GE90-equipped Boeing 777s have been the best-selling long-range large wide-body aircraft in the 2000s at the expense of the <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A340" title="Airbus A340">Airbus A340</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> The -94B for the -200LR is being retrofitted<sup id="cite_ref-tu2015-04_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tu2015-04-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> with some of the first FAA-approved <a href="/wiki/3D_printing#Space" title="3D printing">3D-printed components</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ge2015-3d_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ge2015-3d-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The GE90 series are physically the largest engines in aviation history, the fan diameter of the original series being 312&#160;cm (123&#160;in). The largest variant, the GE90-115B, has a fan diameter of 325&#160;cm (128&#160;in). As a result, GE90 engines can only be airfreighted in assembled form by outsize cargo aircraft such as the <a href="/wiki/Antonov_An-124_Ruslan" title="Antonov An-124 Ruslan">Antonov An-124 <i>Condor</i></a>, presenting unique problems if, due to emergency diversions, a 777 were stranded in a place without the proper spare parts. If the fan is removed from the core, then the engines may be shipped on a <a href="/wiki/Boeing_747" title="Boeing 747">747 Freighter</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Records">Records</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Electric_GE90&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Records">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In October 2003, a <a href="/wiki/777-300ER" title="777-300ER" class="mw-redirect">Boeing 777-300ER</a> broke the <a href="/wiki/ETOPS" title="ETOPS">ETOPS</a> record by being able to fly five and a half hours (330 minutes) with one engine shut down.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> The aircraft, with GE90-115B engines, flew from Seattle to Taiwan as part of the ETOPS certification programme.</p>
<p>According to the <i><a href="/wiki/Guinness_World_Records" title="Guinness World Records">Guinness Book of Records</a></i>, at 127,900 pounds-force (569&#160;kN), the engine holds the record for the highest thrust (although rated at 115,300 pounds-force (513&#160;kN)). This thrust record was accomplished inadvertently as part of a one-hour, triple-red-line engine stress test. In order to accommodate the increase in torsional stresses an entirely new steel alloy (GE1014) had to be created and then machined to extreme tolerances.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> The new record was set during testing of a GE90-115B development engine at GE Aviations' Peebles Test Operation, which is an outdoor test complex outside Peebles, Ohio. It eclipsed the engine's previous Guinness world record of 122,965 pounds-force (546.98&#160;kN).<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>On November 10, 2005, the GE90 entered the Guinness World Records for a second time. The GE90-110B1 powered a 777-200LR during the world's longest flight by a commercial airliner, though there were no fare-paying passengers on the flight, only journalists and invited guests. The 777-200LR flew 13,422 miles (21,601&#160;km) in 22 hours, 42 minutes, flying from <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a> to <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> "the long way": over the Pacific, over the continental U.S., then over the Atlantic to London.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Airworthiness_Directive">Airworthiness Directive</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Electric_GE90&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Airworthiness Directive">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The FAA issued an Airworthiness Directive on May 16, 2013 and sent it to owners and operators of General Electric Company (GE) GE90-110B1 and GE90-115B turbofan engines. This emergency AD was prompted by reports of two failures of transfer gearbox assemblies (TGBs) which resulted in in-flight shutdowns (IFSDs). Investigation has revealed that the failures were caused by TGB radial gear cracking and separation. Further inspections found two additional radial gears with cracks. This condition, if not corrected, could result in additional IFSDs of one or more engines, loss of thrust control, and damage to the airplane. The Airworthiness Directive requires compliance by taking remedial measures within 5 days of receipt of the AD.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Incidents">Incidents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Electric_GE90&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Incidents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On September 8 2015, a GE90-85B powering a <a href="/wiki/Boeing_777-200" title="Boeing 777-200" class="mw-redirect">Boeing 777-236ER</a> on <a href="/wiki/British_Airways_Flight_2276" title="British Airways Flight 2276">British Airways Flight 2276</a>, suffered an uncontained failure during take-off roll<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> leading to a fire. NTSB and FAA investigations have been started to determine the cause of the incident.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>On May 28th 2012 an Air Canada 777 taking off from Toronto en route to Japan suffered failure of a GE90-115B at 1500 feet and returned safely. Engine debris was found on the ground.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="General_characteristics">General characteristics</span></h4>
<ul>
<li><b>Type:</b> axial flow, twin-shaft, bypass <a href="/wiki/Turbofan" title="Turbofan">turbofan</a> engine</li>
<li><b>Length:</b> 287 in (7,290 mm)<sup id="cite_ref-GE90-94B_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GE90-94B-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></li>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Derivatives">Derivatives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Electric_GE90&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Derivatives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="GEnx">GEnx</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Electric_GE90&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: GEnx">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/General_Electric_GEnx" title="General Electric GEnx">General Electric GEnx</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/General_Electric_GEnx" title="General Electric GEnx">GEnx</a> engine, that has been developed for the <a href="/wiki/Boeing_787_Dreamliner" title="Boeing 787 Dreamliner">Boeing 787 Dreamliner</a> and <a href="/wiki/Boeing_747-8" title="Boeing 747-8">747-8</a>, is derived from a smaller core variant of the GE90, but features a fan with swept rotor blades. GE Aviation has also set up a cooperative venture with <a href="/wiki/Pratt_%26_Whitney" title="Pratt &amp; Whitney">Pratt &amp; Whitney</a>, named <a href="/wiki/Engine_Alliance" title="Engine Alliance">Engine Alliance</a>, under which the companies have developed an engine for the <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A380" title="Airbus A380">Airbus A380</a>, called the <a href="/wiki/Engine_Alliance_GP7000" title="Engine Alliance GP7000">GP7000</a>, from the larger GE90-110B/115B core.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="GE9X">GE9X</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Electric_GE90&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: GE9X">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In February 2012 GE announced studies on a slightly smaller derivative engine, dubbed the GE9X, to power the new <a href="/wiki/Boeing_777X" title="Boeing 777X">Boeing 777-8X/9X</a> aircraft. It was to feature the same fan diameter as the GE90-115B (128 in (326&#160;cm)) with an overall thrust decrease of 15,800&#160;lbf to a new rating of 99,500&#160;lbf per engine for the -9X, and a derated 88,000&#160;lbf version for the -8X. The new engines are expected to contribute to a 10% increase in fuel efficiency with a planned 10:1 bypass ratio, 60:1 overall pressure ratio and 27:1 high pressure compressor ratio.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>GE recently updated the GE9X specifications to reflect growing concerns that the 777X was under-powered. Design changes include a slight increase of thrust to 105,000&#160;lbf and a new fan diameter of 133.5 in, giving the new engine the largest fan GE has ever produced.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
