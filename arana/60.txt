<p>The <b>Luzhniki disaster</b> was a deadly <a href="/wiki/Stampede#Human_stampedes" title="Stampede">human crush</a> that took place at the Grand Sports Arena of the Central <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Lenin" title="Vladimir Lenin">Lenin</a> <a href="/wiki/Stadium" title="Stadium">Stadium</a> (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru">Большая спортивная арена Центрального стадиона им. В. И. Ленина</span>) (now known as <a href="/wiki/Luzhniki_Stadium" title="Luzhniki Stadium">Luzhniki Stadium</a>) in <a href="/wiki/Moscow" title="Moscow">Moscow</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> (USSR; now <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>) during the <a href="/wiki/1982%E2%80%9383_UEFA_Cup" title="1982–83 UEFA Cup">1982–83 UEFA Cup</a> match between <a href="/wiki/FC_Spartak_Moscow" title="FC Spartak Moscow">FC Spartak Moscow</a> and <a href="/wiki/HFC_Haarlem" title="HFC Haarlem">HFC Haarlem</a> on 20 October 1982.</p>
<p>Sixty-six FC Spartak Moscow <a href="/wiki/Fan_(person)#Sports" title="Fan (person)">fans</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-iz_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iz-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ize_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ize-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-lan_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lan-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> mostly <a href="/wiki/Adolescence" title="Adolescence">adolescents</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-mem_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mem-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> died in the stampede, which made it Russia's <a href="/wiki/List_of_accidents_and_disasters_by_death_toll#Sporting_events" title="List of accidents and disasters by death toll">worst sporting disaster</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-jw_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jw-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The number of fatalities in this crush was not officially revealed until seven years later, in 1989. Until then, this figure varied in press reports from 3 to 340 fatalities.</p>
<p>The circumstances of this disaster are similar to those of the <a href="/wiki/1971_Ibrox_disaster" title="1971 Ibrox disaster">second Ibrox disaster</a>.</p>

<p>On 20 October 1982, the weather in Moscow was snowy and extraordinarily cold, −10&#160;°C (14&#160;°F), for the middle of October.<sup id="cite_ref-ss0_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ss0-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> There were 82,000 match tickets,<sup id="cite_ref-iz_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iz-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> but due to the freezing weather conditions only 16,500 tickets were sold.<sup id="cite_ref-sb_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sb-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> According to some reports the total number of tickets sold was 16,643.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Grand Arena of Central Lenin Stadium (also called Olympic Stadium) did not have a roof over the seating at the time (it was installed in the 1997 improvements). In preparation for the match, the stadium management decided to open only two of the four stands for fans: the East Stand ("C") and the West Stand ("A"), to have enough time to clean snow from the stands before the game.<sup id="cite_ref-iz_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iz-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-sb_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sb-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> Each stand had seating for 23,000 spectators.<sup id="cite_ref-iz_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iz-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> Most of the fans (about 12,000) went to the East Stand,<sup id="cite_ref-sb_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sb-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> since it was closer to the <a href="/wiki/Vorobyovy_Gory_(Moscow_Metro)" title="Vorobyovy Gory (Moscow Metro)">Metro station</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>note 1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-iz_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iz-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> There were approximately 100 Dutch supporters; the vast majority of fans in attendance were fans of Spartak Moscow.<sup id="cite_ref-jr_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jr-10"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-sb_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sb-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The match started at 7:00 pm. In the 16th minute Spartak took the lead through an <a href="/wiki/Edgar_Gess" title="Edgar Gess">Edgar Gess</a> strike.<sup id="cite_ref-fh_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fh-11"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> The rest of the game was largely uneventful.<sup id="cite_ref-jr_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jr-10"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> Minutes before the end of the game, several hundred fans began to leave the stadium in an attempt to get to the Metro station ahead of the crowds.<sup id="cite_ref-jr_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jr-10"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>There were (and are) two covered stairways in Luzhniki under each stand, leading down to the exits. All of the exits at both stands were open.<sup id="cite_ref-iz_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iz-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ize_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ize-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> However, most of the fans from the East Stand rushed to Stairway 1, because it was closer to the Metro station.<sup id="cite_ref-iz_1-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iz-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Crush">Crush</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luzhniki_disaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Crush">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to the witnesses who were interviewed during the investigation, one of the fans fell at the lower steps of Stairway 1.<sup id="cite_ref-iz_1-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iz-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> According to some reports, it was a young woman, who had lost her shoe on the stairs and stopped, trying to retrieve it and put it back on.<sup id="cite_ref-sb_7-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sb-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> A couple of people also stopped, trying to help the fan in need, but the moving dense crowd on the stairs, limited by metal banisters, promptly crushed them down.<sup id="cite_ref-iz_1-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iz-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> People began to stumble over the bodies of those who were crushed in a <a href="/wiki/Domino_effect" title="Domino effect">domino effect</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-iz_1-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iz-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>More and more mostly teenage fans were joining the crowd on the stairs, trying to push their way down and unaware of the tragedy unfolding below, which caused a massive chain-reaction pile-up of people.<sup id="cite_ref-iz_1-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iz-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> The stampede coincided with the second goal for Spartak, which was scored by <a href="/wiki/Sergei_Shvetsov" title="Sergei Shvetsov">Sergei Shvetsov</a> only twenty seconds before the final whistle.<sup id="cite_ref-iz_1-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iz-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The injured were taken by ambulances to the NV Sklifosovsky Scientific Research Institute of First Aid in Moscow. The next day <a href="/wiki/Yuri_Andropov" title="Yuri Andropov">Yuri Andropov</a> (who replaced <a href="/wiki/Leonid_Brezhnev" title="Leonid Brezhnev">Leonid Brezhnev</a> as <a href="/wiki/List_of_leaders_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="List of leaders of the Soviet Union">leader of the country</a>, <a href="/wiki/Death_and_funeral_of_Leonid_Brezhnev" title="Death and funeral of Leonid Brezhnev" class="mw-redirect">less than a month</a> after this stampede) visited the institute and met with several doctors and relatives of the injured.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The bodies of the dead were taken to the Moscow <a href="/wiki/Morgue" title="Morgue">morgues</a> for <a href="/wiki/Autopsy" title="Autopsy">autopsy</a><sup id="cite_ref-iz1_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iz1-13"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Body_identification" title="Body identification">identification</a>. Later the bodies were returned to the victims' relatives for burial.</p>
<p>A total of 66 people died in this stampede.<sup id="cite_ref-iz_1-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iz-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ize_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ize-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-lan_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lan-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> According to the post-mortem examinations, all of the deceased victims died of <a href="/wiki/Asphyxia#Compressive_asphyxia" title="Asphyxia">compressive asphyxia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-iz1_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iz1-13"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> 61 people were injured,<sup id="cite_ref-iz_1-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iz-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ize_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ize-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> including 21 seriously.<sup id="cite_ref-sb_7-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sb-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> 45 of the 66 dead were <a href="/wiki/Adolescence" title="Adolescence">adolescents</a>, as young as 14, including five young women.<sup id="cite_ref-mem_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mem-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> The Luzhniki Stadium tragedy was the Soviet Union's worst sporting disaster.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luzhniki_disaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Investigation">Investigation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luzhniki_disaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Investigation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A thorough investigation of the Luzhniki disaster corresponded with the new policies of <a href="/wiki/Yuri_Andropov" title="Yuri Andropov">Yuri Andropov</a>, a former <a href="/wiki/KGB" title="KGB">KGB</a> head, who became the leader of the country just one month after the tragedy.<sup id="cite_ref-us_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-us-14"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> He became known in the Soviet Union for his efforts to restore discipline at all levels of the society that had been loosened by the last years of Brezhnev's rule.<sup id="cite_ref-us_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-us-14"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> On 17 December 1982, two months after the stampede, he even went as far as firing the interior minister <a href="/wiki/Nikolai_Shchelokov" title="Nikolai Shchelokov">Nikolai Shchelokov</a> (<i>Николай Щёлоков</i>), the Soviet Union's top police officer, after learning of the corruption allegations against him.<sup id="cite_ref-lat_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lat-15"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> Shchelokov was later stripped of all state decorations, and committed suicide when he was about to go to trial.<sup id="cite_ref-lat_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lat-15"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The criminal investigation of this disaster was launched by the Moscow <a href="/wiki/Prosecutor" title="Prosecutor">Prosecutor's</a> Office. Detective Aleksandr Shpeyer (<i>Александр Шпеер</i>) was appointed in charge of it.<sup id="cite_ref-iz_1-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iz-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ize_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ize-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> 150 witnesses were interviewed during the investigation.<sup id="cite_ref-sb_7-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sb-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> It produced 10 volumes of evidence,<sup id="cite_ref-iz_1-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iz-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-sb_7-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sb-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> and took about three months to complete.</p>
<p>On 26 November, one month after the disaster, the first criminal charges were made against Stadium Director Victor Kokryshev (<i>Виктор Кокрышев</i>) and Stadium Manager Yuri Panchikhin (<i>Юрий Панчихин</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-sb_7-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sb-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> They were <a href="/wiki/Remand_(detention)" title="Remand (detention)">detained</a> and placed in <a href="/wiki/Butyrka_prison" title="Butyrka prison">Butyrka prison</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-sb_7-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sb-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Trial">Trial</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luzhniki_disaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Trial">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Four officials were eventually <a href="/wiki/Criminal_charge" title="Criminal charge">charged</a> in relation to this disaster: Stadium Director V. Kokryshev, Stadium Manager Y. Panchikhin, Stadium Deputy Director K. Lyzhin (<i>К. Лыжин</i>) and the chief of the <a href="/wiki/Militsiya" title="Militsiya">police</a> guards at the East Stand, S. Koryagin (<i>С. Корягин</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-iz_1-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iz-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The trial of the first two was held on 8 February 1983, three and a half months after the tragedy.<sup id="cite_ref-jr_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jr-10"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> Both were found <a href="/wiki/Guilt_(law)" title="Guilt (law)">guilty</a> of <a href="/wiki/Negligence" title="Negligence">negligence</a> and both were <a href="/wiki/Sentence_(law)" title="Sentence (law)">sentenced</a> to three years of <a href="/wiki/Incarceration" title="Incarceration" class="mw-redirect">imprisonment</a>, the maximum penalty for such a <a href="/wiki/Crime" title="Crime">crime</a> in the Soviet <a href="/wiki/Criminal_code" title="Criminal code">Criminal code</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-iz_1-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iz-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> However, Victor Kokryshev (as a person previously <a href="/wiki/State_decoration" title="State decoration">decorated</a> by the state) was eligible for a recent <a href="/wiki/Amnesty" title="Amnesty">amnesty</a> (on the occasion of the 60th anniversary of the <a href="/wiki/History_of_Soviet_Russia_and_the_Soviet_Union_(1917%E2%80%931927)#Creation_of_the_USSR" title="History of Soviet Russia and the Soviet Union (1917–1927)" class="mw-redirect">creation of the USSR</a>)<sup id="cite_ref-sb_7-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sb-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> and was released.<sup id="cite_ref-iz_1-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iz-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> For Yuri Panchikhin, according to the same amnesty rules, the sentence was halved.<sup id="cite_ref-iz_1-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iz-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-sb_7-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sb-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The other two officials, Deputy Director Lyzhin and police chief <a href="/wiki/Major" title="Major">Major</a> Koryagin, did not stand trial in February for medical reasons.<sup id="cite_ref-iz_1-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iz-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> Lyzhin, a <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> veteran, was admitted to the hospital after a <a href="/wiki/Myocardial_infarction" title="Myocardial infarction">heart attack</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-iz_1-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iz-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> Koryagin was badly injured during his attempt to prevent more people from going into the stampede.<sup id="cite_ref-iz_1-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iz-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-sb_7-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sb-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> Later, both of them were given amnesty.<sup id="cite_ref-iz_1-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iz-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Memorial">Memorial</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luzhniki_disaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Memorial">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1992, on the 10th anniversary of the disaster, and three years after the information about it was revealed to the public, a monument was erected near the site of the tragedy.<sup id="cite_ref-jr_10-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jr-10"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>On 20 October 2007, on the 25th anniversary, a memorial match was played at Luzhniki between the former players of FC Spartak Moscow and HFC Haarlem.<sup id="cite_ref-jr_10-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jr-10"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Luzhniki disaster is sometimes compared with the <a href="/wiki/1971_Ibrox_disaster" title="1971 Ibrox disaster">second Ibrox disaster</a> because of some striking similarities.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-re_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-re-17"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> Both of the crushes happened at the end of the match, when a fall on the stairs of one of the spectators caused a massive <a href="/wiki/Chain_reaction" title="Chain reaction">chain-reaction</a> pile-up. There was also the same number of fatalities in both crushes&#160;– 66, many of whom were youths. Furthermore, both crushes coincided with a last-minute goal on the pitch.</p>
<p>The Luzhniki stadium has now been awarded the highest (4th) <a href="/wiki/UEFA_stadium_categories" title="UEFA stadium categories">category status</a> by <a href="/wiki/UEFA" title="UEFA">UEFA</a>. It hosted the <a href="/wiki/2007%E2%80%9308_UEFA_Champions_League" title="2007–08 UEFA Champions League">2008</a> <a href="/wiki/UEFA_Champions_League" title="UEFA Champions League">UEFA Champions League</a> <a href="/wiki/2008_UEFA_Champions_League_Final" title="2008 UEFA Champions League Final">Final</a><sup id="cite_ref-jr_10-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jr-10"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> and will host the <a href="/wiki/FIFA_World_Cup" title="FIFA World Cup">FIFA World Cup</a> <a href="/wiki/List_of_FIFA_World_Cup_finals" title="List of FIFA World Cup finals">Final</a> in <a href="/wiki/2018_FIFA_World_Cup" title="2018 FIFA World Cup">2018</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Media_coverage">Media coverage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luzhniki_disaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Media coverage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Before_1989">Before 1989</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luzhniki_disaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Before 1989">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The only information about the tragedy in the <a href="/wiki/Printed_media_in_the_Soviet_Union" title="Printed media in the Soviet Union">Soviet media</a> immediately after the disaster was a short note in a local daily, <i><a href="/wiki/Vechernyaya_Moskva" title="Vechernyaya Moskva">Vechernyaya Moskva</a></i>, the next day. It said:</p>
<table class="cquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
<tr>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">“</td>
<td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">On 20 October 1982, after the football match at the Grand Sports Arena of the Central Lenin Stadium, as spectators were on their way out, an accident took place due to disturbances in the movement of people. There were casualties. An investigation into the circumstances of the accident is under way<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>note 2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-vm_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vm-19"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></td>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">”</td>
</tr>
</table>
<p>On 21 and 24 October 1982, two Soviet <a href="/wiki/Newspaper#National" title="Newspaper">national</a> sports newspapers&#160;– the daily <i><a href="/wiki/Sovetsky_Sport" title="Sovetsky Sport">Sovetsky Sport</a></i><sup id="cite_ref-ss0_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ss0-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> and weekly <i>Football-Hockey</i><sup id="cite_ref-fh_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fh-11"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup>&#160;– published detailed accounts of this match, but did not even mention any spectator accident.</p>
<p>The article in <i>Vechernyaya Moskva</i> did not go unnoticed by the West. It was reproduced by the Italian news agency <i><a href="/wiki/Agenzia_Nazionale_Stampa_Associata" title="Agenzia Nazionale Stampa Associata">ANSA</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-lss_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lss-20"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> On 22 October, just two days after the tragedy, <i><a href="/wiki/La_Stampa" title="La Stampa">La Stampa</a></i> published a front-page article, where it revealed the information from the Soviet newspaper to its readers and speculated whether the word "casualties" should be understood as "injured" or "injured and killed".<sup id="cite_ref-lss_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lss-20"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> In this article <i>La Stampa</i> also said that the stampede was probably caused by the fall of a woman,<sup id="cite_ref-lss_20-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lss-20"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> although <i>La Stampa'</i>s source of that information is unclear, as this detail was not revealed by <i>Vechernyaya Moskva</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-vm_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vm-19"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>On the next day, 23 October, Italian,<sup id="cite_ref-ls1_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ls1-21"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> Spanish<sup id="cite_ref-ep1_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ep1-22"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> and other Western newspapers stated that there were <b>3</b> people killed and 60 injured in this stampede, citing the Dutch journalists who were present at the match.<sup id="cite_ref-ls1_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ls1-21"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> They also mentioned, that, according to the Dutch journalists, both exits at the stand were open.<sup id="cite_ref-ls1_21-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ls1-21"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ep1_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ep1-22"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> According to <i><a href="/wiki/El_Pa%C3%ADs" title="El País">El País</a></i>, the information about 3 fatalities and 60 injured was distributed by the Dutch news agency <i><a href="/wiki/Algemeen_Nederlands_Persbureau" title="Algemeen Nederlands Persbureau">ANP</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-ep1_22-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ep1-22"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Three days later, on 26 October <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> wrote that "more than <b>20</b> persons were killed and dozens were injured in a panic at Lenin Stadium".<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Ten days later, in the article published on 5 November 1982, <i><a href="/wiki/La_Stampa" title="La Stampa">La Stampa</a></i> stated that "it seems that <b>72</b>" people were killed and "at least 150" were injured in the Luzhiniki disaster, citing the unnamed "unofficial sources".<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>By 1987, <i><a href="/wiki/El_Pa%C3%ADs" title="El País">El País</a></i> had lowered its number of estimated fatalities to <b>68</b>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Until 1989, none of these figures were either confirmed or challenged by the Soviet officials.</p>
<p>Soviet citizens were able to learn the details of this disaster only from the reports of the <i><a href="/wiki/Voice_of_America" title="Voice of America">Voice of America</a></i> and other Western shortwave-radio broadcasters.<sup id="cite_ref-jr_10-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jr-10"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1989">1989</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luzhniki_disaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: 1989">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first publications in the Soviet Union about the number of fatalities of the Luzhniki tragedy appeared only after the introduction of the <a href="/wiki/Glasnost" title="Glasnost">Glasnost</a> policy by Soviet leader <a href="/wiki/Mikhail_Gorbachev" title="Mikhail Gorbachev">Mikhail Gorbachev</a> at the end of the 1980s.<sup id="cite_ref-jr_10-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jr-10"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="18_April">18 April</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luzhniki_disaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: 18 April">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>On 18 April 1989, three days after the <a href="/wiki/Hillsborough_disaster" title="Hillsborough disaster">Hillsborough disaster</a>, and seven years after the Luzhniki tragedy, <i><a href="/wiki/Sovetsky_Sport" title="Sovetsky Sport">Sovetsky Sport</a></i> published a list of the football disasters in history, and mentioned the Luzhniki disaster among them.<sup id="cite_ref-rep_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rep-26"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-elp_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-elp-27"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> The journalists noticed that no information about the number of fatalities in Luzhniki had ever been revealed in the Soviet media and suggested that there were about <b>100</b> fatalities, without providing any reference.<sup id="cite_ref-rep_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rep-26"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-lm_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lm-28"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-elp_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-elp-27"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The information from this article was immediately reproduced by Italian,<sup id="cite_ref-rep_26-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rep-26"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> French,<sup id="cite_ref-lm_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lm-28"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> Spanish<sup id="cite_ref-elp_27-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-elp-27"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> and other international media outlets.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="8_July">8 July</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luzhniki_disaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: 8 July">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Three months later, on 8 July 1989, <i>Sovetsky Sport</i> published another article, "Luzhniki's Dark Secret",<sup id="cite_ref-ss_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ss-29"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> which received even more publicity in the West. A pair of journalists admitted in the article that they were not familiar with the <a href="/wiki/Archive#Government" title="Archive">archived</a> evidence from the criminal investigation and therefore they did not know even the number of fatalities.<sup id="cite_ref-ss_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ss-29"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> So, they loosely estimated it at <b>340</b> fatalities, citing the unnamed "parents of the children who died",<sup id="cite_ref-ss_29-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ss-29"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> but admitting that it is "an unverified figure".<sup id="cite_ref-ss_29-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ss-29"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> The journalists went further, accusing the police officers at the stadium of provoking this disaster and making some other allegations.<sup id="cite_ref-ss_29-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ss-29"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Though full of numerous factual mistakes and fabricated details,<sup id="cite_ref-iz_1-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iz-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ize_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ize-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-lan_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lan-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-iz1_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iz1-13"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> this article immediately became a sensation in the Western media. <i><a href="/wiki/Reuters" title="Reuters">Reuters</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-nyt_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-32"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Associated_Press" title="Associated Press">Associated Press</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-ak_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ak-33"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Agence_France-Presse" title="Agence France-Presse">Agence France-Presse</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-taz_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-taz-34"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/United_Press_International" title="United Press International">UPI</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-lm1_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lm1-35"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup> and other <a href="/wiki/News_agency" title="News agency">news agencies</a> replicated the news about the "340 fatalities" of the "worst-ever sporting disaster in the history". By the end of next day, <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-nyt_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-32"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Times" title="Los Angeles Times">Los Angeles Times</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-ak_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ak-33"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Washington_Post" title="The Washington Post">The Washington Post</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/La_Stampa" title="La Stampa">La Stampa</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-ls3_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ls3-37"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/La_Repubblica" title="La Repubblica">la Repubblica</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Le_Monde" title="Le Monde">Le Monde</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-lm1_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lm1-35"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Die_Tageszeitung" title="Die Tageszeitung">die Tageszeitung</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-taz_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-taz-34"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup> and other leading newspapers publicized this information all over the world.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="20_July">20 July</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luzhniki_disaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: 20 July">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Two weeks later, on 20 July 1989, the Soviet <a href="/wiki/Newspaper_of_record#Newspapers_of_record_.28by_reputation.29" title="Newspaper of record">newspaper of record</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Izvestia" title="Izvestia">Izvestia</a></i> published an interview with a Detective Aleksandr Shpeyer, who was in charge of the 1982 investigation of the Luzhniki disaster. In this article, named "The Tragedy at Luzhniki: Facts and Fabrication",<sup id="cite_ref-iz_1-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iz-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ize_2-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ize-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Detective Shpeyer provided various factual details of the disaster and revealed the real number of fatalities (<b>66</b>) and injured (61).<sup id="cite_ref-iz_1-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iz-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ize_2-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ize-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-lan_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lan-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> When being asked, why this information was hidden from the public for so many years, Shpeyer replied that Prosecutor's Office did not hide any information.<sup id="cite_ref-iz_1-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iz-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ize_2-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ize-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> The archives are open and any researcher could explore the evidence for themselves, after making an official, but simple request, the detective advised.<sup id="cite_ref-iz_1-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iz-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Unlike the "dark secret" article of <i>Sovetsky Sport</i>, the article in <a href="/wiki/Broadsheet#Connotations" title="Broadsheet">broadsheet</a> <i>Izvestia</i> was hardly mentioned by the international media.<sup id="cite_ref-lan_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lan-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="21_July">21 July</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luzhniki_disaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: 21 July">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The next day, <i>Sovetsky Sport</i> in its <a href="/wiki/Editorial" title="Editorial">editorial</a> admitted that its journalists, who wrote the <a href="/wiki/Sensationalism" title="Sensationalism">sensational</a> article two weeks earlier, had to use "conjectures" to provide details of this tragedy.<sup id="cite_ref-ss1_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ss1-39"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup> At the same time, the editors expressed their satisfaction over the worldwide response evoked by their article.<sup id="cite_ref-ss1_39-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ss1-39"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In a special press conference in Moscow in August 1989, the Moscow Prosecutor's Office confirmed that there had been 66 fatalities in the disaster in Luzhniki.<sup id="cite_ref-iz1_13-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iz1-13"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="27_September">27 September</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luzhniki_disaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: 27 September">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>On 27 September 1989, <i>Sovetsky Sport</i> finally admitted that information provided by their journalists "could not be confirmed"<sup id="cite_ref-ize_2-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ize-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> and that "emotions had prevailed over the facts".<sup id="cite_ref-ize_2-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ize-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> The author of this article, Vladimir Geskin, stated that "there were no reasons to doubt the results of the investigation",<sup id="cite_ref-ize_2-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ize-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> reported by <i>Izvestia</i> on 20 July.<sup id="cite_ref-ize_2-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ize-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Some controversy in the media coverage of the Luzhniki football disaster is not unique. In 1989, three months before the "dark secret" article in <i>Sovetsky Sport</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sun_(United_Kingdom)" title="The Sun (United Kingdom)">The Sun</a></i> newspaper published a <a href="/wiki/Hillsborough_disaster#The_Sun" title="Hillsborough disaster">sensational article</a> in the UK about the <a href="/wiki/Hillsborough_disaster" title="Hillsborough disaster">Hillsborough disaster</a>, in which 96 people lost their lives. That article also contained strong allegations and cited unnamed sources.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="After_1989">After 1989</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luzhniki_disaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: After 1989">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The sensational versions of the Luzhniki disaster proved to be much more popular with the media than the facts provided by the investigation.</p>
<p>Despite its refutation in 1989, the figure of 340 fatalities or its variations ("more than 300", "closer to 350", "hundreds", etc.), is still often reproduced by some international media.<sup id="cite_ref-jr_10-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jr-10"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-re_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-re-17"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> Other details from the "dark secret" article in <i>Sovetsky Sport</i> (e.g., that only one stand and one exit were opened for spectators, or that there was a head-on collision of two fans' crowds moving in the opposite directions after the second goal) also sometimes resurface in modern publications.<sup id="cite_ref-jw_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jw-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In 2007 <i><a href="/wiki/NTV_(Russia)" title="NTV (Russia)">NTV</a></i> aired its "Fatal Goal" (<i>Роковой гол</i>) <a href="/wiki/Documentary_film" title="Documentary film">documentary</a> in Russia about the Luzhniki disaster.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup> In 2008, <i><a href="/wiki/ESPN_Classic" title="ESPN Classic">ESPN Classic</a></i> aired a Dutch documentary "Russian Night, the hidden football disaster" throughout Europe.</p>
<p>The only book about this disaster, <i>Drama in het Lenin-stadion</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup> was published in Dutch in the Netherlands in 2007.</p>
