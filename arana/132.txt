<p><i><b>Phellodon</b></i> is a <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> of <a href="/wiki/Tooth_fungi" title="Tooth fungi" class="mw-redirect">tooth fungi</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> <a href="/wiki/Bankeraceae" title="Bankeraceae">Bankeraceae</a>. Species have small- to medium-sized <a href="/wiki/Basidiocarp" title="Basidiocarp">fruitbodies</a> with white spines on the underside from which <a href="/wiki/Spore" title="Spore">spores</a> are released. All <i>Phellodon</i> have a short stalk or <a href="/wiki/Stipe_(mycology)" title="Stipe (mycology)">stipe</a>, and so the genus falls into the group known as "stipitate hydnoid fungi". The tough and leathery <a href="/wiki/Trama_(mycology)" title="Trama (mycology)">flesh</a> usually has a pleasant, fragrant odor, and develops a <a href="/wiki/Cork_(material)" title="Cork (material)">cork</a>-like texture when dry. Neighboring fruitbodies can fuse together, sometimes producing large mats of joined caps. <i>Phellodon</i> species produce a white <a href="/wiki/Spore_print" title="Spore print">spore print</a>, while the individual <a href="/wiki/Basidiospore" title="Basidiospore">spores</a> are roughly spherical to <a href="/wiki/Ellipsoid" title="Ellipsoid">ellipsoid</a> in shape, with spiny surfaces.</p>
<p>The genus, with about 20 described species, has a distribution that includes to Asia, Europe, North America, South America, Australia, and New Zealand. About half of the species are found in the <a href="/wiki/Southeastern_United_States" title="Southeastern United States">southeastern United States</a>, including three species added to the genus in 2013–14. Several <i>Phellodon</i> species were placed on a preliminary <a href="/wiki/Regional_Red_List" title="Regional Red List">Red List</a> of threatened British fungi because of a general decline of the genus in Europe. Species grow in a symbiotic <a href="/wiki/Mycorrhiza" title="Mycorrhiza">mycorrhizal</a> association with trees from the families <a href="/wiki/Fagaceae" title="Fagaceae">Fagaceae</a> (<a href="/wiki/Beech" title="Beech">beeches</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oak" title="Oak">oaks</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Pinaceae" title="Pinaceae">Pinaceae</a> (<a href="/wiki/Pine" title="Pine">pines</a>). Accurate DNA-based methods have been developed to determine the presence of <i>Phellodon</i> species in the soil, even in the extended absence of visible fruitbodies. Although <i>Phellodon</i> fruitbodies are considered inedible due to their fibrous flesh, the <a href="/wiki/Type_species" title="Type species">type species</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Phellodon_niger" title="Phellodon niger">P.&#160;niger</a></i>, is used in <a href="/wiki/Mushroom_dye" title="Mushroom dye">mushroom dyeing</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phellodon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Phellodon</i> was <a href="/wiki/Circumscription_(taxonomy)" title="Circumscription (taxonomy)">circumscribed</a> in 1881 by Finnish mycologist <a href="/wiki/Petter_Karsten" title="Petter Karsten" class="mw-redirect">Petter Karsten</a> to contain white-toothed fungi. Karsten included three species: <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phellodon_cyathiformis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Phellodon cyathiformis (page does not exist)">P.&#160;cyathiformis</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Phellodon_melaleucus" title="Phellodon melaleucus">P.&#160;melaleucus</a></i>, and the <a href="/wiki/Type_species" title="Type species">type</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Phellodon_niger" title="Phellodon niger">P.&#160;niger</a></i> (originally published with the <a href="/wiki/Botanical_name" title="Botanical name">epithet</a> <i>nigrum</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-Karsten_1881_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Karsten_1881-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>P.&#160;nigrum</i> was originally described by <a href="/wiki/Elias_Fries" title="Elias Fries" class="mw-redirect">Elias Fries</a> in 1815 as a species of <i><a href="/wiki/Hydnum" title="Hydnum">Hydnum</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Fries_1815_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fries_1815-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Some early authors did not consider <i>Phellodon</i> distinct enough to be a separate genus, and folded species assigned to this genus into <i><a href="/wiki/Hydnellum" title="Hydnellum">Hydnellum</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Miller_1933_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Miller_1933-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Ragab_1953_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ragab_1953-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><i>Hydnellum</i> is <a href="/wiki/Classification_(biology)" title="Classification (biology)" class="mw-redirect">classified</a> in the family <a href="/wiki/Bankeraceae" title="Bankeraceae">Bankeraceae</a>, which was circumscribed by <a href="/wiki/Marinus_Anton_Donk" title="Marinus Anton Donk">Marinus Anton Donk</a> in 1961. Donk's original family concept included the genera <i><a href="/wiki/Bankera" title="Bankera">Bankera</a></i> and <i>Phellodon</i>, whose species produce <a href="/wiki/Hyaline" title="Hyaline">hyaline</a> (translucent) and echinulate <a href="/wiki/Basidiospore" title="Basidiospore">spores</a> (covered with small spines). Donk also noted that Bankeraceae species lacked <a href="/wiki/Clamp_connection" title="Clamp connection">clamp connections</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Donk_1961_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Donk_1961-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> When clamp connections were discovered in <i>Phellodon fibulatus</i> and tuberculate spore ornamentation (the presence of small nodules on the spores) was found in <i>P.&#160;niger</i>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kenneth_Harrison_(mycologist)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Kenneth Harrison (mycologist) (page does not exist)">Kenneth Harrison</a> thought the family Bankeraceae was superfluous, and placed <i>Phellodon</i> and <i>Bankera</i> in the family <a href="/wiki/Hydnaceae" title="Hydnaceae">Hydnaceae</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Harrison_1972_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harrison_1972-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> This taxonomic rearrangement was rejected by <a href="/wiki/Rudolph_Arnold_Maas_Geesteranus" title="Rudolph Arnold Maas Geesteranus">Rudolph Arnold Maas Geesteranus</a> in 1974, who showed that the tuberculate spores of <i>P.&#160;niger</i> were the result of an immature specimen.<sup id="cite_ref-Maas_Geesteranus_1974_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Maas_Geesteranus_1974-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> Richard Baird and Saeed Khan investigated spore ornamentation in North American <i>Phellodon</i> species using <a href="/wiki/Scanning_electron_microscopy" title="Scanning electron microscopy" class="mw-redirect">scanning electron microscopy</a>, and rejected the placement of <i>Phellodon</i> in the Bankeraceae, preferring to leave it and <i>Bankera</i> in the Hydnaceae.<sup id="cite_ref-Baird_1987_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baird_1987-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> Modern <a href="/wiki/Molecular_phylogenetic" title="Molecular phylogenetic" class="mw-redirect">molecular phylogenetic</a> analysis places <i>Phellodon</i> in the thelephoroid <a href="/wiki/Clade" title="Clade">clade</a> (roughly equivalent to the order <a href="/wiki/Thelephorales" title="Thelephorales">Thelephorales</a>) along with the related genera <i>Bankera</i>, <i>Hydnellum</i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Sarcodon" title="Sarcodon">Sarcodon</a></i>. Although the status of the Bankeraceae has not been fully clarified with molecular genetic techniques,<sup id="cite_ref-Binder_2005_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Binder_2005-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>Phellodon</i> is classified in this family by authorities on fungal taxonomy.<sup id="cite_ref-Cannon_2007_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cannon_2007-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Kirk_2008_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kirk_2008-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-urlMycoBank:_Phellodon_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-urlMycoBank:_Phellodon-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The generic name is derived from <i>phell</i>-, meaning "cork", and -<i>don</i>, meaning "tooth". In North America, <i>Phellodon</i> species are <a href="/wiki/Common_name" title="Common name">commonly</a> known as "cork hydnums".<sup id="cite_ref-McKnight_1998_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McKnight_1998-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/British_Mycological_Society" title="British Mycological Society">British Mycological Society</a>, in their recommended list of common names for fungi in the United Kingdom, name <i>Phellodon</i> species in the form "descriptor word" plus "tooth": fused tooth (<i>P.&#160;confluens</i>), grey tooth (<i>P.&#160;melaleucus</i>), black tooth (<i>P.&#160;niger</i>), and woolly tooth (<i>P.&#160;tomentosus</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-BMS_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BMS-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phellodon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Phellodon_tomentosus_I_Slavkovsky_les_CZ.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Phellodon_tomentosus_I_Slavkovsky_les_CZ.jpg/220px-Phellodon_tomentosus_I_Slavkovsky_les_CZ.jpg" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Phellodon_tomentosus_I_Slavkovsky_les_CZ.jpg/330px-Phellodon_tomentosus_I_Slavkovsky_les_CZ.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Phellodon_tomentosus_I_Slavkovsky_les_CZ.jpg/440px-Phellodon_tomentosus_I_Slavkovsky_les_CZ.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2524" data-file-height="1671" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Phellodon_tomentosus_I_Slavkovsky_les_CZ.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The caps of some <i>Phellodon</i> species (<i><a href="/wiki/Phellodon_tomentosus" title="Phellodon tomentosus">P.&#160;tomentosus</a></i> pictured) can fuse together during growth.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Basidiocarp" title="Basidiocarp">fruit bodies</a> of <i>Phellodon</i> species have <a href="/wiki/Pileus_(mycology)" title="Pileus (mycology)">caps</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stipe_(mycology)" title="Stipe (mycology)">stipe</a>, and thus fall into the general category "stipitate hydnoid fungi". The cap surfaces are initially velvety to <a href="/wiki/Tomentose" title="Tomentose">tomentose</a>, eventually becoming matted. The surface is rough, with pits and ridges, and sometimes with <a href="/wiki/Concentric" title="Concentric">concentrical</a> zones of color or texture. The color can vary considerably, from cream to yellowish, brownish, greenish, greyish or black.<sup id="cite_ref-Stalpers_1993_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stalpers_1993-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> Neighboring fruitbodies can fuse together, forming intertwined caps and partially fused stipes.<sup id="cite_ref-Pegler_1997_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pegler_1997-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Alexander_H._Smith" title="Alexander H. Smith">Alexander H. Smith</a> wrote of <i>P.&#160;tomentosus</i>, "It often occurs in large mats of fused caps almost producing a ceiling over large areas of the moss under conifers."<sup id="cite_ref-Smith_1975_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Smith_1975-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>Phellodon</i> fruitbodies can envelop nearby grass or twigs. The stipe is thickly tomentose or smooth, typically the same color as the cap or darker.<sup id="cite_ref-Zhishu_1993_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zhishu_1993-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> In <i>P.&#160;niger</i>, the outer covering of the stipe is a thick felty layer of <a href="/wiki/Mycelium" title="Mycelium">mycelium</a> that absorbs water like a sponge.<sup id="cite_ref-Harrison_1961_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harrison_1961-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Hymenophore" title="Hymenophore">hymenophore</a> (the fertile, spore-bearing surface) is on the underside of the cap. The spines become grey at maturity.<sup id="cite_ref-Zhishu_1993_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zhishu_1993-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> In conditions of high humidity, <i>P.&#160;niger</i> can form striking drops of black liquid on the actively growing caps.<sup id="cite_ref-Harrison_1961_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harrison_1961-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

Spines of <i>Phellodon tomentosus</i></div>

<p>The fibrous <a href="/wiki/Trama_(mycology)" title="Trama (mycology)">flesh</a> is single to double-layered; duplex layering results from differences in compactness or in the alignment of the constituent <a href="/wiki/Hypha" title="Hypha">hyphae</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Stalpers_1993_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stalpers_1993-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> Tough and leathery when fresh, the flesh develops a corklike texture when dry. In the dried state it often has an odor of <a href="/wiki/Fenugreek" title="Fenugreek">fenugreek</a> or <a href="/wiki/Curry_powder" title="Curry powder">curry powder</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Pegler_1997_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pegler_1997-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>Phellodon</i> species are often free of insect damage, suggesting that they may have <a href="/wiki/Plant_defense_against_herbivory" title="Plant defense against herbivory">defensive chemicals</a> that deter predation.<sup id="cite_ref-Marren_2000_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marren_2000-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> Fruitbodies are not considered <a href="/wiki/Edible_mushroom" title="Edible mushroom">edible</a> due to their fibrous flesh.<sup id="cite_ref-Smith_1975_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Smith_1975-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The hyphal system is monomitic, containing only generative hyphae. These hyphae are not less than 6&#160;<a href="/wiki/Micrometre" title="Micrometre">μm</a> in diameter. All European species lack <a href="/wiki/Clamp_connection" title="Clamp connection">clamp connections</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Pegler_1997_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pegler_1997-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> but they are present in the North American species <i><a href="/wiki/Phellodon_fibulatus" title="Phellodon fibulatus">P.&#160;fibulatus</a></i><sup id="cite_ref-Harrison_1972_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harrison_1972-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/Phellodon_mississippiensis" title="Phellodon mississippiensis">P.&#160;mississippiensis</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Baird_2013_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baird_2013-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Basidia" title="Basidia" class="mw-redirect">basidia</a> (spore-bearing cells) are four-spored.<sup id="cite_ref-Zhishu_1993_18-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zhishu_1993-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Cystidia" title="Cystidia" class="mw-redirect">Cystidia</a> are either absent, or present infrequently as incompletely differentiated cystidioles (sterile cells about the size of an immature basidium).<sup id="cite_ref-Pegler_1997_16-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pegler_1997-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Spore_print" title="Spore print">In mass</a>, the spores are white.<sup id="cite_ref-McKnight_1998_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McKnight_1998-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Basidiospore" title="Basidiospore">Spores</a> are broadly <a href="/wiki/Ellipsoid" title="Ellipsoid">ellipsoid</a> to roughly spherical, and echinulose (covered with small spines). They are also hyaline and <a href="/wiki/Amyloid_(mycology)" title="Amyloid (mycology)">inamyloid</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Zhishu_1993_18-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zhishu_1993-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat_and_distribution">Habitat and distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phellodon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Habitat and distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Fruit bodies grow on the ground. <i>Phellodon</i> species, like all members of the order Thelephorales, are thought to be <a href="/wiki/Mycorrhiza" title="Mycorrhiza">mycorrhizal</a>, forming <a href="/wiki/Symbiosis" title="Symbiosis">symbiotic</a> relationships with trees. Usual hosts include species from the families <a href="/wiki/Fagaceae" title="Fagaceae">Fagaceae</a> (<a href="/wiki/Beech" title="Beech">beeches</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oak" title="Oak">oaks</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Pinaceae" title="Pinaceae">Pinaceae</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Stalpers_1993_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stalpers_1993-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Ectomycorrhiza" title="Ectomycorrhiza">ectomycorrhizae</a> that <i>P.&#160;niger</i> forms with <a href="/wiki/Norway_spruce" title="Norway spruce" class="mw-redirect">Norway spruce</a> (<i>Picea abies</i>) has been comprehensively described. It is distinguished from the ectomycorrhizae of other Thelephorales species by the unique shape of its <a href="/wiki/Chlamydospore" title="Chlamydospore">chlamydospores</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Agerer_1992_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Agerer_1992-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Stable_isotope_ratio" title="Stable isotope ratio">Stable isotope ratio</a> analysis of the abundance of the stable isotope <a href="/wiki/Carbon-13" title="Carbon-13">carbon-13</a> shows that <i>P.&#160;niger</i> has a metabolic signature close to that of <a href="/wiki/Saprotrophic" title="Saprotrophic" class="mw-redirect">saprotrophic</a> fungi, indicating that it may be able to obtain carbon from sources other than a tree host.<sup id="cite_ref-H.C3.B6gberg_1999_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H.C3.B6gberg_1999-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Taylor_2003_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Taylor_2003-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Many <i>Phellodon</i> species are known from the southeastern United States, where they have been extensively researched. According to Baird and colleagues, there are nine distinct <i>Phellodon</i> species from <a href="/wiki/Great_Smoky_Mountains" title="Great Smoky Mountains">Great Smoky Mountains</a> and the surrounding southern <a href="/wiki/Appalachian_Mountains" title="Appalachian Mountains">Appalachian Mountains</a>. Three additional species from this area, <i><a href="/wiki/Phellodon_mississippiensis" title="Phellodon mississippiensis">P.&#160;mississippiensis</a></i>, <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phellodon_brunneo-olivaceus&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Phellodon brunneo-olivaceus (page does not exist)">P.&#160;brunneo-olivaceus</a></i>, and <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phellodon_fuligineoalbus&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Phellodon fuligineoalbus (page does not exist)">P.&#160;fuligineoalbus</a></i> were added to the genus in 2013–14.<sup id="cite_ref-Baird_2013_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baird_2013-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Baird_2013b_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baird_2013b-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation">Conservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phellodon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Conservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Phellodon_melaleucus_264419.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Phellodon_melaleucus_264419.jpg/220px-Phellodon_melaleucus_264419.jpg" width="220" height="164" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Phellodon_melaleucus_264419.jpg/330px-Phellodon_melaleucus_264419.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Phellodon_melaleucus_264419.jpg/440px-Phellodon_melaleucus_264419.jpg 2x" data-file-width="841" data-file-height="628" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Phellodon_melaleucus_264419.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>Phellodon melaleucus</i></div>

<p><i>Phellodon</i> species, like other members of the family Bankeraceae, are sensitive to <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">air pollution</a> and <a href="/wiki/Soil_pollution" title="Soil pollution" class="mw-redirect">soil pollution</a>, and are in general decline in western Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-Stalpers_1993_15-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stalpers_1993-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> In a preliminary assessment for a <a href="/wiki/Regional_Red_List" title="Regional Red List">red list</a> of <a href="/wiki/Threatened_species" title="Threatened species">threatened</a> British Fungi, <i>P.&#160;confluens</i>, <i>P.&#160;tomentosus</i>, and <i>P.&#160;melaleucus</i> are considered <a href="/wiki/Vulnerable_species" title="Vulnerable species">vulnerable</a>, and <i>P.&#160;niger</i> is <a href="/wiki/Rare_species" title="Rare species">rare</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ENRR_2004_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ENRR_2004-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Conservation_biology" title="Conservation biology">Conservation</a> efforts for stipitate hydnoid fungi are hampered by a dearth of information about their basic <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecology</a>, and so molecular genetic techniques are increasingly employed in attempts to better understand these fungi. In the case of <i>Phellodon tomentosus</i>, for example, there is little correlation between fruitbody appearance and below-ground mycelium, making it hard to determine the distribution and rarity of the fungus with standard surveying techniques.<sup id="cite_ref-van_der_Linde_2009_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-van_der_Linde_2009-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>Phellodon melaleucus</i> and <i>P.&#160;niger</i> were included in a Scottish study to develop species-specific <a href="/wiki/PCR" title="PCR" class="mw-redirect">PCR</a> primers that can be used to detect the mycelia of stipitate hydnoids in soil.<sup id="cite_ref-van_der_Linde_2008_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-van_der_Linde_2008-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> DNA testing of collections labelled as <i>P.&#160;melaleucus</i> and <i>P.&#160;niger</i> from the United Kingdom revealed additional <a href="/wiki/Cryptic_species" title="Cryptic species" class="mw-redirect">cryptic species</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Parfitt_2007_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Parfitt_2007-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Ainsworth_2010_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ainsworth_2010-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> PCR analysis can be used to determine the presence of a <i>Phellodon</i> species up to four years after the appearance of fruitbodies, allowing a more accurate determination of their possible decline and threat of extinction.<sup id="cite_ref-van_der_Linde_2012_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-van_der_Linde_2012-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemistry">Chemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phellodon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Chemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Phellodon</i> species contain <a href="/wiki/Thelephoric_acid" title="Thelephoric acid">thelephoric acid</a>, a metabolite of the <a href="/wiki/Shikimic_acid_pathway" title="Shikimic acid pathway" class="mw-redirect">shikimic acid pathway</a>. Thelephoric acid is a <a href="/wiki/Terphenyl" title="Terphenyl">terphenyl</a> quinone—a <a href="/wiki/1,4-benzoquinone" title="1,4-benzoquinone" class="mw-redirect">1,4-benzoquinone</a> wherein positions carbon-2 and carbon-5 are substituted with <a href="/wiki/Phenyl" title="Phenyl" class="mw-redirect">phenyl</a> groups. The <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hirsutane&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Hirsutane (page does not exist)">hirsutane</a> derivative phellodonic acid is found in <i>P.&#160;melaleucus</i>. Phellodonic acid, which exhibits <a href="/wiki/Antibiotic" title="Antibiotic" class="mw-redirect">antibiotic</a> activity towards bacteria and other fungi, was the first <a href="/wiki/Bioactive_compound" title="Bioactive compound">bioactive compound</a> reported from any member of the order Thelephorales.<sup id="cite_ref-Stadler_1993_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stadler_1993-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Total_synthesis" title="Total synthesis">total synthesis</a> was described for phellodonic acid in 2008 using <i>cis</i>-1,2-dihydrocatechol as the starting material.<sup id="cite_ref-Reekie_2008_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reekie_2008-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup> The compound <a href="/wiki/Atromentin" title="Atromentin">atromentin</a> was reported to occur in fruitbodies of <i>P.&#160;melaleucus</i>, but this was not confirmed in a later analysis.<sup id="cite_ref-Baird_1987b_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baird_1987b-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>P.&#160;niger</i> has been a source for several bioactive compounds: the cyathane-type <a href="/wiki/Diterpenoid" title="Diterpenoid" class="mw-redirect">diterpenoids</a>, nigernin A and B; a nitrogenous terphenyl derivative, phellodonin; 2',3'-diacetoxy-3,4,5',6',4''-pentahydroxy-<i>p</i>-terphenyl; <a href="/wiki/Grifolin" title="Grifolin">grifolin</a>; and 4-<i>O</i>-methylgrifolic acid.<sup id="cite_ref-Fang_2010_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fang_2010-35"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>P.&#160;niger</i> has also been used for <a href="/wiki/Mushroom_dye" title="Mushroom dye">mushroom dyeing</a>, in which it produces gray-blue and green colors.<sup id="cite_ref-Roberts_2011_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Roberts_2011-36"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
