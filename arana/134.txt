<p><i><b>Phellodon niger</b></i>, commonly known as the <b>black tooth</b>,<sup id="cite_ref-BMS_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BMS-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> is a species of <a href="/wiki/Tooth_fungus" title="Tooth fungus" class="mw-redirect">tooth fungus</a> in the family <a href="/wiki/Bankeraceae" title="Bankeraceae">Bankeraceae</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Type_species" title="Type species">type species</a> of the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Phellodon" title="Phellodon">Phellodon</a></i>. It was originally <a href="/wiki/Species_description" title="Species description">described</a> by <a href="/wiki/Elias_Magnus_Fries" title="Elias Magnus Fries">Elias Magnus Fries</a> in 1815 as a species of <i><a href="/wiki/Hydnum" title="Hydnum">Hydnum</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Fries_1815_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fries_1815-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Petter_Karsten" title="Petter Karsten" class="mw-redirect">Petter Karsten</a> included it as one of the original three species when he <a href="/wiki/Circumscription_(taxonomy)" title="Circumscription (taxonomy)">circumscribed</a> <i>Phellodon</i> in 1881.<sup id="cite_ref-Karsten_1881_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Karsten_1881-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phellodon_niger&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>P.&#160;nigrum</i> was originally <a href="/wiki/Species_description" title="Species description">described</a> by Swedish mycologist <a href="/wiki/Elias_Fries" title="Elias Fries" class="mw-redirect">Elias Fries</a> in 1815 as a species of <i><a href="/wiki/Hydnum" title="Hydnum">Hydnum</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Fries_1815_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fries_1815-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> The genus <i><a href="/wiki/Phellodon" title="Phellodon">Phellodon</a></i> was <a href="/wiki/Circumscription_(taxonomy)" title="Circumscription (taxonomy)">circumscribed</a> in 1881 by Finnish mycologist <a href="/wiki/Petter_Karsten" title="Petter Karsten" class="mw-redirect">Petter Karsten</a> to contain white-toothed fungi. Karsten included three species: <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phellodon_cyathiformis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Phellodon cyathiformis (page does not exist)">P.&#160;cyathiformis</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Phellodon_melaleucus" title="Phellodon melaleucus">P.&#160;melaleucus</a></i>, and the <a href="/wiki/Type_species" title="Type species">type</a>, <i>P.&#160;niger</i> (originally published with the <a href="/wiki/Botanical_name" title="Botanical name">epithet</a> <i>nigrum</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-Karsten_1881_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Karsten_1881-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Variety_(botany)" title="Variety (botany)">variety</a> <i>Phellodon niger</i> var. <i>alboniger</i>, published by <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kenneth_Harrison_(mycologist)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Kenneth Harrison (mycologist) (page does not exist)">Kenneth Harrison</a> in 1961,<sup id="cite_ref-Harrison_1961_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harrison_1961-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> is considered <a href="/wiki/Synonym_(biology)" title="Synonym (biology)" class="mw-redirect">synonymous</a> with <i><a href="/wiki/Phellodon_melaleucus" title="Phellodon melaleucus">Phellodon melaleucus</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Index_Fungorum:_Phellodon_niger_var._alboniger_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Index_Fungorum:_Phellodon_niger_var._alboniger-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><i>Phellodon niger</i> is <a href="/wiki/Common_name" title="Common name">commonly</a> known as the "black scented spine fungus",<sup id="cite_ref-Pegler_1997_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pegler_1997-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> and the "black tooth".<sup id="cite_ref-BMS_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BMS-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phellodon_niger&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:2011-07-09_Phellodon_niger_2_71274_cropped.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/2011-07-09_Phellodon_niger_2_71274_cropped.jpg/220px-2011-07-09_Phellodon_niger_2_71274_cropped.jpg" width="220" height="171" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/2011-07-09_Phellodon_niger_2_71274_cropped.jpg/330px-2011-07-09_Phellodon_niger_2_71274_cropped.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/2011-07-09_Phellodon_niger_2_71274_cropped.jpg/440px-2011-07-09_Phellodon_niger_2_71274_cropped.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2000" data-file-height="1550" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:2011-07-09_Phellodon_niger_2_71274_cropped.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Underside of fruitbody, showing spines</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Fruitbodies of <i>Phellodon niger</i> have a <a href="/wiki/Pileus_(mycology)" title="Pileus (mycology)">cap</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Stipe_(mycology)" title="Stipe (mycology)">stipe</a>, and so fall into the general class of "stipitate hydnoid fungi". Individual caps are up to 5&#160;cm (2.0&#160;in) in diameter, but caps of neighboring fruitbodies often fuse together to create larger compound growths. Caps are flat to depressed to somewhat funnel-shaped, with a felt-like texture at first before developing concentric pits, wrinkles, and ridges. Initially whitish (sometimes with purplish tints), the cap later darkens in the center to grey, grey-brown, or black. The stipe, measuring up to 4&#160;cm (1.6&#160;in) long, is roughly the same color as the cap. On the underside of the caps are grey spines, up to 4&#160;mm long.<sup id="cite_ref-Pegler_1997_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pegler_1997-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> The outer covering of the stipe is a thick felty layer of <a href="/wiki/Mycelium" title="Mycelium">mycelium</a> that absorbs water like a sponge. In conditions of high humidity, <i>P.&#160;niger</i> can form striking drops of black liquid on the actively growing caps.<sup id="cite_ref-Harrison_1961_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harrison_1961-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Trama_(mycology)" title="Trama (mycology)">flesh</a> has an odor of <a href="/wiki/Fenugreek" title="Fenugreek">fenugreek</a>] when it is dry.<sup id="cite_ref-Pegler_1997_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pegler_1997-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Ellipsoid" title="Ellipsoid">ellipsoid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hyaline" title="Hyaline">hyaline</a> (translucent) spores measure 3.5–5 by 3–4&#160;<a href="/wiki/Micrometre" title="Micrometre">µm</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Basidia" title="Basidia" class="mw-redirect">basidia</a> (spore-bearing cells) are club-shaped, four-spored, and measure 2540 by 5–7&#160;µm. <i>Phellodon niger</i> has a monomitic <a href="/wiki/Hypha" title="Hypha">hyphal</a> system, producing generative hyphae with a diameter of 2.5–5&#160;µm.<sup id="cite_ref-Pegler_1997_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pegler_1997-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat_and_distribution">Habitat and distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phellodon_niger&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Habitat and distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Ectomycorrhiza" title="Ectomycorrhiza">ectomycorrhizae</a> that <i>P.&#160;niger</i> forms with <a href="/wiki/Norway_spruce" title="Norway spruce" class="mw-redirect">Norway spruce</a> (<i>Picea abies</i>) has been comprehensively described. It is distinguished from the ectomycorrhizae of other Thelephorales species by the unique shape of its <a href="/wiki/Chlamydospore" title="Chlamydospore">chlamydospores</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Agerer_1992_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Agerer_1992-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Stable_isotope_ratio" title="Stable isotope ratio">Stable isotope ratio</a> analysis of the abundance of the stable isotope <a href="/wiki/Carbon-13" title="Carbon-13">carbon-13</a> shows that <i>P.&#160;niger</i> has a metabolic signature close to that of <a href="/wiki/Saprotrophic" title="Saprotrophic" class="mw-redirect">saprotrophic</a> fungi, indicating that it may be able to obtain carbon from sources other than a tree host.<sup id="cite_ref-H.C3.B6gberg_1999_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H.C3.B6gberg_1999-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Taylor_2003_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Taylor_2003-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><i>Phellodon niger</i> is found in continental Europe, where it has a widespread distribution,<sup id="cite_ref-Pegler_1997_7-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pegler_1997-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> and in North America. In a preliminary assessment for a <a href="/wiki/Regional_Red_List" title="Regional Red List">red list</a> of <a href="/wiki/Threatened_species" title="Threatened species">threatened</a> British Fungi, <i>P.&#160;niger</i> is considered <a href="/wiki/Rare_species" title="Rare species">rare</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ENRR_2004_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ENRR_2004-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>P.&#160;niger</i> was included in a Scottish study to develop species-specific <a href="/wiki/PCR" title="PCR" class="mw-redirect">PCR</a> primers that can be used to detect the mycelia of stipitate hydnoids in soil.<sup id="cite_ref-van_der_Linde_2008_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-van_der_Linde_2008-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> DNA testing of collections labelled as <i>P.&#160;niger</i> from the United Kingdom revealed additional <a href="/wiki/Cryptic_species" title="Cryptic species" class="mw-redirect">cryptic species</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Parfitt_2007_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Parfitt_2007-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Ainsworth_2010_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ainsworth_2010-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> PCR analysis can be used to determine the presence of a <i>Phellodon</i> species up to four years after the appearance of fruitbodies, allowing a more accurate determination of their possible decline and threat of extinction.<sup id="cite_ref-van_der_Linde_2012_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-van_der_Linde_2012-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemistry">Chemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phellodon_niger&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Chemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>P.&#160;niger</i> has been a source for several bioactive compounds: the cyathane-type <a href="/wiki/Diterpenoid" title="Diterpenoid" class="mw-redirect">diterpenoids</a>, nigernin A and B; a nitrogenous terphenyl derivative, phellodonin; 2',3'-diacetoxy-3,4,5',6',4''-pentahydroxy-<i>p</i>-terphenyl; <a href="/wiki/Grifolin" title="Grifolin">grifolin</a>; and 4-<i>O</i>-methylgrifolic acid.<sup id="cite_ref-Fang_2010_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fang_2010-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> Additional nigernins (C through F) were reported in 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-Fang_2011_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fang_2011-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Fruiting bodies are used to <a href="/wiki/Mushroom_dye" title="Mushroom dye">make dye</a>, in which it produces gray-blue and green colors.<sup id="cite_ref-Roberts_2011_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Roberts_2011-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
