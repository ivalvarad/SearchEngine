<p><b>El Portal</b> is a village in <a href="/wiki/Miami-Dade_County,_Florida" title="Miami-Dade County, Florida">Miami-Dade County</a>, <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. The village name is derived from the Spanish term for "the gate," after two wooden gates that once stood as a gateway to the village. El Portal was originally a neighborhood of the <a href="/wiki/City_of_Miami" title="City of Miami" class="mw-redirect">City of Miami</a> when it was annexed into the City of Miami in 1925. With the arrival of the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a>, the City of Miami gave up its jurisdiction and El Portal was incorporated as its own village in 1937.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=El_Portal,_Florida&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>El Portal is a small, diverse <a href="/wiki/Enclave" title="Enclave" class="mw-redirect">enclave</a> between <a href="/wiki/Miami_Shores" title="Miami Shores" class="mw-redirect">Miami Shores</a> and <a href="/wiki/Miami" title="Miami">Miami</a>. It was <a href="/wiki/Incorporation_(municipal_government)" title="Incorporation (municipal government)" class="mw-redirect">incorporated</a> on December 6, 1937. The enclave was originally the capital of the Tequesta tribal area, and was visited by <a href="/wiki/Pedro_Men%C3%A9ndez_de_Avil%C3%A9s" title="Pedro Menéndez de Avilés">Pedro Menéndez de Avilés</a> in the 1560s.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Three small subdivisions (now <a href="/wiki/Neighbourhood" title="Neighbourhood">neighborhoods</a>)—<i>Sherwood Forest</i>, <i>El Jardin</i> (<a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a> for "The Garden"), and <i>El Portal</i>—merged into the Village of El Portal.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Its borders include 91st Street on the north, 85th Street on the south, Northeast Fifth Avenue on the east and Northwest Fifth Avenue on the west.</p>
<p>The village's name is a <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a> term meaning "the gate," referring to two huge wooden gates on Northeast Second Avenue that were taken down in the 1940s.</p>
<p>The village was also designated as a bird sanctuary by the state for more than 50 years, which means that the birds and trees cannot be harmed in any way. A nature trail winds its way through the village. El Portal also boasts links to prehistoric Indian life at the Little River Mound, a four-foot-high, innocuous grassy knoll that is actually an ancient burial ground. The Little River Mound, located in the Sherwood Forest neighborhood, is the first archaeological site to be publicly recognized and preserved in <a href="/wiki/Miami-Dade_County" title="Miami-Dade County" class="mw-redirect">Miami-Dade County</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=El_Portal,_Florida&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>El Portal is located at <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=El_Portal%2C_Florida&amp;params=25_51_19_N_80_11_39_W_type:city"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">25°51′19″N</span> <span class="longitude">80°11′39″W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">25.85528°N 80.19417°W</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">25.85528; -80.19417</span></span></span></a></span> (25.855173, -80.194168).<sup id="cite_ref-GR1_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GR1-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Census_Bureau" title="United States Census Bureau">United States Census Bureau</a>, the village has a total area of 0.4 square miles (1.0&#160;km<sup>2</sup>), all land.</p>

<p>As of 2010, there were 939 households out of which 9.8% were vacant. In 2000, 33.8% had children under the age of 18 living with them, 39.2% were <a href="/wiki/Marriage" title="Marriage">married couples</a> living together, 21.7% had a female householder with no husband present, and 32.5% were non-families. 24.7% of all households were made up of individuals and 6.2% had someone living alone who was 65 years of age or older. The average household size was 2.98 and the average family size was 3.59.</p>
<p>In 2000, the village population was spread out with 25.4% under the age of 18, 9.3% from 18 to 24, 30.6% from 25 to 44, 24.4% from 45 to 64, and 10.4% who were 65 years of age or older. The median age was 36 years. For every 100 females there were 97.1 males. For every 100 females age 18 and over, there were 96.7 males.</p>
<p>In 2000, the median income for a household in the village was $39,681, and the median income for a family was $41,029. Males had a median income of $27,222 versus $22,409 for females. The <a href="/wiki/Per_capita_income" title="Per capita income">per capita income</a> for the village was $14,782. About 16.3% of families and 22.2% of the population were below the <a href="/wiki/Poverty_line" title="Poverty line" class="mw-redirect">poverty line</a>, including 28.9% of those under age 18 and 9.3% of those age 65 or over.</p>
<p>As of 2000, speakers of <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> was spoken as a <a href="/wiki/First_language" title="First language">first language</a> by 51.96% of residents, while speakers of <a href="/wiki/Haitian_Creole" title="Haitian Creole">French Creole</a> made up 23.72% of the populace, <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a> at 22.38%, <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> 1.08%, and <a href="/wiki/Jamaican_patois" title="Jamaican patois" class="mw-redirect">Patois</a> was the <a href="/wiki/Mother_tongue" title="Mother tongue" class="mw-redirect">mother tongue</a> for 0.86% of the population.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>As of 2000, El Portal had the second highest percentage of <a href="/wiki/The_Bahamas" title="The Bahamas">Bahamian</a> residents in the US, with 2.20% of the US populace.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> It had the third highest percentage of <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">Haitan</a> residents in the US, at 23.00% of the village's population (tied with <a href="/wiki/Spring_Valley,_New_York" title="Spring Valley, New York">Spring Valley, New York</a>,)<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> and the sixty-seventh highest percentage of <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">Dominican</a> residents in the US, at 2.12% of the its population.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> It also had the seventy-sixth most <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cubans</a> in the US, at 4.19% of the population,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> while it had the thirty-second highest percentage of <a href="/wiki/Honduras" title="Honduras">Hondurans</a>, at 1.24% of all residents.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> El Portal's <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaican</a> community had the tenth highest percentage of residents, which was at 8.50% of its residents.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> It's also home to the thirtieth highest percentage of <a href="/wiki/Nicaragua" title="Nicaragua">Nicaraguan</a> residents in the US, at 1.40% of the population.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
