<p><br />
The <b>Brotherly Leader and Guide of the Revolution</b> (<a href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language" class="mw-redirect">Arabic</a>: <span lang="ar" dir="rtl" xml:lang="ar">الشقيق القائد ومرشد الثورة</span>‎) was an unofficial title held by former <a href="/wiki/Libya" title="Libya">Libyan</a> leader <a href="/wiki/Muammar_Gaddafi" title="Muammar Gaddafi">Muammar Gaddafi</a>, who claimed to be merely a symbolic figurehead of the country's official <a href="/wiki/General_People%27s_Committee" title="General People's Committee">governance structure</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-telegraph_gaddafi_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-telegraph_gaddafi-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-jazeera_gaddafi_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jazeera_gaddafi-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> However, critics have long described him as a <a href="/wiki/Demagogue" title="Demagogue">demagogue</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-nytimes_green_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes_green-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> referring to his position as the <i><a href="/wiki/De_jure" title="De jure">de jure</a></i> former <a href="/wiki/Political_office" title="Political office" class="mw-redirect">political office</a>, despite the Libyan state's denial of him holding any power.<sup id="cite_ref-telegraph_gaddafi_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-telegraph_gaddafi-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-jazeera_gaddafi_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jazeera_gaddafi-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brotherly_Leader_and_Guide_of_the_Revolution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Libya_under_Muammar_Gaddafi" title="History of Libya under Muammar Gaddafi">History of Libya under Muammar Gaddafi</a></div>
<p>After the <a href="/wiki/History_of_Libya_under_Muammar_Gaddafi#Coup_d.27.C3.A9tat_of_1969" title="History of Libya under Muammar Gaddafi">coup d'état on 1 September 1969</a>, in which <a href="/wiki/Idris_of_Libya" title="Idris of Libya">King Idris I</a> was deposed, Libya was governed by the <a href="/wiki/Libyan_Revolutionary_Command_Council" title="Libyan Revolutionary Command Council">Revolutionary Command Council</a> (RCC) headed by Gaddafi. On 2 March 1977, after the adoption of the <i>Declaration on the Establishment of the Authority of the People</i>, the RCC was abolished and the supreme power passed into the hands of the <a href="/wiki/General_People%27s_Congress_(Libya)" title="General People's Congress (Libya)">General People's Congress</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Gaddafi then became Secretary-General of the General People's Congress.</p>
<p>On 2 March 1979, Gaddafi renounced all public functions and was designated the "Leader" (<i><span title="DIN 31635 Arabic" class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none">Qāʾid</span></i>) of the Libyan state and was accorded the honorifics "Guide of the First of September Great Revolution of the Socialist People's Libyan Arab Jamahiriya" or "Brotherly Leader and Guide of the Revolution" in government statements and the official press.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Although this title was officially used only from 1979, it is often applied for the <a href="/wiki/History_of_Libya_under_Muammar_Gaddafi" title="History of Libya under Muammar Gaddafi">whole period of Gaddafi's rule</a>. Although Gaddafi had no official government function from 1979, it was understood that he exercised near-absolute control over the government and the country. His 42 years in power prior to the <a href="/wiki/2011_Libyan_Civil_War" title="2011 Libyan Civil War" class="mw-redirect">civil war</a> in 2011 have made him the fourth <a href="/wiki/List_of_longest_ruling_non-royal_national_leaders_since_1870" title="List of longest ruling non-royal national leaders since 1870" class="mw-redirect">longest-ruling non-royal national leader</a> since 1870, as well as the longest-ruling Arab leader.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Although <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Tripoli_(2011)" title="Battle of Tripoli (2011)">ousted from Tripoli</a> at the end of August 2011, Gaddafi retained this <i>de facto</i> position as leader of the <a href="/wiki/Armed_Forces_of_the_Libyan_Arab_Jamahiriya" title="Armed Forces of the Libyan Arab Jamahiriya">Pro-Gaddafi forces</a>, until his defeat and <a href="/wiki/Death_of_Muammar_Gaddafi" title="Death of Muammar Gaddafi">murder</a> by the <a href="/wiki/National_Liberation_Army_(Libya)" title="National Liberation Army (Libya)">Anti-Gaddafi forces</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Sirte_(2011)" title="Battle of Sirte (2011)">Battle of Sirte</a> on 20 October 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_system_of_Libya_under_Gaddafi">Political system of Libya under Gaddafi</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brotherly_Leader_and_Guide_of_the_Revolution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Political system of Libya under Gaddafi">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_Libya_under_Muammar_Gaddafi" title="Politics of Libya under Muammar Gaddafi">Politics of Libya under Muammar Gaddafi</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:232px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Green_book.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Green_book.jpg/230px-Green_book.jpg" width="230" height="173" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Green_book.jpg/345px-Green_book.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Green_book.jpg/460px-Green_book.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2272" data-file-height="1704" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Green_book.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The Green Book</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Libyan Arab Jamahiriya was formally managed on the basis of political ideology set out in <a href="/wiki/The_Green_Book_(Muammar_Gaddafi)" title="The Green Book (Muammar Gaddafi)">The Green Book</a>. The ideology was based on the idea of <a href="/wiki/Direct_democracy" title="Direct democracy">direct democracy</a>, and direct decision-making of all citizens on all political matters. Across the country there were the basic people's congresses, which were considered the original holders of sovereignty. These included the composition of its adult citizens - both men and women. Of their managerial and executive bodies formed the municipal people's congresses and committees, and then the General People's Congress (legislative body) and the <a href="/wiki/General_People%27s_Committee" title="General People's Committee">General People's Committee</a> (executive body).</p>
<p>Gaddafi rejected the parliamentary representation of the people or by elected representatives, as the General People's Congress was seen as a coordinating body that brought together all the popular congresses and committees, and not elected members who made decisions on behalf of the people. All laws were approved by the basic people's congresses, and then finally adopted by the General People's Congress.</p>
