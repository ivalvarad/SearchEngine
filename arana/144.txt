<p><i><b>Red Star Over China</b></i>, a 1937 book by <a href="/wiki/Edgar_Snow" title="Edgar Snow">Edgar Snow</a>, is an account of the <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_China" title="Communist Party of China">Communist Party of China</a> written when they were a <a href="/wiki/Guerrilla_warfare" title="Guerrilla warfare">guerrilla</a> army still obscure to Westerners. Along with Pearl Buck's <i><a href="/wiki/The_Good_Earth" title="The Good Earth">The Good Earth</a></i> (1931) it was the most influential book on Western understanding and sympathy for Red China in the 1930s.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Star_Over_China&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <i>Red Star Over China</i>, <a href="/wiki/Edgar_Snow" title="Edgar Snow">Edgar Snow</a> recounts the months that he spent with the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_Red_Army" title="Chinese Red Army" class="mw-redirect">Chinese Red Army</a> in 1936. Snow uses his extensive interviews with Mao and the other top leaders to present vivid descriptions of the <a href="/wiki/Long_March" title="Long March">Long March</a>, as well as biographical accounts of leaders on both sides of the conflicts, including <a href="/wiki/Zhou_Enlai" title="Zhou Enlai">Zhou Enlai</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peng_Dehuai" title="Peng Dehuai">Peng Dehuai</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lin_Biao" title="Lin Biao">Lin Biao</a>, <a href="/wiki/He_Long" title="He Long">He Long</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mao_Zedong" title="Mao Zedong">Mao Zedong</a>'s own account of his life.</p>
<p>When Snow wrote, there were no reliable reports reaching the West of what was going on in the <a href="/wiki/Communist-controlled_China_(1927%E2%80%9349)#History" title="Communist-controlled China (1927–49)">Communist-controlled areas</a>. Other writers, such as <a href="/wiki/Agnes_Smedley" title="Agnes Smedley">Agnes Smedley</a>, had written in some detail of the Chinese Communists before the Long March, but none had visited them or had first hand interviews. Snow's status as an international journalist not previously identified with the communist movement gave his reports the stamp of authenticity. The glowing pictures of life in the communist areas contrasted with the gloom and corruption of the <a href="/wiki/Kuomintang" title="Kuomintang">Kuomintang</a> government. Many Chinese learned about Mao and the communist movement from the almost immediate translations of Mao's autobiography, and readers in North America and Europe, especially those with liberal views, were heartened to learn of a movement which they interpreted as being <a href="/wiki/Anti-fascist" title="Anti-fascist" class="mw-redirect">anti-fascist</a> and progressive. Snow reported the new <a href="/wiki/Second_United_Front" title="Second United Front">Second United Front</a> which Mao said would leave violent <a href="/wiki/Class_struggle" title="Class struggle" class="mw-redirect">class struggle</a> behind.</p>
<p>Although Snow made clear that Mao's ultimate aim was control of China, many readers got the impression that the Chinese communists were "agrarian reformers." <sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Snow's Preface to the revised edition of 1968 describes the book's original context:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>The Western powers, in self-interest, were hoping for a miracle in China. They dreamed of a new birth of nationalism that would keep Japan so bogged down that she would never be able to turn upon the Western colonies—her true objective. <i>Red Star Over China</i> tended to show that the Chinese Communists could indeed provide that <i>nationalist</i> leadership needed for effective anti-Japanese resistance. How dramatically the United States' policy-making attitudes have altered since then […] It provided not only for non-Chinese readers, but also for the entire Chinese people—including all but the Communist leaders themselves—the first authentic account of the Chinese Communist Party and the first connected story of their long struggle to carry through the most thoroughgoing social revolution in China's three millenniums of history. Many editions were published in China … <sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Publishing_history">Publishing history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Star_Over_China&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Publishing history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Snow was not available to read proofs of the initial London and New York editions, but he revised the text of the 1939 and 1944 editions. The Publisher's Note of the 1939 edition explains that Snow added a "substantial new section" of six chapters bringing the narrative up to July 1938 as well as "many textual changes." Snow made the textual changes partly to polish but he also responded to friends and reviewers. Some of them felt Snow's account of party history had been too critical of Soviet policy, and others felt that he had given too much credit to Mao for independent Chinese strategies. Snow toned down but did not remove the implicit criticisms of Stalin.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> The 1944 edition was allowed to go out of print in the 1950s, but Snow made substantial revisions and annotations for the Grove Press reprint of 1968.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Edgar Snow, <i>Red Star over China</i> London: <a href="/wiki/Left_Book_Club" title="Left Book Club">Left Book Club</a>, <a href="/wiki/Victor_Gollancz" title="Victor Gollancz">Victor Gollancz</a>, 1937; this edition reprinted as <i>Red Star Over China - The Rise Of The Red Army</i>. (2006; <a href="/wiki/Special:BookSources/1406798215" class="internal mw-magiclink-isbn">ISBN 1-4067-9821-5</a>).</p>
<p>--, (New York: Random House, 1938). Some changes from the London edition.</p>
<p>--, (Garden City, 1939) "Revised," with six extra chapters.</p>
<p>--, (New York: Modern Library, 1944).</p>
<p>--, (New York: Grove, 1968) Extensive revisions, with notes and annotations added.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Assessments_and_criticisms">Assessments and criticisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Star_Over_China&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Assessments and criticisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The book has been called the "scoop of the century" <sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> and it clearly played a role in swaying Western and Chinese opinion in favor of Mao. Indeed, Mao commented that the book "had merit no less than <a href="/wiki/Great_Yu" title="Great Yu" class="mw-redirect">Great Yu</a> controlling the floods." As claimed by <a href="/wiki/Jung_Chang" title="Jung Chang">Jung Chang</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jon_Halliday" title="Jon Halliday">Jon Halliday</a> in their book <i><a href="/wiki/Mao:_The_Unknown_Story" title="Mao: The Unknown Story">Mao: The Unknown Story</a></i>, Snow probably believed what he was told to be true, and much of it is still of basic significance, especially the "Autobiography of Mao." But Mao omitted key elements from his accounts of party history and Snow missed others.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> Recent scholarship also demonstrates that Snow submitted the transcripts of his interviews to be edited and approved by Party officials and critics charge that changes in the American edition were made in response to the <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_the_United_States" title="Communist Party of the United States" class="mw-redirect">Communist Party of the United States</a>. <sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrady200342-44_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrady200342-44-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> The account of the <a href="/wiki/Long_March" title="Long March">Long March</a> has come under particular criticism, only to have others reassert its basic validity.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In his 1966 biography of Mao, the American Sinologist <a href="/wiki/Stuart_R._Schram" title="Stuart R. Schram">Stuart R. Schram</a> (1924–2012) asserted that <i>Red Star Over China</i> was "irreplaceable" in learning about Mao's early years.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> He went on to note that despite "many errors of detail", it remains "by far the most important single source regarding his life" and offered important insights into "Mao's own vision of his past."<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
