<p><b>Siyyid `Alí Muḥammad Shírází</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="/eɪ/ long 'a' in 'base'">eɪ</span><span title="/./ syllable break">.</span><span title="/j/ 'y' in 'yes'">j</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/æ/ short 'a' in 'bad'">æ</span><span title="/./ syllable break">.</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/iː/ long 'e' in 'bead'">iː</span><span title="/./ syllable break">.</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/oʊ/ long 'o' in 'code'">oʊ</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'h' in 'hi'">h</span><span title="/æ/ short 'a' in 'bad'">æ</span><span title="/./ syllable break">.</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span><span title="/./ syllable break">.</span><span title="/ʃ/ 'sh' in 'shy'">ʃ</span><span title="/i/ 'y' in 'happy'">i</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/ɑː/ 'a' in 'father'">ɑː</span><span title="'z' in 'Zion'">z</span><span title="/i/ 'y' in 'happy'">i</span></span>/</a></span></span>, <a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">Persian</a>: <span lang="fa" dir="rtl" xml:lang="fa">سيد علی ‌محمد شیرازی</span>‎‎; October 20, 1819 – July 9, 1850) was the founder of <a href="/wiki/B%C3%A1bism" title="Bábism">Bábism</a>, and one of three central figures of the <a href="/wiki/Bah%C3%A1%27%C3%AD_Faith" title="Bahá'í Faith">Bahá'í Faith</a>. He was a merchant from <a href="/wiki/Shiraz,_Iran" title="Shiraz, Iran" class="mw-redirect">Shiraz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Persia" title="Persia" class="mw-redirect">Persia</a> who, at the age of twenty-four (on the evening of May 22, 1844), claimed to be an inspired interpreter of the Qur'an within the Shaykhi school of <a href="/wiki/Twelver" title="Twelver">Twelver Shi'ism</a>. He made bolder claims as time passed, and in 1847, during a trial in Tabriz, asserted a claim to be the Shi'i 'promised one' or <a href="/wiki/Al-Qa%27im_(person)" title="Al-Qa'im (person)">Qá'im</a> (or <a href="/wiki/Mahdi" title="Mahdi">Mahdi</a>). After his declaration he took the title of <b>Báb</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'b' in 'buy'">b</span><span title="/ɑː/ 'a' in 'father'">ɑː</span><span title="'b' in 'buy'">b</span></span>/</a></span></span>, <a href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language" class="mw-redirect">Arabic</a>: <span lang="ar" dir="rtl" xml:lang="ar">باب</span>‎) meaning "Gate" or "Door". He composed numerous letters and books in which he stated his messianic claims and defined his teachings, which constituted a new <i>sharí'ah</i> or religious law. His movement eventually acquired thousands of supporters, was opposed by Iran's Shi'i clergy, and was suppressed by the Iranian government, leading to the persecution and killing of between two and three thousand of his followers, called Bábís. In 1850, at the age of thirty, the Báb was shot by a firing squad in <a href="/wiki/Tabriz" title="Tabriz">Tabriz</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Bah%C3%A1%27%C3%AD_Faith" title="Bahá'í Faith">Bahá'ís</a> claim that the Báb was also the spiritual return of <a href="/wiki/Elijah" title="Elijah">Elijah</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_the_Baptist" title="John the Baptist">John the Baptist</a>, that he was the <i><a href="/wiki/Saoshyant" title="Saoshyant">saoshyant</a></i> referred to in the <a href="/wiki/Zoroastrianism" title="Zoroastrianism">Zoroastrian</a> scriptures,<sup id="cite_ref-gpb_58_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gpb_58-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> and that he was the forerunner of their own religion. <a href="/wiki/Bah%C3%A1%27u%27ll%C3%A1h" title="Bahá'u'lláh">Bahá'u'lláh</a>, the founder of the Bahá'í Faith, was a follower of the Báb and claimed to be the fulfillment of his promise that God would send another messenger.<sup id="cite_ref-buck_eschatologic_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-buck_eschatologic-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life">Life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B%C3%A1b&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B%C3%A1b&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bab-calligraphic-exercise.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Bab-calligraphic-exercise.jpg/220px-Bab-calligraphic-exercise.jpg" width="220" height="233" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Bab-calligraphic-exercise.jpg/330px-Bab-calligraphic-exercise.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Bab-calligraphic-exercise.jpg/440px-Bab-calligraphic-exercise.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1941" data-file-height="2055" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bab-calligraphic-exercise.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Calligraphic exercise of the Báb written before he was ten years old.</div>

<p>Siyyid `Alí Muḥammad Shírází was born on October 20, 1819, in <a href="/wiki/Shiraz,_Iran" title="Shiraz, Iran" class="mw-redirect">Shiraz</a> to a middle-class <a href="/wiki/Merchant" title="Merchant">merchant</a> of the city. His father was Siyyid Muhammad Ridá, and his mother was Fátimih Begum (1800–1881), a daughter of a prominent merchant in Shiraz (she later became a <a href="/wiki/Bah%C3%A1%27%C3%AD" title="Bahá'í" class="mw-redirect">Bahá'í</a>). His father died while he was quite young and the boy was raised by his maternal uncle, <a href="/wiki/Seven_Martyrs_of_Tehran#H.C3.A1j.C3.AD_M.C3.ADrz.C3.A1_Siyyid_.60Al.C3.AD" title="Seven Martyrs of Tehran">Hájí Mírzá Siyyid `Alí</a>, who was also a merchant.<sup id="cite_ref-EoI_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EoI-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-balyuzi_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-balyuzi-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> He claimed to be a descendant from <a href="/wiki/Muhammad" title="Muhammad">Muhammad</a> (a <a href="/wiki/Sayyid" title="Sayyid">Sayyid</a>) through <a href="/wiki/Imam_Husayn" title="Imam Husayn" class="mw-redirect">Imam Husayn</a> through both his parents.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> When he was in Shiraz his uncle sent him to <a href="/wiki/Maktab" title="Maktab">Maktab</a> (school) and he was there for six or seven years.<sup id="cite_ref-iranica_bab_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iranica_bab-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> Sometime between when he was 15 and 20, he joined his uncle in the family business, a trading house, and became a merchant in the city of <a href="/wiki/Bushehr" title="Bushehr">Bushehr</a>, Iran, near the <a href="/wiki/Persian_Gulf" title="Persian Gulf">Persian Gulf</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-EoI_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EoI-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-iranica_bab_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iranica_bab-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> Some of his earlier writings suggest that he did not enjoy the family business and instead applied himself to the study of religious literature.<sup id="cite_ref-iranica_bab_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iranica_bab-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> A contemporary described him as "very taciturn, and [he] would never utter a word unless it was absolutely necessary. He did not even answer our questions. He was constantly absorbed in his own thoughts, and was preoccupied with repetition of his prayers and verses. He is described as a handsome man with a thin beard, dressed in clean clothes, wearing a green shawl and a black turban."<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>An English physician described the young man by saying: "He was a very mild and delicate-looking man, rather small in stature and very fair for a Persian, with a melodious soft voice, which struck me much".<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Marriage">Marriage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B%C3%A1b&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Marriage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1842 he married <a href="/wiki/Khad%C3%ADjih-Bagum" title="Khadíjih-Bagum">Khadíjih-Bagum</a> (1822–1882); he was 23 and she was 20.<sup id="cite_ref-iranica_bab_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iranica_bab-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> She was the daughter of a prominent merchant in Shíráz. The marriage proved to be a happy one,<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> and they had one child, a boy named Ahmad who died the year he was born (1843).<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> The pregnancy jeopardized Khadíjih-Bagum's life, and she never conceived again. The young couple occupied a modest house in Shíráz along with the Báb's mother. Later, Khadíjih-Bagum became a Bahá'í.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Shaykhi_movement">The Shaykhi movement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B%C3%A1b&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The Shaykhi movement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the 1790s in Persia, <a href="/wiki/Shaykh_Ahmad-i-Ahsa%27i" title="Shaykh Ahmad-i-Ahsa'i" class="mw-redirect">Shaykh Aḥmad</a> (1753–1826) began a religious movement within <a href="/wiki/Shi%27a_Islam" title="Shi'a Islam" class="mw-redirect">Shi'a Islam</a>. His followers, who became known as <a href="/wiki/Shaykhism" title="Shaykhism">Shaykhis</a>, were expecting the imminent appearance of the <a href="/wiki/Al-Qa%27im_(person)" title="Al-Qa'im (person)">Qá'im</a> of the House of Muhammad, also called the <a href="/wiki/Mahdi" title="Mahdi">Mahdi</a>. After the death of Shaykh Ahmad, leadership was passed on to <a href="/wiki/Sayyid_Kazim_Rashti" title="Sayyid Kazim Rashti">Sayyid Kázim</a> of <a href="/wiki/Rasht" title="Rasht">Rasht</a> (1793–1843).</p>
<p>In 1841 the Báb went on pilgrimage to <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a>, and for seven months stayed mostly in and around <a href="/wiki/Karbala" title="Karbala">Karbala</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> There he is believed to have met the leader of the Shaykhis, Sayyid Kázim, who showed a high regard for him.<sup id="cite_ref-EoI_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EoI-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> He is believed to have attended some of Siyyid Kazim's lectures; however, this period is almost entirely undocumented.<sup id="cite_ref-iranica_bab_9-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iranica_bab-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>As of his death in December 1843, Sayyid Kázim had counselled his followers to leave their homes to seek the Mahdi, who, according to his prophecies, would soon appear.<sup id="cite_ref-EoI_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EoI-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> One of these followers, named <a href="/wiki/Mull%C3%A1_Husayn" title="Mullá Husayn">Mullá Husayn</a>, after keeping vigil for forty days in a mosque, travelled to <a href="/wiki/Shiraz,_Iran" title="Shiraz, Iran" class="mw-redirect">Shiraz</a>, where he met the Báb.<sup id="cite_ref-balyuzi13_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-balyuzi13-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Declaration_to_Mull.C3.A1_Husayn">Declaration to Mullá Husayn</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B%C3%A1b&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Declaration to Mullá Husayn">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<p>The Báb's first religious inspiration experience claimed, witnessed by his wife, is dated to about the evening of April 3.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> The Báb's first public connection with his sense of a mission came with the arrival of <a href="/wiki/Mull%C3%A1_Husayn" title="Mullá Husayn">Mullá Husayn</a> in Shiraz. On the night of May 22, 1844, Mullá Husayn was invited by the Báb to his home; on that night Mullá Husayn told him that he was searching for the possible successor to Siyyid Kázim, the Promised One. The Báb told Mullá Husayn that he was Siyyid Kázim's successor and the bearer of divine knowledge.<sup id="cite_ref-iranica_bab_9-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iranica_bab-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>After some consideration, Mullá Husayn became the first to accept the Báb's claims to be an inspired figure and a likely successor to the late Shaykhi leader, Sayyid Kazim Rashti. .<sup id="cite_ref-EoI_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EoI-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-iranica_bab_9-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iranica_bab-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> The Báb had replied satisfactorily to all of Mullá Husayn's questions and had written in his presence, with extreme rapidity, a long commentary of <a href="/wiki/Yusuf_(sura)" title="Yusuf (sura)">Surah of Joseph</a>, which has come to be known as the <a href="/wiki/Selections_from_the_Writings_of_the_B%C3%A1b#Qayy.C3.BAmu.27l-Asm.C3.A1.27" title="Selections from the Writings of the Báb">Qayyúmu'l-Asmá'</a> and is considered the Báb's first revealed work.<sup id="cite_ref-EoI_4-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EoI-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Letters_of_the_Living">Letters of the Living</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B%C3%A1b&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Letters of the Living">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Mullá Husayn was the Báb's first disciple. Within five months, seventeen other disciples of Siyyid Káẓim had independently recognized the Báb as a Manifestation of God.<sup id="cite_ref-BBCHistory_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBCHistory-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> Among them was one woman, Fátimih Zarrín Táj Bara<u>gh</u>áni, a poetess, who later received the name of <a href="/wiki/T%C3%A1hirih" title="Táhirih">Táhirih</a> (the Pure). These 18 disciples were later to be known as the <a href="/wiki/Letters_of_the_Living" title="Letters of the Living">Letters of the Living</a> and were given the task of spreading the new faith across Iran and Iraq.<sup id="cite_ref-iranica_bab_9-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iranica_bab-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> The Báb emphasized the spiritual station of these 18 individuals, who, along with himself, made the first "Unity" of his religion<sup id="cite_ref-amanat191_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-amanat191-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> (in <a href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language" class="mw-redirect">Arabic</a> the term "unity" (Wáhid) has a numerical value of 19 using <a href="/wiki/Abjad_numerals" title="Abjad numerals">Abjad numerals</a>). The Báb, in his book the <a href="/wiki/Persian_Bay%C3%A1n" title="Persian Bayán">Persian Bayán</a>, gives the metaphorical identity of the Letters of the Living as the <a href="/wiki/The_Fourteen_Infallibles" title="The Fourteen Infallibles">Fourteen Infallibles</a> in Shí'í Islam (<a href="/wiki/Muhammad" title="Muhammad">Muhammad</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Twelve_Imams" title="Twelve Imams" class="mw-redirect">Twelve Imams</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Fatimah" title="Fatimah">Fatimah</a>) and the four <a href="/wiki/Archangels" title="Archangels" class="mw-redirect">archangels</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-amanat191_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-amanat191-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> In some ways, they parallel the Twelve Apostles of Christ.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Proclamation">Proclamation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B%C3%A1b&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Proclamation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In his early writings, the Báb appears to identify himself as the gate (<i>báb</i>) to the <a href="/wiki/Muhammad_al-Mahdi" title="Muhammad al-Mahdi">Hidden Twelfth Imam</a>, and later he begins explicitly to proclaim his station as that of the Hidden Imam and a new messenger from God.<sup id="cite_ref-saiedi19_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-saiedi19-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> Rather than being a discontinued or evolving consciousness, Saiedi states that the works of the Báb are unitary throughout, and that the gradual disclosure of the Báb's identity is defined by the principle of unity in diversity throughout reality.<sup id="cite_ref-saiedi19_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-saiedi19-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

</div>
</div>
<p>In the Báb's early writings, the exalted identity he was claiming was unmistakable, but because of the reception of the people, his writings appear to convey the impression that he is only the gate to the Hidden Twelfth Imam.<sup id="cite_ref-saiedi19_20-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-saiedi19-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> To his circle of early believers, the Báb was equivocal about his exact status, gradually confiding in them that he was not merely a gate to the Hidden Imam, but the Manifestation of the Hidden Imam and the <a href="/wiki/Al-Qa%27im_(person)" title="Al-Qa'im (person)">Qa'im</a> himself.<sup id="cite_ref-EoA_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EoA-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> During his early meetings with <a href="/wiki/Mull%C3%A1_Husayn" title="Mullá Husayn">Mullá Husayn</a>, the Báb described himself as the Master and the Promised One; he did not consider himself to be simply <a href="/wiki/Sayyid_Kazim_Rashti" title="Sayyid Kazim Rashti">Siyyid Kazim</a>'s successor, but claimed a prophetic status, a kind of deputy, delegated not just by the Hidden Imam but through Divine authority.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> His early texts such as the <a href="/wiki/Selections_from_the_Writings_of_the_B%C3%A1b#Qayy.C3.BAmu.27l-Asm.C3.A1.27" title="Selections from the Writings of the Báb">"Commentary on the Surih of Joseph"</a> used Quranic language that implied divine authority and identified himself effectively with the Imam.<sup id="cite_ref-iranica_bab_9-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iranica_bab-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-amanat230_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-amanat230-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> When Mullá `Alí Basṭámí, the second Letter of the Living, was put on trial in <a href="/wiki/Baghdad" title="Baghdad">Baghdad</a> for preaching about the Báb, clerics studied the "Commentary on the Surih of Joseph," recognized in it a claim to divine revelation, and quoted from it extensively to prove that the author had made a messianic claim.<sup id="cite_ref-amanat230_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-amanat230-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>However, in the early phase of his declaration to the public, the title <i>báb</i> was emphasized as that of the gate leading to the Hidden Imam, as the Báb had told his early believers not to fully disclose his claims or reveal his name.<sup id="cite_ref-manuchehri_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-manuchehri-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> The approach of laying claim to a lower position was intended to create a sense of anticipation for the appearance of the Hidden Imam, as well to avoid persecution and imprisonment, because a public proclamation of <a href="/wiki/Mahdi" title="Mahdi">mahdi</a> status could have brought upon the Báb a swift penalty of death.<sup id="cite_ref-manuchehri_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-manuchehri-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> After a couple of months, as the Báb observed further acceptance and readiness among his believers and the public, he gradually shifted his public claim to that of the Hidden Imam.<sup id="cite_ref-manuchehri_24-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-manuchehri-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> Then in his final years he publicly announced his station as a <a href="/wiki/Manifestation_of_God" title="Manifestation of God">Manifestation of God</a>; in his <a href="#Trial">trial</a>, he boldly proclaimed himself, in the presence of the Heir to the Throne of <a href="/wiki/Persian_Empire" title="Persian Empire">Persia</a> and other notables, to be the Promised One.<sup id="cite_ref-manuchehri_24-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-manuchehri-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-trial_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-trial-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> In the early months of his public declarations, the adoption of a cautious policy had essentially achieved maximum attention with minimum controversy.<sup id="cite_ref-manuchehri_24-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-manuchehri-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The gradual unfolding of his claims, however, did cause some confusion, both among the public and for some of his believers. A number of his early followers had instantly recognized his station as a messenger from God with divine authority, and this resulted in disagreement within the Bábi community.<sup id="cite_ref-manuchehri_24-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-manuchehri-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> Furthermore, even though the Báb had intended to convey his message with discretion, many of his followers (such as <a href="/wiki/T%C3%A1hirih" title="Táhirih">Táhirih</a>) openly declared the coming of the promised Hidden Imam and Mahdi.<sup id="cite_ref-manuchehri_24-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-manuchehri-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Travels_and_imprisonment">Travels and imprisonment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B%C3%A1b&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Travels and imprisonment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After the eighteen Letters of the Living had recognized him, the Báb and the eighteenth Letter of the Living, <a href="/wiki/Qudd%C3%BAs" title="Quddús">Quddús</a>, left on a pilgrimage to <a href="/wiki/Mecca" title="Mecca">Mecca</a> and <a href="/wiki/Medina" title="Medina">Medina</a>, the sacred cities of Islam.<sup id="cite_ref-iranica_bab_9-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iranica_bab-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> At the <a href="/wiki/Kaaba" title="Kaaba">Kaaba</a> in Mecca, the Báb publicly declared his claim to be the <a href="/wiki/Al-Qa%27im_(person)" title="Al-Qa'im (person)">Qa'im</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> He also wrote to the <a href="/wiki/Sharif_of_Mecca" title="Sharif of Mecca">Sharif of Mecca</a>, the Custodian of the Kaaba, proclaiming his mission. After their pilgrimage, the Báb and Quddús returned to <a href="/wiki/Bushehr" title="Bushehr">Bushehr</a>, Iran.<sup id="cite_ref-balyuzi_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-balyuzi-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>After some time, preaching by the Letters of the Living led to opposition by the Islamic clergy, prompting the Governor of Shiraz to order the Báb's arrest. The Báb, upon hearing of the arrest order, left Bushehr for Shiraz in June 1845 and presented himself to the authorities. He was placed under house arrest at the home of his uncle until a cholera epidemic broke out in the city in September 1846.<sup id="cite_ref-iranica_bab_9-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iranica_bab-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> The Báb was released and departed for <a href="/wiki/Isfahan_(city)" title="Isfahan (city)" class="mw-redirect">Isfahan</a>. There, many came to see him at the house of the imám jum'ih, head of the local clergy, who became sympathetic. After an informal gathering where the Báb debated the local clergy and displayed his speed in producing instantaneous verses, his popularity soared.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> After the death of the Governor of Isfahan, <a href="/wiki/Manuchehr_Khan_Gorji" title="Manuchehr Khan Gorji" class="mw-redirect">Manuchehr Khan Gorji</a>, who had become his supporter, pressure from the clergy of the province led to the Shah, <a href="/wiki/Mohammad_Shah_Qajar" title="Mohammad Shah Qajar">Mohammad Shah Qajar</a>, ordering the Báb to <a href="/wiki/Tehran" title="Tehran">Tehran</a> in January 1847.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> After spending several months in a camp outside Tehran, and before the Báb could meet the Shah, the Prime Minister sent the Báb to <a href="/wiki/Tabriz" title="Tabriz">Tabriz</a> in the northwestern corner of the country, where he was confined.<sup id="cite_ref-iranica_bab_9-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iranica_bab-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>After forty days in Tabriz, the Báb was then transferred to the fortress of <a href="/wiki/Maku,_Iran" title="Maku, Iran">Máh-Kú</a> in the province of <a href="/wiki/Iranian_Azarbaijan" title="Iranian Azarbaijan" class="mw-redirect">Azarbaijan</a> close to the Turkish border. During his incarceration there, the Báb began his most important work, the <a href="/wiki/Persian_Bay%C3%A1n" title="Persian Bayán">Persian Bayán</a>, which he never finished. Because of the Báb's growing popularity in Máh-Kú and the governor of Máh-Kú converting, the prime minister transferred him to the fortress of <a href="/wiki/Chihr%C3%ADq" title="Chihríq" class="mw-redirect"><u>Ch</u>ihríq</a> in April 1848.<sup id="cite_ref-EoI_4-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EoI-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> In that place as well, the Báb's popularity grew and his jailors relaxed restrictions on him. It was at this time that Áqa Bálá Big Shíshvání Naqshbandí painted the portrait of the Báb.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> Hence the Prime Minister ordered the Báb back to Tabriz where the government called on religious authorities to put the Báb on trial for blasphemy and apostasy.<sup id="cite_ref-iranica_bab_9-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iranica_bab-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Trial">Trial</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B%C3%A1b&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Trial">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The trial, attended by the Crown Prince, occurred in July 1848 and involved numerous local clergy. They questioned the Báb about the nature of his claims, his teachings, and demanded that he produce miracles to prove his divine authority. They admonished him to recant his claims. There are nine extant eyewitness reports of the trial, of which several may originate from an earlier source. Six of the reports are from Muslim accounts, and portray the Báb in an unfavourable light.<sup id="cite_ref-trial_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-trial-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> There are 62 questions that are found in the nine sources, however eighteen occur in only one source, fifteen in two, eight in three, five in four, thirteen in five, and three in six. Not including "yes" and "he did not answer", there are thirty-five answers, of which ten occur in one source, eight in two, six in three, three in four, two in five, five in six. Only one answer is found in all nine eyewitness sources, where the Báb states that "I am that person you have been awaiting for one thousand years."<sup id="cite_ref-trial_25-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-trial-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The trial did not bring a decisive result. Some clergy called for capital punishment, but the government pressured them to issue a lenient judgement because the Báb was popular. The government asked medical experts to declare the Báb insane so that he could not be executed. It is also likely that the government, to appease the religious clergy, spread rumours that the Báb had recanted.<sup id="cite_ref-amanat_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-amanat-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Shaykh_al-Islam" title="Shaykh al-Islam" class="mw-redirect">Shaykh al-Islam</a> (a very prominent local cleric), the champion of the anti-Bábí campaign, who was not at the Báb's trial, issued a conditional death sentence if the Báb was found to be sane. A <a href="/wiki/Fatwa" title="Fatwa">fatwa</a> was issued establishing the Báb's <a href="/wiki/Apostasy_in_Islam" title="Apostasy in Islam">apostasy</a> and stated "The repentance of an incorrigible apostate is not accepted, and the only thing which has caused the postponement of thy execution is a doubt as to thy sanity of mind."<sup id="cite_ref-amanat_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-amanat-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The crown prince's physician, William Cormick, examined the Báb and complied with the government's request to find grounds for clemency.<sup id="cite_ref-trial_25-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-trial-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> The physician's opinion saved the Báb from execution for a time, but the clergy insisted that he face corporal punishment instead, so the Báb was <a href="/wiki/Foot_whipping" title="Foot whipping">bastinadoed</a> (administered twenty lashes to the bottoms of his feet).<sup id="cite_ref-amanat_30-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-amanat-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> The official report states that because of his harsh beating, the Báb recanted, apologized, and stated that he would not continue to advance claims of divinity.<sup id="cite_ref-msbr_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-msbr-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>While various government sources indicate that the Báb recanted his claim, there is little non-governmental evidence of their validity. Some theorise that the assertions were made to embarrass the Báb and undermine his credibility with the public.<sup id="cite_ref-amanat_30-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-amanat-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> There exists an unsigned and undated document that was supposedly written shortly after the Báb's trial in Tabriz where the Báb recants his claims to a divine station. But the language of this document is very different from the Báb's usual style; it could have been prepared by the authorities, but the Báb refused to sign it.<sup id="cite_ref-trial_25-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-trial-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-amanat_30-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-amanat-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> The Báb was finally ordered back to the fortress of <u>Ch</u>ihríq.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Execution">Execution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B%C3%A1b&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Execution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Execution_of_the_B%C3%A1b" title="Execution of the Báb">Execution of the Báb</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>In mid-1850 a new prime-minister, <a href="/wiki/Amir_Kabir" title="Amir Kabir">Amir Kabir</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-gpb_52_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gpb_52-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup> ordered the execution of the Báb, probably because various Bábí insurrections had been defeated and the movement's popularity appeared to be waning. The Báb was brought back to Tabríz from <u>Ch</u>ihríq, so that he could be shot by a <a href="/wiki/Execution_by_firing_squad" title="Execution by firing squad">firing squad</a>. The night before his execution, as he was being conducted to his cell, a young Bábí, Muḥammad-`Alíy-i-Zunúzí, called Anís, threw himself at the feet of the Báb and begged to be killed with him. He was immediately arrested and placed in the same cell as the Báb.</p>
<p>On the morning of July 9, 1850, the Báb was taken to the courtyard of the barracks in which he was being held, where thousands of people had gathered to watch his execution. The Báb and Anís were suspended on a wall and a large firing squad of Christian soldiers prepared to shoot.<sup id="cite_ref-iranica_bab_9-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iranica_bab-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> Numerous eye-witness reports, including those of Western diplomats, recount the result.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup> The order was given to fire and the barracks square filled with musket smoke. When it cleared the Báb was no longer in the courtyard and his companion stood there unharmed; the bullets apparently had not harmed either man, but had cut the rope suspending them from the wall.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup> There was a great commotion, many in the crowd believing the Báb had ascended to heaven or simply disappeared. But the soldiers subsequently found the Báb in another part of the barracks, completely unharmed, giving his final instructions to his <a href="/wiki/Letters_of_the_Living#Siyyid_H.CC.A3usayn_Yazd.C3.AD" title="Letters of the Living">secretary</a>. He and Anís were tied up for execution a second time, a second firing squad of Muslim soldiers was ranged in front of them, and a second order to fire was given. This time, the Báb and his companion were killed.<sup id="cite_ref-iranica_bab_9-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iranica_bab-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/B%C3%A1b%C3%AD" title="Bábí" class="mw-redirect">Bábí</a>–<a href="/wiki/Bah%C3%A1%27%C3%AD_Faith" title="Bahá'í Faith">Bahá'í</a> tradition, the failure of the first firing squad to kill the Báb is believed to have been a miracle. Their remains were dumped outside the gates of the town to be eaten by animals.</p>
<p>The remains, however, were clandestinely rescued by a handful of Bábis and were hidden. Over time the remains were secretly transported according to instructions of Bahá'u'lláh and then `Abdu'l-Bahá by way of Isfahan, Kirmanshah, <a href="/wiki/Baghdad" title="Baghdad">Baghdad</a> and <a href="/wiki/Damascus" title="Damascus">Damascus</a>, to <a href="/wiki/Beirut" title="Beirut">Beirut</a> and thence by sea to <a href="/wiki/Acre,_Israel" title="Acre, Israel">Acre</a> on the plain below <a href="/wiki/Mount_Carmel" title="Mount Carmel">Mount Carmel</a> in 1899.<sup id="cite_ref-gpb_18_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gpb_18-35"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup> On March 21, 1909, the remains were then interred in a special <a href="/wiki/Shrine_of_the_B%C3%A1b" title="Shrine of the Báb">tomb</a>, erected for this purpose by <a href="/wiki/%60Abdu%27l-Bah%C3%A1" title="`Abdu'l-Bahá">`Abdu'l-Bahá</a>, on Mount Carmel in present-day <a href="/wiki/Haifa" title="Haifa">Haifa</a>, Israel.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Bah%C3%A1%27%C3%AD_World_Centre" title="Bahá'í World Centre">Bahá'í World Centre</a> is located close to this site and visitors are welcome to tour the gardens.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Succession">Succession</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B%C3%A1b&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Succession">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Bah%C3%A1%27%C3%AD/B%C3%A1b%C3%AD_split" title="Bahá'í/Bábí split">Bahá'í/Bábí split</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bahai_center_Haifa_2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Bahai_center_Haifa_2.jpg/170px-Bahai_center_Haifa_2.jpg" width="170" height="227" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Bahai_center_Haifa_2.jpg/255px-Bahai_center_Haifa_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Bahai_center_Haifa_2.jpg/340px-Bahai_center_Haifa_2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="384" data-file-height="512" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bahai_center_Haifa_2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Shrine_of_the_B%C3%A1b" title="Shrine of the Báb">Shrine of the Báb</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In most of his prominent writings, The Báb alluded to a Promised One, most commonly referred to as <i>man yazhiruhu'lláh</i>, "<a href="/wiki/He_whom_God_shall_make_manifest" title="He whom God shall make manifest">Him Whom God shall make manifest</a>", and that he himself was "but a ring upon the hand of Him Whom God shall make manifest." Within 20 years of the Báb's death, over 25 people claimed to be the Promised One, most significantly <a href="/wiki/Bah%C3%A1%27u%27ll%C3%A1h" title="Bahá'u'lláh">Bahá'u'lláh</a>.</p>
<p>Before the Báb's death, he sent a letter to Mírzá Yahyá, titled <a href="/wiki/Subh-i-Azal" title="Subh-i-Azal">Subh-i-Azal</a>, which some consider to be a will and testament.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup> The letter is recognized as appointing Subh-i-Azal to be the leader of the Bábí community after the death of the Báb. He is also ordered to obey the Promised One when he appears.<sup id="cite_ref-Manuchehri_.282004.29_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Manuchehri_.282004.29-38"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup> At the time Subh-i-Azal was still a teenager, had never demonstrated leadership in the Bábí movement, and was still living in the house of his older brother, Bahá'u'lláh. All of this lends credence to the Bahá'í claim that the Báb appointed Subh-i-Azal the head of the Bábí Faith so as to divert attention away from Bahá'u'lláh, while allowing Bábís to visit Bahá'u'lláh and consult with him freely, and allowing Bahá'u'lláh to write to Bábís easily and freely. Furthermore, there is a long history in Shí`ism of hidden leaders, with their deputies wielding the true power (the four bábs themselves are the first examples of this, as is `Alí-Muhammad's choice of the title "the Báb").</p>
<p>Bahá'u'lláh claimed that in 1852, while a prisoner in Tehran, he was visited by a "<a href="/wiki/Maid_of_Heaven" title="Maid of Heaven">Maid of Heaven</a>", which symbolically marked the beginning of his mission as a Messenger of God. Ten years later in Baghdad, he made his first public declaration and eventually was recognized by the vast majority of Bábís as "He Whom God shall make manifest". His followers began calling themselves <a href="/wiki/Bah%C3%A1%27%C3%AD_Faith" title="Bahá'í Faith">Bahá'ís</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-cole_bahabio_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cole_bahabio-39"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Subh-i-Azal continued to live with or close to Bahá'u'lláh throughout the latter's exiles from Iran to Baghdad and then to Istanbul and Edirne, even though Bahá'u'lláh's claim to be a Manifestation of God in 1863 theoretically rendered moot Subh-i-Azal's authority as the head of the Bábí community. In September 1867, in Edirne, the rival claims to authority came to a head. Subh-i-Azal challenged Bahá'u'lláh to a test of the divine will in a local mosque in <a href="/wiki/Edirne" title="Edirne">Edirne</a> (Adrianople), such that "God would strike down the impostor". Bahá'u'lláh agreed and went to the Sultan Selim Mosque at the appointed time, but Subh-i-Azal failed to show up.<sup id="cite_ref-Browne_.281918.29_p._18_.26_Salm.C3.A1n.C3.AD_.281982.29_pp._94-95_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Browne_.281918.29_p._18_.26_Salm.C3.A1n.C3.AD_.281982.29_pp._94-95-40"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Subh-i-Azal's followers became known as <a href="/wiki/Azali" title="Azali">Azalis</a> or Azali Bábís. For the Bábís who did not recognize Bahá'u'lláh, Subh-i-Azal remained their leader until his death in 1912. Whether or not he had a successor is disputed. Bahá'í sources report that 11 of the 18 "witnesses" appointed by Subh-i-Azal to oversee the Bábí community became Bahá'ís, as well as his son. The man allegedly appointed by Subh-i-Azal to succeed him, Hadíy-i-Dawlat-Abádí, later publicly recanted his faith in the Báb and Subh-i-Azal.<sup id="cite_ref-GPB.2C_p._233_.26_Momen_.281991.29_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GPB.2C_p._233_.26_Momen_.281991.29-41"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Ultimately, Bahá'u'llah emerged more successful and nearly all of the Báb's followers abandoned Subh-i-Azal and became Bahá'ís. Today Bahá'ís have several million followers, while estimates of the number of Azalís are generally around one thousand, isolated in Iran.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Teachings">Teachings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B%C3%A1b&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Teachings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Teachings_of_the_B%C3%A1b" title="Teachings of the Báb">Teachings of the Báb</a></div>
<p>The Báb's teachings can be grouped into three broad stages which each have a dominant thematic focus. His earliest teachings are primarily defined by his interpretation of the <a href="/wiki/Qur%27an" title="Qur'an" class="mw-redirect">Qur'an</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Hadith" title="Hadith">Islamic traditions</a>. While this interpretive mode continues throughout all three stages of his teachings, a shift takes place where his emphasis moves to philosophical elucidation and finally to <a href="/wiki/Legislation" title="Legislation">legislative</a> pronouncements. In the second philosophical stage, the Báb gives an explanation of the <a href="/wiki/Metaphysics" title="Metaphysics">metaphysics</a> of being and creation, and in the third legislative stage his mystical and historical principles are explicitly united.<sup id="cite_ref-saiedi27_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-saiedi27-43"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup> An analysis of the Báb's writings throughout the three stages shows that all of his teachings were animated by a common principle that had multiple dimensions and forms.<sup id="cite_ref-saiedi49_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-saiedi49-44"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Writings">Writings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B%C3%A1b&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Writings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="mbox-small plainlinks sistersitebox" style="border:1px solid #aaa;background-color:#f9f9f9">
<tr>
<td class="mbox-image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Wikiquote-logo.svg/34px-Wikiquote-logo.svg.png" width="34" height="40" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Wikiquote-logo.svg/51px-Wikiquote-logo.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Wikiquote-logo.svg/68px-Wikiquote-logo.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="300" data-file-height="355" /></td>
<td class="mbox-text plainlist">Wikiquote has quotations related to: <i><b><a href="//en.wikiquote.org/wiki/Special:Search/B%C3%A1b" class="extiw" title="q:Special:Search/Báb">Báb</a></b></i></td>
</tr>
</table>
<p>Most of the writings of the Báb have been lost. The Báb himself stated they exceeded five hundred thousand verses in length; the Qur'an, in contrast, is 6300 verses in length. If one assumes 25 verses per page, that would equal 20,000 pages of text.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup> Nabíl-i-Zarandí, in <i>The Dawn-breakers</i>, mentions nine complete commentaries on the Qur'an, revealed during the Báb's imprisonment at Máh-Kú, which have been lost without a trace.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup> Establishing the true text of the works that are still extant, as already noted, is not always easy, and some texts will require considerable work. Others, however, are in good shape; several of the Báb's major works are available in the handwriting of his trusted secretaries.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Most works were revealed in response to specific questions by Bábís. This is not unusual; the genre of the letter has been a venerable medium for composing authoritative texts as far back as the <a href="/wiki/Apostle_Paul" title="Apostle Paul" class="mw-redirect">Apostle Paul</a>. Three quarters of the chapters of the <a href="/wiki/New_Testament" title="New Testament">New Testament</a> are letters, were composed to imitate letters, or contain letters within them.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup> Sometimes the Báb revealed works very rapidly by chanting them in the presence of a secretary and eyewitnesses.</p>
<p>The Archives Department at the <a href="/wiki/Bah%C3%A1%27%C3%AD_World_Centre" title="Bahá'í World Centre">Bahá'í World Centre</a> currently holds about 190 <a href="/wiki/Tablet_(religious)" title="Tablet (religious)">Tablets</a> of the Báb.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup> Excerpts from several principal works have been published in the only English-language compilation of the Báb's writings: <i><a href="/wiki/Selections_from_the_Writings_of_the_B%C3%A1b" title="Selections from the Writings of the Báb">Selections from the Writings of the Báb</a></i>. Denis MacEoin, in his <i>Sources for Early Bābī Doctrine and History</i>, gives a description of many works; much of the following summary is derived from that source. In addition to major works, the Báb revealed numerous letters to his wife and followers, many prayers for various purposes, numerous commentaries on verses or chapters of the Qur'an, and many <i>khutbihs</i> or sermons (most of which were never delivered). Many of these have been lost; others have survived in compilations.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Báb has been criticized for his inconsistent use of correct and incorrect <a href="/wiki/Arabic_grammar" title="Arabic grammar">Arabic grammar</a> in his religious works, though in his Arabic letters made very few mistakes.<sup id="cite_ref-grammar_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-grammar-51"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup> A reason for this inconsistency could be to distinguish those who could not see past the outer form of the words from those that could understand the deeper meaning of his message.<sup id="cite_ref-grammar_51-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-grammar-51"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52"><span>[</span>52<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Writings_before_his_declaration">Writings before his declaration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B%C3%A1b&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Writings before his declaration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="floatright"><a href="/wiki/File:Selectionfromthebab.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Selectionfromthebab.jpg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/9/98/Selectionfromthebab.jpg" width="120" height="186" data-file-width="120" data-file-height="186" /></a></div>
<p>Todd Lawson noted this in his doctoral dissertation about the <i>Tafsír-i-súrih-i-baqarih</i> or "Commentary on the Surih of the Cow", a work the Báb wrote on a chapter of the Qur'an.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53"><span>[</span>53<span>]</span></a></sup> This Qur'an commentary was started by the Báb in November or December 1843, some six months before declaring his mission. The first half was completed by February or March 1844; the second half was revealed after the Báb's declaration. It is the only work of the Báb's revealed before his declaration that has survived intact. It also sheds light on the Báb's attitude toward Shí`í beliefs.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54"><span>[</span>54<span>]</span></a></sup> His wife also refers to important episodes before his declaration.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55"><span>[</span>55<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Shiraz.2C_May_.E2.80.93_September_1844">Shiraz, May – September 1844</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B%C3%A1b&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Shiraz, May – September 1844">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>The first chapter of the <i><a href="/wiki/Selections_from_the_Writings_of_the_B%C3%A1b#Qayy.C3.BAmu.27l-Asm.C3.A1.27" title="Selections from the Writings of the Báb">Qayyúmu'l-Asmá'</a></i> ("Commentary on the Súrih of Joseph")<sup id="cite_ref-listofwritings_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-listofwritings-56"><span>[</span>56<span>]</span></a></sup> was written by the Báb on the evening of his declaration to <a href="/wiki/Mull%C3%A1_Husayn" title="Mullá Husayn">Mullá Husayn</a>, on the evening of May 22, 1844. The entire work, which is several hundred pages in length and is considered to be revelation by Bahá'ís, required forty days to write; it is one of the Báb's longer Arabic works. It was widely distributed in the first year of the Bábí movement, functioning as something of a Qur'an or Bible for the Bábís. In the book the Báb states his claim to be a <a href="/wiki/Manifestation_of_God" title="Manifestation of God">Manifestation of God</a>, though the claim is disguised with other statements that he is the servant of the Hidden Imám.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57"><span>[</span>57<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/T%C3%A1hirih" title="Táhirih">Táhirih</a> translated the work into Persian.</li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li><i>Sahífih-yi-makhzúnih</i>, revealed before his departure for Mecca in September 1844, and consists of a collection of fourteen prayers, mostly to be recited on specific Muslim Holy Days and festivals. Its content remained within the expectations of Islam.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58"><span>[</span>58<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pilgrimage.2C_September_1844_.E2.80.93_June_1845">Pilgrimage, September 1844 – June 1845</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B%C3%A1b&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Pilgrimage, September 1844 – June 1845">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During his <span class="frac nowrap">9<span class="visualhide">&#160;</span><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>2</sub></span>-month pilgrimage to <a href="/wiki/Mecca" title="Mecca">Mecca</a>, the Báb composed many works:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>Khasá'il-i-sab`ih</i>: A work composed by the Báb on his sea journey back to <a href="/wiki/Bushehr" title="Bushehr">Bushehr</a> after his pilgrimage, which listed some regulations to be followed by the Bábí community. A copy of the manuscript probably still exists in Iran.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59"><span>[</span>59<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li><i>Kitáb-i-Rúḥ</i> ("Book of the Spirit"): This book contains 700 or 900 verses and was written while the Báb was sailing back to Bushehr from pilgrimage. The original was nearly destroyed when the Báb was arrested. Several manuscript copies are extant.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60"><span>[</span>60<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li><i>Sahífih baynu'l-haramayn</i> ("Treatise Between the Two Sanctuaries"): This Arabic work was written while the Báb traveled from Mecca to Medina in early 1845 and is in response to questions posed to him by a prominent Shaykhí leader.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61"><span>[</span>61<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li><i>Kitáb-i-Fihrist</i> ("The Book of the Catalogue"): A list of the Báb's works, composed by the Báb himself after he returned from pilgrimage to Mecca, June 21, 1845. It is a bibliography of his earliest writings.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62"><span>[</span>62<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bushehr_and_Shiraz.2C_March_1845_.E2.80.93_September_1846">Bushehr and Shiraz, March 1845 – September 1846</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B%C3%A1b&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Bushehr and Shiraz, March 1845 – September 1846">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Báb was in Bushehr March through June 1845, then in Shiraz.</p>
<ul>
<li><i>Sahífih-yi-Ja`fariyyih</i>: The Báb wrote this treatise to an unknown correspondent in 1845. Over a hundred pages in length, it states many of his basic teachings, especially in relation to some <a href="/wiki/Shaykhi" title="Shaykhi" class="mw-redirect">Shaykhi</a> beliefs.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63"><span>[</span>63<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li><i>Tafsír-i-Súrih-i-Kawthar</i> ("Commentary on the Chapter on Abundance"): The Báb wrote this commentary for Siyyid Yahyá Dárábí Vahíd while he was in Shiraz; it is the most important work revealed during the Shiraz period. Though the súrih is only three verses in length, being the shortest in the Qur'an, the commentary on it is over two hundred pages in length. The work was widely distributed, and at least a dozen early manuscripts are extant.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64"><span>[</span>64<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Isfahan.2C_September_1846_.E2.80.93_March_1847">Isfahan, September 1846 – March 1847</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B%C3%A1b&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Isfahan, September 1846 – March 1847">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li><i>Nubuvvih khásish</i>: This work, of fifty pages' length, was revealed in two hours in response to a question by Manúchihr Khán. It discusses the <i>special prophethood</i> of Muhammad, an important subject discussed in debates between Muslims and Christians.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65"><span>[</span>65<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li><i>Tafsír-i-Súrih-i-va'l-`asr</i> (Commentary on the Chapter named "Age"): This is one of the two important works the Báb penned in Isfahan. It was written spontaneously and publicly in response to a request by Mír Sayyid Muḥammad, the chief cleric of the city; much of it was written in one evening, to the astonishment to those present.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66"><span>[</span>66<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="M.C3.A1h-K.C3.BA.2C_late_summer_1847_.E2.80.93_May_1848">Máh-Kú, late summer 1847 – May 1848</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B%C3%A1b&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Máh-Kú, late summer 1847 – May 1848">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Báb left <a href="/wiki/Isfahan" title="Isfahan">Isfahán</a> in March 1847, sojourned outside Tehran several months, then was sent to a fortress at <a href="/wiki/Maku,_Iran" title="Maku, Iran">Máh-Kú</a>, close to the Turkish border. It witnessed the composition of some of the Báb's most important works.</p>
<ul>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Persian_Bay%C3%A1n" title="Persian Bayán">Persian Bayán</a></i>: This is undoubtedly the most important work of the Báb and contains a mature summary of his teachings. It was composed in Máh-Kú in late 1847 or early 1848. The work consists of nine chapters titled váhids or "unities", which in turn are usually subdivided into nineteen bábs or "gates"; the one exception is the last unity, which has only ten bábs. The Báb explained that it would be the task of "He Whom God shall make manifest" to complete the work; Bahá'ís believe Bahá'u'lláh's <i><a href="/wiki/Kit%C3%A1b-i-Iq%C3%A1n" title="Kitáb-i-Iqán" class="mw-redirect">Kitáb-i-Iqán</a></i> to be the completion of the Bayán. Each unity begins with an Arabic summary of its contents, which makes it easier to read than many of the Báb's works. Extracts of this work are published in <i>Selections from the Writings of the Báb</i>; A. L. M. Nicholas translated the entire work into French in four 150-page volumes.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67"><span>[</span>67<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Arabic_Bay%C3%A1n" title="Arabic Bayán">Arabic Bayán</a></i>: This is the shorter and less important of the two Bayáns. It consists of eleven váhids or "unities", each with nineteen bábs or "gates". It offers a succinct summary of the Báb's teachings and laws. It was composed at Máh-Kú in late 1847 or early 1848.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68"><span>[</span>68<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Selections_from_the_Writings_of_the_B%C3%A1b#Dal.C3.A1.27il-i-Sab.27ih_.28The_Seven_Proofs.29" title="Selections from the Writings of the Báb">Dalá'il-i-Sab'ih</a></i> ("Seven Proofs"): There are two works by this name, the longer one in Persian, the shorter one in Arabic; both were composed in Máh-Kú in late 1847 or early 1848. Nicholas called the Persian Seven Proofs "the most important of the polemical works that issued from the pen of Sayyid `Alí Muhammad".<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69"><span>[</span>69<span>]</span></a></sup> The work was written to either a non-Bábí or to a follower whose faith had been shaken, but the recipient's identity is unknown.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The Arabic text summarizes the seven proofs found in the Persian text.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chihr.C3.ADq.2C_May_1848_.E2.80.93_July_1850"><u>Ch</u>ihríq, May 1848 – July 1850</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B%C3%A1b&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Chihríq, May 1848 – July 1850">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Báb spent two years in Chihríq, except for his brief visit to <a href="/wiki/Tabriz" title="Tabriz">Tabriz</a> for his trial. The works he produced there were more esoteric or mystical and less thematically organized.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70"><span>[</span>70<span>]</span></a></sup> Two major books were produced, in addition to many minor works:</p>
<ul>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Kitabu%27l-Asm%C3%A1%27" title="Kitabu'l-Asmá'">Kitabu'l-Asmá'</a></i> ("The Book of Names"): This is an extremely long book about the names of God. It was penned during the Báb's last days at Chihríq, before his execution. The various manuscript copies contain numerous variations in the text; the book will require considerable work to reconstruct its original text.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71"><span>[</span>71<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li><i>Kitáb-i-panj sha'n</i> ("Book of Five Grades"): Having been composed in March and April 1850, this is one of the Báb's last works. The book consists of eighty-five sections arranged in seventeen groups, each under the heading of a different name of God. Within each group are five "grades", that is, five different sorts of sections: verses, prayers, homilies, commentaries, and Persian language pieces. Each group was sent to a different person and was composed on a different day. Thus the work is a kind of miscellany of unrelated material. Some of the sections represent further exposition of basic themes in the Báb's teachings; others consists of lengthy iterations of the names of God, and variations on their roots.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72"><span>[</span>72<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commemorations_in_the_Bah.C3.A1.27.C3.AD_calendar">Commemorations in the Bahá'í calendar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B%C3%A1b&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Commemorations in the Bahá'í calendar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
