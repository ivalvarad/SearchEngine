<p><b>Dysentery</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Inflammation" title="Inflammation">inflammation</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Intestine" title="Intestine" class="mw-redirect">intestine</a> causing <a href="/wiki/Diarrhea" title="Diarrhea">diarrhea</a> with blood.<sup id="cite_ref-Dorlands_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dorlands-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Other symptoms may include <a href="/wiki/Fever" title="Fever">fever</a>, <a href="/wiki/Abdominal_pain" title="Abdominal pain">abdominal pain</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Rectal_tenesmus" title="Rectal tenesmus">rectal tenesmus</a> (a feeling of incomplete defecation).</p>
<p>It is caused by a number of types of <a href="/wiki/Infection" title="Infection">infection</a> such as bacteria, viruses, parasitic worms, or protozoa. It is a type of <a href="/wiki/Gastroenteritis" title="Gastroenteritis">gastroenteritis</a>. The mechanism is an <a href="/wiki/Inflammation" title="Inflammation">inflammatory</a> disorder of the <a href="/wiki/Intestine" title="Intestine" class="mw-redirect">intestine</a>, especially of the <a href="/wiki/Colon_(anatomy)" title="Colon (anatomy)" class="mw-redirect">colon</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Signs_and_symptoms">Signs and symptoms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dysentery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Signs and symptoms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In developed countries, the most common form of dysentery is bacillary dysentery which is typically a mild illness, causing symptoms normally consisting of mild stomach pains and frequent passage of stool or diarrhea. Symptoms normally present themselves after one to three days and are usually no longer present after a week. The frequency of urges to defecate, the large volume of liquid feces passed, and the presence of mucus, pus and blood depends on the <a href="/wiki/Pathogen" title="Pathogen">pathogen</a> that is causing the disease. Temporary <a href="/wiki/Lactose_intolerance" title="Lactose intolerance">lactose intolerance</a> can occur. In some caustic occasions severe abdominal pain, <a href="/wiki/Fever" title="Fever">fever</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shock_(circulatory)" title="Shock (circulatory)">shock</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Delirium" title="Delirium">delirium</a> can all be symptoms.<sup id="cite_ref-Dorlands_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dorlands-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-pmid359524_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid359524-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-pmid2664748_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid2664748-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In extreme cases, dysentery patients may pass over one litre of fluid per hour. More often, individuals will complain of <a href="/wiki/Nausea" title="Nausea">nausea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Abdominal_pain" title="Abdominal pain">abdominal pain</a>, and frequent watery and usually foul-smelling <a href="/wiki/Diarrhea" title="Diarrhea">diarrhea</a>, accompanied by <a href="/wiki/Mucus" title="Mucus">mucus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Blood" title="Blood">blood</a>, rectal pain, and <a href="/wiki/Fever" title="Fever">fever</a>. <a href="/wiki/Vomiting" title="Vomiting">Vomiting</a>, rapid weight-loss, and generalized <a href="/wiki/Muscle_ache" title="Muscle ache" class="mw-redirect">muscle aches</a> sometimes also accompany dysentery. On rare occasions, the amoebic parasite will invade the body through the bloodstream and <a href="/wiki/Systemic_disease" title="Systemic disease">spread beyond the intestines</a>. In such cases, it may more seriously infect other organs such as the <a href="/wiki/Human_brain" title="Human brain">brain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Human_lungs" title="Human lungs" class="mw-redirect">lungs</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Liver" title="Liver">liver</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Diagnosis_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Diagnosis-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mechanism">Mechanism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dysentery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Mechanism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Dysentery results from <a href="/wiki/Virus" title="Virus">viral</a> infections, <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacterial</a> infections, or <a href="/wiki/Parasitism" title="Parasitism">parasitic</a> infestations. These pathogens typically reach the <a href="/wiki/Large_intestine" title="Large intestine">large intestine</a> after entering orally, through ingestion of contaminated food or water, oral contact with contaminated objects or hands, and so on.</p>
<p>Each specific pathogen has its own mechanism or pathogenesis, but in general the result is damage to the intestinal lining, leading to the <a href="/wiki/Inflammation" title="Inflammation">inflammatory immune response</a>. This can cause elevated temperature, painful spasms of the intestinal muscles (<a href="/wiki/Cramping" title="Cramping" class="mw-redirect">cramping</a>), swelling due to water leaking from capillaries of the intestine (<a href="/wiki/Edema" title="Edema">edema</a>), and further tissue damage by the body's immune cells and the chemicals, called <a href="/wiki/Cytokine" title="Cytokine">cytokines</a>, they release to fight the infection. The result can be impaired nutrient absorption, excessive water and mineral loss through the stools due to breakdown of the control mechanisms in the intestinal tissue that normally remove water from the stools, and in severe cases the entry of pathogenic organisms into the bloodstream.</p>
<p>Some microorganisms – for example, bacteria of the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Shigella" title="Shigella">Shigella</a></i> – secrete substances known as <a href="/wiki/Cytotoxin" title="Cytotoxin" class="mw-redirect">cytotoxins</a>, which kill and damage intestinal tissue on contact. Viruses directly attack the intestinal cells, taking over their metabolic machinery to make copies of themselves, which leads to cell death.</p>
<p>Definitions of <i>dysentery</i> can vary by region and by medical specialty. The U. S. Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) limits its definition to "diarrhea with visible blood."<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> Others define the term more broadly.<sup id="cite_ref-TFD_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TFD-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> These differences in definition must be taken into account when defining mechanisms. For example, using the CDC definition requires that intestinal tissue be so severely damaged that blood vessels have ruptured, allowing visible quantities of blood to be lost with defecation. Other definitions require less specific damage.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Amoebic_dysentery">Amoebic dysentery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dysentery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Amoebic dysentery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Amoebic_dysentery" title="Amoebic dysentery">Amoebic dysentery</a></div>
<p>Dysentery caused by <a href="/wiki/Amoebiasis" title="Amoebiasis">amoebiasis</a>, an infection by the <a href="/wiki/Amoeba" title="Amoeba">amoeba</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Entamoeba_histolytica" title="Entamoeba histolytica">Entamoeba histolytica</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> which is found mainly in tropical areas, is known as amoebic dysentery.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> Proper treatment of the underlying infection of amoebic dysentery is important; insufficiently treated amoebiasis can lie dormant for years and subsequently lead to <a href="/wiki/Amoebiasis#Complications" title="Amoebiasis">severe, potentially fatal, complications</a>.</p>
<p>When amoebas inside the bowel of an infected person are ready to leave the body, they group together and form a shell that surrounds and protects them. This group of amoebas is known as a cyst, which is then passed out of the person's body in the feces and can survive outside the body. If hygiene standards are poor; for example, if the person does not dispose of the feces hygienically, it can contaminate the surroundings, such as nearby food and water. If another person then eats or drinks food or water that has been contaminated with feces containing the cyst, that person will also become infected with the amoeba. Amoebic dysentery is particularly common in parts of the world where human feces are used as fertilizer. After entering the person's body through the mouth, the cyst will travel down into the stomach. The amoebas inside the cyst are protected from the stomach's digestive acid. From the stomach, the cyst will travel to the intestines where it will break open and release the amoebas, causing the infection. The amoebas can burrow into the walls of the intestines and cause small abscesses and ulcers to form. The cycle then begins again.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bacillary_dysentery">Bacillary dysentery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dysentery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Bacillary dysentery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Dysentery may also be caused by <a href="/wiki/Shigellosis" title="Shigellosis">shigellosis</a>, an infection by <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a> of the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Shigella" title="Shigella">Shigella</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> and is then known as bacillary dysentery (or Marlow Syndrome).</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Enteroinvasive_Escherichia_coli" title="Enteroinvasive Escherichia coli">Enteroinvasive Escherichia coli</a> may also cause a dysentery syndrome.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Diagnosis">Diagnosis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dysentery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Diagnosis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A clinical diagnosis may be made by taking a history and doing a brief examination. Treatment is usually started without or before confirmation by laboratory analysis.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_exam">Physical exam</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dysentery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Physical exam">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The mouth, skin, and lips may appear dry due to dehydration. Lower abdominal tenderness may also be present.<sup id="cite_ref-Diagnosis_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Diagnosis-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stool_and_blood_tests">Stool and blood tests</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dysentery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Stool and blood tests">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Cell_culture" title="Cell culture">Cultures</a> of <a href="/wiki/Stool_test" title="Stool test">stool samples</a> are examined in order to identify the <a href="/wiki/Organism" title="Organism">organism</a> causing dysentery. Usually, several samples must be obtained due to the changing number of amoeba, which changes daily.<sup id="cite_ref-Diagnosis_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Diagnosis-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Blood_test" title="Blood test">Blood tests</a> can be used to measure abnormalities in the levels of essential <a href="/wiki/Electrolyte" title="Electrolyte">minerals and salts</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Diagnosis_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Diagnosis-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Treatment">Treatment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dysentery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Treatment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Dysentery is managed by maintaining fluids by using <a href="/wiki/Oral_rehydration_therapy" title="Oral rehydration therapy">oral rehydration therapy</a>. If this treatment cannot be adequately maintained due to vomiting or the profuseness of diarrhea, hospital admission may be required for <a href="/wiki/Intravenous" title="Intravenous" class="mw-redirect">intravenous</a> fluid replacement. In ideal situations, no antimicrobial therapy should be administered until microbiological microscopy and culture studies have established the specific infection involved. When laboratory services are not available, it may be necessary to administer a combination of drugs, including an <a href="/wiki/Amebicide" title="Amebicide">amoebicidal</a> drug to kill the <a href="/wiki/Parasite" title="Parasite" class="mw-redirect">parasite</a>, and an <a href="/wiki/Antibiotic" title="Antibiotic" class="mw-redirect">antibiotic</a> to treat any associated bacterial infection.</p>
<p>If shigella is suspected and it is not too severe, letting it run its course may be reasonable — usually less than a week. If the shigella is severe, antibiotics, such as <a href="/wiki/Ciprofloxacin" title="Ciprofloxacin">ciprofloxacin</a> or <a href="/wiki/Trimethoprim/sulfamethoxazole" title="Trimethoprim/sulfamethoxazole">TMP-SMX</a> may be useful. However, many strains of shigella are becoming resistant to common antibiotics, and effective medications are often in short supply in developing countries. If necessary, a doctor may have to reserve antibiotics for those at highest risk for death, including young children, people over 50, and anyone suffering from dehydration or malnutrition.</p>
<p>Amoebic dysentery is often treated with two antimicrobial drug such as <a href="/wiki/Metronidazole" title="Metronidazole">metronidazole</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paromomycin" title="Paromomycin">paromomycin</a> or <a href="/wiki/Iodoquinol" title="Iodoquinol" class="mw-redirect">iodoquinol</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Prognosis">Prognosis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dysentery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Prognosis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>With correct treatment, most cases of amoebic and bacterial dysentery subside within ten days, and most individuals will achieve a full recovery within two to four weeks after beginning proper treatment. If the disease is left untreated, the prognosis varies with the immune status of the individual patient and the severity of disease. Extreme dehydration can prolong recovery and significantly raises the risk for serious complications.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Epidemiology">Epidemiology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dysentery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Epidemiology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The WHO estimates that there are 80 million cases and 700,000 deaths from <i>Shigellosis</i> annually.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>Amebiasis</i> infects over 50 million people each year, of whom 50,000 die.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Society_and_culture">Society and culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dysentery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Society and culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The seed, leaves, and bark of the <a href="/wiki/Kapok" title="Kapok" class="mw-redirect">kapok</a> tree have been used in traditional medicine by indigenous peoples of the rain-forest regions in <a href="/wiki/The_Americas" title="The Americas" class="mw-redirect">the Americas</a>, West-Central Africa, and South East Asia to treat this disease.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Bacillus_subtilis" title="Bacillus subtilis">Bacillus subtilis</a></i> was marketed throughout America and Europe from 1946 as an immunostimulatory aid in the treatment of gut and urinary tract diseases such as Rotavirus and Shigella,<sup id="cite_ref-mazza_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mazza-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> but declined in popularity after the introduction of cheap consumer antibiotics, despite causing less chance of allergic reaction and significantly lower toxicity to normal gut flora.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_cases">Notable cases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dysentery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Notable cases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li><b>1216</b> – King <a href="/wiki/John_of_England" title="John of England" class="mw-redirect">John of England</a> died of dysentery at <a href="/wiki/Newark_Castle,_Nottinghamshire" title="Newark Castle, Nottinghamshire">Newark Castle</a> on 18 October 1216.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li><b>1422</b> – King <a href="/wiki/Henry_V_of_England" title="Henry V of England">Henry V of England</a> died suddenly from dysentery in 1422. He was 35 years old.</li>
<li><b>1596</b> – Sir <a href="/wiki/Francis_Drake" title="Francis Drake">Francis Drake</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vice_Admiral" title="Vice Admiral" class="mw-redirect">Vice Admiral</a>, died of dysentery on 27 January 1596 while attacking <a href="/wiki/San_Juan,_Puerto_Rico" title="San Juan, Puerto Rico">San Juan, Puerto Rico</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> He was buried at sea in a lead coffin, near Portobelo.</li>
<li><b>1605</b> – <a href="/wiki/Akbar" title="Akbar">Akbar</a> the Great, ruler of the Mughal Empire of South Asia, died of dysentery. On 3 October 1605, he fell ill with an attack of dysentery, from which he never recovered. He is believed to have died on or about 27 October 1605, after which his body was buried at <a href="/wiki/Tomb_of_Akbar_the_Great" title="Tomb of Akbar the Great">a mausoleum</a> in <a href="/wiki/Agra" title="Agra">Agra</a>, present-day <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li><b>1675</b> – <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Marquette" title="Jacques Marquette">Jacques Marquette</a> died of dysentery on his way north from what is today Chicago, traveling to the mission where he intended to spend the rest of his life.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li><b>1676</b> – <a href="/wiki/Nathaniel_Bacon" title="Nathaniel Bacon" class="mw-disambig">Nathaniel Bacon</a> died of dysentery after taking control of Virginia following <a href="/wiki/Bacon%27s_Rebellion" title="Bacon's Rebellion">Bacon's Rebellion</a>. He is believed to have died in October, 1676, allowing Virginia's ruling elite to regain control. <sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li><b>19th century</b> – As late as the nineteenth century, the 'bloody flux,' it is estimated, killed more soldiers and sailors than did combat.<sup id="cite_ref-TFD_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TFD-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> Typhus and dysentery decimated Napoleon's <i><a href="/wiki/Grande_Arm%C3%A9e" title="Grande Armée">Grande Armée</a></i> in Russia. More than 80,000 Union soldiers died of dysentery during the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li><b>1896</b> – <a href="/wiki/Phan_%C4%90%C3%ACnh_Ph%C3%B9ng" title="Phan Đình Phùng">Phan Đình Phùng</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Vietnamese_people" title="Vietnamese people">Vietnamese</a> revolutionary who led rebel armies against <a href="/wiki/French_colonial_empire" title="French colonial empire">French colonial forces</a> in Vietnam, died of dysentery as the French surrounded his forces on January 21, 1896.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li><b>1930</b> – The French explorer and writer, <a href="/wiki/Michel_Vieuchange" title="Michel Vieuchange">Michel Vieuchange</a>, died of dysentery in <a href="/wiki/Agadir" title="Agadir">Agadir</a> on 30 November 1930, on his return from the "forbidden city" of <a href="/wiki/Smara" title="Smara">Smara</a>. He was nursed by his brother, Doctor <a href="/wiki/Jean_Vieuchange" title="Jean Vieuchange">Jean Vieuchange</a>, who was unable to save him. The notebooks and photographs, edited by Jean Vieuchange, went on to become bestsellers.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li><b>1942</b> – The <a href="/wiki/Selarang_Barracks_Incident" title="Selarang Barracks Incident" class="mw-redirect">Selarang Barracks Incident</a> in the summer of 1942 during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> involved the forced crowding of 17,000 <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">Anglo</a>-<a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australian</a> <a href="/wiki/Prisoners-of-war" title="Prisoners-of-war" class="mw-redirect">prisoners-of-war</a> (POWs) by their <a href="/wiki/Empire_of_Japan" title="Empire of Japan">Japanese captors</a> in the areas around the barracks square for nearly five days with little water and no sanitation after the Selarang Barracks POWs refused to sign a pledge not to escape. The incident ended with the capitulation of the Australian commanders due to the spreading of dysentery among their men.<sup id="cite_ref-Thompson_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thompson-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Research">Research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dysentery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Vaccines currently in development may eventually become a critical part of the strategy to reduce the incidence and severity of diarrhea, particularly among children in low-resource settings. For example, <i>Shigella</i> is a longstanding World Health Organization (WHO) target for vaccine development, and sharp declines in age-specific diarrhea/dysentery attack rates for this pathogen indicate that natural immunity does develop following exposure; thus, vaccination to prevent this disease should be feasible. Currently, no licensed vaccine targeting <i>Shigella</i> exists. The development of vaccines against these types of infection has been hampered by technical constraints, insufficient support for coordination, and a lack of market forces for research and development. Most vaccine development efforts are taking place in the public sector or as research programs within biotechnology companies. Several vaccine candidates are currently in various phases of research and development, including a number of ongoing clinical trials.<sup id="cite_ref-ETEC.2FShig_VRL_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ETEC.2FShig_VRL-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
