<p><i><b>Banksia verticillata</b></i>, commonly known as <b>granite banksia</b> or <b>Albany banksia</b>, is a species of <a href="/wiki/Shrub" title="Shrub">shrub</a> or (rarely) tree of the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Banksia" title="Banksia">Banksia</a></i> in the family <a href="/wiki/Proteaceae" title="Proteaceae">Proteaceae</a>. It is native to the <a href="/wiki/Southwest_botanical_province" title="Southwest botanical province" class="mw-redirect">southwest</a> of <a href="/wiki/Western_Australia" title="Western Australia">Western Australia</a> and can reach up to 3&#160;m (10&#160;ft) in height. It can grow taller to 5&#160;m (16&#160;ft) in sheltered areas, and much smaller in more exposed areas. This species has elliptic green leaves and large, bright golden yellow <a href="/wiki/Inflorescence" title="Inflorescence">inflorescences</a> or flower spikes, appearing in summer and autumn. The <a href="/wiki/New_Holland_honeyeater" title="New Holland honeyeater">New Holland honeyeater</a> (<i>Phylidonyris novaehollandiae</i>) is the most prominent <a href="/wiki/Pollination" title="Pollination">pollinator</a>, although several other species of <a href="/wiki/Honeyeater" title="Honeyeater">honeyeater</a>, as well as bees, visit the flower spikes.</p>
<p>A declared <i>Vulnerable</i> species, it occurs in two <a href="/wiki/Disjunct_distribution" title="Disjunct distribution">disjunct</a> populations on <a href="/wiki/Granite" title="Granite">granite</a> outcrops along the south coast of <a href="/wiki/Western_Australia" title="Western Australia">Western Australia</a>, with the main population near <a href="/wiki/Albany,_Western_Australia" title="Albany, Western Australia">Albany</a> and a smaller population near <a href="/wiki/Walpole,_Western_Australia" title="Walpole, Western Australia">Walpole</a>, and is threatened by dieback (<i><a href="/wiki/Phytophthora_cinnamomi" title="Phytophthora cinnamomi">Phytophthora cinnamomi</a></i>) and aerial canker (<i><a href="/wiki/Zythiostroma" title="Zythiostroma">Zythiostroma</a></i>). <i>B. verticillata</i> is killed by bushfire and new plants regenerate from seed afterwards. Populations take over a decade to produce seed and fire intervals of greater than twenty years are needed to allow the <a href="/wiki/Canopy_seed_bank" title="Canopy seed bank">canopy seed bank</a> to accumulate.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Banksia_verticillata&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Banksia verticillata</i> grows as a spreading, bushy shrub with many branches up to 3&#160;m (10&#160;ft) high, but can reach 5&#160;m (16&#160;ft) high in sheltered locations.<sup id="cite_ref-Kelcoates_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kelcoates-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> It may be much lower or even adopt a <a href="/wiki/Prostrate_shrub" title="Prostrate shrub">prostrate</a> habit in highly exposed areas which are blasted by high wind,<sup id="cite_ref-Kelcoates_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kelcoates-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> or occasionally grow as a single-trunked tree. The rough grey bark has fissures, the stems and branches are finely hairy when young and become smooth with age. The leathery bright green leaves are <a href="/wiki/Leaf_arrangement#Pattern_structure" title="Leaf arrangement" class="mw-redirect">arranged</a> whorled, or alternately on branches, and are borne on 0.5–1.1&#160;mm long <a href="/wiki/Petiole_(botany)" title="Petiole (botany)">petioles</a>. They measure 3–9&#160;cm (1.4–3.8&#160;in) in length, and 0.7–1.2&#160;cm (0.3–0.5&#160;in) in width, and are <a href="/wiki/Leaf_shape" title="Leaf shape">elliptic</a> in shape with entire (straight) <a href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/recurved" class="extiw" title="wikt:recurved">recurved</a> margins. They are initially hairy and become smooth with maturity, although their undersides remain covered with white hair.<sup id="cite_ref-Kelcoates_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kelcoates-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> The golden-yellow <a href="/wiki/Inflorescence" title="Inflorescence">inflorescences</a> appear in summer and autumn (January to April) and are 8–20&#160;cm (3–8&#160;in) high and 6.5&#160;cm (2.6&#160;in) wide. The smooth pistils are 3–3.5&#160;cm long and hooked at the end.<sup id="cite_ref-George_1999_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-George_1999-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Individual flowers open from the base of the flower spike, the wave of <a href="/wiki/Anthesis" title="Anthesis">anthesis</a> moving up the inflorescence. Occasionally, flowers on exposed parts may open early. It takes around 9.5 days for all flowers to open, and rates are similar during the day and night.<sup id="cite_ref-Rees_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rees-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> The inflorescences age to grey and the individual old flowers linger for some time before falling. Up to 100 small woody <a href="/wiki/Follicle_(fruit)" title="Follicle (fruit)">follicles</a> may follow on old flower spikes. Measuring 1.1–1.5&#160;cm wide, 3–4&#160;mm wide, and jutting out 2–3&#160;mm from the spike, they open after several years, releasing the seed.<sup id="cite_ref-George_1999_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-George_1999-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Follicles more commonly appear in the middle third of the spike. The reasons for this are unknown, although timing of visits by pollinators or some anatomical factor may be relevant.<sup id="cite_ref-Kelcoates_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kelcoates-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Banksia_verticillata&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery_and_naming">Discovery and naming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Banksia_verticillata&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Discovery and naming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a href="/wiki/File:King_George%27s_Sound,_looking_north_(Westall_collection).jpeg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/King_George%27s_Sound%2C_looking_north_%28Westall_collection%29.jpeg/400px-King_George%27s_Sound%2C_looking_north_%28Westall_collection%29.jpeg" width="400" height="272" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/66/King_George%27s_Sound%2C_looking_north_%28Westall_collection%29.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/66/King_George%27s_Sound%2C_looking_north_%28Westall_collection%29.jpeg 2x" data-file-width="544" data-file-height="370" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:King_George%27s_Sound,_looking_north_(Westall_collection).jpeg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>King George's Sound, view on the peninsula to the north of Peak Head</i>, a field sketch executed by <a href="/wiki/William_Westall" title="William Westall">William Westall</a> in December 1801. The sprawling shrub in the foreground has been tentatively identified as <i>B.&#160;verticillata</i>, which would make this sketch, together with another Westall sketch thought to depict <i>B.&#160;verticillata</i>, the earliest known drawings of the species.<sup id="cite_ref-Stehn_2005_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stehn_2005-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The earliest known botanical collection of <i>B.&#160;verticillata</i> was made by Scottish surgeon and naturalist <a href="/wiki/Archibald_Menzies" title="Archibald Menzies">Archibald Menzies</a> during the visit of the <a href="/wiki/Vancouver_Expedition" title="Vancouver Expedition">Vancouver Expedition</a> to <a href="/wiki/King_George_Sound" title="King George Sound">King George Sound</a> in September and October 1791. As a result of this collection the species was introduced into cultivation in England,<sup id="cite_ref-Hooker_1825_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hooker_1825-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> yet it did not result in formal publication of the species.</p>
<p>The next known collection was in December 1801, during the visit of <a href="/wiki/HMS_Investigator_(1798)" title="HMS Investigator (1798)" class="mw-redirect">HMS <i>Investigator</i></a> to King George Sound. Little is known of the circumstances of this collection, other than what is written on the specimen label: "King Georges Sound Dec[embe]r 1801".<sup id="cite_ref-FloraBase_Brown_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FloraBase_Brown-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> The specimen is credited to <a href="/wiki/Robert_Brown_(botanist)" title="Robert Brown (botanist)">Robert Brown</a>, but gardener <a href="/wiki/Peter_Good" title="Peter Good">Peter Good</a> and the botanical artist <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_Bauer" title="Ferdinand Bauer">Ferdinand Bauer</a> also contributed to Brown's specimen collection, often without attribution.<sup id="cite_ref-Hopper_2003_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hopper_2003-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> A more precise date and location cannot be given, as neither Brown nor Good mentions the collection in his diary.<sup id="cite_ref-Brown_2001_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown_2001-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Good_1981_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Good_1981-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> Bauer did not publish an illustration of the species<sup id="cite_ref-Norst_1989_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Norst_1989-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> and his original field sketches are lost,<sup id="cite_ref-Pignatti-Wikus_2000_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pignatti-Wikus_2000-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> but <a href="/wiki/William_Westall" title="William Westall">William Westall</a> appears to have incorporated it into two of his field sketches, and certainly included it in the foreground of one of the <a href="/wiki/Oil_painting" title="Oil painting">oil paintings</a> that he later worked up for the <a href="/wiki/Admiralty" title="Admiralty">Admiralty</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Stehn_2005_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stehn_2005-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Brown formally described and named the species in his 1810 <i><a href="/wiki/On_the_Proteaceae_of_Jussieu" title="On the Proteaceae of Jussieu" class="mw-redirect">On the Proteaceae of Jussieu</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Brown_1810_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown_1810-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> He did not identify a <a href="/wiki/Type_specimen" title="Type specimen" class="mw-redirect">type specimen</a>, but the one specimen in his collection has since been formally declared the <a href="/wiki/Lectotype" title="Lectotype" class="mw-redirect">lectotype</a> for the species.<sup id="cite_ref-George_1981_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-George_1981-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> He also did not explicitly give an <a href="/wiki/Etymology" title="Etymology">etymology</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Specific_name_(botany)" title="Specific name (botany)" class="mw-redirect">specific epithet</a>, but it is accepted that the name derives from the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>verticillatus</i> ("whorled"), in reference to the whorled leaf arrangement.<sup id="cite_ref-Collins_2008_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Collins_2008-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>No <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> or <a href="/wiki/Variety_(botany)" title="Variety (botany)">varieties</a> of <i>Banksia verticillata</i> have been identified; it has no <a href="/wiki/Taxonomic_synonym#Botany" title="Taxonomic synonym" class="mw-redirect">taxonomic synonyms</a>; and its only <a href="/wiki/Taxonomic_synonym#Botany" title="Taxonomic synonym" class="mw-redirect">nomenclatural synonym</a> is <i>Sirmuellera verticillata</i> (R.Br.) Kuntze, which arose from <a href="/wiki/Otto_Kuntze" title="Otto Kuntze">Otto Kuntze</a>'s unsuccessful 1891 attempt to replace <i>Banksia</i> with the new name <i>Sirmuellera</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Kuntze_.281891.29_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kuntze_.281891.29-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-APNI_55720_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-APNI_55720-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-APNI_23553_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-APNI_23553-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Infrageneric_placement">Infrageneric placement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Banksia_verticillata&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Infrageneric placement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Brown%27s_taxonomic_arrangement_of_Banksia" title="Brown's taxonomic arrangement of Banksia">Brown's arrangement of <i>Banksia</i></a>, <i>B.&#160;verticillata</i> was placed between <i>B.&#160;compar</i> (now <a href="/wiki/Banksia_integrifolia_subsp._compar" title="Banksia integrifolia subsp. compar"><i>B.&#160;integrifolia</i> subsp. <i>compar</i></a>) and <i><a href="/wiki/Banksia_coccinea" title="Banksia coccinea">B.&#160;coccinea</a></i> (Scarlet Banksia) in <a href="/wiki/Phyletic_order" title="Phyletic order" class="mw-redirect">phyletic order</a>. No infrageneric arrangement was provided other than the removal of one distinctive species into a subgenus of its own, because of its unusual domed flower head. As <i>B.&#160;verticillata</i> flowers occur in characteristic flower spikes, it was retained in <i><a href="/wiki/Banksia_verae" title="Banksia verae" class="mw-redirect">Banksia verae</a></i>, the "true banksias".<sup id="cite_ref-Brown_1810_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown_1810-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>Banksia verae</i> was renamed <i><a href="/wiki/Eubanksia" title="Eubanksia" class="mw-redirect">Eubanksia</a></i> by Austrian botanist <a href="/wiki/Stephan_Ladislaus_Endlicher" title="Stephan Ladislaus Endlicher" class="mw-redirect">Stephan Endlicher</a> in 1847, with <i>B.&#160;verticillata</i> remaining between the same two species as in Brown's sequence.<sup id="cite_ref-Endlicher_1847_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Endlicher_1847-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Meissner%27s_taxonomic_arrangement_of_Banksia" title="Meissner's taxonomic arrangement of Banksia">A more detailed arrangement</a> was published by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Meissner" title="Carl Meissner">Carl Meissner</a> in 1856. <i>Eubanksia</i> was demoted to sectional rank, and divided it into four series. <i>B.&#160;verticillata</i> was placed in series <a href="/wiki/Banksia_ser._Salicinae" title="Banksia ser. Salicinae"><i>Salicinae</i></a> because its leaves are more or less linear, and have white undersides.<sup id="cite_ref-Meissner_1856_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Meissner_1856-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> Based as they were on leaf characters, Meissner's series were highly heterogeneous,<sup id="cite_ref-George_1981_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-George_1981-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/George_Bentham" title="George Bentham">George Bentham</a> discarded them all in <a href="/wiki/Bentham%27s_taxonomic_arrangement_of_Banksia" title="Bentham's taxonomic arrangement of Banksia">his 1870 revision of <i>Banksia</i></a>. <i>B.&#160;verticillata</i> was instead placed in a new section, <i><a href="/wiki/Banksia_sect._Oncostylis" title="Banksia sect. Oncostylis">Oncostylis</a></i>, because of its hooked styles.<sup id="cite_ref-Bentham_1870_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bentham_1870-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> This arrangement would stand for over a century.</p>
<p>For many years there was confusion between <i>B.&#160;verticillata</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Banksia_littoralis" title="Banksia littoralis">B.&#160;littoralis</a></i> (Swamp Banksia). Until 1984, the latter was circumscribed as encompassing what is now <i><a href="/wiki/Banksia_seminuda" title="Banksia seminuda">Banksia seminuda</a></i> (River Banksia), which has whorled leaves like <i>B.&#160;verticillata</i>. Thus it was easy to perceive <i>B.&#160;verticillata</i> as falling within the range of variation of this broadly defined species. The confusion was largely cleared up once <i>B.&#160;seminuda</i> was recognised as a distinct <a href="/wiki/Taxon" title="Taxon">taxon</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Holliday_1975_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Holliday_1975-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Alex_George" title="Alex George">Alex George</a> published a new <a href="/wiki/Taxonomy_(biology)" title="Taxonomy (biology)">taxonomic</a> arrangement of <i>Banksia</i> in his landmark 1981 monograph <i><a href="/wiki/The_genus_Banksia_L.f._(Proteaceae)" title="The genus Banksia L.f. (Proteaceae)">The genus Banksia L.f. (Proteaceae)</a></i>. Endlicher's <i>Eubanksia</i> became <a href="/wiki/Banksia_subg._Banksia" title="Banksia subg. Banksia"><i>B.</i>&#160;subg. <i>Banksia</i></a>, and was divided into three sections, one of which was <i>Oncostylis</i>. <i>Oncostylis</i> was further divided into four series, with <i>B.&#160;verticillata</i> placed in series <a href="/wiki/Banksia_ser._Spicigerae" title="Banksia ser. Spicigerae"><i>Spicigerae</i></a> because its inflorescences are cylindrical.<sup id="cite_ref-George_1999_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-George_1999-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In 1996, <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Thiele" title="Kevin Thiele">Kevin Thiele</a> and Pauline Ladiges published a new arrangement for the genus, after <a href="/wiki/Cladistics" title="Cladistics">cladistic</a> analyses yielded a <a href="/wiki/Cladogram" title="Cladogram">cladogram</a> significantly different to George's arrangement. With respect to <i>B.&#160;verticillata</i>, their findings largely accorded with George's arrangement: section <i>Oncostylis</i> was discarded as <a href="/wiki/Polyphyletic" title="Polyphyletic" class="mw-redirect">polyphyletic</a>, but series <i>Spicigerae</i> was inferred to be monophyletic, and <i>B.&#160;verticillata</i> appeared in a succession of <a href="/wiki/Clade" title="Clade">clades</a> with the species previously identified as its closest relatives: first <i>B.&#160;littoralis</i>, then <i>B.&#160;seminuda</i>, then <i>B.&#160;brownii</i>, and finally <i><a href="/wiki/Banksia_occidentalis" title="Banksia occidentalis">B.&#160;occidentalis</a></i> (Red Swamp Banksia):<sup id="cite_ref-Thiele_1996_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thiele_1996-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Banksia_verticillata&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Banksia verticillata</i> is found in scattered populations in two disjunct segments: one clustered around <a href="/wiki/Walpole,_Western_Australia" title="Walpole, Western Australia">Walpole</a>, and the other around <a href="/wiki/Albany,_Western_Australia" title="Albany, Western Australia">Albany</a> and eastwards to Cheynes Beach. All but one are located within 2&#160;km (1.5&#160;mi) of the coast, the exception is less than 10&#160;km (6&#160;mi) inland. Plants grow on exposed coastal <a href="/wiki/Granite" title="Granite">granite</a> outcrops, often in cracks within boulders as well as shallow rocky soils. It is the only <i>Banksia</i> which grows exclusively in a <a href="/wiki/Granite" title="Granite">granite</a> soil.<sup id="cite_ref-Atlas_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Atlas-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> It grows in association with <i><a href="/wiki/Taxandria_marginata" title="Taxandria marginata">Taxandria marginata</a></i>, Western Australian peppermint (<i><a href="/wiki/Agonis_flexuosa" title="Agonis flexuosa">Agonis flexuosa</a></i>), <i><a href="/wiki/Andersonia_sprengelioides" title="Andersonia sprengelioides">Andersonia sprengelioides</a></i> and species of <i><a href="/wiki/Hakea" title="Hakea">Hakea</a></i> in <a href="/wiki/Shrubland" title="Shrubland">scrub</a> and <a href="/wiki/Heath_(habitat)" title="Heath (habitat)" class="mw-redirect">heath</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Kelcoates_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kelcoates-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Banksia_verticillata&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Ecology_of_Banksia" title="Ecology of Banksia">Ecology of Banksia</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/New_Holland_honeyeater" title="New Holland honeyeater">New Holland honeyeater</a> (<i>Phylidonyris novaehollandiae</i>) is a major visitor and pollinator of <i>Banksia verticillata</i>. These birds can travel 15&#160;m (50&#160;ft) between inflorescences in a feeding session, and preferentially choose flower spikes with partly opened flowers. Other honeyeater species observed, the <a href="/wiki/White-cheeked_honeyeater" title="White-cheeked honeyeater">white-cheeked honeyeater</a> (<i>Phylidonyris nigra</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Western_spinebill" title="Western spinebill">western spinebill</a> (<i>Acanthorhynchus superciliosus</i>), visit this species to a much lesser extent.<sup id="cite_ref-Kelcoates_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kelcoates-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Brown_honeyeater" title="Brown honeyeater">brown honeyeater</a> (<i>Lichmera indistincta</i>) has also been recorded as a visitor.<sup id="cite_ref-Barker_1984_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Barker_1984-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> Small mammals are not major pollinators, although <a href="/wiki/Bush_rat" title="Bush rat">bush rats</a> (<i>Rattus fuscipes</i>) and <a href="/wiki/House_mouse" title="House mouse">house mice</a> (<i>Mus musculus</i>) have been recorded. <a href="/wiki/Honey_bee" title="Honey bee">Honey bees</a> (<i>Apis mellifera</i>) visit flower spikes but are not effective pollinators.<sup id="cite_ref-Kelcoates_2-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kelcoates-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Rees_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rees-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><i>B. verticillata</i> is significantly threatened by at least three microorganisms. Several populations have reduced or vanished from dieback (<i><a href="/wiki/Phytophthora_cinnamomi" title="Phytophthora cinnamomi">Phytophthora cinnamomi</a></i>), such as those at <a href="/wiki/Two_Peoples_Bay_Nature_Reserve" title="Two Peoples Bay Nature Reserve">Two Peoples Bay Nature Reserve</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gull_Rock_National_Park" title="Gull Rock National Park">Gull Rock National Park</a>. The honey fungus <i><a href="/wiki/Armillaria_luteobubalina" title="Armillaria luteobubalina">Armillaria luteobubalina</a></i> has killed plants in <a href="/wiki/Torndirrup_National_Park" title="Torndirrup National Park">Torndirrup National Park</a>, and aerial canker (<i><a href="/wiki/Zythiostroma" title="Zythiostroma">Zythiostroma</a></i>) has decimated populations at <a href="/wiki/Waychinicup_National_Park" title="Waychinicup National Park">Waychinicup National Park</a> east of Albany.<sup id="cite_ref-Kelcoates_2-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kelcoates-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><i>B. verticillata</i> plants are generally killed by fire and regenerate from seed. A <a href="/wiki/Field_study" title="Field study" class="mw-redirect">field study</a> after a mild fire in <a href="/wiki/Torndirrup_National_Park" title="Torndirrup National Park">Torndirrup National Park</a> published in 1994 found that plants burnt by fire were ten times as likely to have seedlings come up under their crown as unburnt plants (with an average of 25.2 seedlings per burnt plant), and burnt spikes released double the number of seeds as unburnt spikes. Despite this, interfire recruitment (seedlings arising between fires) has also been recorded, and might be more common than in other <i>Banksia</i> species. Observations at several of the populations showed many plants produced their first seed anywhere from 13 to 17 years of age, leading to a recommendation of 20 years between fires to allow seed banks to accumulate.<sup id="cite_ref-Kelcoates_2-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kelcoates-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> If fire occurs too frequently, plants are burned before reaching maturity or before they have produced sufficient seed to ensure regeneration of the population. This may cause a population decline or even local extinction. Too long a time between fires also causes population decline, as more plants die of natural attrition without releasing their seed, resulting in seed wastage.<sup id="cite_ref-Lamont_1996_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lamont_1996-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation">Conservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Banksia_verticillata&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Conservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Banksia verticillata</i> has been declared <i>Vulnerable</i> under the federal <i><a href="/wiki/Environment_Protection_and_Biodiversity_Conservation_Act_1999" title="Environment Protection and Biodiversity Conservation Act 1999">Environment Protection and Biodiversity Conservation Act 1999</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-SPRAT_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SPRAT-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> and <i>Rare</i> under Western Australia's <i><a href="/wiki/Wildlife_Conservation_Act_1950" title="Wildlife Conservation Act 1950">Wildlife Conservation Act 1950</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-FloraBase_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FloraBase-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> A 1995 census yielded an estimated total population size of 4500 plants.<sup id="cite_ref-Kelcoates_2-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kelcoates-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Apart from dieback and aerial canker, frequency of <a href="/wiki/Bushfire" title="Bushfire" class="mw-redirect">bushfires</a> and illegal picking have been cited as threats. Ongoing management includes monitoring of current populations, gathering more data on best response to fire, and restricting access to populations.<sup id="cite_ref-advice_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-advice-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> Seed has been collected from many populations, but germination rates after ten years of storage are much lower than in <i><a href="/wiki/Banksia_brownii" title="Banksia brownii">Banksia brownii</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Kelcoates_2-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kelcoates-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Translocation_(wildlife_conservation)" title="Translocation (wildlife conservation)" class="mw-redirect">Translocation</a> is considered an option in the future,<sup id="cite_ref-advice_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-advice-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> as is spraying with <a href="/wiki/Phosphite" title="Phosphite">phosphite</a>, particularly in the vicinity of <a href="/wiki/Walpole,_Western_Australia" title="Walpole, Western Australia">Walpole</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Kelcoates_2-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kelcoates-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Used successfully on <i>B.&#160;brownii</i> but as yet untrialled with <i>B.&#160;verticillata</i>, phosphite boosts the resistance of both infected and uninfected plants, and also acts as a direct <a href="/wiki/Fungicide" title="Fungicide">fungicide</a>. Aerial spraying of phosphite boosts plant survival and slows the spread of infection,<sup id="cite_ref-Cochrane_2005_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cochrane_2005-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Barrett_2003_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Barrett_2003-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup> but must be carefully managed as studies have shown that <a href="/wiki/Foliar_spraying" title="Foliar spraying" class="mw-redirect">foliar spraying</a> of phosphite adversely affects root and shoot growth.<sup id="cite_ref-Barrett_2002_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Barrett_2002-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultivation">Cultivation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Banksia_verticillata&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Cultivation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Banksia verticillata</i> is seldom seen in <a href="/wiki/Plant_cultivation" title="Plant cultivation" class="mw-redirect">cultivation</a>. The natural growing conditions point to a sunny aspect and good drainage as being important in cultivation. It is good for coastal situations and erosion control.<sup id="cite_ref-enc_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-enc-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup> Very sensitive to dieback, <i>B. verticillata</i> (like most other western Australian banksias) perishes quickly in humid conditions or poor drainage. It has been <a href="/wiki/Grafting" title="Grafting">grafted</a> successfully onto <i><a href="/wiki/Banksia_integrifolia" title="Banksia integrifolia">Banksia integrifolia</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup> Seeds do not require any treatment, and take 19 to 49 days to <a href="/wiki/Germinate" title="Germinate" class="mw-redirect">germinate</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Sweedman_2006_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sweedman_2006-36"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
