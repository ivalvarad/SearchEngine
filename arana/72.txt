<p><b>Lake of the Woods</b> (<a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr"><i>lac des Bois</i></span>) is a <a href="/wiki/Lake" title="Lake">lake</a> occupying parts of the Canadian provinces of <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a> and <a href="/wiki/Manitoba" title="Manitoba">Manitoba</a> and the <a href="/wiki/U.S._state" title="U.S. state">U.S. state</a> of <a href="/wiki/Minnesota" title="Minnesota">Minnesota</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> It separates a small land area of Minnesota from the rest of the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Angle" title="Northwest Angle">Northwest Angle</a> and the town of <a href="/wiki/Angle_Township,_Lake_of_the_Woods_County,_Minnesota" title="Angle Township, Lake of the Woods County, Minnesota">Angle Township</a> can only be reached from the rest of Minnesota by crossing the lake or by traveling through <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>. The Northwest Angle is the northernmost part of the <a href="/wiki/Contiguous_United_States" title="Contiguous United States">contiguous United States</a>. Its "<a href="/wiki/Northwestern_point_of_the_Lake_of_the_Woods" title="Northwestern point of the Lake of the Woods">northwesternmost point</a>" served as a problematic landmark in treaties defining the international border.</p>
<p>Lake of the Woods is fed by the <a href="/wiki/Rainy_River_(Minnesota%E2%80%93Ontario)" title="Rainy River (Minnesota–Ontario)">Rainy River</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shoal_Lake_(Ontario)" title="Shoal Lake (Ontario)">Shoal Lake</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kakagi_Lake" title="Kakagi Lake">Kakagi Lake</a> and other smaller rivers. The lake drains into the <a href="/wiki/Winnipeg_River" title="Winnipeg River">Winnipeg River</a> and then into <a href="/wiki/Lake_Winnipeg" title="Lake Winnipeg">Lake Winnipeg</a>. Ultimately, its outflow goes north through the <a href="/wiki/Nelson_River" title="Nelson River">Nelson River</a> to <a href="/wiki/Hudson_Bay" title="Hudson Bay">Hudson Bay</a>.</p>
<p>Lake of the Woods is over 70 miles (110&#160;km) long and wide, and contains more than 14,552 islands and 65,000 miles (105,000&#160;km) of shoreline. It would amount to the longest coastline of any Canadian lake, except that the lake is not entirely within Canada. Lake of the Woods is also the <a href="/wiki/List_of_largest_lakes_of_the_United_States_by_area" title="List of largest lakes of the United States by area">sixth largest</a> <a href="/wiki/Fresh_water" title="Fresh water">freshwater</a> lake located (at least partially) in the United States, after the five <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes" title="Great Lakes">Great Lakes</a>.</p>
<p>The lake's islands provide nesting habitat for the <a href="/wiki/Piping_plover" title="Piping plover">piping plover</a> and large numbers of <a href="/wiki/American_white_pelican" title="American white pelican">American white pelicans</a>. There are also several hundred nesting pairs of <a href="/wiki/Bald_eagle" title="Bald eagle">bald eagles</a> in this area.</p>
<p>Lake of the Woods, a translation of the original French name <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr"><i>lac des Bois</i></span>, was so named from its wooded setting.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> However, it may have been a mistranslation of the Indian name. "The earliest name we find the lake known by is that given by Verandrye in his journey in 1731. He says it was called Lake Minitie (Cree Ministik) or Des Bois. (1) The former of these names, Minitie, seems to be Ojibway, and to mean Lake of the Islands, probably referring to the large number of islands found in the northern half of the lake. The other name (2) Lac des Bois, or Lake of the Woods, seems to have been a mis-translation of the Indian name (ojibway) by which the Lake was known." <sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Governance">Governance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake_of_the_Woods&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Governance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The construction of dams at the Lake of the Woods outlets in present-day Kenora in the late 19th century led to concerns over high and low water levels on the lake early in the 20th century. The federal governments of Canada and the United States referred the matter to the <a href="/wiki/International_Joint_Commission" title="International Joint Commission">International Joint Commission</a> (IJC) in 1912. In 1917 the IJC recommended the creation of control boards and the operating conditions they would apply to lake level management. The first of these boards, the Lake of the Woods Control Board (LWCB), was established by Canadian Order-in-Council in 1919. Two additional acts provided statutory establishment of the LWCB, defined its jurisdiction and powers, and provided for board members appointed by Canada and Ontario: the Lake of the Woods Control Board Act, Canada, 1921, the Lake of the Woods Control Board Act, Ontario, 1922. In 1922 the Canada-Ontario-Manitoba Tripartite Agreement was signed by the respective governments. Initially only Canada and Ontario appointed members to the board as, at that time, natural resources in Manitoba were administered by Canada. In 1958, having gained control over its natural resources, Manitoba passed its own Lake of the Woods Control Board Act. That same year, Canada and Ontario amended their original versions of the acts. As a result of these legislative changes, the LWCB now has one member appointed by Canada, two appointed by Ontario, and one appointed by Manitoba.</p>
<p>Following the IJC recommendations of 1917, discussions between the federal governments of Canada and the United States resulted in the 1925 Canada/USA Convention and Protocol regarding Lake of the Woods. This treaty established the water level operating range on Lake of the Woods, defined the purpose and general mode of operation, and provided for two boards to control regulation. The previously established Canadian LWCB was to regulate the lake on an ongoing basis, but its decisions were to be subject to approval by an International Lake of the Woods Control Board (ILWCB) whenever lake levels rose above or fell below certain limits. In cases where agreement could not be reached between Canadian and American members of the international board, the disputed matter would be referred to the IJC for final decision. The International Lake of the Woods Control Board, however, is not a board created by the IJC. The board's members (one American and one Canadian) are appointed by the respective federal governments.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Shoal_Lake_(Kenora_District,_Ontario)" title="Shoal Lake (Kenora District, Ontario)">Shoal Lake</a> is adjacent to the Lake of the Woods and is the source for the City of <a href="/wiki/Winnipeg,_Manitoba" title="Winnipeg, Manitoba" class="mw-redirect">Winnipeg</a> drinking water via the <a href="/wiki/Greater_Winnipeg_Water_District_aqueduct" title="Greater Winnipeg Water District aqueduct">Greater Winnipeg Water District aqueduct</a>, so the administration of the lake is a continuing interest of the governments of Winnipeg and Manitoba.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lands_within_Lake_of_the_Woods">Lands within Lake of the Woods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake_of_the_Woods&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Lands within Lake of the Woods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aulneau_Peninsula">Aulneau Peninsula</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake_of_the_Woods&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Aulneau Peninsula">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The largest land feature in Lake of the Woods is the Aulneau Peninsula. It is connected to the mainland with a tiny neck of land at its southeast corner, but a canal (Turtle Portage) was cut through at this point, effectively making the Aulneau an island. The canal has now been filled back in. A manually run portage for small- to medium-sized boats is in its place. The Aulneau is approximately twenty miles long (32&#160;km) and ten miles (16&#160;km) wide. It contains within it over eighty lakes, the largest of which is Arrow Lake, with thirteen islands in it.</p>
<p>The Aulneau Peninsula was named after the <a href="/wiki/Society_of_Jesus" title="Society of Jesus">Jesuit</a> Father <a href="/wiki/Jean-Pierre_Aulneau" title="Jean-Pierre Aulneau">Jean-Pierre Aulneau</a>, a <a href="/wiki/French_people" title="French people">French</a> <a href="/wiki/Priesthood_(Catholic_Church)" title="Priesthood (Catholic Church)">Catholic priest</a>, who was killed by natives on 8 June 1736 on Lake of the Woods. The Catholic Church in <a href="/wiki/Warroad,_Minnesota" title="Warroad, Minnesota">Warroad, Minnesota</a>, is named Father Aulneau Memorial Church in his honor.</p>

<li>Chapel Island An island about 15&#160;km out from Kenora, where a chapel once stood. When the natives would canoe from end to end for purposes of trading. Chapel Island was a rest stop where the white man would try and convert the Native population, and offered a salvation in case of bad weather. The chapel has since burnt down, and very few remains remain.</li>
<li>Copeland Island, otherwise known as <a href="/w/index.php?title=Camp_Stephens&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Camp Stephens (page does not exist)">Camp Stephens</a>. <li><a href="/wiki/Massacre_Island,_Ontario" title="Massacre Island, Ontario">Massacre Island, Ontario</a> a small island in the middle of the lake, the possible site where twenty French men as well as a group of Cree traders were beheaded in the mid 18th century by Sioux. The site is marked by a large wooden cross in the middle of the island. This incident sparked decades of war between the Sioux and the Ojibway, allies of both the French and the Cree.</li>

<li><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sultana_Mine&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Sultana Mine (page does not exist)">Sultana Mine</a> A mine located on an island in the northern area of the lake. Mine in the late 1800s, before an accident shut down the mine. Lore Stories about it being haunted by miners exist to this day.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recreation_on_Lake_of_the_Woods">Recreation on Lake of the Woods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake_of_the_Woods&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Recreation on Lake of the Woods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Tourism is a large part of the local economy of Lake of the Woods, and there are many recreational opportunities available on the lake and in the surrounding countryside. Much of the lake is fairly remote, but resorts and equipment outfitters offer options for those who do not have access to their own boats and equipment.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Camping">Camping</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake_of_the_Woods&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Camping">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Minnesota's <a href="/wiki/Zippel_Bay_State_Park" title="Zippel Bay State Park">Zippel Bay State Park</a> offers a wide variety of services including campsites, toilet facilities, a marina with access to the Zippel River, and a beach. Minnesota and Ontario both offer state sanctioned parks and campsites, which can be located through the respective governments. Backcountry campers can locate a prospective campsite on Lake of the Woods by boat, landing and examining the site in person. There is abundant wildlife even on the small islands on the lake.</p>
<p>Numerous marinas and resorts on the lake provide accommodation and dining, houseboat rentals, nautical charts, camping and fishing advice, and knowledge on how to most enjoy the lake.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fishing">Fishing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake_of_the_Woods&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Fishing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Lake of the Woods is home to <a href="/wiki/Walleye" title="Walleye">walleye</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northern_pike" title="Northern pike">northern pike</a>, <a href="/wiki/Perch" title="Perch">perch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sauger" title="Sauger">sauger</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crappie" title="Crappie">crappie</a>, panfish, <a href="/wiki/Smallmouth_bass" title="Smallmouth bass">smallmouth bass</a>, <a href="/wiki/Largemouth_bass" title="Largemouth bass">largemouth bass</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lake_trout" title="Lake trout">lake trout</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lake_sturgeon" title="Lake sturgeon">lake sturgeon</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Muskellunge" title="Muskellunge">muskellunge</a>.</p>
