<p><b>Project 629</b>, also known by the <a href="/wiki/NATO_reporting_name" title="NATO reporting name">NATO reporting name</a> of <b>Golf class</b>, were diesel electric <a href="/wiki/Ballistic_missile_submarine" title="Ballistic missile submarine">ballistic missile submarines</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Navy" title="Soviet Navy">Soviet Navy</a>. They were designed after six <a href="/wiki/Zulu_class_submarine" title="Zulu class submarine" class="mw-redirect">Zulu class submarines</a> and were successfully modified to carry and launch <a href="/wiki/Scud_missile" title="Scud missile" class="mw-redirect">Scud missiles</a>. All Golf boats had left Soviet service by 1990. All have been since disposed of.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> According to some sources at least one single Golf-class submarine is operated by <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, to test new SLBMs.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Class_history">Class history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Golf-class_submarine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Class history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Project 629 was started in the mid-1950s along with the D-2 missile system which it was to carry, and was based on the <a href="/wiki/Foxtrot_class_submarine" title="Foxtrot class submarine" class="mw-redirect">Foxtrot</a>. Design task was assigned to OKB-16, one of the two predecessors (the other being SKB-143) of the famous <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.malachite-spb.ru/">Malachite Central Design Bureau</a>, which would eventually become one of the three Soviet/Russian submarine design centers, along with <a href="/wiki/Rubin_Design_Bureau" title="Rubin Design Bureau">Rubin Design Bureau</a> and <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://cdb-lazurit.ru/indexe.php?0">Lazurit Central Design Bureau</a> ("Lazurit" is the Russian word for <a href="/wiki/Lazurite" title="Lazurite">lazurite</a>). The submarine was originally designed to carry three <a href="/wiki/Scud" title="Scud">R-11FM</a> ballistic missiles with a range of around 150&#160;km. These were carried in three silos fitted in the rear of the large sail behind the bridge. They could only be fired with the submarine surfaced and the missile raised above the sail but the submarine could be underway at the time. Only the first three boats were equipped with these—the remaining ones were equipped with the longer range <a href="/wiki/R-13_(missile)" title="R-13 (missile)">R-13</a> missiles.</p>
<p>The first boats were commissioned in 1958 and the last in 1962.</p>
<p>The boats were built at two shipyards — 16 in <a href="/wiki/Severodvinsk" title="Severodvinsk">Severodvinsk</a> and 7 in <a href="/wiki/Komsomolsk-na-Amure" title="Komsomolsk-na-Amure" class="mw-redirect">Komsomolsk-na-Amure</a> in the Far East. Fourteen were extensively modified in 1966–1972 and became known as 629A's by the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Navy" title="Soviet Navy">Soviet Navy</a> and Golf IIs by <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a> (the original version having been designated Golf I). The major change was the upgrade of the missile system to carry <a href="/wiki/R-21_(missile)" title="R-21 (missile)">R-21</a> missiles which could be launched from inside their tubes with the submarine submerged and increased speed. In later years a few were converted to test new missiles and others had different conversions.</p>
<p>All boats had left <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet</a> service by 1990. In 1993, ten were sold to <a href="/wiki/North_Korea" title="North Korea">North Korea</a> for scrap.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> However, they have since then focused on getting these boats back into service.<sup id="cite_ref-giz_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-giz-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In 1959 the project technology were also sold to <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="People's Republic of China" class="mw-redirect">China</a> which built a single modified example in 1966 which is apparently still in service.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Project_Azorian">Project Azorian</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Golf-class_submarine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Project Azorian">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Project_Azorian" title="Project Azorian">Project Azorian</a></div>
<p>On March 8, 1968, 1,560 nautical miles (2,890&#160;km) northwest of <a href="/wiki/Oahu" title="Oahu">Oahu</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a> the Golf II class submarine <a href="/wiki/Soviet_submarine_K-129_(1960)" title="Soviet submarine K-129 (1960)">K-129</a> exceeded its crush depth for unknown reasons and imploded, the accident being registered by the <a href="/wiki/SOSUS" title="SOSUS">SOSUS</a> network. The entire crew of 98 was lost and the vessel sank with three ballistic nuclear missiles as well as two nuclear torpedoes. The <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> recovered parts of the submarine in July 1974 from a depth of around 5&#160;km, in an operation named <a href="/wiki/Project_Azorian" title="Project Azorian">Project Azorian</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-StudiesIntell85_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-StudiesIntell85-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Two nuclear submarines that had been facing retirement, <a href="/wiki/USS_Halibut_(SSGN-587)" title="USS Halibut (SSGN-587)">USS&#160;<i>Halibut</i>&#160;(SSGN-587)</a> and <a href="/wiki/USS_Seawolf_(SSN-575)" title="USS Seawolf (SSN-575)">USS&#160;<i>Seawolf</i>&#160;(SSN-575)</a>, were rebuilt and pressed into service as deep sea search vehicles. After <i>Halibut</i> discovered a sunken Soviet submarine containing at least one intact ballistic missile complete with nuclear warhead, <a href="/wiki/Melvin_Laird" title="Melvin Laird">Melvin Laird</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_Defense" title="United States Secretary of Defense">United States Secretary of Defense</a> under President <a href="/wiki/Richard_Nixon" title="Richard Nixon">Richard Nixon</a>, approved Azorian. Six years later, 350 nautical miles (648&#160;km) north of the <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaiian</a> <a href="/wiki/Northwestern_Hawaiian_Islands" title="Northwestern Hawaiian Islands">Leeward Islands</a>, a mechanical claw descended 17,000 feet (5,200&#160;m) to the bottom of the Pacific and, guided by computers on board the <a href="/wiki/Glomar_Explorer" title="Glomar Explorer" class="mw-redirect">Glomar Explorer</a>, clamped onto the mass of twisted, rusting steel and began slowly raising it to the surface. It is unknown for sure how successful the effort was, but the United States has admitted to recovering a portion of K-129, which included six bodies of Soviet sailors that were buried at sea with full honors.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Variants">Variants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Golf-class_submarine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Variants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><b>Project 629</b>: Original design received <a href="/wiki/NATO_reporting_name" title="NATO reporting name">NATO reporting name</a>: <b>Golf I</b>, with a total of 22 boats built between 1958 through 1962. Dimension are 98.4 m for length, 8.2 m for beam and 7.85 m for draft. Surfaced/submerged displacement are 2794 / 3553 <a href="/wiki/Ton" title="Ton">tons</a> respectively. Surfaced/submerged speed are 15 / 12.5 kn respectively. Project 629 is crewed by 80 men, and is equipped by either D-1 launching system for <a href="/wiki/R-11_Zemlya" title="R-11 Zemlya">SS-N-1</a> or D-2 launching systems for <a href="/wiki/R-13_(missile)" title="R-13 (missile)">SS-N-4</a> <a href="/wiki/Submarine-launched_ballistic_missile" title="Submarine-launched ballistic missile">SLBMs</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Golf_SSB_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Golf_SSB-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li><b>Project 629B</b>: K-229 was converted in 1958 in <a href="/wiki/Severodvinsk" title="Severodvinsk">Severodvinsk</a>, under Project 629B to test newly designed on board missile control system, which was the first generation design fully based on semiconductors.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li><b>Project 629A</b>: 14 Project 629/Golf I class submarines were converted to Project 629A between 1966 thru 1972, and received NATO reporting name <b>Golf II</b>. Changes include the increase of dimension, with length and draft increased to 98.9 / 8.5 m respectively, the surfaced/submerged displaced increased to 2300-2820 / 2700-3553 <a href="/wiki/T" title="T">t</a> respectively, surfaced/submerged speed increased to 17 / 14 kn respectively. Crew is increased to 83, and the launching system is D-4 for <a href="/wiki/R-21_(missile)" title="R-21 (missile)">SS-N-5</a> SLBMs.<sup id="cite_ref-Golf_SSB_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Golf_SSB-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li><b>Project 601</b>: K-118 was converted from 1969 thru 1974 and received NATO reporting name <b>Golf III</b>, with displacement increased to 4000 tons and number of SLBMs increased to 6, and the missile is <a href="/wiki/R-29_Vysota" title="R-29 Vysota">SS-N-8</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Golf_SSB_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Golf_SSB-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li><b>Project 605</b>: K-102 was converted from 1969 thru 1973 and received NATO reporting name <b>Golf IV</b>, lengthened 18.3 m with 4 SLBMs to test <a href="/wiki/R-27_Zyb#Variants" title="R-27 Zyb">R-27K (SS-N-13)</a> SLBM.<sup id="cite_ref-Golf_SSB_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Golf_SSB-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li><b>Project 619</b>: K-153 was converted in 1976 to test <a href="/wiki/R-39_Rif" title="R-39 Rif">SS-N-20</a> SLBM, and received NATO reporting name <b>Golf V</b>.<sup id="cite_ref-Golf_SSB_9-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Golf_SSB-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li><b>Project 629R</b>: "K-61", "B-42" and "K-107" were converted in <a href="/wiki/Vladivostok" title="Vladivostok">Vladivostok</a> into command posts between 1973 - 1979, with the missile and torpedo tubes removed, received NATO reporting name <b>Golf SSQ</b>.<sup id="cite_ref-Golf_SSB_9-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Golf_SSB-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li><b>Type 6631</b>: Chinese built Golf-class submarine built in <a href="/wiki/Dalian" title="Dalian">Dalian</a> between 1960 thru 1966, after receiving technical data from USSR in 1959. China did not purchase SLBM, and thus Type 6631 was equipped with simulation systems and mainly used for training. Originally with <a href="/wiki/Pennant_number" title="Pennant number">pennant number</a> 1101, it was subsequently changed to 200 in 1967. The second unit was supposed to be built and delivered by USSR (pennant number K-208), but due to <a href="/wiki/Sino-Soviet_split" title="Sino-Soviet split">Sino-Soviet split</a>, most on board equipment was not delivered, and the boat was used as sample for reverse engineering to complete the first unit, which entered Chinese service in August 1966. In June 1968, Type 6631 went through a major refit because China decided to adopt solid fueled SLBM instead of liquid fueled SLBM Soviet used, so the simulation equipment was replaced by support equipments of solid fueled SLBM. In addition, the planned Chinese SLBM had different dimension than the original Russian SLBM equipped Golf class submarine, so the number of missile tubes were reduced to two from the original three.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li><b>Type 031</b>: Type 6631 went through a second major refit which was completed in November 1978. The most important improvement is the ability to launch SLBM underwater. In addition to changing to Type 031 from the original Type 6631, the unit also received the name <a href="/wiki/Great_Wall" title="Great Wall" class="mw-redirect">Great Wall</a>, when China begun to restored the practice to name its warships in the 1980s. The unit is hence usually referred as Great Wall 200.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>
