<p>The <b>Han Chinese</b> are an <a href="/wiki/Ethnic_group" title="Ethnic group">ethnic group</a> native to <a href="/wiki/East_Asia" title="East Asia">East Asia</a>. They constitute approximately 92% of the <a href="/wiki/Population" title="Population">population</a> of <a href="/wiki/Mainland_China" title="Mainland China">Mainland China</a>, 93% of the population of <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a>, 92% of the population of <a href="/wiki/Macau" title="Macau">Macau</a>, 98% of the population of <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>, 74% of the population of <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a>, 24.5% of the population of <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a>, and about 19% of the entire <a href="/wiki/World_population" title="World population">global human population</a>, making them the largest ethnic group in the world. The Han Chinese are regarded as a subset of the <a href="/wiki/Zhonghua_minzu" title="Zhonghua minzu">Chinese nation</a> (<i>Zhonghua minzu</i>). They sometimes refer to themselves as <i><a href="/wiki/Yan_Huang_Zisun" title="Yan Huang Zisun">Yan Huang Zisun</a></i>, meaning the "descendants of (god-emperors) <a href="/wiki/Yandi" title="Yandi" class="mw-redirect">Yan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Huangdi" title="Huangdi" class="mw-redirect">Huang</a>".</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terms_and_etymology">Terms and etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Han_Chinese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Terms and etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The name <i>Han</i> comes from the <a href="/wiki/Han_dynasty" title="Han dynasty">Han dynasty</a>, which succeeded the short-lived <a href="/wiki/Qin_dynasty" title="Qin dynasty">Qin dynasty</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Han_dynasty" title="Han dynasty">Han dynasty</a>'s founding emperor, <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Gaozu_of_Han" title="Emperor Gaozu of Han">Liu Bang (Emperor Gao)</a>, was originally known as the king of the region of <a href="/wiki/Hanzhong" title="Hanzhong">Hanzhong</a> after which the dynasty was named. The name Hanzhong, in turn, is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Han_River_(Yangtze_River_tributary)" title="Han River (Yangtze River tributary)" class="mw-redirect">Han River</a>, which flows through the Hanzhong Plain (<i>zhong</i> means middle).</p>
<p>Prior to the Han dynasty, Chinese scholars used the term as "<a href="/wiki/Huaxia" title="Huaxia">Huaxia</a> people" (華夏族, interpreted to mean "civilized society"), citing<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (May 2011)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> the ancient text description of <a href="/wiki/China_proper" title="China proper">China proper</a> as an area of magnificent prosperity and culture. The Han Dynasty was considered a classical period in Chinese <a href="/wiki/Civilization" title="Civilization">civilization</a>, in that it was able to expand its power and influence over <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Northeast_Asia" title="Northeast Asia">Northeast Asia</a>. As a result of the Han dynasty's prominence, in inter-ethnic and inter-(premodern)-national matters, many Chinese began identifying themselves as "people of Han" (漢人), a name that has since been carried down. In the <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English language</a>, the Han are often referred to as simply "Chinese".</p>
<p>Among some southern Han Chinese, in varieties such as <a href="/wiki/Yue_Chinese" title="Yue Chinese">Cantonese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hakka_Chinese" title="Hakka Chinese">Hakka</a>, <a href="/wiki/Minnan" title="Minnan" class="mw-redirect">Minnan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Teochew_dialect" title="Teochew dialect">Teochew</a> a different term exists – <i>Tángrén</i> (<a href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%94%90%E4%BA%BA" class="extiw" title="wikt:唐人">唐人</a>, literally "<a href="/wiki/Tang_Chinese" title="Tang Chinese">the people of Tang</a>"). This term is derived from a later Chinese dynasty, the <a href="/wiki/Tang_dynasty" title="Tang dynasty">Tang dynasty</a>, regarded as another zenith of Chinese civilization. The term is used in everyday conversation and is also an element in the Cantonese word for <a href="/wiki/Chinatown" title="Chinatown">Chinatown</a>: <a href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%94%90%E4%BA%BA%E8%A1%97" class="extiw" title="wikt:唐人街">唐人街</a> (<a href="/wiki/Jyutping" title="Jyutping">Jyutping</a>: tong4 jan4 gaai1, <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: Tángrénjiē); literally meaning "Street of the people of Tang".</p>
<p>Another term commonly used by <a href="/wiki/Overseas_Chinese" title="Overseas Chinese">Overseas Chinese</a> is <i>Huaren</i> (simplified Chinese: <span lang="zh-Hans" xml:lang="zh-Hans"><a href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%8D%8E%E4%BA%BA" class="extiw" title="wikt:华人">华人</a></span>; traditional Chinese: <span lang="zh-Hant" xml:lang="zh-Hant"><a href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E8%8F%AF%E4%BA%BA" class="extiw" title="wikt:華人">華人</a></span>; pinyin: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin" xml:lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">huárén</span></i>), derived from <i>Zhonghua</i> (simplified Chinese: <span lang="zh-Hans" xml:lang="zh-Hans"><a href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E4%B8%AD%E5%8D%8E" class="extiw" title="wikt:中华">中华</a></span>; traditional Chinese: <span lang="zh-Hant" xml:lang="zh-Hant"><a href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E4%B8%AD%E8%8F%AF" class="extiw" title="wikt:中華">中華</a></span>; pinyin: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin" xml:lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">zhōnghuá</span></i>), a <a href="/wiki/Names_of_China" title="Names of China">literary name for China</a>. The usual translation is "ethnic Chinese". The term refers to "Chinese" as a cultural and ethnic affiliation and is inclusive of both Chinese in China, and persons of Chinese descent residing abroad.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Han_Chinese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mainland_China.2C_Hong_Kong_and_Macau">Mainland China, Hong Kong and Macau</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Han_Chinese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Mainland China, Hong Kong and Macau">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The vast majority of Han Chinese – over 1.2 billion – live in areas under the jurisdiction of the <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="People's Republic of China" class="mw-redirect">People's Republic of China</a> (PRC), where they constitute about 92% of its population. Within the People's Republic of China, Han Chinese are the majority in every <a href="/wiki/Administrative_divisions_of_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="Administrative divisions of the People's Republic of China" class="mw-redirect">province, municipality, and autonomous region</a> except for the autonomous regions of <a href="/wiki/Xinjiang" title="Xinjiang">Xinjiang</a> (45% in 2010) and <a href="/wiki/Tibet_Autonomous_Region" title="Tibet Autonomous Region">Tibet</a> (8% in 2014). Han Chinese also constitute the majority in both of the <a href="/wiki/Special_Administrative_Region_(People%27s_Republic_of_China)" title="Special Administrative Region (People's Republic of China)" class="mw-redirect">special administrative regions</a> of the PRC, about 95% of the population of <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a><sup id="cite_ref-census1_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-census1-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> and about 96% of the population of <a href="/wiki/Macau" title="Macau">Macau</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2006_by-census_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2006_by-census-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Republic_of_China_.28Taiwan.29">Republic of China (Taiwan)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Han_Chinese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Republic of China (Taiwan)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Taiwanese_people" title="Taiwanese people">Taiwanese people</a> and <a href="/wiki/Demographics_of_Taiwan" title="Demographics of Taiwan">Demographics of Taiwan</a></div>
<p>Over 22 million Han Chinese are in Taiwan. The Han Chinese began migrating from southeastern coastal provinces of mainland China to Taiwan in the 17th century. At first, these immigrants chose to settle in locations that bore a resemblance to the areas they had left behind in mainland China, regardless of whether they arrived in the north or south of Taiwan. <a href="/wiki/Hoklo_people" title="Hoklo people">Hoklo</a> immigrants from <a href="/wiki/Quanzhou" title="Quanzhou">Quanzhou</a> settled in coastal regions, and those from <a href="/wiki/Zhangzhou" title="Zhangzhou">Zhangzhou</a> tended to gather on inland plains, while <a href="/wiki/Hakka_people" title="Hakka people">Hakka</a> immigrants inhabited hilly areas. Clashes between these groups over land, water, and cultural differences led to the relocation of some communities, and, as time passed, varying degrees of intermarriage and assimilation took place.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="South_East_Asia">South East Asia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Han_Chinese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: South East Asia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Overseas_Chinese" title="Overseas Chinese">Overseas Chinese</a></div>
<p>Of about 40 million "<a href="/wiki/Overseas_Chinese" title="Overseas Chinese">overseas Chinese</a>" worldwide, nearly 30 million live in <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a>. They are collectively called Nanyang Chinese. <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a> has the largest majority overseas Chinese population at 74% of the country's population. <a href="/wiki/Christmas_Island" title="Christmas Island">Christmas Island</a> also has a Chinese majority at 70%. Large Chinese populations also live in <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a> (25%), <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a> (14%), <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a> (7%), and the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>.</p>
<p>Malaysia and Singapore used to have the largest overseas Chinese population in the world before the country split up in 1965. The first place ranking has been replaced by Thailand.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Others">Others</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Han_Chinese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Others">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Elsewhere in the world, 3 million people of Chinese descent live in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> where they constitute about 1% of the population, over 1 million in <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> (3.7%), over 1.3 million in <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a> (4.3%), over 600,000 in <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> (3.5%), nearly 150,000 in <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> (3.7%), and as many as 750,000 in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Han_Chinese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_China" title="History of China">History of China</a></div>
<p>Because of the overwhelming numerical and cultural dominance of the Han people in China, most of the written <a href="/wiki/History_of_China" title="History of China">history of China</a> can be read as "a history of the Han Chinese", with only passing references to the <a href="/wiki/Ethnic_minorities_in_China" title="Ethnic minorities in China">ethnic minorities in China</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Prehistory_and_the_Huaxia">Prehistory and the Huaxia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Han_Chinese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Prehistory and the Huaxia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Huaxia" title="Huaxia">Huaxia</a></div>
<p>The prehistory of the Han Chinese ethnic group is closely intertwined with their history. Han Chinese trace their ancestry from the <a href="/wiki/Huaxia" title="Huaxia">Huaxia</a> people, who lived along the <i>Huang He</i> or <a href="/wiki/Yellow_River" title="Yellow River">Yellow River</a> in China.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> Writers during the Western Zhou and Han periods derived ancestral lineages from the Huaxia (later known as Xia) based on Shang-era legendary materials.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> The famous Chinese historian <a href="/wiki/Sima_Qian" title="Sima Qian">Sima Qian</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Records_of_the_Grand_Historian" title="Records of the Grand Historian">Records of the Grand Historian</a></i> places the reign of Huang Di (also known as the <a href="/wiki/Yellow_Emperor" title="Yellow Emperor">Yellow Emperor</a>), the legendary ancestor of the Huaxia, at the beginning of Chinese history. He is traditionally considered to have united the Huaxia following the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Banquan" title="Battle of Banquan">Battle of Banquan</a>.</p>
<p>Although study of this period of history is complicated by the absence of contemporary records, the discovery of <a href="/wiki/Archaeological_site" title="Archaeological site">archaeological sites</a> has enabled a succession of <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> cultures to be identified along the Yellow River. Along the central reaches of the Yellow River were the <a href="/wiki/Jiahu" title="Jiahu">Jiahu</a> culture (ca. 7000 to 6600 BCE), <a href="/wiki/Yangshao_culture" title="Yangshao culture">Yangshao culture</a> (ca. 5000 to 3000 BCE) and <a href="/wiki/Longshan_culture" title="Longshan culture">Longshan culture</a> (ca. 3000 to 2000 BCE). Along the lower reaches of the river were the Qingliangang culture (ca. 5400 to 4000 BCE), the <a href="/wiki/Dawenkou_culture" title="Dawenkou culture">Dawenkou culture</a> (ca. 4300 to 2500 BCE), the <a href="/wiki/Longshan_culture" title="Longshan culture">Longshan culture</a> (ca. 2500 to 2000 BCE), and the <a href="/wiki/Yueshi_culture" title="Yueshi culture">Yueshi culture</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history">Early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Han_Chinese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Early history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Xia_dynasty" title="Xia dynasty">Xia dynasty</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shang_dynasty" title="Shang dynasty">Shang dynasty</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zhou_dynasty" title="Zhou dynasty">Zhou dynasty</a></div>
<p>The first dynasty to be described in Chinese historical records is the <a href="/wiki/Xia_dynasty" title="Xia dynasty">Xia dynasty</a>, a legendary period for which scant archaeological evidence exists. They were overthrown by peoples from the east<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, who founded the <a href="/wiki/Shang_dynasty" title="Shang dynasty">Shang dynasty</a> (1600–1046 BCE). The earliest archaeological examples of Chinese writing date back to this period, from characters inscribed on <a href="/wiki/Oracle_bone" title="Oracle bone">oracle bone</a> divination, but the well-developed oracle characters hint at a much earlier origin of writing in China.</p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Shang_dynasty" title="Shang dynasty">Shang dynasty</a>, people of the <a href="/wiki/Wu_(region)" title="Wu (region)">Wu area</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/Yangtze_River_Delta" title="Yangtze River Delta">Yangtze River Delta</a>, were considered a different tribe, and were described as being scantily dressed, tattooed and speaking a distinct language.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-The_Han_Chinese_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Han_Chinese-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup> Later <a href="/wiki/Taibo_of_Wu" title="Taibo of Wu">Taibo</a>, elder uncle of <a href="/wiki/King_Wen_of_Zhou" title="King Wen of Zhou">Ji Chang</a>, realising that his younger brother, Jili, was wiser than him and deserved to inherit the throne, fled to Wu<sup id="cite_ref-chinaknowledge.de_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chinaknowledge.de-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup> and settled there. Three generations later, <a href="/wiki/King_Wu_of_Zhou" title="King Wu of Zhou">King Wu</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Zhou_dynasty" title="Zhou dynasty">Zhou dynasty</a> defeated <a href="/wiki/King_Zhou_of_Shang" title="King Zhou of Shang">King Zhou</a> (the last Shang king), and enfeoffed the descendants of Taibo in Wu,<sup id="cite_ref-chinaknowledge.de_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chinaknowledge.de-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup> this mirrors<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> the later history of <a href="/wiki/Nanyue" title="Nanyue">Nanyue</a>, where a Chinese king and his soldiers ruled a local non-Han population, and mixed with the local inhabitants who were sinicized over time. By the <a href="/wiki/Tang_dynasty" title="Tang dynasty">Tang dynasty</a>, however, this area had become part of the Han Chinese heartland<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. The Shang were eventually overthrown by the people of Zhou, which had emerged as a state along the Yellow River in the 2nd millennium BCE.<sup id="cite_ref-The_Han_Chinese_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Han_Chinese-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Zhou_dynasty" title="Zhou dynasty">Zhou dynasty</a> was the successor to the Shang. Sharing the language and culture of the Shang people, they extended their reach to encompass much of the area north of the <a href="/wiki/Yangtze_River" title="Yangtze River" class="mw-redirect">Yangtze River</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-China_The_Zhou_Period_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-China_The_Zhou_Period-36"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup> Through conquest and colonization, much of this area came under the influence of <a href="/wiki/Sinicization" title="Sinicization">sinicization</a> and this culture extended south.<sup id="cite_ref-The_Han_Chinese_33-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Han_Chinese-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-China_The_Zhou_Period_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-China_The_Zhou_Period-36"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup> However, the power of the Zhou kings fragmented, and many independent states emerged. This period is traditionally divided into two parts, the <a href="/wiki/Spring_and_Autumn_period" title="Spring and Autumn period">Spring and Autumn period</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Warring_States_period" title="Warring States period">Warring States period</a>. This period was an era of major cultural and philosophical development known as the <a href="/wiki/Hundred_Schools_of_Thought" title="Hundred Schools of Thought">Hundred Schools of Thought</a>. Among the most important surviving philosophies from this era are the teachings of <a href="/wiki/Confucianism" title="Confucianism">Confucianism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Taoism" title="Taoism">Taoism</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Imperial_history">Imperial history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Han_Chinese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Imperial history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Qin_dynasty" title="Qin dynasty">Qin dynasty</a></div>
<p>Many Chinese scholars such as Ho Ping-Ti believe that the concept of a Han ethnicity is an ancient one, dating from the Han dynasty itself.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup> The Warring States period came to an end with the unification of China by the <a href="/wiki/Qin_dynasty" title="Qin dynasty">Qin dynasty</a> after it conquered all other rival states<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (February 2015)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup>. The King of Qin, <a href="/wiki/Qin_Shi_Huang" title="Qin Shi Huang">Ying Zheng</a>, declared himself the first emperor, using a newly created title "<a href="/wiki/Qin_Shi_Huang" title="Qin Shi Huang">Qin Shi Huang</a>" (literally "First Emperor of Qin"), thus setting the precedent for the next two millennia. He established a new centralized and bureaucratic state to replace the old feudal system, creating many of the institutions of imperial China, and unified the country economically and culturally by decreeing a unified standard of weights, measures, currency, and writing.</p>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Han_dynasty" title="Han dynasty">Han dynasty</a></div>

<p>However, the reign of the first imperial dynasty was to be short-lived. Due to Qin Shi Huang's autocratic rule, and his massive construction projects such as the <a href="/wiki/Great_Wall_of_China" title="Great Wall of China">Great Wall</a> which fomented rebellion among the populace, the Qin dynasty fell soon after his death. The <a href="/wiki/Han_dynasty" title="Han dynasty">Han dynasty</a> (206 BCE – 220 CE) emerged from the succession struggle and succeeded in establishing a much longer lasting dynasty. It continued many of the institutions created by Qin Shi Huang but adopted a more moderate rule. Under the Han dynasty, arts and culture flourished, while the dynasty <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Han_dynasty" title="History of the Han dynasty">expanded militarily in all directions</a>. This period is considered one of the greatest periods of the history of China, and the Han Chinese take their name from this dynasty.</p>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Three_Kingdoms" title="Three Kingdoms">Three Kingdoms</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sixteen_Kingdoms" title="Sixteen Kingdoms">Sixteen Kingdoms</a>, <a href="/wiki/Southern_and_Northern_Dynasties" title="Southern and Northern Dynasties">Southern and Northern Dynasties</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sui_dynasty" title="Sui dynasty">Sui dynasty</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tang_dynasty" title="Tang dynasty">Tang dynasty</a></div>
<p>The fall of the Han dynasty was followed by an age of fragmentation and several centuries of disunity amid warfare by rival kingdoms. During this time, areas of northern China were overrun by <a href="/wiki/Five_Barbarians" title="Five Barbarians">various non-Han nomadic peoples</a> which came to establish kingdoms of their own, the most successful of which was <a href="/wiki/Northern_Wei" title="Northern Wei">Northern Wei</a> established by the <a href="/wiki/Xianbei" title="Xianbei">Xianbei</a>. Starting from this period, the native population of China proper began to be referred to as <b>Hanren</b>, or the "People of Han", to distinguish from the nomads from the steppe; "Han" refers to the old dynasty. Warfare and invasion led to one of the first great migrations in Han population history, as the population fled south to the <a href="/wiki/Yangtze_River" title="Yangtze River" class="mw-redirect">Yangtze</a> and beyond, shifting the Chinese demographic center south and speeding up <a href="/wiki/Sinicization" title="Sinicization">Sinicization</a> of the far south. At the same time, in the north, most of the nomads in northern China came to be Sinicized as they ruled over large Chinese populations and adopted elements of Chinese culture and Chinese administration. Of note, the Xianbei rulers of the Northern Wei ordered a policy of systematic Sinicization, <a href="/wiki/Change_of_Xianbei_names_to_Han_names" title="Change of Xianbei names to Han names">adopting Han surnames</a>, institutions, and culture.</p>
<p>The Sui (581–618) and Tang (618–907) dynasties saw the continuation of the complete Sinicization of the south coast of what is now China proper, including what are now the provinces of <a href="/wiki/Fujian" title="Fujian">Fujian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Guangdong" title="Guangdong">Guangdong</a>. The later part of the Tang dynasty, as well as the Five Dynasties period that followed, saw continual warfare in north and central China; the relative stability of the south coast made it an attractive destination for refugees.</p>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Song_dynasty" title="Song dynasty">Song dynasty</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yuan_dynasty" title="Yuan dynasty">Yuan dynasty</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ming_dynasty" title="Ming dynasty">Ming dynasty</a> and <a href="/wiki/Qing_dynasty" title="Qing dynasty">Qing dynasty</a></div>

<p>The next few centuries saw successive invasions of non-Han peoples from the north, such as the <a href="/wiki/Khitan_people" title="Khitan people">Khitans</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jurchen_people" title="Jurchen people">Jurchens</a>. In 1279, the <a href="/wiki/Mongols" title="Mongols">Mongols</a> conquered all of China, becoming the first non-Han ethnic group to do so, and established the <a href="/wiki/Yuan_dynasty" title="Yuan dynasty">Yuan dynasty</a>. The Mongols divided society into four classes, with themselves occupying the top class and Han Chinese into the bottom two classes. <a href="/wiki/Emigration" title="Emigration">Emigration</a>, seen as disloyal to ancestors and ancestral land, was banned by the Song and Yuan dynasties.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In 1368, Han Chinese rebels drove out the Mongols and, after some infighting, established the <a href="/wiki/Ming_dynasty" title="Ming dynasty">Ming dynasty</a> (1368–1644). Settlement of Han Chinese into peripheral regions continued during this period, with <a href="/wiki/Yunnan" title="Yunnan">Yunnan</a> in the southwest receiving a large number of migrants.</p>
<p>In 1644, the Ming capital, Beijing, was captured by <a href="/wiki/Li_Zicheng" title="Li Zicheng">Li Zicheng</a>'s peasant rebels and the <a href="/wiki/Chongzhen_Emperor" title="Chongzhen Emperor">Chongzhen Emperor</a> committed suicide. The <a href="/wiki/Manchu_people" title="Manchu people">Manchus</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Qing_dynasty" title="Qing dynasty">Qing dynasty</a> then allied with former Ming general <a href="/wiki/Wu_Sangui" title="Wu Sangui">Wu Sangui</a> and seized control of Beijing. Remnant Ming forces led by <a href="/wiki/Koxinga" title="Koxinga">Koxinga</a> fled to <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a> and established the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Tungning" title="Kingdom of Tungning">Kingdom of Tungning</a>, which eventually capitulated to Qing forces in 1683. Taiwan, previously inhabited mostly by non-Han aborigines, was Sinicized via large-scale migration accompanied with assimilation during this period, despite efforts by the Manchus to prevent this, as they found it difficult to maintain control over the island. In 1681, the <a href="/wiki/Kangxi_Emperor" title="Kangxi Emperor">Kangxi Emperor</a> ordered construction of the <a href="/wiki/Willow_Palisade" title="Willow Palisade">Willow Palisade</a> to prevent Han Chinese migration to the three northeastern provinces, which nevertheless harbored a significant Chinese population for centuries, especially in the southern <a href="/wiki/Liaodong" title="Liaodong" class="mw-redirect">Liaodong</a> area. The Manchus designated Jilin and Heilongjiang as the Manchu homeland, to which the Manchus could hypothetically escape and regroup if the Qing dynasty fell.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup> But because of increasing Russian territorial encroachment and annexation of neighboring territory, the Qing later reversed its policy and allowed the consolidation of a demographic Han majority in northeast China.</p>
<p>In the 19th century, Chinese migrants went in large numbers to other parts of the world, including <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>. See <a href="/wiki/Overseas_Chinese" title="Overseas Chinese">Overseas Chinese</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Han_Chinese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Chinese_culture" title="Chinese culture">Chinese culture</a></div>
<p>China is one of the world's oldest and most complex <a href="/wiki/Civilization" title="Civilization">civilizations</a>. Chinese culture dates back thousands of years. Han Chinese maintain cultural affinities to Chinese lands outside of their locale by <a href="/wiki/Ancestor_worship" title="Ancestor worship" class="mw-redirect">ancestor worship</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chinese_clan" title="Chinese clan" class="mw-redirect">Chinese clan</a> associations, which often identify famous figures from Chinese history or myth as ancestors of current members.<sup id="cite_ref-Cohen_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cohen-40"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup> Such patriarchs include the <a href="/wiki/Yellow_Emperor" title="Yellow Emperor">Yellow Emperor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yan_Emperor" title="Yan Emperor">Yan Emperor</a>, who according to legend lived thousands of years ago and gave Han people the sobriquet "<i><a href="/wiki/Descendants_of_Yan_and_Huang_Emperor" title="Descendants of Yan and Huang Emperor" class="mw-redirect">Descendants of Yan and Huang Emperor</a></i>" (<span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">炎黃子孫</span>; <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">炎黄子孙</span>), a phrase which has reverberative connotations in a divisive <a href="/wiki/Politics" title="Politics">political</a> climate, as in that between <a href="/wiki/Mainland_China" title="Mainland China">mainland China</a> and <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>.</p>

<p>Throughout the <a href="/wiki/History_of_China" title="History of China">history of China</a>, Chinese culture has been heavily influenced by <a href="/wiki/Confucianism" title="Confucianism">Confucianism</a>. Credited with shaping much of Chinese thought, Confucianism was the official <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a> throughout most of <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Chinese" title="Imperial Chinese" class="mw-redirect">Imperial China</a>'s history, institutionalizing values like <a href="/wiki/Filial_piety" title="Filial piety">filial piety</a> which implied the performance of certain <a href="/wiki/Li_(Confucian)" title="Li (Confucian)">shared rituals</a>. Thus, villagers lavished on <a href="/w/index.php?title=Chinese_funeral&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Chinese funeral (page does not exist)">funeral</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chinese_wedding" title="Chinese wedding" class="mw-redirect">wedding</a> ceremonies that imitated the Confucian standards of the Emperors.<sup id="cite_ref-Cohen_40-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cohen-40"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup> Mastery of Confucian texts provided the primary criterion for <a href="/wiki/Imperial_examination" title="Imperial examination">entry into the imperial bureaucracy</a>, but even those degree-holders who did not enter the bureaucracy or who left it held increased social influence in their home areas, contributing to the homogenizing of Han Chinese culture. Other factors contributing to the development of a shared Han culture included <a href="/wiki/Urbanization" title="Urbanization">urbanization</a> and geographically vast but integrated commodity markets.<sup id="cite_ref-Cohen_40-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cohen-40"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Language">Language</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Han_Chinese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Language">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese language</a></div>
<p>Han Chinese speak various forms of the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese language</a> that are descended from a common early language;<sup id="cite_ref-Cohen_40-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cohen-40"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup> one of the names of the language group is <i>Hanyu</i> (simplified Chinese: <span lang="zh-Hans" xml:lang="zh-Hans"><a href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E6%B1%89%E8%AF%AD" class="extiw" title="wikt:汉语">汉语</a></span>; traditional Chinese: <span lang="zh-Hant" xml:lang="zh-Hant"><a href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E6%BC%A2%E8%AA%9E" class="extiw" title="wikt:漢語">漢語</a></span>), literally the "Han language". Similarly, <a href="/wiki/Chinese_characters" title="Chinese characters">Chinese characters</a>, used to write the language, are called <i>Hanzi</i> (simplified Chinese: <span lang="zh-Hans" xml:lang="zh-Hans"><a href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E6%B1%89%E5%AD%97" class="extiw" title="wikt:汉字">汉字</a></span>; traditional Chinese: <span lang="zh-Hant" xml:lang="zh-Hant"><a href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E6%BC%A2%E5%AD%97" class="extiw" title="wikt:漢字">漢字</a></span>), or "Han characters".</p>
<p>In the late imperial period, more than two thirds of the Han Chinese population used a variant of <a href="/wiki/Mandarin_Chinese" title="Mandarin Chinese">Mandarin Chinese</a> as their native tongue.<sup id="cite_ref-Cohen_40-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cohen-40"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup> However, there was a larger variety of languages in certain areas of southeast China, like <a href="/wiki/Shanghai" title="Shanghai">Shanghai</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guangzhou" title="Guangzhou">Guangzhou</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Guangxi" title="Guangxi">Guangxi</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Cohen_40-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cohen-40"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup> Since the <a href="/wiki/Qin_dynasty" title="Qin dynasty">Qin dynasty</a>, which standardized the various forms of writing that existed in China, a standard <a href="/wiki/Classical_Chinese" title="Classical Chinese">literary Chinese</a> had emerged with vocabulary and grammar that was significantly different from the various <a href="/wiki/Varieties_of_Chinese" title="Varieties of Chinese">forms of spoken Chinese</a>. A simplified and elaborated version of this written standard was used in business contracts, notes for <a href="/wiki/Chinese_opera" title="Chinese opera">Chinese opera</a>, ritual texts for <a href="/wiki/Chinese_folk_religion" title="Chinese folk religion">Chinese folk religion</a>, and other daily documents for educated people.<sup id="cite_ref-Cohen_40-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cohen-40"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>During the early 20th century, <a href="/wiki/Written_vernacular_Chinese" title="Written vernacular Chinese">written vernacular Chinese</a> based on Mandarin dialects, which had been developing for several centuries, was standardized and adopted to replace Literary Chinese. While written vernacular forms of other varieties of China exist, such as <a href="/wiki/Written_Cantonese" title="Written Cantonese">written Cantonese</a>, written Chinese based on Mandarin is widely understood by speakers of all Chinese varieties and has taken up the dominant position among written Chinese forms, formerly occupied by Literary Chinese. Thus, although the residents of different regions would not necessarily understand each other's speech, they generally share a common written language.</p>
<p>Beginning in the 1950s, <a href="/wiki/Simplified_Chinese_characters" title="Simplified Chinese characters">Simplified Chinese characters</a> were adopted in <a href="/wiki/Mainland_China" title="Mainland China">mainland China</a> and later in <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a>, while Chinese communities in <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a>, <a href="/wiki/Macau" title="Macau">Macau</a>, <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a> and overseas countries continue to use <a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_characters" title="Traditional Chinese characters">Traditional Chinese characters</a>. While significant differences exist between the two character sets, they are largely <a href="/wiki/Mutually_intelligible" title="Mutually intelligible" class="mw-redirect">mutually intelligible</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Names">Names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Han_Chinese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Chinese_name" title="Chinese name">Chinese name</a> and <a href="/wiki/List_of_common_Chinese_surnames" title="List of common Chinese surnames">List of common Chinese surnames</a></div>
<p>Chinese names are typically two or three syllables in length, with the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_surname" title="Chinese surname">surname</a> preceding the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_given_name" title="Chinese given name">given name</a>. Surnames are typically one character in length, though a few uncommon surnames are two or more syllables long, while given names are one or two syllables long. There are 4,000 to 6,000 surnames in China, of which about 1,000 surnames are most common.</p>
<p>In historical China, <a href="/wiki/List_of_common_Chinese_surnames" title="List of common Chinese surnames">hundred surnames</a> (百家姓) was a crucial identity of Han people. Besides the common culture and writings, common origin rooted in the surnames was another major factor that contributed towards Han Chinese identity.<sup id="cite_ref-Anth_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Anth-41"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dress">Dress</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Han_Chinese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Dress">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hanfu" title="Hanfu">Hanfu</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Today, Han Chinese usually wear <a href="/wiki/Western_world" title="Western world">Western</a>-style clothing. Few wear traditional <a href="/wiki/Hanfu" title="Hanfu">Hanfu</a> on a regular basis. It is, however, preserved in religious and ceremonial costumes. For example, <a href="/wiki/Taoism" title="Taoism">Taoist</a> priests dress in fashion typical of scholars of the <a href="/wiki/Han_Dynasty" title="Han Dynasty" class="mw-redirect">Han Dynasty</a>. Both the <a href="/wiki/Kimono" title="Kimono">Kimono</a> from <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Hanbok" title="Hanbok">Hanbok</a> in <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a> were heavily influenced by traditional Chinese <a href="/wiki/Hanfu" title="Hanfu">Hanfu</a>. The Vietnamese <a href="/wiki/Ao_dai" title="Ao dai">Ao dai</a> bears similarities with the Chinese <a href="/wiki/Cheongsam" title="Cheongsam">Cheongsam</a> but ultimately descends from the <a href="/wiki/%C3%81o_t%E1%BB%A9_th%C3%A2n" title="Áo tứ thân">áo tứ thân</a>, which was influenced by the fashion of China's imperial court. In Vietnam, the court attire or royal clothing worn by the <a href="/wiki/Nguyen_dynasty" title="Nguyen dynasty" class="mw-redirect">Nguyen dynasty</a> descended from Ming clothing with some influence from the newly established Qing dynasty. Now, the most popular traditional Chinese clothing worn by many women on important occasions such as wedding banquets and New Year is called the <a href="/wiki/Qipao" title="Qipao" class="mw-redirect">qipao</a>. However, this attire comes not from the Han Chinese but from a modified dress-code of the <a href="/wiki/Manchu" title="Manchu" class="mw-redirect">Manchus</a>, the ethnic group that ruled China between the 17th (1644) and the early 20th century (1911).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Family">Family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Han_Chinese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Family">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Chinese_kinship" title="Chinese kinship">Han Chinese families</a> throughout China have traditionally had a certain set of prescribed roles, such as the family head (<span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">家長</span>, <i>jiāzhǎng</i>), who represents the family to the outside world; and the family manager (<span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">當家</span>, <i>dāngjiā</i>), who is in charge of the earnings. Because farmland was commonly bought, sold, or <a href="/wiki/Mortgage" title="Mortgage" class="mw-redirect">mortgaged</a>, families were run like enterprises, with set rules for family division (<span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">分家</span>, <i>fēnjiā</i>) of pooled earnings and assets.<sup id="cite_ref-Cohen_40-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cohen-40"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Han Chinese houses are different from place to place. Chinese Han people in Beijing traditionally commonly lived with the whole family in large houses that were rectangular in shape. This house is called a <i><a href="/wiki/Siheyuan" title="Siheyuan">siheyuan</a></i>. These houses had four rooms in the front: the guest room, <a href="/wiki/Kitchen" title="Kitchen">kitchen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Toilet_(room)" title="Toilet (room)">lavatory</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Servants%27_quarters" title="Servants' quarters">servants' quarters</a>. Across the large double doors was a wing for the elderly in the family. This wing consisted of three rooms, a central room where the four tablets, heaven, earth, ancestor, and teacher, were worshipped. There the two rooms attached to the left and right were <a href="/wiki/Bedrooms" title="Bedrooms" class="mw-redirect">bedrooms</a> for the grandparents. The east wing of the house was inhabited by the eldest son and his family, while the west wing sheltered the second son and his family. Each wing had a <a href="/wiki/Veranda" title="Veranda">veranda</a>, some had a "sunroom" made from a surrounding fabric supported by a wooden or <a href="/wiki/Bamboo" title="Bamboo">bamboo</a> frame. Every wing is also built around a central courtyard used for study, exercise, or nature viewing.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Food">Food</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Han_Chinese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Food">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Chinese_cuisine" title="Chinese cuisine">Chinese cuisine</a></div>
<p>China's cuisine varies from <a href="/wiki/Sichuan" title="Sichuan">Sichuan</a>'s famously <a href="/wiki/Szechuan_cuisine" title="Szechuan cuisine" class="mw-redirect">spicy food</a> to <a href="/wiki/Guangdong" title="Guangdong">Guangdong</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Dim_Sum" title="Dim Sum" class="mw-redirect">Dim Sum</a> and fresh seafood. Analysis reveals the main staple of China to be rice. During China's neolithic period, southernwestern rice growers transitioned to millet in the northwest when they could not find a suitable northwestern ecology, where it was typically dry and cold, to sustain the generous yields for their staple as well as it could in other areas such as along the eastern Chinese coast.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Literature">Literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Han_Chinese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Chinese_literature" title="Chinese literature">Chinese literature</a></div>
<p>Chinese has a rich history of classical literature dating back several thousand years. Important early works include <a href="/wiki/Chinese_classic_texts" title="Chinese classic texts" class="mw-redirect">classics texts</a> such as <i><a href="/wiki/Analects_of_Confucius" title="Analects of Confucius" class="mw-redirect">Analects of Confucius</a></i>, the <i><a href="/wiki/I_Ching" title="I Ching">I Ching</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Tao_Te_Ching" title="Tao Te Ching">Tao Te Ching</a></i>, and the <i><a href="/wiki/The_Art_of_War" title="The Art of War">Art of War</a></i>. Some of the most important Han Chinese poets in the pre-modern era include <a href="/wiki/Li_Bai" title="Li Bai">Li Bai</a>, <a href="/wiki/Du_Fu" title="Du Fu">Du Fu</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Su_Shi" title="Su Shi">Su Dongpo</a>. The most important novels in Chinese literature, or the <a href="/wiki/Four_Great_Classical_Novels" title="Four Great Classical Novels">Four Great Classical Novels</a>, are: <i><a href="/wiki/Dream_of_the_Red_Chamber" title="Dream of the Red Chamber">Dream of the Red Chamber</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Water_Margin" title="Water Margin">Water Margin</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Romance_of_the_Three_Kingdoms" title="Romance of the Three Kingdoms">Romance of the Three Kingdoms</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Journey_to_the_West" title="Journey to the West">Journey to the West</a></i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Contributions_to_humanity">Contributions to humanity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Han_Chinese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Contributions to humanity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

Chinese astronomers were also among the first to record observations of a <a href="/wiki/Supernova" title="Supernova">supernova</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Chinese_art" title="Chinese art">Chinese art</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chinese_cuisine" title="Chinese cuisine">Chinese cuisine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chinese_philosophy" title="Chinese philosophy">Chinese philosophy</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Chinese_literature" title="Chinese literature">Chinese literature</a> all have thousands of years of development, while numerous Chinese sites, such as the <a href="/wiki/Great_Wall_of_China" title="Great Wall of China">Great Wall of China</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Terracotta_Army" title="Terracotta Army">Terracotta Army</a>, are <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Sites</a>. Since the start of the program in 2001, aspects of Chinese culture have been listed by <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> as <a href="/wiki/Masterpieces_of_the_Oral_and_Intangible_Heritage_of_Humanity" title="Masterpieces of the Oral and Intangible Heritage of Humanity">Masterpieces of the Oral and Intangible Heritage of Humanity</a>.</p>
<p>Throughout much of history, successive <a href="/wiki/Chinese_Dynasties" title="Chinese Dynasties" class="mw-redirect">Chinese Dynasties</a> have exerted influence on their neighbors in the areas of art, music, religion, food, dress, philosophy, <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">language</a>, <a href="/wiki/Government" title="Government">government</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Culture" title="Culture">culture</a>. In modern times, Han Chinese form the largest ethnic group in China, while an <a href="/wiki/Overseas_Chinese" title="Overseas Chinese">overseas Chinese</a> diaspora numbering in the tens of millions has settled in and contributed to countries throughout the world.</p>
<p>In modern times, Han Chinese have continued to contribute to mathematics and sciences. Among them are <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize" title="Nobel Prize">Nobel Prize</a> recipients <a href="/wiki/Steven_Chu" title="Steven Chu">Steven Chu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Samuel_C._C._Ting" title="Samuel C. <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">Religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Han_Chinese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Religion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Religion_in_China" title="Religion in China">Religion in China</a></div>

<p>Chinese culture has been long characterized by <a href="/wiki/Religious_pluralism" title="Religious pluralism">religious pluralism</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Chinese_folk_religion" title="Chinese folk religion">Chinese folk religion</a> has always maintained a profound influence. Indigenous <a href="/wiki/Confucianism" title="Confucianism">Confucianism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Taoism" title="Taoism">Taoism</a> share aspects of being a philosophy or a religion, and neither demand exclusive adherence, resulting in a culture of tolerance and <a href="/wiki/Syncretism" title="Syncretism">syncretism</a> where multiple religions or belief systems are often practiced in concert, along with local customs and traditions. Han Chinese culture has also been long influenced by <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhism</a>, while in recent centuries <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a> has also gained a foothold in the population.</p>
<p>Confucianism, a governing philosophy and moral code with some religious elements like <a href="/wiki/Ancestor_worship" title="Ancestor worship" class="mw-redirect">ancestor worship</a>, is deeply ingrained in Chinese culture and was the official state philosophy in China from the Han Dynasty until the fall of imperial China in the 20th century.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Chinese_folk_religion" title="Chinese folk religion">Chinese folk religion</a> is the set of worship traditions of the ethnic deities of the Han people. It involves worship of various figures in <a href="/wiki/Chinese_mythology" title="Chinese mythology">Chinese mythology</a>, folk heroes such as <a href="/wiki/Guan_Yu" title="Guan Yu">Guan Yu</a> and <a href="/wiki/Qu_Yuan" title="Qu Yuan">Qu Yuan</a>, mythological creatures such as the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_dragon" title="Chinese dragon">Chinese dragon</a>, or family, clan and national <a href="/wiki/Ancestor_worship" title="Ancestor worship" class="mw-redirect">ancestors</a>. These practices vary from region to region, and do not characterize an organized religion, though many <a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_holidays" title="Traditional Chinese holidays">traditional Chinese holidays</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Duanwu_Festival" title="Duanwu Festival">Duanwu (or Dragon Boat) Festival</a>, <a href="/wiki/Qingming_Festival" title="Qingming Festival">Qingming</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Mid-Autumn_Festival" title="Mid-Autumn Festival">Mid-Autumn Festival</a> come from the most popular of these traditions.</p>
<p>Taoism, another indigenous religion, is also widely practiced in both its folk religion forms and as an organized religion, and has influenced Chinese art, poetry, philosophy, <a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_medicine" title="Traditional Chinese medicine">medicine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chinese_astronomy" title="Chinese astronomy">astronomy</a>, alchemy and chemistry, cuisine, <a href="/wiki/Chinese_martial_arts" title="Chinese martial arts">martial arts</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Chinese_architecture" title="Chinese architecture">architecture</a>. Taoism was the state religion of the early Han Dynasty, and also often enjoyed state patronage under subsequent emperors and dynasties.</p>
<p>In Han Dynasty, <a href="/wiki/Confucian" title="Confucian" class="mw-redirect">Confucian</a> ideals were the dominant ideology. Near the end of the dynasty, <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhism</a> entered China and later gained popularity. Historically, Buddhism alternated between state tolerance and even patronage, and <a href="/wiki/Four_Buddhist_Persecutions_in_China" title="Four Buddhist Persecutions in China">persecution</a>. In its original form, Buddhism was at odds with the native Chinese religions, especially the elite, as certain Buddhist values often conflicted with Chinese sensibilities. However, through centuries of assimilation, adaptation, and <a href="/wiki/Syncretism" title="Syncretism">syncretism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chinese_Buddhism" title="Chinese Buddhism">Chinese Buddhism</a> gained an accepted place in the culture. Buddhism would come to be influenced by Confucianism and Taoism,<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61"><span>[</span>61<span>]</span></a></sup> and exerted influence in turn, such as in the form of <a href="/wiki/Neo-Confucianism" title="Neo-Confucianism">Neo-Confucianism</a>.</p>
<p>Though <a href="/wiki/Christianity_in_China" title="Christianity in China">Christian influence in China</a> existed as early as the 7th century, Christianity did not begin to gain a significant influence in China until contact with Europeans during the <a href="/wiki/Ming_Dynasty" title="Ming Dynasty" class="mw-redirect">Ming</a> and <a href="/wiki/Qing_Dynasty" title="Qing Dynasty" class="mw-redirect">Qing</a> dynasties. Chinese practices at odds with Christian beliefs resulted in the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_Rites_controversy" title="Chinese Rites controversy">Chinese Rites controversy</a>, and subsequent reduction in Christian influence. Christianity grew considerably following the <a href="/wiki/First_Opium_War" title="First Opium War">First Opium War</a>, after which foreign missionaries in China enjoyed the protection of the Western powers, and widespread proselytism took place.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="DNA_analysis">DNA analysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Han_Chinese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: DNA analysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Y-chromosome <a href="/wiki/Haplogroup_O3_(Y-DNA)" title="Haplogroup O3 (Y-DNA)" class="mw-redirect">haplogroup O3</a> is a common DNA marker in Han Chinese, as it appeared in China in prehistoric times. It is found in more than 50% of Chinese males, and ranging up to over 80% in certain regional subgroups of the Han ethnicity.<sup id="cite_ref-Hurles2005_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hurles2005-62"><span>[</span>62<span>]</span></a></sup> However, the <a href="/wiki/Mitochondrial_DNA" title="Mitochondrial DNA">mitochondrial DNA</a> of Han Chinese increases in diversity as one looks from northern to southern China, which suggests that male migrants from northern China married with women from local peoples after arriving in <a href="/wiki/Guangdong" title="Guangdong">Guangdong</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fujian" title="Fujian">Fujian</a>, and other regions of southern China.<sup id="cite_ref-table_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-table-63"><span>[</span>63<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-EJH_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EJH-64"><span>[</span>64<span>]</span></a></sup> Despite this, tests comparing the genetic profiles of northern Han, southern Han and southern natives determined that haplogroups O1b-M110, O2a1-M88 and O3d-M7, which are prevalent in southern natives, were only observed in some southern Hans (4% on average), but not in northern Hans. Therefore, this proves that the male contribution of southern natives in southern Hans is limited,if we assume that the frequency distribution of Y lineages in southern natives represents that before the expansion of Han culture that started 2,000 yr ago.<sup id="cite_ref-gene_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gene-65"><span>[</span>65<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-table_63-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-table-63"><span>[</span>63<span>]</span></a></sup> In contrast, there are consistent strong genetic similarities in the Y chromosome haplogroup distribution between the southern and northern Chinese population, and the result of principal component analysis indicates almost all Han populations form a tight cluster in their Y chromosome. However, other research has also shown that the paternal lineages Y-DNA O-M119,<sup id="cite_ref-Li_et_al_.282008.29_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Li_et_al_.282008.29-66"><span>[</span>66<span>]</span></a></sup> O-P201,<sup id="cite_ref-Karafet_et_al_.282010.29_67-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Karafet_et_al_.282010.29-67"><span>[</span>67<span>]</span></a></sup> O-P203<sup id="cite_ref-Karafet_et_al_.282010.29_67-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Karafet_et_al_.282010.29-67"><span>[</span>67<span>]</span></a></sup> and O-M95<sup id="cite_ref-Karafet_et_al_2005_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Karafet_et_al_2005-68"><span>[</span>68<span>]</span></a></sup> are found in both southern Han Chinese and South Chinese minorities, more common in the latter. In fact, these paternal markers are in turn less frequent in northern Han Chinese.<sup id="cite_ref-Y-DNA_haplogroups_by_populations_of_East_and_Southeast_Asia_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Y-DNA_haplogroups_by_populations_of_East_and_Southeast_Asia-69"><span>[</span>69<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Han_Chinese_Y-DNA_by_region_70-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Han_Chinese_Y-DNA_by_region-70"><span>[</span>70<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Yan_et_al_.282011.29_71-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Yan_et_al_.282011.29-71"><span>[</span>71<span>]</span></a></sup> Another study puts Han Chinese into two groups: northern Han Chinese and southern Han Chinese, and it finds that the genetic characteristics of nowadays northern Han Chinese was already formed prior to 3,000 years ago in the "Central Plain area."<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72"><span>[</span>72<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Additionally, the estimated contribution of northern Hans to southern Hans is substantial in both paternal and maternal lineages and a geographic cline exists for mtDNA. As a result, the northern Hans are the primary contributors to the gene pool of the southern Hans. However, it is noteworthy that the expansion process was dominated by males, as is shown by a greater contribution to the Y-chromosome than the mtDNA from northern Hans to southern Hans. These genetic observations are in line with historical records of continuous and large migratory waves of northern China inhabitants escaping warfare and famine, to southern China. Aside from these large migratory waves, other smaller southward migrations also occurred during almost all periods in the past two millennia.<sup id="cite_ref-table_63-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-table-63"><span>[</span>63<span>]</span></a></sup> Moreover, a study by the Chinese Academy of Sciences into the gene frequency data of Han subpopulations and ethnic minorities in China, showed that Han subpopulations in different regions are also genetically close to the local ethnic minorities, and it means that in many cases blood of ethnic minorities has mixed into Han, while at the same time, blood of Han also has mixed into the local ethnicities.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73"><span>[</span>73<span>]</span></a></sup> A recent, and to date the most extensive, genome-wide association study of the Han population shows that geographic-genetic stratification from north to south has occurred and centrally placed populations act as the conduit for outlying ones.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74"><span>[</span>74<span>]</span></a></sup> Ultimately, with the exception in some ethnolinguistic branches of the Han Chinese, such as <a href="/wiki/Pinghua" title="Pinghua">Pinghua</a>, there is coherent genetic structure in all Han Chinese populace.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75"><span>[</span>75<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
