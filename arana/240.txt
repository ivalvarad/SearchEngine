<p><i><b>Portal</b></i> is a series of first person shooter/puzzle video games developed by <a href="/wiki/Valve_Corporation" title="Valve Corporation">Valve Corporation</a>. Set in the <i><a href="/wiki/Half-Life_(series)" title="Half-Life (series)">Half-Life</a></i> universe, the two games in the series, <i><a href="/wiki/Portal_(video_game)" title="Portal (video game)">Portal</a></i> (2007) and <i><a href="/wiki/Portal_2" title="Portal 2">Portal 2</a></i> (2011), center on a human woman, <a href="/wiki/Chell_(Portal)" title="Chell (Portal)">Chell</a>, forced to undergo a series of tests within the Aperture Science Enrichment Center by a malicious <a href="/wiki/Artificial_intelligence" title="Artificial intelligence">artificial intelligence</a> computer, <a href="/wiki/GLaDOS" title="GLaDOS">GLaDOS</a>, that controls the facility. Each test involves using the "Aperture Science Handheld Portal Device" - the "portal gun" - that creates a human-sized wormhole-like connection between nearly any two flat surfaces. Not only can the player-character or objects in the game world move through portals, their momentum is conserved. This allows complex "flinging" maneuvers to be used to cross wide gaps or perform other feats to reach the exit for each test chamber. A number of other mechanics, such as lasers, light bridges, tractor funnels, and turrets, exist to aid or hinder the player's goal to reach the exit.</p>
<p>The <i>Portal</i> games are noted for bringing students and their projects from the <a href="/wiki/DigiPen_Institute_of_Technology" title="DigiPen Institute of Technology">DigiPen Institute of Technology</a> into Valve and extend their ideas into the full games. The portal concept was introduced by the game <i><a href="/wiki/Narbacular_Drop" title="Narbacular Drop">Narbacular Drop</a></i> and led to the basis for the first game. Another game, <i><a href="/wiki/Aperture_Tag" title="Aperture Tag">Tag: The Power of Paint</a></i>, formed the basis of surface-altering "gels" introduced in <i>Portal 2</i>.</p>
<p>Both games have received near-universal praise, and have sold millions of copies. The first game was released as part of a three-game compilation, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Orange_Box" title="The Orange Box">The Orange Box</a></i>, and though intended as a short bonus feature of the compilation, was instead considered the highlight of the three. Its success led to the creation of the much longer <i>Portal 2</i>, which included both single player and cooperative player modes; it too received mostly positive critical reviews. In addition to the challenging puzzle elements, both games are praised for its <a href="/wiki/Black_comedy" title="Black comedy">dark humor</a>, written by <a href="/wiki/Erik_Wolpaw" title="Erik Wolpaw">Erik Wolpaw</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chet_Faliszek" title="Chet Faliszek">Chet Faliszek</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jay_Pinkerton" title="Jay Pinkerton">Jay Pinkerton</a>, voice work by actors <a href="/wiki/Ellen_McLain" title="Ellen McLain">Ellen McLain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Merchant" title="Stephen Merchant">Stephen Merchant</a>, and <a href="/wiki/J.K._Simmons" title="J.K. Simmons" class="mw-redirect">J.K. Simmons</a>, and musical songs by <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Coulton" title="Jonathan Coulton">Jonathan Coulton</a>. A number of spin-off media have been developed alongside the games, and several of the game elements have become parts of <a href="/wiki/Internet_meme" title="Internet meme">Internet memes</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Setting_and_characters">Setting and characters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Portal_(series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Setting and characters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Characters_in_the_Portal_series" title="Characters in the Portal series" class="mw-redirect">Characters in the Portal series</a></div>

<p>Both <i>Portal</i> games take place in the fictional "Aperture Science Enrichment Center". Aperture Science was founded by <a href="/wiki/Cave_Johnson_(Portal)" title="Cave Johnson (Portal)">Cave Johnson</a> (voiced by <a href="/wiki/J.K._Simmons" title="J.K. Simmons" class="mw-redirect">J.K. Simmons</a>) and originally sought to make shower curtains for the military. Its research happened upon the discovery of portal technology, and soon became a direct competitor with <a href="/wiki/Black_Mesa_Research_Facility" title="Black Mesa Research Facility" class="mw-redirect">Black Mesa Research Facility</a> (from the <i><a href="/wiki/Half-Life_(series)" title="Half-Life (series)">Half-Life</a></i> series) for government funding. Johnson acquired the rights to a disused salt mine in the Upper Peninsula of Michigan, where they started building a labyrinth set of offices, laboratories, facilities, and test chambers. During this time, Johnson became poisoned from exposure to moon dust, a key component of the paint needed for the portal technology, and became increasingly deranged.</p>
<p>In <i>Portal 2</i>, the player explores these long-abandoned areas of Aperture, learning that the company had moved from testing on the country's finest, to paid volunteers, and ultimately to forcing its own employees to participate in testing. Near the point of his death, Johnson ordered his lifelong assistant Caroline (voiced by <a href="/wiki/Ellen_McLain" title="Ellen McLain">Ellen McLain</a>) to be the first test subject for a mind-to-computer transfer; her personality would ultimately form the core of <a href="/wiki/GLaDOS" title="GLaDOS">GLaDOS</a> (also McLain). Sometime after Johnson's death, the old sections of the facility were vitrified, and a more modern facility built atop those ruins. GLaDOS was built to control the facility and monitor the tests, but researchers found that the computer had villainous tendencies, threatening to kill the entire staff before it was shut down in time. The Aperture researches constructed a number of "personality cores" that would fit onto GLaDOS to prevent her from turning against them. Despite this, on the day she was officially activated (coincidentally on "<a href="/wiki/Take_Your_Daughter_to_Work_Day" title="Take Your Daughter to Work Day" class="mw-redirect">Take Your Daughter to Work Day</a>"), she turned against the researchers and killed nearly everyone in the facility with lethal doses of neurotoxin gas. In the games and the comic <i>Lab Rat</i>, one employee Doug Rattmann survived due to his schizophrenia and distrust of GLaDOS. In trying to find a way to defeat GLaDOS, he finds that <a href="/wiki/Chell_(Portal)" title="Chell (Portal)">Chell</a>, one of the human subjects kept in cryogenic storage within Aperture, has a high level of tenacity, and arranges for the events of <i>Portal</i> to occur by moving her to the top of GLaDOS' testing list. GLaDOS remain driven to test human subjects despite the lack of humans.</p>

<p>The player is introduced to Aperture in <i>Portal</i>, which is said by Valve to be set sometime between the events of <i><a href="/wiki/Half-Life_(video_game)" title="Half-Life (video game)">Half-Life</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Half-Life_2" title="Half-Life 2">Half-Life 2</a></i>. The player-character Chell is awakened by GLaDOS for testing. Chell resists GLaDOS' lies and verbal ploys and succeeds to defeat GLaDOS' core, the destruction creating a portal implosion that sends Chell to the surface, unconscious. Rattmann, who has helped Chell by writing warning messages and directions to maintenance areas on the facility walls and had observed the final battle, escapes Aperture, but on witnessing a robot dragging Chell's body back inside, sacrifices his escape to assure that Chell is put into indefinite cryogenic storage. He himself is critically wounded but appears to make it to another cryogenic chamber, though his ultimate fate is not revealed.</p>
<p><i>Portal 2</i> takes place numerous years after the events of the first game; the Aperture facility has fallen into disrepair without GLaDOS. A personality core named Wheatley (<a href="/wiki/Stephen_Merchant" title="Stephen Merchant">Stephen Merchant</a>) wakes Chell from her sleep to help her stop a reactor failure, but inadvertently awaken GLaDOS, who had redundantly backed up her personality. Though they defeat GLaDOS by putting Wheatley in her place, Wheatley is overwhelmed with power, sending Chell and GLaDOS, temporarily reduced to a small computer powered by a potato, to the old core of Aperture, where GLaDOS rediscovers her relation to Caroline. They return to the surface where they are forced to defeat Wheatley before his ineptitude with the Aperture systems causes the facility reactors to become critical and explode. GLaDOS is returned to her rightful place and returns the facility to normal. GLaDOS then lets Chell go, realizing that the prospect of trying to kill her is too much trouble. Instead, she turns to two robots of her own creation, Atlas and P-Body, to locate a mythical store of additional human subjects kept in cryogenic sleep for her to continue testing on.</p>
<p>In addition to these characters, the game includes numerous laser-seeking turrets that seek to kill the player-characters, though are apologetic for it; most are voiced by McLain, though some defective ones in the sequel are voiced by <a href="/wiki/Nolan_North" title="Nolan North">Nolan North</a>. GLaDOS introduces Chell to the "Weighted Companion Cube", appearing similar to other Weighed Cubes (crates) in the game, but decorated with hearts on its sides; GLaDOS attempts to make Chell believe the Companion Cube is a sentient object and a key to her survival, before making Chell dispose of it in an incinerator prior to leaving a test chamber. Both games feature other personality cores that were constructed to keep GLaDOS in check; the first game includes three cores, the Morality, Curiosity, and Intelligence cores, voiced by McLain as well as a snarling Anger core voiced by <a href="/wiki/Mike_Patton" title="Mike Patton">Mike Patton</a>. In <i>Portal 2</i>, three more such cores (beyond Wheatley) are introduced including the irrelevant Fact Core, the bold Adventure Core, and the space-obsessed Space Core, each voiced by North.</p>

<div class="thumb tmulti tnone center">

<p>The player controls the main character (Chell in the single player campaigns, or one of Atlas and P-Body in the cooperative mode) from a first-person view, running, jumping, and interacting with switches or other devices. The player-characters are able to withstand large drops, but can be killed by falling in the toxic water of the facility, crushed to death, passing through laser grids, or fired on repeatedly by turrets.</p>
<p>Both games are generally divided into a series of test chambers; other sections of the game are more exploratory areas that connect these chambers. Each chamber has an exit door that must be reached, often requiring that certain conditions have been met such as having weighed down a large button with a "Weighted Cube", effectively a crate. These puzzles require the use of the Aperture Science Handheld Portal Device, the portal gun. The gun can shoot two portals, colored differently for identification, on any flat surface that can accept a portal. Once both portal ends are placed, the player can walk the character between them, or carry objects with the portal gun through them. Portal ends can be re positioned as often as necessary, but certain actions, such as walking through "emancipation grills" or moving a surface with a portal will cause the portals to dissipate.</p>
<p>A critical feature of portals is that they retain the magnitude of momentum when an object travels through it; as stated by GLaDOS to the player in the first game, "Speedy thing goes in, speedy thing comes out". When portals are placed on non-parallel planes, this can create the effect of "flinging". Commonly, one uses gravity to build up their momentum when they fall into a portal, which flings them out of the other side to gain speed and distance that normal jumping and running could not generate. A leapfrogging effect can be used by placing portals in series during this flinging, gaining further momentum with each use.</p>
<p>Portals will also allow light and other objects to transfer through them, and numerous puzzles involve using portals to manipulate bouncing energy balls, lasers, "hard light" bridges, and tractor beams to access new locations or direct objects to specific receptacles that must be activated to open the level's exit. <i>Portal 2</i> introduces "mobility gels" that can paint surfaces, including turrets and cubes, that can also move through portals though not directly by the player. The gels can create a surface that repels the player (Repulsion Gel), increases the player's speed (Propulsion Gel), or allows the surface to accept portals (Conversion Gel).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Portal_(series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The concept of <i>Portal</i> was born out of a student project from the <a href="/wiki/DigiPen_Institute_of_Technology" title="DigiPen Institute of Technology">DigiPen Institute of Technology</a>, entitled <i><a href="/wiki/Narbacular_Drop" title="Narbacular Drop">Narbacular Drop</a></i>. The game included the aspects of placing portals on any flat surfaces and using them to maneuver around levels.<sup id="cite_ref-narbdrop-confirm_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-narbdrop-confirm-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-gilombardi_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gilombardi-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Valve's employees, attending a DigiPen career fair, saw their game and shortly later offered the entire team jobs at Valve to help expand on their idea.<sup id="cite_ref-seattletimes_portal_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-seattletimes_portal-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Valve originally saw <i>Portal</i> as an experimental game to be included with its upcoming compilation, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Orange_Box" title="The Orange Box">The Orange Box</a></i>, alongside its release of <i><a href="/wiki/Half-Life_2:_Episode_Two" title="Half-Life 2: Episode Two">Half-Life 2: Episode Two</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Team_Fortress_2" title="Team Fortress 2">Team Fortress 2</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-1up_beyond_the_box_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1up_beyond_the_box-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> To give the game character, a minimal story, tied loosely with the <i>Half-Life</i> world, was written by Valve's <a href="/wiki/Erik_Wolpaw" title="Erik Wolpaw">Erik Wolpaw</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marc_Laidlaw" title="Marc Laidlaw">Marc Laidlaw</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-gi_portal_hl_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gi_portal_hl-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> They needed a character to guide the player through the game, coming onto a polite but humorous artificial intelligence, which would ultimately become the character of <a href="/wiki/GLaDOS" title="GLaDOS">GLaDOS</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-gi_portal_hl_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gi_portal_hl-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><i>Portal</i>‍ '​s release with <i>The Orange Box</i> received near-universal praise, with the standalone game earning an aggregate <a href="/wiki/MetaCritic" title="MetaCritic" class="mw-redirect">MetaCritic</a> rating of 90 out of 100.<sup id="cite_ref-metacritic_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-metacritic-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> With success of the game, work on an expanded sequel began nearly immediately, expanding the development team from 8 to about 30-40 programmers.<sup id="cite_ref-gamasutra_wolpaw_interview_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gamasutra_wolpaw_interview-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> Initial ideas for <i>Portal 2</i> retained the idea of solving puzzles through scientific concepts, but eliminating the use of portals altogether; these versions did not fare well with test audiences nor with Gabe Newell, Valve's president; these ideas were dropped though saved for potential reuse in a different title by Valve.<sup id="cite_ref-gamasutra_wolpaw_behindthescenes_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gamasutra_wolpaw_behindthescenes-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>Portal 2</i> development was restarted specifically to keep the portal concept but adding new elements to freshen the gameplay.<sup id="cite_ref-kotaku_ipadapp_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kotaku_ipadapp-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-final_hours_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-final_hours-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>During this period, Valve had witnessed another student project out of Digipen, <i><a href="/wiki/Tag:_The_Power_of_Paint" title="Tag: The Power of Paint">Tag: The Power of Paint</a></i> which allows the player to spray paint onto surfaces to alter their behavior, and brought them into Valve, though not initially as part of the <i>Portal</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-gamasutra_wolpaw_interview_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gamasutra_wolpaw_interview-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> The <i>Tag</i> team had found a way to incorporate their paints with real-time fluid dynamics code previously made by Valve, and soon their concept for paints had become the "conversion gels" as part of <i>Portal 2</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-wsj_pax_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wsj_pax-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>To expand the title, Valve included a co-operative play mode, based on their own observations and stories from players about working out the solutions to <i>Portal</i>‍ '​s puzzles in a group environment.<sup id="cite_ref-gamasutra_wolpaw_interview_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gamasutra_wolpaw_interview-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-wsj_pax_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wsj_pax-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-guardian_e3_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-guardian_e3-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> With this feature, they sought the ability to enable cross-platform play of <i>Portal 2</i> between computers and consoles through Steamworks. This led to a surprise reveal by Newell that not only would <i>Portal 2</i> be on the <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_3" title="PlayStation 3">PlayStation 3</a> platform, after previously stating the difficulties in supporting this console, but that it would include support for cross-platform play between personal computers and PlayStation 3 players through a limited Steamworks interface.<sup id="cite_ref-final_hours_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-final_hours-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>With the larger title, Valve brought in writer <a href="/wiki/Jay_Pinkerton" title="Jay Pinkerton">Jay Pinkerton</a> formerly with <i><a href="/wiki/National_Lampoon_(magazine)" title="National Lampoon (magazine)">National Lampoon</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Left_4_Dead" title="Left 4 Dead">Left 4 Dead</a></i> writer <a href="/wiki/Chet_Faliszek" title="Chet Faliszek">Chet Faliszek</a> to assist Wolpaw with the larger story. They built on the character of the Aperture Science facility, providing a deeper story for GLaDOS and Aperture's CEO Cave Johnson, as well as developing several concepts for "personality cores" ultimately to the creation of the Wheatley core character.<sup id="cite_ref-gamasutra_wolpaw_behindthescenes_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gamasutra_wolpaw_behindthescenes-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-eurogamer_wolpaw_interview_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eurogamer_wolpaw_interview-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><i>Portal 2</i> was similarly released with high praise with a 95 out of 100 score on Metacritic.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> Valve has continued to support the game through the release of two separate <a href="/wiki/Downloadable_content" title="Downloadable content">downloadable content</a> packages, one introducing a new co-operative campaign,<sup id="cite_ref-fastcompany_portal2_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fastcompany_portal2-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-eurog_peer_review_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eurog_peer_review-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> and a second that incorporated an easy-to-learn level editor that allowed players to make their own test chambers and share these through the Steam Workshop to others.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> Further on this, Valve has created a special version of the <i>Portal</i> level editor to be used alongside its "Steam in Schools" program as a means of using the game and editor to teach students about physics, math, and other lessons; Valve released this version of <i>Portal 2</i> for free for educational use.<sup id="cite_ref-gamasutra_teachwithportals_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gamasutra_teachwithportals-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Games">Games</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Portal_(series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Games">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Portal"><i>Portal</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Portal_(series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Portal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Portal_(video_game)" title="Portal (video game)">Portal</a></div>
<p><i>Portal</i> was initially released in October 2007 as part of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Orange_Box" title="The Orange Box">The Orange Box</a></i>, a compilation of the <i><a href="/wiki/Half-Life_2" title="Half-Life 2">Half-Life 2</a></i> and its two episodes, <i><a href="/wiki/Team_Fortress_2" title="Team Fortress 2">Team Fortress 2</a></i>, and <i>Portal</i>. Valve had considered the inclusion of <i>Portal</i> as a bonus feature of the compilation; the game was purposely kept short such that if it did not meet expectations, players would have the rest of the content of <i>The Orange Box</i> as a "safety net".<sup id="cite_ref-gi_p2_reveal_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gi_p2_reveal-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>Portal</i> has since been repackaged on Microsoft Windows as a standalone game in April 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-joystiq_orange_split_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-joystiq_orange_split-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> A Mac OS X client was introduced simultaneously with the release of the Steam client for that platform in May 2010; as part of its promotion, the game was released free of charge for both platforms during which at least 1.5 million players downloaded the title.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Portal:_Still_Alive"><i>Portal: Still Alive</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Portal_(series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Portal: Still Alive">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Portal: Still Alive</i> was a standalone version of <i>Portal</i> with additional content for the <a href="/wiki/Xbox_Live_Arcade" title="Xbox Live Arcade">Xbox Live Arcade</a>, released in October 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> The game included new achievements, additional challenges from the existing test chambers, and additional non-story levels based on those found in the <a href="/wiki/Adobe_Flash" title="Adobe Flash">Flash</a>-based <i>Portal: The Flash Version</i> created by We Create Stuff.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Portal_2"><i>Portal 2</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Portal_(series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Portal 2">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Portal_2" title="Portal 2">Portal 2</a></div>
<p><i>Portal 2</i> was released as a standalone game in April 2011 on both computers and consoles.</p>

<p>To help develop the fictional history of Aperture Science, Valve created a digital comic to tell the story of the "Rat Man", a schizophrenic who is unseen in the games themselves but creates murals and scrawlings that guide Chell in both games.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> The comic, "Portal 2: Lab Rat", takes place both during and after <i>Portal</i>, explaining the events that led to <i>Portal 2</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-telegraph_interview_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-telegraph_interview-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> The Rat Man's artwork appears early in <i>Portal 2</i>, where it retells the plot of <i>Portal</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-ign_story_interview_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ign_story_interview-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Michael_Avon_Oeming" title="Michael Avon Oeming">Michael Avon Oeming</a>, who had worked on comics for Valve games <i><a href="/wiki/Team_Fortress_2" title="Team Fortress 2">Team Fortress 2</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Left_4_Dead_(series)" title="Left 4 Dead (series)">Left 4 Dead</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup> and Valve in-house artist Andrea Wicklund drew the comic. Ted Kosmatka wrote most of the story with input from the <i>Portal 2</i> writers.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup> The 27-page comic was made available online in two parts about two weeks before the game's release<sup id="cite_ref-labrat_part_1_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-labrat_part_1-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-labrat_part_2_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-labrat_part_2-35"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup> and was also bundled with the game itself. <a href="/wiki/Dark_Horse_Comics" title="Dark Horse Comics">Dark Horse Comics</a> has published "Portal 2: Lab Rat" in a printed anthology of Valve comics, <i>Valve Presents: The Sacrifice and Other Steam-Powered Stories</i>, in November 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In the comic, Doug Rattmann (also known as The Rat Man) is a scientist working in the Aperture facility. He escapes GLaDOS's initial neurotoxin attack, but suffers symptoms as his <a href="/wiki/Schizophrenia" title="Schizophrenia">schizophrenia</a> medication runs out, causing hallucinations of his Weighted Companion Cube talking. Noticing that Chell is uniquely tenacious among the test subjects held by Aperture, Rattmann moves her to the top of the queue of testing subjects, thus starting the events of the first <i>Portal</i>. After Chell defeats GLaDOS, Rattmann escapes Aperture, but returns against the Companion Cube's objections when he sees the Party Escort Bot dragging an unconscious Chell back inside and into a disabled cryo chamber. He ensures that Chell is kept in indefinite <a href="/wiki/Suspended_animation" title="Suspended animation">suspended animation</a>, but he is shot by a <a href="/wiki/Characters_in_the_Portal_series#Turrets" title="Characters in the Portal series" class="mw-redirect">turret</a> in the process. He then enters a stasis pod himself, leaving his fate afterward unknown.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Final_Hours_of_Portal_2">The Final Hours of Portal 2</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Portal_(series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: The Final Hours of Portal 2">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>The Final Hours of Portal 2</i> is a digital book written and created by <a href="/wiki/Geoff_Keighley" title="Geoff Keighley">Geoff Keighley</a>. This digital book gives insight on the creation of <i>Portal 2</i>. Keighley had previously worked as an editor at <a href="/wiki/GameSpot" title="GameSpot">GameSpot</a>, writing several 10,000-word "Final Hours" pieces on various games where he visited the studios during the late development phases to document the creation of the game. One piece, "The Final Hours of Half-Life 2", allowed Keighley to interact with Valve during 2003 and 2004 and talk with the staff as they completed work on <i>Half-Life 2</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup> Keighley wanted to recreate a similar work for <i>Portal 2</i>, with focus on making it an interactive work for the <a href="/wiki/IPad" title="IPad">iPad</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-finalhours_usatoday_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-finalhours_usatoday-39"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup> Keighley was granted "fly on the wall" access to Valve when <i>Portal 2</i> was being produced.<sup id="cite_ref-finalhours_pcgamer_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-finalhours_pcgamer-41"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup> The initial iPad release was written by Keighley with work by Joe Zeff Design, a studio that had also produced digital applications for <i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">Time</a></i> magazine.<sup id="cite_ref-finalhours_usatoday_39-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-finalhours_usatoday-39"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup> The interactive work provides movie clips and short applications to demonstrate the various mechanics of the game and stages of the game's development. The work was later ported into a non-interactive eBook, and into an application with the same iPad interactivity on the Steam platform.<sup id="cite_ref-finalhours_pcgamer_41-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-finalhours_pcgamer-41"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup> With the iPad and Steam version, Keighley is able to offer live updates to the work; upon release of the "Peer Review" downloadable content pack, the work was updated with an additional chapter discussing the creation of the new content and what new features players could expect in the future from <i>Portal 2</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-kotaku_keighley_editor_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kotaku_keighley_editor-42"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Potato_Sack">Potato Sack</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Portal_(series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Potato Sack">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Potato_Sack" title="Potato Sack">Potato Sack</a> was an <a href="/wiki/Alternate_reality_game" title="Alternate reality game">alternate reality game</a> conceived by Valve and 13 <a href="/wiki/Independent_video_game_development" title="Independent video game development">indie video game developers</a> as a prelude to the release of <i>Portal 2</i>. <i>Portal 2</i> had been announced by a similar game, where a patch applied to the Steam version of <i>Portal</i> in March 2010, provided clues heralding the official announcement. The Potato Sack game, launched on April 1, 2011, led to the reveal of "GLaDOS@home", a spoof of distributed computer challenges, to get players to cooperate on playing the independent games as to unlock <i>Portal 2</i> on Steam about 10 hours before its planned release.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Portal:_Uncooperative_Cake_Acquisition_Game"><i>Portal: Uncooperative Cake Acquisition Game</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Portal_(series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Portal: Uncooperative Cake Acquisition Game">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Board_game" title="Board game">board game</a> version of <i>Portal</i> is currently being developed by <a href="/wiki/Cryptozoic_Entertainment" title="Cryptozoic Entertainment">Cryptozoic Entertainment</a> with oversight from Valve. Tentatively titled <i>Portal: Uncooperative Cake Acquisition Game</i>, the game was slated for release in October 2014. The <i>Uncooperative Cake Acquisition Game</i> is based on players manipulating their tokens - which are representative of unwitting test subjects - through various test chambers in the Aperture Laboratories. The goal being to test the most lucrative chambers while attempting to stall the progress of other players. Valve had approached Cryptozoic with the core concepts of the board game, which the publisher found only needed small modifications in gameplay for the purpose of balance.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><br /></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Portal_in_education">Portal in education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Portal_(series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Portal in education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <i>Portal</i> games have found application in educational aspects outside of game development. The first game was praised as an example of <a href="/wiki/Instructional_scaffolding" title="Instructional scaffolding">instructional scaffolding</a> where the student is first given an environment to learn new tools with sufficient hand-holding, but these facets are slowly removed as the student proceeds.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52"><span>[</span>52<span>]</span></a></sup> At least one college, <a href="/wiki/Wabash_College" title="Wabash College">Wabash College</a>, introduced <i>Portal</i> as part of required coursework; at Wabash, the game is used as an example of <a href="/wiki/Erving_Goffman" title="Erving Goffman">Erving Goffman</a>'s dissemination on <a href="/wiki/Dramaturgy_(sociology)" title="Dramaturgy (sociology)">dramaturgy</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Presentation_of_Self_in_Everyday_Life" title="The Presentation of Self in Everyday Life">The Presentation of Self in Everyday Life</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53"><span>[</span>53<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54"><span>[</span>54<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55"><span>[</span>55<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>At a mid-2011 presentation at the 2011 <a href="/wiki/Games_for_Change" title="Games for Change">Games for Change</a> Festival at <a href="/wiki/New_York_University" title="New York University">New York University</a>, Gabe Newell stated Valve's intention to direct <i>Portal</i> and <i>Portal 2</i> towards education. Newell stated that Valve "doesn’t see divide between making a game that can do well and be educational", and was already working with schools to develop lesson plans around the game.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56"><span>[</span>56<span>]</span></a></sup> In one example, Valve brought in students from nearby <a href="/wiki/The_Evergreen_School" title="The Evergreen School">Evergreen School</a> to watch them interact with the game in an educational setting.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57"><span>[</span>57<span>]</span></a></sup> As part of this effect, the company promoted <i>Portal</i> for free use by any user during September 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58"><span>[</span>58<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In speaking at the 2012 Games for Change Festival, Newell said that the response to these efforts was praised by educators.<sup id="cite_ref-wired_schools_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wired_schools-59"><span>[</span>59<span>]</span></a></sup> Their efforts culminated in a "Teach with Portals" program that Newell announced at the Festival. The effort is built on a standalone "Puzzle Maker" that incorporates the level editor for <i>Portal 2</i> that was released as free content for the game in early 2012. Valve had built the Puzzle Maker with the aid of educators, as to make it suitable for lesson plans as well as making it as easy for teachers to use to construct such plans. The Puzzle Maker is not limited to physics, but designed to be modular so that other fields, such as fundamental electronics or chemistry, could be included.<sup id="cite_ref-wired_schools_59-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wired_schools-59"><span>[</span>59<span>]</span></a></sup> The "Teach with Portals" initiative is built atop of a stripped-down version of the Steam client, "Steam for Schools", that is designed to be used in schools, allowing instructors to control the installation of the games and lesson plans on the students' computers. These tools, as well as copies of <i>Portal 2</i> and the Puzzle Maker, are being offered for free for all educators.<sup id="cite_ref-wired_schools_59-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wired_schools-59"><span>[</span>59<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Portal_in_popular_culture">Portal in popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Portal_(series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Portal in popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Several elements of the <i>Portal</i> series have entered the popular culture, typically as bases for Internet memes.</p>
<ul>
<li>During <i>Portal</i>, the player explores areas outside of the test chambers where scrawled messages left by Rattmann and others warn of GLaDOS' deception. In particular, while GLaDOS promises that Chell will receive cake for completing the training courses, the messages alert that this reward does not exist, and that "the cake is a lie". The phrase "the cake is a lie" became an Internet meme, leading to numerous cake-related jokes, as well as its adaption as a term relating to a false promise. When writing <i>Portal 2</i>, Wolpaw stated that they were so sick of cake jokes that they purposely avoided any reference to that, save for one subtle nod.</li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li>In one <i>Portal</i> level, the player is given an inanimate weighted cube with hearts on its side, called the "Weighted Companion Cube" by GLaDOS. GLaDOS proceeds to taunt the player by asserting that the Cube is alive, that it is Chell's friend, and otherwise attempting to form an attraction to it. The player is required to use the Weighted Companion Cube to complete the level, but to exit the level, the player must then drop the Cube into an incinerator. The Weighted Companion Cube became a popular items to include on <i>Portal</i>-themed merchandise.</li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li><i>Portal</i>‍ '​s credit sequence plays over the song "<a href="/wiki/Still_Alive" title="Still Alive">Still Alive</a>", composed by <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Coulton" title="Jonathan Coulton">Jonathan Coulton</a>, and, in its original form, song by Ellen McLain in the GLaDOS voice. The song, sung from GLaDOS's point of view as considering her defeat as "a success", contributed to the positive response of the game as well as highlight Coulton's work to players, and leading to Valve bringing back Coulton to record a credits song for <i>Portal 2</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60"><span>[</span>60<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li><i>Portal</i> is referenced by <a href="/wiki/Glenn_Rhee" title="Glenn Rhee">Glenn</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Walking_Dead_(TV_series)" title="The Walking Dead (TV series)">The Walking Dead</a></i> episode "<a href="/wiki/Pretty_Much_Dead_Already" title="Pretty Much Dead Already">Pretty Much Dead Already</a>".</li>
</ul>
<p>In considering the popularity of the cake meme from <i>Portal</i>, Wolpaw and the team set up to write <i>Portal 2</i> without intentionally aiming to include any meme-worthy material in the game. Despite this, several elements have been reused in humor across the Internet.</p>
<ul>
<li>When the player completes the final test chamber in the underground Aperture facility, a pre-recorded message from CEO Cave Johnson plays. In it, Cave Johnson, having been lethally poisoned with moon dust, gives a speech about the popular saying "When life gives you lemons, make lemonade." Here, he trash talks the analogy, saying the proper response to "life giving lemons" is to invent combustible lemons, and use them to burn life's house down.</li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li>One of the personality cores introduced in the end game is obsessed with getting into outer space, and does end up there after the finale, shouting annoyingly, "I'm in space!" among other lines. This "Space Core" has become its own meme, and furthering it, Valve prepared a special bit of downloadable content for the game <i><a href="/wiki/The_Elder_Scrolls_V:_Skyrim" title="The Elder Scrolls V: Skyrim">The Elder Scrolls V: Skyrim</a></i>, where the player can find and collect the Space Core in their inventory. The Space Core's name itself is a pun on the term 'space corps', and might also be interpreted as 'astronaut corps'.</li>
</ul>
