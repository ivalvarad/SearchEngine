<p><i><b>Melipona beecheii</b></i> (Bennett, 1831) is a species of <a href="/wiki/Eusocial" title="Eusocial" class="mw-redirect">eusocial</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stingless_bee" title="Stingless bee">stingless bees</a> of the order <a href="/wiki/Hymenoptera" title="Hymenoptera">Hymenoptera</a> and the genus <a href="/wiki/Melipona" title="Melipona">Melipona</a>. It is native to Central America from the <a href="/wiki/Yucat%C3%A1n_Peninsula" title="Yucatán Peninsula">Yucatán Peninsula</a> in the north to <a href="/wiki/Costa_Rica" title="Costa Rica">Costa Rica</a> in the south.<sup id="cite_ref-de_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-de-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>M. beecheii</i> was cultivated in the <a href="/wiki/Yucat%C3%A1n_Peninsula" title="Yucatán Peninsula">Yucatán Peninsula</a> starting in the <a href="/wiki/Pre-Colombian_era" title="Pre-Colombian era" class="mw-redirect">pre-Colombian era</a> by the ancient <a href="/wiki/Maya_civilization" title="Maya civilization">Maya civilization</a>. The Mayan name for <i>M. beecheii</i> is "<i>xunan kab</i>," which translates roughly to "regal lady bee."<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>M. beecheii</i> once served as the subject of various Mayan religious ceremonies.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_phylogeny">Taxonomy and phylogeny</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Melipona_beecheii&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy and phylogeny">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Melipona beecheii</i> is a member of the family <a href="/wiki/Apidae" title="Apidae">Apidae</a> of eusocial bees within the order <a href="/wiki/Hymenoptera" title="Hymenoptera">Hymenoptera</a>. The subfamily <a href="/wiki/Meliponini" title="Meliponini" class="mw-redirect">Meliponini</a> is commonly referred to as "stingless bees." The genus <i>Melipona</i> contains nearly 50 other species.<sup id="cite_ref-Ramirez_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ramirez-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description_and_identification">Description and identification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Melipona_beecheii&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description and identification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>M. beecheii</i> has a golden-yellowish and brown striped body with translucent wings attached to the back. It has a rounded body composed of a head, <a href="/wiki/Thorax" title="Thorax">thorax</a>, and abdomen, none of which are segmented, but rather are separated more subtly. Antennae protrude from the head and stick straight out. <i>M. beecheii</i> has small, white hairs covering the head, thorax, and abdomen.<sup id="cite_ref-Biesmeijer_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Biesmeijer-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Queens, workers, and <a href="/wiki/Drone_(bee)" title="Drone (bee)">drones</a> are all roughly the same size. Moreover, all members of the hive regardless of their future role develop in identical, mass-provisioned, sealed cells. This allows for self-determination of roles, the root of caste conflict within <i>M. beecheii</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-colony_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-colony-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Melipona_beecheii&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>M. beecheii</i> is a tropical <a href="/wiki/Eusocial" title="Eusocial" class="mw-redirect">eusocial</a> wasp that has been observed in a variety of geographic locations with tropical climates.<sup id="cite_ref-Villanueva_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Villanueva-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>M. beecheii</i> can be definitively found in Central America, especially in the southern <a href="/wiki/Yucat%C3%A1n_Peninsula" title="Yucatán Peninsula">Yucatán Peninsula</a> of <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guatemala" title="Guatemala">Guatemala</a>, <a href="/wiki/El_Salvador" title="El Salvador">El Salvador</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nicaragua" title="Nicaragua">Nicaragua</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Costa_Rica" title="Costa Rica">Costa Rica</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Francoy_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Francoy-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>M. beecheii</i> builds its nests inside hollow cavities of trees and, as a result, is especially prone to deforestation.<sup id="cite_ref-nest_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nest-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation_status">Conservation status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Melipona_beecheii&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Conservation status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>M. beecheii</i> has been observed primarily in <a href="/wiki/Tropical_rain_forests" title="Tropical rain forests" class="mw-redirect">tropical rain forests</a>, both humid and arid.<sup id="cite_ref-Biesmeijer_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Biesmeijer-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> There is much justified concern surrounding the conservation status of <i>M. beecheii</i>. Because the species nests in trees, deforestation of its natural habitat has been very detrimental to the presence of the species. Moreover, there is an increasing use of insecticides in the forest and a decrease of interest in the ancient Mayan practice of beekeeping. As a result, beekeepers who work with <i>M. beecheii</i> from the Mayan zone in Quintana Roo state, Mexico have found a 93% decrease in hives throughout the past twenty five years.<sup id="cite_ref-Villanueva_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Villanueva-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Colony_cycle">Colony cycle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Melipona_beecheii&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Colony cycle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>M. beecheii</i> is unique in that all females, even those that are designated to be workers, have the ability to develop as queens. As a result of this ability to self-determine, females may try to develop as queens in order to gain greater reproductive opportunities. New colonies are established when the previous queen dies and a new queen is selected. However, as a result of self-determination, an overproduction of queens can be expected. To combat this overproduction, workers kill extra queens by biting off the heads, abdomens, and other limbs of other potential queens. The average life expectancy of developing queens is 47 hours.<sup id="cite_ref-colony_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-colony-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Advantages to having many excess queens include the provision of spare queens in case of queen failure, provision of stock from which to pick the best queens, and a continuous stock of queens handy.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behavior">Behavior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Melipona_beecheii&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Behavior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Division_of_labor">Division of labor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Melipona_beecheii&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Division of labor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>M. beecheii</i> exhibits <a href="/wiki/Foraging" title="Foraging">foraging</a> specialization within the community. Single-foraging organisms are responsible for harvesting one single commodity, such as <a href="/wiki/Pollen" title="Pollen">pollen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nectar" title="Nectar">nectar</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Resin" title="Resin">resin</a>, in a single day while multi-foraging organisms will forage for two or three of those resources. About half of the community members are single-foraging and the other half are multi-foraging. A switch in foraging specialization occurs very rapidly, often within a single day.</p>
<p>It was found that no one style was advantageous over the other in terms of feeding and resource acquisition opportunities. However, activity patterns and longevity differed significantly between groups. Individuals responsible for foraging nectar were observed to be active all day and died, on average, after three full days of foraging. On the other hand, pollen foragers were only observed to be active for about 1–3 hours per day and lived for an average of 12 days. In spite of this contrast, it was found that both styles of foraging resulted in an equal amount of flights in a lifetime. The way in which individual <i>M. beecheii</i> organisms' foraging style is designated within the community is still unknown.<sup id="cite_ref-forage_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-forage-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dominance_heirarchy">Dominance heirarchy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Melipona_beecheii&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Dominance heirarchy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Caste determination in <i>M. beecheii</i> can be either trophogenic or genetic. Trophogenic caste determination dictates that an organism's caste is dictated by the quality and quantity of food it received as a larva. Genetic caste determination dictates that an organism's caste is inherent in its genetics. Trophogenic caste determination is much more common, genetic caste determination is quite rare. In other words, in most cases, a community member's caste and role in society are determined by the amount of food it was provided when it was larva.<sup id="cite_ref-colony_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-colony-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reproduction">Reproduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Melipona_beecheii&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Reproduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Similar to most beehives, M. beecheii has only one fertile queen and numerous worker bees. The queen that is fertile and lays eggs is known as the physogastric queen. However, an aspect that is more specific to M. beecheii is the reproduction of many virgin queens, as opposed to the physogastric queen. These virgin queens develop in excess and are almost always are killed by worker bees before they are able to lay eggs. Thus, the physogastric queen is the only member of the community that is able to lay fertile eggs.<sup id="cite_ref-reproduce_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reproduce-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pre-discharge_phase">Pre-discharge phase</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Melipona_beecheii&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Pre-discharge phase">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The physogastric queen’s reproduction begins with the workers. The workers arrange the cells and feed each cell only once the queen performs oviposition, the process of depositing eggs into a medium.<sup id="cite_ref-repro_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-repro-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> Once the queen arrives at a cell, the pre-discharge phase begins by a worker inserting themselves into a cell.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Discharge_phase">Discharge phase</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Melipona_beecheii&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Discharge phase">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Once the pre-discharge phase has taken place, the discharge phase begins with the queen tapping the worker with her antennae and forelegs. This contact between individuals serves to signal the worker to inserting larval food into the cell. After the discharge phase, the queen checks the cells and eats the trophic egg, an unfertilized egg that is made specifically for the queen’s nutrition.<sup id="cite_ref-repro_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-repro-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Abiotic_factors">Abiotic factors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Melipona_beecheii&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Abiotic factors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>M. beecheii</i> reproduction depends on many abiotic factors, including the time of year, climate, and resources available. Extreme reproduction was observed at the end of the rainy season wherein an excess of resources were able to be stored. In contrast, due to poor resource obtainment, <i>M. beecheii</i> populations decreased when there were good foraging conditions and food storage had built up.<sup id="cite_ref-repro_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-repro-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> When foraging conditions are good, less energy is expended on reproduction.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Interaction_with_other_species">Interaction with other species</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Melipona_beecheii&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Interaction with other species">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diet">Diet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Melipona_beecheii&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Diet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>M. beecheii</i>, like most other bee species, consumes nectar and pollen. <i>M. beecheii</i> exhibits foraging specialization within the community.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Predators">Predators</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Melipona_beecheii&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Predators">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The typical predators of <i>M. beecheii</i> are many different species of birds, spiders, lizards, and other bugs.<sup id="cite_ref-forage_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-forage-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>M. beecheii</i> is an easier target because it is unable to sting as a form of defense.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Parasites">Parasites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Melipona_beecheii&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Parasites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>M. beecheii</i> is parasitized primarily by the parasitic phorid fly, <i>Pseudohypocera kerteszi</i>. Other than this organism, there are not many parasites or disease-causing organisms that affect <i>M. beecheii</i>. In general, American foulbrood, which is caused by <i>Paenibacillus</i> larvae, is a harmful disease for all honey-producing bees, but there is not much data regarding its specific effect on <i>M. beecheii</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-disease_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-disease-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mutualism">Mutualism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Melipona_beecheii&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Mutualism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>M. beecheii</i> often has mutualisms with flowering plants. <i>M. beecheii</i> pollinates the flowers by carrying both nectar and pollen between plants, allowing them to collect food for themselves and their colony in the process. While it is not yet clear which specific flowering plants <i>M. beecheii</i> forms mutualistic relationships with, the patterns and habits have been studied so a mutualistic relationship has been confirmed.<sup id="cite_ref-forage_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-forage-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Competitors">Competitors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Melipona_beecheii&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Competitors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>M. beecheii'</i>s main competitors are other pollinating insects. For <i>M. beecheii</i> in its tropical environment, its other pollinating, stingless bee competitors include <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Melipona_fasciata&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Melipona fasciata (page does not exist)">Melipona fasciata</a></i> and other nests of <i>M. beecheii</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-compet_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-compet-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Defense">Defense</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Melipona_beecheii&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Defense">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Though <i>M. beecheii</i> is a stingless bee, it has the ability to bite other organisms when it feels threatened or is under attack. When in a communal setting, <i>M. beecheii</i> coordinates attack signals via secretions from their mandibular glands. The main pheromone involved in eliciting a communal attack response in <i>M. beecheii</i> is farnesyl acetate. When secreted, all individuals in the nest are stimulated to attack.<sup id="cite_ref-mandib_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mandib-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Human_importance">Human importance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Melipona_beecheii&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Human importance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Beekeepers in the Yucatán peninsula have harvested honey from the nests of <i>M. beecheii</i> until <a href="/wiki/African_bee" title="African bee">African honeybee</a>s arrived. As a result, <i>M. beecheii</i> is facing loss of habitat and the ancient tradition of stingless beekeeping is on the verge of dying out.<sup id="cite_ref-Villanueva_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Villanueva-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Agriculture">Agriculture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Melipona_beecheii&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Agriculture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Because <i>M. beecheii</i> is a stingless bee and eats nectar, its <a href="/wiki/Ecological_niche" title="Ecological niche">ecological niche</a> does not involve hunting pest insects. However, <i>M. beecheii</i> plays an extremely important role in honey production, as they have been kept for centuries almost exclusively in log nests primarily by people who live in the Yucatán Peninsula of Mexico. <i>M. beecheii</i> is an important source of honey both in the region and around the world. Due to the concern that stingless bee keeping is going extinct, the possibility of negative effects resulting from the impact of competition from feral African Apis mellifera, over-harvesting, failure to transfer colonies to hives or divide them, deforestation, hurricane damage and lack of instruction and incentive for new stingless bee keepers has been postulated and explored.<sup id="cite_ref-Villanueva_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Villanueva-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stings">Stings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Melipona_beecheii&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Stings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>M. beecheii</i> is a stingless bee and, as a result, cannot sting humans or other organisms. This characteristic makes them ideal for <a href="/wiki/Beekeeping" title="Beekeeping">beekeeping</a>. That being said, when agitated, <i>M. beecheii</i> does have the capacity to bite other organisms. However, there is no medical hazard or allergy factor involved.<sup id="cite_ref-Villanueva_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Villanueva-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="History_and_art">History and art</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Melipona_beecheii&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: History and art">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In accordance with Mayan history, <i>M. beecheii</i> are the embodiment of a link to the spirit world, an endowment of the god <a href="/wiki/Ah-Muzen-Cab" title="Ah-Muzen-Cab">Ah Muzen Cab</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>M. beecheii</i> once served as the subject of various Mayan religious ceremonies.<sup id="cite_ref-Villanueva_1-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Villanueva-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>M. beecheii</i> has been greatly revered by Mayan culture throughout history, leading to its keeping by the Mayan people as a means of attaining a higher-being, one which is closer to God. According to the Maya tradition, a priest would harvest <i>M. beecheii</i> honey during a religious ceremony that would take place twice a year. As a means of increasing the number of nests and honey production, beekeepers would regularly divide existing nests. This would also help to alleviate the predicament of excess queens that occurs with <i>M. beecheii</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Villanueva_1-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Villanueva-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
