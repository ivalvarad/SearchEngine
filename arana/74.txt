			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Douglas MacArthur's escape from the Philippines</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

<p><b>The escape of <a href="/wiki/Douglas_MacArthur" title="Douglas MacArthur">Douglas MacArthur</a> from the Philippines</b> began on 11 March 1942, during World War II, when he left <a href="/wiki/Corregidor_Island" title="Corregidor Island" class="mw-redirect">Corregidor Island</a> in a <a href="/wiki/PT_boat" title="PT boat">PT boat</a>. After travelling for two days through stormy seas patrolled by Japanese warships, he reached <a href="/wiki/Mindanao" title="Mindanao">Mindanao</a>. From there, MacArthur and his party flew to Australia, ultimately arriving in <a href="/wiki/Melbourne" title="Melbourne">Melbourne</a> on 21 March. This was the occasion of his famous speech in which he declared, "I came through and I shall return."</p>
<p>Douglas MacArthur was a well-known and experienced officer with a distinguished record in World War I, who had retired from the United States Army in 1937 and had become a defense advisor to the Philippine government. He was recalled to active duty with the United States Army in July 1941, a few months before the outbreak of the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_War" title="Pacific War">Pacific War</a> between the United States and the <a href="/wiki/Empire_of_Japan" title="Empire of Japan">Empire of Japan</a>. By March 1942, the <a href="/wiki/Philippines_Campaign_(1941%E2%80%931942)" title="Philippines Campaign (1941–1942)" class="mw-redirect">Japanese invasion of the Philippines</a> had compelled MacArthur to withdraw his forces on <a href="/wiki/Luzon" title="Luzon">Luzon</a> to <a href="/wiki/Bataan" title="Bataan">Bataan</a>, while his headquarters and his family moved to Corregidor Island. The doomed defense of Bataan captured the imagination of the American public, and MacArthur became a living symbol of Allied resistance to the Japanese.</p>
<p>Fearing that Corregidor would fall, and MacArthur would be taken prisoner, President <a href="/wiki/Franklin_D._Roosevelt" title="Franklin D. Roosevelt">Franklin D. Roosevelt</a> ordered MacArthur to go to Australia. A submarine was made available, but MacArthur elected to break through the Japanese blockade in PT boats. He set out after sunset on 11 March, and after two days of being bounced about in rough seas, and nearly being spotted by a Japanese warship, he reached Cagayan on Mindanao. From there, MacArthur and his party flew to Australia from <a href="/wiki/Del_Monte_Field" title="Del Monte Field" class="mw-redirect">Del Monte Field</a> in a pair of <a href="/wiki/Boeing_B-17_Flying_Fortress" title="Boeing B-17 Flying Fortress">Boeing B-17 Flying Fortresses</a>. The staff he brought with him, who became known as the "Bataan Gang", would become the nucleus of his General Headquarters (GHQ) <a href="/wiki/South_West_Pacific_Area_(command)" title="South West Pacific Area (command)">Southwest Pacific Area</a> (SWPA).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Douglas_MacArthur%27s_escape_from_the_Philippines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Douglas_MacArthur" title="Douglas MacArthur">Douglas MacArthur</a> was a well-known and experienced officer. The son of <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant_General_(United_States)" title="Lieutenant General (United States)" class="mw-redirect">Lieutenant General</a> <a href="/wiki/Arthur_MacArthur,_Jr." title="Arthur MacArthur, Jr.">Arthur MacArthur, Jr.</a>, who was awarded the <a href="/wiki/Medal_of_Honor" title="Medal of Honor">Medal of Honor</a> for his services in the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a>, MacArthur had graduated at the top of the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Military_Academy" title="U.S. Military Academy" class="mw-redirect">U.S. Military Academy</a> class of 1903.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMorton195318.E2.80.9319_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMorton195318.E2.80.9319-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> He was an <a href="/wiki/Aide-de-camp" title="Aide-de-camp">aide-de-camp</a> to his father from 1905 to 1906, and to President <a href="/wiki/Theodore_Roosevelt" title="Theodore Roosevelt">Theodore Roosevelt</a> from 1906 to 1907.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEManchester197866.E2.80.9369_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEManchester197866.E2.80.9369-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> During World War I he commanded the 84th Brigade of the <a href="/wiki/42nd_Infantry_Division_(United_States)" title="42nd Infantry Division (United States)">42nd (Rainbow) Division</a> in the fighting on the <a href="/wiki/Western_Front_(World_War_I)" title="Western Front (World War I)">Western Front</a>. After the war he served as <a href="/wiki/List_of_United_States_Military_Academy_alumni_(Superintendents)" title="List of United States Military Academy alumni (Superintendents)" class="mw-redirect">Superintendent of the U.S. Military Academy</a>, and as <a href="/wiki/Chief_of_Staff_of_the_United_States_Army" title="Chief of Staff of the United States Army">Chief of Staff of the United States Army</a>. He retired from the United States Army in 1937, and became a <a href="/wiki/Field_marshal_(Philippines)" title="Field marshal (Philippines)">field marshal</a> in the Philippine Army.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMorton195318.E2.80.9319_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMorton195318.E2.80.9319-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> His job was to advise the Philippine government on defense matters, and prepare the Philippine defense forces for the day when the Philippines became fully independent.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJames1970500.E2.80.93505_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJames1970500.E2.80.93505-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> When he was recalled from retirement in July 1941 to become commander of <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army_Forces_in_the_Far_East" title="United States Army Forces in the Far East">United States Army Forces in the Far East</a> (USAFFE) at the age of 61, he united the Philippine and United States Armies under one command.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMorton195318.E2.80.9319_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMorton195318.E2.80.9319-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:INF3-76_pt4_General_MacArthur_Artist_Tim.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
General Douglas MacArthur became a symbol of Allied resistance to the Japanese.</div>

<p>MacArthur faced an enormous task in getting the <a href="/wiki/Philippine_Army" title="Philippine Army">Philippine Army</a> ready for war.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMorton195325.E2.80.9330_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMorton195325.E2.80.9330-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> The Philippines had no navy at all, but, on a visit to the United States in 1937, MacArthur lobbied the <a href="/wiki/Navy_Department" title="Navy Department" class="mw-redirect">Navy Department</a> for the development of <a href="/wiki/PT_boat" title="PT boat">PT boats</a>—small, fast boats armed with torpedoes—for which he believed that the geography of the Philippines, with its shallow waters and many coves, was ideally suited.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMacArthur1964106_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMacArthur1964106-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESmith200552.E2.80.9353_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmith200552.E2.80.9353-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Philippine_Navy" title="Philippine Navy">Philippine Navy</a> acquired three, known as Q boats, after <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_Philippines" title="President of the Philippines">President</a> <a href="/wiki/Manuel_L._Quezon" title="Manuel L. Quezon">Manuel L. Quezon</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESmith200570_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmith200570-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> In August 1941, the U.S. Navy created <a href="/wiki/Motor_Torpedo_Boat_Squadron_Three" title="Motor Torpedo Boat Squadron Three">Motor Torpedo Boat Squadron Three</a>, under the command of <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant_(junior_grade)" title="Lieutenant (junior grade)">Lieutenant (junior grade)</a> <a href="/wiki/John_D._Bulkeley" title="John D. Bulkeley">John D. Bulkeley</a>. It was a half-strength squadron, with only six PT boats instead of the normal twelve, numbered 31 to 35 and 41.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESmith200552.E2.80.9353_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmith200552.E2.80.9353-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> It arrived at Manila in September 1941.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBulkley19621_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBulkley19621-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Soon after the <a href="/wiki/Philippines_Campaign_(1941%E2%80%931942)" title="Philippines Campaign (1941–1942)" class="mw-redirect">Japanese invasion of the Philippines</a>, MacArthur, in accordance with the pre-war plan, declared Manila an <a href="/wiki/Open_city" title="Open city">open city</a>, and ordered his forces on <a href="/wiki/Luzon" title="Luzon">Luzon</a> to withdraw to <a href="/wiki/Bataan" title="Bataan">Bataan</a>. The Philippine government, the <a href="/wiki/High_Commissioner" title="High Commissioner">High Commissioner</a>'s office and MacArthur's USAFFE headquarters moved to <a href="/wiki/Corregidor_Island" title="Corregidor Island" class="mw-redirect">Corregidor Island</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMorton1953160.E2.80.93164_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMorton1953160.E2.80.93164-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> Although the dependants of U.S. military personnel had been sent back to the United States, MacArthur was, until his recall from retirement, a Philippine government employee, so his family had remained in the Philippines.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEManchester1978179.E2.80.93180_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEManchester1978179.E2.80.93180-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> MacArthur's wife, <a href="/wiki/Jean_MacArthur" title="Jean MacArthur">Jean MacArthur</a>, and young son, <a href="/wiki/Arthur_MacArthur_IV" title="Arthur MacArthur IV">Arthur MacArthur IV</a>, went with him to Corregidor.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJames197574.E2.80.9375_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJames197574.E2.80.9375-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> Arthur celebrated his fourth birthday on Corregidor, on 21 February 1942.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEManchester1978229_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEManchester1978229-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> When an aide asked about Arthur's possible fate, MacArthur replied: "He is a soldier's son."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEManchester1978250_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEManchester1978250-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Most of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Asiatic_Fleet" title="United States Asiatic Fleet">United States Asiatic Fleet</a> retired to the south of the Philippines. A small force was left behind under the command of <a href="/wiki/Rear_Admiral_(United_States)" title="Rear Admiral (United States)" class="mw-redirect">Rear Admiral</a> <a href="/wiki/Francis_W._Rockwell_(admiral)" title="Francis W. Rockwell (admiral)">Francis W. Rockwell</a> consisting of the <a href="/wiki/Submarine_tender" title="Submarine tender">submarine tender</a> <a href="/wiki/USS_Canopus_(AS-9)" title="USS Canopus (AS-9)">USS&#160;<i>Canopus</i></a>, the submarine rescue ship <a href="/wiki/USS_Pigeon_(ASR-6)" title="USS Pigeon (ASR-6)"><i>Pigeon</i></a>, <a href="/wiki/Gunboat" title="Gunboat">gunboats</a> <a href="/wiki/USS_Oahu_(PR-6)" title="USS Oahu (PR-6)"><i>Oahu</i></a>, <a href="/wiki/USS_Luzon_(PR-7)" title="USS Luzon (PR-7)" class="mw-redirect"><i>Luzon</i></a> and <a href="/wiki/USS_Mindanao_(PR-8)" title="USS Mindanao (PR-8)"><i>Mindanao</i></a>, <a href="/wiki/Minesweeper_(ship)" title="Minesweeper (ship)" class="mw-redirect">minesweepers</a> <a href="/wiki/USS_Finch_(AM-9)" title="USS Finch (AM-9)"><i>Finch</i></a>, <a href="/wiki/USS_Tanager_(AM-5)" title="USS Tanager (AM-5)"><i>Tanager</i></a> and <a href="/wiki/USS_Quail_(AM-15)" title="USS Quail (AM-15)"><i>Quail</i></a>, five <a href="/wiki/Tugboat" title="Tugboat">tugboats</a>, three small patrol boats, and the PT boats of Motor Torpedo Boat Squadron Three.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBulkley19626.E2.80.9311_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBulkley19626.E2.80.9311-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> The loss of Manila and the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Naval_Base_Subic_Bay" title="U.S. Naval Base Subic Bay">U.S. Naval Base Subic Bay</a> meant that fuel and spare parts became scarce. The PT boats relied on <i>Canopus</i> and the <a href="/wiki/Floating_dry_dock" title="Floating dry dock" class="mw-redirect">floating dry dock</a> <a href="/wiki/USS_Dewey_(YFD-1)" title="USS Dewey (YFD-1)">USS&#160;<i>Dewey</i></a> for assistance with maintenance. Despite this, Motor Torpedo Boat Squadron Three continued to patrol. On 17 December, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Motor_Torpedo_Boat_PT-32&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Motor Torpedo Boat PT-32 (page does not exist)"><i>PT-32</i></a>, <a href="/wiki/Motor_Torpedo_Boat_PT-34" title="Motor Torpedo Boat PT-34"><i>PT-34</i></a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Motor_Torpedo_Boat_PT-35&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Motor Torpedo Boat PT-35 (page does not exist)"><i>PT-35</i></a> rescued 296 survivors from <a href="/wiki/HMS_Engadine_(1911)" title="HMS Engadine (1911)">SS <i>Corregidor</i></a>, which had been carrying refugees to Australia when it struck a mine and sank in Manila Bay. A week later, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Motor_Torpedo_Boat_PT-33&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Motor Torpedo Boat PT-33 (page does not exist)"><i>PT-33</i></a> ran aground while patrolling south of Manila Bay, and was set on fire to prevent her being salvaged by the Japanese. <a href="/w/index.php?title=Motor_Torpedo_Boat_PT-31&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Motor Torpedo Boat PT-31 (page does not exist)"><i>PT-31</i></a> met a similar fate a month later, after its engines failed and it drifted onto a reef.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBulkley19626.E2.80.9311_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBulkley19626.E2.80.9311-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> The PT boats attacked enemy barges off Luzon on the night of 23 January 1942, a small Japanese warship on 1 February, and a small vessel, probably a fishing trawler, on 17 February.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBulkley196213.E2.80.9315_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBulkley196213.E2.80.9315-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Decision">Decision</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Douglas_MacArthur%27s_escape_from_the_Philippines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Decision">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In a message to President <a href="/wiki/Franklin_D._Roosevelt" title="Franklin D. Roosevelt">Franklin D. Roosevelt</a> in Washington, D.C., on 11 February, MacArthur announced that he and his family intended to "share the fate of the garrison".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMorton1962194_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMorton1962194-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> Three days later, the Chief of Staff of the United States Army, <a href="/wiki/George_C._Marshall" title="George C. Marshall" class="mw-redirect">George C. Marshall</a>, urged MacArthur to send his family away, but MacArthur ignored this part of the message. In Washington, the possibility that Corregidor would fall and MacArthur would be taken prisoner was considered. MacArthur was America's most experienced general, but would be of little use in a prisoner of war camp.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMorton1962193.E2.80.93194_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMorton1962193.E2.80.93194-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> Moreover, he had become a living symbol of Allied resistance to the Japanese. The brave but doomed defense of Bataan had captured the imagination of the American public,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEManchester1978242_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEManchester1978242-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> who saw MacArthur as the only Allied general who knew how to fight the Japanese.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEManchester1978250_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEManchester1978250-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_State" title="United States Secretary of State">Secretary of State</a> <a href="/wiki/Cordell_Hull" title="Cordell Hull">Cordell Hull</a> raised the possibility of MacArthur's evacuation.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMorton1962193.E2.80.93194_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMorton1962193.E2.80.93194-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Brigadier_General_(United_States)" title="Brigadier General (United States)" class="mw-redirect">Brigadier General</a> <a href="/wiki/Dwight_Eisenhower" title="Dwight Eisenhower" class="mw-redirect">Dwight Eisenhower</a> wrote in his diary:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>I cannot help thinking that we are disturbed by editorials and reacting to "public opinion" rather than to military logic. <a href="/wiki/Edwin_%22Pa%22_Watson" title="Edwin &quot;Pa&quot; Watson">"Pa" Watson</a> is certain we must get MacArthur out, as being worth "five Army corps."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESmith2005166_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmith2005166-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>On 23 February, MacArthur received a message that had been drafted by the President, <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_War" title="Secretary of War" class="mw-redirect">Secretary of War</a> <a href="/wiki/Henry_L._Stimson" title="Henry L. Stimson">Henry L. Stimson</a> and Marshall. <sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEManchester1978252.E2.80.93254_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEManchester1978252.E2.80.93254-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> It read:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The President directs that you make arrangements to leave and proceed to Mindanao. You are directed to make this change as quickly as possible ... From Mindanao you will proceed to Australia where you will assume command of all United States troops ... Instructions will be given from here at your request for the movement of submarine or plane or both to enable you to carry out the foregoing instructions. You are authorized to take your chief of staff General Sutherland.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESmith2005165_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmith2005165-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>MacArthur responded with a request that he might select the time of his departure. "Unless the right moment is chosen for this delicate operation", he wrote, "a sudden collapse might occur."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESmith2005166_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmith2005166-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> "With regard to the actual movement", he went on, "I deem it advisable to go to Mindanao by combined use of surface craft and submarine, and thence by air, further movement by submarine being too time consuming."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESmith2005166_19-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmith2005166-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> Marshall replied that the President would allow him to choose the time and method of his departure.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEManchester1978252.E2.80.93254_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEManchester1978252.E2.80.93254-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>MacArthur inspected the PT boat squadron on 1 March. With air cover provided by his four remaining <a href="/wiki/Curtiss_P-40_Warhawk" title="Curtiss P-40 Warhawk">Curtiss P-40 Warhawks</a>, MacArthur and his wife Jean took a half-hour ride on <i><a href="/wiki/PT-41" title="PT-41" class="mw-redirect">PT-41</a></i>. Although the sea was tranquil, Jean still felt queasy.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEManchester1978252.E2.80.93254_20-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEManchester1978252.E2.80.93254-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> Ostensibly, the purpose of MacArthur's visit was presenting Bulkeley with the <a href="/wiki/Distinguished_Service_Cross_(United_States)" title="Distinguished Service Cross (United States)">Distinguished Service Cross</a> for sinking an "unidentified 5,000-ton enemy ship with torpedoes without serious damage to his ship or casualty to his crew",<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBulkley1962494_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBulkley1962494-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> but afterwards MacArthur took Bulkeley aside and asked him if it would be possible to make the 600-mile (970&#160;km) journey through uncharted waters at night in PT boats.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESmith2005168.E2.80.93169_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmith2005168.E2.80.93169-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> Bulkeley told him that it would be "a piece of cake."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESmith2005169_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmith2005169-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>When some days passed without any further word on the matter, follow-up messages were sent on 6 and 9 March. MacArthur replied that he planned to depart on 15 March,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEManchester1978252.E2.80.93254_20-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEManchester1978252.E2.80.93254-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> when the submarine <a href="/wiki/USS_Permit_(SS-178)" title="USS Permit (SS-178)">USS&#160;<i>Permit</i></a> was scheduled to arrive at Corregidor. However radio broadcasts in the United States calling for MacArthur to be placed in charge in Australia had been picked up by MacArthur's headquarters in Corregidor, and it had to be assumed that the Japanese had heard them too. There were ominous signs: Japanese surface patrols had been stepped up in the <a href="/wiki/Subic_Bay" title="Subic Bay">Subic Bay</a> area, and there were reports of Japanese destroyers heading north from the southern Philippines.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBulkley196216_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBulkley196216-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> MacArthur therefore elected not to wait for the <i>Permit</i>, but to leave as soon as possible, by PT boat on the night of 11 March.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEManchester1978252.E2.80.93254_20-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEManchester1978252.E2.80.93254-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> Major General <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_M._Wainwright_(general)" title="Jonathan M. Wainwright (general)">Jonathan Wainwright</a> would be left in command.</p>
<p>Of the decision to depart by PT boat rather than wait for the submarine, <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant_(navy)" title="Lieutenant (navy)">Lieutenant</a> <a href="/wiki/Robert_Kelly_(navy)" title="Robert Kelly (navy)" class="mw-redirect">Robert B. Kelly</a>, Executive Officer of Motor Torpedo Boat Squadron Three, and commander of <i>PT-34</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFowler_.27.27New_York_Times.27.2728_January_1989_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFowler_.27.27New_York_Times.27.2728_January_1989-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> later recalled:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Having served with Lieutenant Bulkeley as his second in command on this and a prior assignment, I was privy to much of what transpired during his conferences with General MacArthur during the decision making process. MacArthur's decision to use the PT boats for the evacuation of his part dramatically emphasized to the American public the overwhelming odds against which the United States was fighting in the Philippines. It evened an old score with the United States Navy. And since he had a tendency towards <a href="/wiki/Claustrophobia" title="Claustrophobia">claustrophobia</a> and did not relish making the trip on a submerged submarine with a commander whom he did not personally know, it provided an acceptable alternative which he elected to exercise.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESmith2005171_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmith2005171-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Escape">Escape</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Douglas_MacArthur%27s_escape_from_the_Philippines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Escape">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparations">Preparations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Douglas_MacArthur%27s_escape_from_the_Philippines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Preparations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Lieutenant_J._D._Bulkeley_k13927.jpg" class="image"><img alt="A man in a dark suit and tie, with a white peaked cap. Hears wears the two gold stripes of a lieutenamt on his sleeve, and rivvons including those of the Distinguished Service Cross and Silver Star." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Lieutenant_J._D._Bulkeley_k13927.jpg/170px-Lieutenant_J._D._Bulkeley_k13927.jpg" width="170" height="213" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Lieutenant_J._D._Bulkeley_k13927.jpg/255px-Lieutenant_J._D._Bulkeley_k13927.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Lieutenant_J._D._Bulkeley_k13927.jpg/340px-Lieutenant_J._D._Bulkeley_k13927.jpg 2x" data-file-width="585" data-file-height="732" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Lieutenant_J._D._Bulkeley_k13927.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Lieutenant J. D. Bulkeley, commander of Motor Torpedo Boat Squadron Three</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Bulkeley and his crews overhauled the PT boats for the voyage. All of the engines had performed hard war service, and had been operated for double the recommended mileage. As a result, they were reduced to operating at half speed. Since there were no replacement parts, the <a href="/wiki/Gasket" title="Gasket">gaskets</a>, which normally would have been discarded, had to be carefully cleaned and replaced. Each PT boat would carry twenty <a href="/wiki/55-gallon_drum" title="55-gallon drum" class="mw-redirect">55-gallon drums</a> of additional fuel on the deck. This reduced the top speed of the boats to about 30 knots (56&#160;km/h; 35&#160;mph).<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESmith2005173_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmith2005173-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> To make room for the passengers, Bulkeley had to leave 32 of his men behind, who would be sent to fight as infantry on Bataan.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESmith2005175_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmith2005175-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>MacArthur's chief of staff, <a href="/wiki/Major_General_(United_States)" title="Major General (United States)" class="mw-redirect">Major General</a> <a href="/wiki/Richard_K._Sutherland" title="Richard K. Sutherland">Richard K. Sutherland</a>, drew up the passenger lists.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERogers1990187.E2.80.93189_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERogers1990187.E2.80.93189-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> Rockwell and his chief of staff, Captain Herbert Ray, were ordered to accompany MacArthur.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBulkley196216_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBulkley196216-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> They were already under orders to return by submarine, but this was switched to accompanying MacArthur when his date of departure was brought forward.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESmith2005175_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmith2005175-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army_Air_Corps" title="United States Army Air Corps">United States Army Air Corps</a> officer, Major General <a href="/wiki/Harold_H._George" title="Harold H. George" class="mw-redirect">Harold H. George</a>, was requested by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army_Air_Forces" title="United States Army Air Forces">United States Army Air Forces</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERogers1990187.E2.80.93189_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERogers1990187.E2.80.93189-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>MacArthur was accompanied by his family: his wife Jean, four-year-old son Arthur,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMacArthur1964107_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMacArthur1964107-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> and Arthur's <a href="/wiki/Cantonese_people" title="Cantonese people">Cantonese</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Amah_(occupation)" title="Amah (occupation)">amah</a></i>, Ah Cheu. MacArthur later defended his decision to take her instead of an American nurse.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEManchester1978256_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEManchester1978256-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup> "Few people outside the <a href="/wiki/Orient" title="Orient">Orient</a>", he wrote, "know how completely a member of the family an <i>amah</i> can become, and Ah Cheu had been with us since Arthur's birth. Because of her relationship to my family, her death would have been certain had she been left behind."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMacArthur1964141_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMacArthur1964141-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In case a doctor was needed, Major Charles H. Morhouse was summoned from Bataan to accompany the party. The remaining thirteen were members of MacArthur's staff. They were loyal and experienced, and some of them had been with MacArthur for years. Creating a new staff in Australia would have taken time, while taking his existing one would enable him to commence work soon after arrival in Australia. They would be more valuable there than in the Philippines. Sutherland included two of his own men: his assistant, Lieutenant Colonel Francis H. Wilson, and his <a href="/wiki/Stenographer" title="Stenographer" class="mw-redirect">stenographer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Master_Sergeant" title="Master Sergeant" class="mw-redirect">Master Sergeant</a> Paul P. Rogers. Rogers, who was promoted from <a href="/wiki/Private_(rank)" title="Private (rank)">private</a> that day, was the only enlisted man on the list, which he typed. A number of men gave him letters to post.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERogers1990187.E2.80.93189_30-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERogers1990187.E2.80.93189-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Because there was no food for the passengers on the PT boats, Jean and MacArthur's aide-de-camp, Lieutenant Colonel Sidney L. Huff, packed tins of food into four <a href="/wiki/Duffel_bag" title="Duffel bag">duffel bags</a>, one for each PT boat.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEManchester1978256_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEManchester1978256-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup> Huff removed the <a href="/wiki/Four-star_rank" title="Four-star rank">four-star rank</a> number plates from MacArthur's car so they could be used in Australia, and took a mattress for the MacArthurs to lie on. Stories later circulated that it was full of cash or gold. Other stories had it that furniture from MacArthur's residence in the <a href="/wiki/Manila_Hotel" title="Manila Hotel">Manila Hotel</a> had been loaded on board the PT boats, even, in one version of the story, the piano.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMacArthur1964141_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMacArthur1964141-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup> In fact, each passenger was limited to one piece of luggage weighing 35 pounds (16&#160;kg) or less. Jean took a small suitcase with some clothes.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEManchester1978256.E2.80.93257_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEManchester1978256.E2.80.93257-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup> It sported a label from the <a href="/wiki/Hotel_New_Grand" title="Hotel New Grand">Hotel New Grand</a> in <a href="/wiki/Yokohama" title="Yokohama">Yokohama</a>, where she stayed during her honeymoon.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESmith2005186_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmith2005186-35"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup> Ah Cheu wrapped her possessions in a handkerchief. MacArthur took nothing.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEManchester1978256.E2.80.93257_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEManchester1978256.E2.80.93257-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="PT_boat_voyage">PT boat voyage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Douglas_MacArthur%27s_escape_from_the_Philippines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: PT boat voyage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Only <i>PT-41</i>, which carried MacArthur and his family, departed from Corregidor's North Dock. The passengers of the remaining boats were taken to Bataan in launches and boarded their PT boats there.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESmith2005180_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmith2005180-37"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup> While his family boarded, MacArthur spoke to Major General <a href="/wiki/George_F._Moore_(general)" title="George F. Moore (general)">George F. Moore</a>, the commander of the <a href="/wiki/Harbor_Defenses_of_Manila_and_Subic_Bays" title="Harbor Defenses of Manila and Subic Bays">Harbor Defenses of Manila and Subic Bays</a>. "George", he told him, "keep the flag flying. I'm coming back."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESmith2005187_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmith2005187-38"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><i>PT-41</i> departed at 19:45 on 11 March and joined the other three 15 minutes later.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESmith2005187_38-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmith2005187-38"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup> A navy minelayer led the PT boats through the protective minefield in single file.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEManchester1978257_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEManchester1978257-39"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup> The boats then assumed a diamond formation, with <i>PT-41</i> in the lead and <i>PT-34</i> bringing up the rear.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESmith2005189_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmith2005189-40"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup> If attacked by the Japanese, <i>PT-41</i> was to flee while the other three boats engaged the enemy.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESmith2005181_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmith2005181-41"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup> The seas were moderate, but most of the passengers quickly became seasick.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESmith2005190_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmith2005190-42"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup> MacArthur later recalled:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The weather deteriorated steadily, and towering waves buffeted our tiny, war-weary, blacked-out vessels. The spray drove against our skin like stinging pellets of <a href="/wiki/Birdshot" title="Birdshot" class="mw-redirect">birdshot</a>. We would fall into a trough, then climb up the steep water peak, only to slide down the other side. The boat would toss crazily back and forth, seeming to hang free in space as though about to breach, and would then break away and go forward with a rush. I recall describing the experience afterward as what it must be like to take a trip in a concrete mixer.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMacArthur1964143.E2.80.93144_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMacArthur1964143.E2.80.93144-43"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Lieutenant_John_D._Bulkeley_g14252.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Three men at the con of a boat. One wears a white peaked cap and holds the wheel." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Lieutenant_John_D._Bulkeley_g14252.jpg/170px-Lieutenant_John_D._Bulkeley_g14252.jpg" width="170" height="214" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Lieutenant_John_D._Bulkeley_g14252.jpg/255px-Lieutenant_John_D._Bulkeley_g14252.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Lieutenant_John_D._Bulkeley_g14252.jpg/340px-Lieutenant_John_D._Bulkeley_g14252.jpg 2x" data-file-width="580" data-file-height="731" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Lieutenant_John_D._Bulkeley_g14252.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Lieutenant John D. Bulkeley at the helm of a PT boat.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>During the night, the four boats became separated. Bulkeley spent time looking for the other three boats, but was unable to find them in the darkness. At dawn he gave up, and headed for one of the alternative hiding places.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEManchester1978259_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEManchester1978259-44"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup> Kelly's <i>PT-34</i> was the first to reach the rendezvous point, a cove on <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tagauayan_Island&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Tagauayan Island (page does not exist)">Tagauayan Island</a>, two hours late at 09:30. There was no sign of the other boats, and Rockwell was far from convinced that Kelly had the correct island. Some repairs were made, and the boat was refueled by hand pumps from the drums. Two men were posted atop the island's tallest hill to watch out for the Japanese and the other boats.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESmith2005193_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmith2005193-45"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><i>PT-32</i>, which had only two good engines, had straggled behind the others. Around dawn, Schumacher spotted what appeared to be a Japanese destroyer heading towards him. He jettisoned his fuel drums so he could increase speed and run from it.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBulkley196217.E2.80.9318_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBulkley196217.E2.80.9318-46"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup> He ordered his crew to man the .50-caliber machine guns and get ready to launch torpedoes. General Akin prepared to toss a barracks bag filled with code books overboard. However, as the light improved, and the vessel drew closer, another look though the binoculars revealed that it was not a Japanese destroyer at all, but <i>PT-41</i>, carrying an angry Bulkeley. Schumacher was ordered to recover the drums he had jettisoned, but this proved to be a time consuming task, and a dangerous one in broad daylight, and it had to be abandoned after only a few drums were recovered. Bulkeley had his gunners sink the rest. The two boats then hid for the day in a nearby cove.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESmith2005194.E2.80.93195_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmith2005194.E2.80.93195-47"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In the afternoon, <i>PT-41</i> and <i>PT-32</i> made their way to Tagauayan, where they found <i>PT-34</i>. There was a discussion about whether to proceed to Mindanao, or wait for <i>Permit</i>. Bulkeley warned that the seas might even be higher. But, since there was no assurance that the submarine would make it, MacArthur decided to continue, departing in daylight at 18:00 so as to be sure to meet their air transport there. Since <i>PT-32</i> had no fuel to make Mindanao, its passengers were divided between <i>PT-41</i> and <i>PT-34</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESmith2005196.E2.80.93197_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmith2005196.E2.80.93197-48"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup> Soon after they had departed, <i>PT-35</i> belatedly arrived at rendezvous point. Akers found the crew of <i>PT-32</i> there, and discovered that the other two boats had been and gone. He therefore set out for <a href="/wiki/Cagayan_de_Oro" title="Cagayan de Oro">Cagayan de Oro</a> as well.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERogers1990191_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERogers1990191-49"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:MacArthur%27s_Escape.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/MacArthur%27s_Escape.jpg/220px-MacArthur%27s_Escape.jpg" width="220" height="312" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/MacArthur%27s_Escape.jpg/330px-MacArthur%27s_Escape.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/MacArthur%27s_Escape.jpg/440px-MacArthur%27s_Escape.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1421" data-file-height="2016" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:MacArthur%27s_Escape.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Route of MacArthur's escape by PT boat (red) and B-17 (blue).</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>At 19:00, about an hour after they had left Tagauayan, <i>PT-34</i> and <i>PT-41</i> spotted a Japanese cruiser. Bulkeley made a sharp turn due west, and headed at top speed, about 20 knots (37&#160;km/h; 23&#160;mph), into the setting sun. Whether because of the high waves, the glare of the sun, or simple inattentiveness, the cruiser did not spot them.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESmith2005199_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmith2005199-50"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBulkley196217.E2.80.9318_46-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBulkley196217.E2.80.9318-46"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup> After midnight, the weather began to worsen, with heavy swells and sporadic squalls. Kelly later recalled:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Big foaming waves fifteen or twenty feet high thundering over the cockpit, drenching everybody. Our binoculars were full of water and our eyes so continuously drenched with stinging salt that we couldn't see, in addition to which it was pitch-black. We were making good speed through strange waters with islands all around us. We could see the outlines of the big ones—Negros and Mindanao—very dimly against the horizon through the storm. But there were dozens of small ones and probably hundreds of reefs.</p>
<p>You had to keep one hand in front of your eyes to avoid the slapping force of the water and yet you needed both to hold on.</p>
<p>The Admiral was pretty wrought up. "I've sailed every type of ship in the Navy except one of these MTBs", he shouted at me above the wind, "and this is the worst bridge I've ever been on. I wouldn't do duty on one of these for anything in the world—you can have them."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESmith2005201_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmith2005201-51"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>By dawn, the winds and swells had subsided, but the delay caused by the bad weather had slowed the two boats, and they now had to travel across the <a href="/wiki/Mindanao_Sea" title="Mindanao Sea" class="mw-redirect">Mindanao Sea</a> in daylight. Cagayan was sighted shortly after 06:30 on 13 March. Although <i>PT-34</i> had led all the way from Tagauayan, Kelly now let Bulkeley take the lead, as he had the channel charts. <i>PT-41</i> therefore pulled up at the wharf first, with MacArthur on the bow. They were met by Colonel William Morse, an officer on the staff of the Brigadier General William F. Sharp, the commander of U.S. forces on Mindanao. MacArthur told Bulkeley "I'm giving every officer and man here the <a href="/wiki/Silver_Star" title="Silver Star">Silver Star</a> for gallantry. You've taken me out of the jaws of death, and I won't forget it."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEManchester1978262.E2.80.93263_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEManchester1978262.E2.80.93263-52"><span>[</span>52<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>A few hours later, <i>PT-35</i> reached Cagayan. Willoughby later recalled:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>We were behind schedule and reached the north coast of Mindanao in broad daylight. It was a clear, dazzling day. Fortunately, no Japanese planes cut across the blue sky, though the enemy was known to make regular mail flights from Mindanao to Luzon. We were pretty conspicuous as the hours dragged on.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWilloughbyChamberlain195450_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWilloughbyChamberlain195450-53"><span>[</span>53<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>USS <i>Permit</i>, under the command of Lieutenant <a href="/w/index.php?title=Wreford_G._Chapple&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Wreford G. Chapple (page does not exist)">Wreford G. Chapple</a>, reached Tagauayan on 13 March, and found <i>PT-32</i>. With two of his three engines out of action, Schumacher felt that his boat was no longer seaworthy. He had Chapple destroy the boat with <i>Permit'</i>s deck gun. Chapple then took the fifteen <i>PT-32</i> crewmen back to Corregidor. There, eight of the crew were disembarked, while Chapple embarked forty more passengers, thirty-six of them <a href="/wiki/Codebreakers" title="Codebreakers" class="mw-redirect">codebreakers</a>. Nonetheless, Chapple was ordered to conduct a regular war patrol, which he did. He finally reached Australia on 7 April.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBlair1975193.E2.80.93194_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBlair1975193.E2.80.93194-54"><span>[</span>54<span>]</span></a></sup> Unaware of this, Bulkeley attempted to locate <i>PT-32</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBulkley196219_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBulkley196219-55"><span>[</span>55<span>]</span></a></sup> Over the next few days he flew over the area as a passenger in various aircraft, including a <a href="/wiki/Seversky_P-35" title="Seversky P-35">P-35</a> and a <a href="/wiki/P-40" title="P-40" class="mw-redirect">P-40</a>, in the hope of finding it.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWhite1942155_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWhite1942155-56"><span>[</span>56<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aircraft">Aircraft</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Douglas_MacArthur%27s_escape_from_the_Philippines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Aircraft">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The commander of U.S. Army Forces in Australia, Lieutenant General <a href="/wiki/George_Brett_(general)" title="George Brett (general)">George H. Brett</a>, received a <a href="/wiki/Radiogram_(message)" title="Radiogram (message)">radiogram</a> from General Marshall in Washington, D.C., alerting him that MacArthur would be requesting bombers to transport his party from Mindanao to Australia. A subsequent message from MacArthur requested his "most experienced pilots, and the best available planes in top condition",<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrett1947139_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrett1947139-57"><span>[</span>57<span>]</span></a></sup> but the only long-range aircraft that Brett had were <a href="/wiki/Boeing_B-17_Flying_Fortress" title="Boeing B-17 Flying Fortress">Boeing B-17 Flying Fortresses</a> of the <a href="/wiki/19th_Airlift_Wing" title="19th Airlift Wing">19th Bombardment Group</a> which had seen hard service in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Philippines_(1941%E2%80%9342)" title="Battle of the Philippines (1941–42)" class="mw-redirect">Philippines</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_East_Indies_campaign" title="Dutch East Indies campaign">Dutch East Indies campaigns</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrett1947139_57-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrett1947139-57"><span>[</span>57<span>]</span></a></sup> He therefore approached <a href="/wiki/Vice_admiral_(United_States)" title="Vice admiral (United States)">Vice Admiral</a> <a href="/wiki/Herbert_F._Leary" title="Herbert F. Leary">Herbert F. Leary</a>, the commander of naval forces in the Anzac Area, to ask for a loan of some of twelve newly arrived Navy B-17s. Leary, who had a reputation for refusing requests unless he could see how the Navy would benefit, turned Brett down.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrett1947140_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrett1947140-58"><span>[</span>58<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:292px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Boeing_B-17Es_1941-42.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Three olive green four-engined propeller aircraft fly over the ocean." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Boeing_B-17Es_1941-42.jpg/290px-Boeing_B-17Es_1941-42.jpg" width="290" height="191" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Boeing_B-17Es_1941-42.jpg/435px-Boeing_B-17Es_1941-42.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Boeing_B-17Es_1941-42.jpg/580px-Boeing_B-17Es_1941-42.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1800" data-file-height="1187" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Boeing_B-17Es_1941-42.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Boeing B-17E Flying Fortresses</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Brett therefore sent four of the 19th Bombardment Group's old planes. Two were forced to turn back with engine trouble. One of the others accidentally dumped 300 US gallons (1,100&#160;l; 250&#160;imp&#160;gal) of its fuel. The pilot flew on, and nearly made it to <a href="/wiki/Del_Monte_Field" title="Del Monte Field" class="mw-redirect">Del Monte Field</a>, but, just a few miles from his destination, the fuel tanks ran dry and the engines stopped. The B-17 crash landed in the sea. Two of the crew were killed, but the rest made it to shore, and thence to Del Monte Field. Only one B-17, piloted by Lieutenant <a href="/wiki/Harl_Pease" title="Harl Pease">Harl Pease</a>, reached Del Monte, and this B-17 was in poor condition, with no brakes and a faulty <a href="/wiki/Supercharger" title="Supercharger">supercharger</a>. Sharp ordered it back to Australia before MacArthur arrived. Despite the lack of brakes, Pease made the return trip, carrying sixteen passengers.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERogers1990191_49-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERogers1990191-49"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrett1947140_58-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrett1947140-58"><span>[</span>58<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Thus, with the arrival of <i>PT-35</i>, all of the passengers reached Mindanao safely, but there were no aircraft at Del Monte Field to meet them. They were taken to the Del Monte Plantation, where they were lodged in the guest houses, and had breakfast in the clubhouse. MacArthur sent a couple of sharp messages to Brett in <a href="/wiki/Melbourne" title="Melbourne">Melbourne</a> and Marshall in Washington. On their second day there, a Filipino woman arrived who wanted to speak to MacArthur. Her son was fighting on Luzon, and she had walked 25 miles (40&#160;km) in the hope that the general would have some news about him. He did not, but the fact that she was aware of MacArthur's presence was disturbing to the party, as the Japanese were only 30 miles (48&#160;km) away, at <a href="/wiki/Davao_City" title="Davao City">Davao</a> on the south coast of Mindanao.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERogers1990192.E2.80.93193_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERogers1990192.E2.80.93193-59"><span>[</span>59<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEManchester1978263_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEManchester1978263-60"><span>[</span>60<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Brett went back to Leary, expecting to be turned down again, but this time, Leary gave Brett the aircraft he wanted.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrett1947140_58-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrett1947140-58"><span>[</span>58<span>]</span></a></sup> The newly formed <a href="/wiki/40th_Reconnaissance_Squadron" title="40th Reconnaissance Squadron" class="mw-redirect">40th Reconnaissance Squadron</a> manned the bombers.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWatson1948407_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWatson1948407-61"><span>[</span>61<span>]</span></a></sup> One B-17 turned back, but two made it to Del Monte Field on 16 March, landing in the dark on a runway lit by flares.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWilloughbyChamberlain195452_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWilloughbyChamberlain195452-62"><span>[</span>62<span>]</span></a></sup> Lieutenant Frank P. Bostrom, the pilot of the first plane, calculated that everyone could be carried in just two planes if they left most of their baggage behind. They divided into two groups and the two bombers took off at 01:30 on 17 March. MacArthur rode in the radio operator's seat, which did not need to be manned as the aircraft were travelling under radio silence.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJames1975106_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJames1975106-63"><span>[</span>63<span>]</span></a></sup> For most of the passengers, the trip was dark and cold, with only a blanket between them and the metal skin of the aircraft.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERogers1990193_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERogers1990193-64"><span>[</span>64<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>As the two planes approached <a href="/wiki/Darwin,_Northern_Territory" title="Darwin, Northern Territory">Darwin</a>, word was received that a Japanese air raid was in progress there. The two B-17s therefore flew on to <a href="/wiki/Batchelor_Airfield" title="Batchelor Airfield">Batchelor Airfield</a>, where they touched down at 09:30. MacArthur awarded Silver Stars to the crews of the two bombers.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJames1975106_63-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJames1975106-63"><span>[</span>63<span>]</span></a></sup> Brett's chief of staff, Brigadier General <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Royce" title="Ralph Royce">Ralph Royce</a>, was on hand to greet them, and Brett had sent two <a href="/wiki/Australian_National_Airways" title="Australian National Airways">Australian National Airways</a> <a href="/wiki/DC-3" title="DC-3" class="mw-redirect">DC-3s</a> to bring them to Melbourne.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrett1947140_58-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrett1947140-58"><span>[</span>58<span>]</span></a></sup> However, Jean now refused to fly any further, so MacArthur asked for a motorcade to take them to the nearest railway station, at <a href="/wiki/Alice_Springs" title="Alice Springs">Alice Springs</a>. Sutherland had received word of an incoming Japanese air raid, and asked Morhouse to intervene. Morhouse told MacArthur that Arthur, who had suffered badly from seasickness and airsickness, was on an intravenous feed, and could not guarantee that he would survive a 1,000-mile (1,600&#160;km) trip across the desert. MacArthur then agreed to take the planes to Alice Springs. Sutherland had Huff hurry everyone onto the aircraft, which took off when the air raid siren sounded.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEManchester1978267_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEManchester1978267-65"><span>[</span>65<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>At Alice Springs, the party split up. MacArthur, his family, Sutherland, Morhouse and Huff took a special train that Brett had borrowed from the Australians, while the rest of the staff flew down to Melbourne via <a href="/wiki/Adelaide" title="Adelaide">Adelaide</a> in the DC-3s.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERogers1990194_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERogers1990194-66"><span>[</span>66<span>]</span></a></sup> His famous speech, in which he said, "I came through and I shall return", was first made at <a href="/wiki/Terowie" title="Terowie" class="mw-redirect">Terowie</a>, a small town in <a href="/wiki/South_Australia" title="South Australia">South Australia</a>, on 20 March, where he changed trains.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE.27.27Advertiser.27.2721_March_1942_67-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE.27.27Advertiser.27.2721_March_1942-67"><span>[</span>67<span>]</span></a></sup> On 21 March, MacArthur's journey was completed when his train rolled into <a href="/wiki/Spencer_Street_Station" title="Spencer Street Station" class="mw-redirect">Spencer Street Station</a>, where he was greeted by the Australian <a href="/wiki/Minister_for_Defence_(Australia)#Ministers_for_the_Army" title="Minister for Defence (Australia)">Minister for the Army</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frank_Forde" title="Frank Forde">Frank Forde</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE.27.27Courier-Mail.27.2723_March_1942_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE.27.27Courier-Mail.27.2723_March_1942-68"><span>[</span>68<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Douglas_MacArthur%27s_escape_from_the_Philippines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In April 1942, Bulkeley led his squadron in an attack on the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_cruiser_Kuma" title="Japanese cruiser Kuma">Japanese cruiser&#160;<i>Kuma</i></a>. The PT boats scored a hit on the cruiser, but the torpedo was a <a href="/wiki/Dud" title="Dud">dud</a>, and failed to explode. No damage resulted.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBulkley196221.E2.80.9324_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBulkley196221.E2.80.9324-69"><span>[</span>69<span>]</span></a></sup> With the loss of <a href="/wiki/Cebu_City" title="Cebu City">Cebu City</a>, there were no more torpedoes, so the active careers of the remaining boats of Bulkeley's squadron came to an end. MacArthur gave PT boat officers a high priority to be flown out from Mindanao. Bulkeley was flown out on MacArthur's orders on 13 April.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESmith2005234_70-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmith2005234-70"><span>[</span>70<span>]</span></a></sup> Knox, Kelly and Akers were evacuated on 23 April, and Brantingham also made one of the last flights out from Mindanao. Sharp surrendered on Mindanao on 10 May.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESmith2005240.E2.80.93241_71-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmith2005240.E2.80.93241-71"><span>[</span>71<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMorton1953574.E2.80.93578_72-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMorton1953574.E2.80.93578-72"><span>[</span>72<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>MacArthur subsequently nominated Bulkeley for the Medal of Honor. The Commander in Chief, <a href="/wiki/U.S._Fleet" title="U.S. Fleet" class="mw-redirect">U.S. Fleet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Admiral_(United_States)" title="Admiral (United States)">Admiral</a> <a href="/wiki/Ernest_King" title="Ernest King">Ernest King</a> was not going to let MacArthur award the Medal of Honor to a naval officer, so he wrote a citation for Bulkeley on behalf of the Navy.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESmith2005244_73-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmith2005244-73"><span>[</span>73<span>]</span></a></sup> President Roosevelt presented it to Bulkeley in a ceremony in the <a href="/wiki/Oval_Office" title="Oval Office">Oval Office</a> on 4 August 1942.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESmith2005250_74-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmith2005250-74"><span>[</span>74<span>]</span></a></sup> Bulkeley wrote a book about his exploits, entitled <i>They Were Expendable</i>. Parts were serialized in <i><a href="/wiki/Reader%27s_Digest" title="Reader's Digest">Reader's Digest</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Life_(magazine)" title="Life (magazine)">Life</a></i> and it became a bestseller in 1942.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWhite_.27.27Life.27.2726_October_1942_75-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWhite_.27.27Life.27.2726_October_1942-75"><span>[</span>75<span>]</span></a></sup> In 1944, it was adapted as a <a href="/wiki/They_Were_Expendable" title="They Were Expendable">movie of the same name</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Robert_Montgomery_(actor)" title="Robert Montgomery (actor)">Robert Montgomery</a> playing a character based on Bulkeley, <a href="/wiki/John_Wayne" title="John Wayne">John Wayne</a> one based on Kelly, and <a href="/wiki/Donna_Reed" title="Donna Reed">Donna Reed</a> in the role of an Army nurse with whom Kelly had a brief liaison.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESmith2005257_76-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmith2005257-76"><span>[</span>76<span>]</span></a></sup> Postwar analysis would show that most of the book's claims were exaggerated.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESmith2005255.E2.80.93256_77-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmith2005255.E2.80.93256-77"><span>[</span>77<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>On Bataan, the reaction to MacArthur's escape was mixed, with many American and Filipino troops feeling bitter and betrayed.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJames1975125.E2.80.93126_78-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJames1975125.E2.80.93126-78"><span>[</span>78<span>]</span></a></sup> When Wainwright broke the news to his generals "they were all at first depressed by the news ... But I soon saw that they understood just as I understood."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJames1975125_79-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJames1975125-79"><span>[</span>79<span>]</span></a></sup> Some people with family members in the Philippines were dismayed. One wrote to Roosevelt that "Nothing you could have done would have broken their morale and that of their parents at home so thoroughly".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJames1975126_80-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJames1975126-80"><span>[</span>80<span>]</span></a></sup> To <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Goebbels" title="Joseph Goebbels">Joseph Goebbels</a>, MacArthur was a "fleeing general", while <a href="/wiki/Benito_Mussolini" title="Benito Mussolini">Benito Mussolini</a> labeled him a coward.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEManchester1978275_81-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEManchester1978275-81"><span>[</span>81<span>]</span></a></sup> Marshall decided that the best way to counter this was to award MacArthur the Medal of Honor.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJames1975130.E2.80.93132_82-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJames1975130.E2.80.93132-82"><span>[</span>82<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The staff that MacArthur brought with him from Corregidor formed the nucleus of General Headquarters (GHQ) <a href="/wiki/South_West_Pacific_Area_(command)" title="South West Pacific Area (command)">Southwest Pacific Area</a> (SWPA).<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMorton1962253_83-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMorton1962253-83"><span>[</span>83<span>]</span></a></sup> The "Bataan Gang", as they came to be called, remained with MacArthur for the duration, and were noted for their fanatical loyalty to him.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESmith2005262_84-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmith2005262-84"><span>[</span>84<span>]</span></a></sup> So too was Bulkeley, who lauded MacArthur as "the greatest general as well as statesman since <a href="/wiki/George_Washington" title="George Washington">George Washington</a>", and hailed his decision to escape on PT boats as a stroke of genius.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESmith2005257_76-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmith2005257-76"><span>[</span>76<span>]</span></a></sup> MacArthur eventually kept his promise, and <a href="/wiki/Philippines_Campaign_(1944%E2%80%9345)" title="Philippines Campaign (1944–45)">returned to the Philippines</a>. The Bataan Gang returned to Corregidor in March 1945 on four PT boats.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESmith2005253_85-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmith2005253-85"><span>[</span>85<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
